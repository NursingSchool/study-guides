## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition and the Stress Response

Complicated stress plays a pivotal role in susceptibility to disease. Stress is unhealthy when individuals cannot adapt or when nutritional resources are depleted. Individuals we care for can show symptoms of stress and these symptoms often require healthcare interventions. One of our roles is to assess for signs and symptoms (i.e., clinical manifestations) of stress and intervene by providing nutritional therapy to promote overall health and well-being.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Review nutritional needs in times of severe stress.
- Review a case example of severe stress (e.g., burn) and nursing care through nutritional support.

Prior to this learning activity, take the time to review foundational nutritional and stress concepts and pathophysiological effects of stress from a hormonal and physiologic response, to include clinical manifestations.

## Section 3: Explore: Nursing Care—Complicated Stress: Burns

Complicated stress, such as in a client who has been burned, leads to not only an increased risk of injury, but injury impairs nutritional status, further decreasing immunity. It is a vicious cycle. The road to recovery from burns is complex and clients have many nutritional needs.

Nutritional therapy in burn care is a collaborative, interdisciplinary team approach to meet the complex needs of these clients. This learning activity will provide an overview of the specific needs of a burned client with complicated stress and associated nursing care.

## Section 4: Explore: Nutritional Needs for Burns

One of the most stressful physical traumas is burn injuries. In addition to pain control and infection management, nutritional support is one of the most significant healthcare interventions for these clients. The nutritional needs for burn injury are highly individualized and complex and can cause severe stress on the body.

According to Grodner et al. (2023), overall goals for burn nutritional therapy aim to:

- Support the skin as a barrier against pathogens.
- Meet accelerated nutritional therapy needs (adequate calories and protein, maintain pre-burn weight, supply visceral proteins, and maintain lean body muscle mass).
- Prevent nutrient deficiencies.
- Prevent Curling's ulcer (gastric stress ulcers secondary to severe burns are known as Curling's ulcer, causing increased acid production and ischemic changes, which increases the risk for bleeding).
- Manage hyperglycemia.

Nursing care involves supporting these nutritional therapy goals.

**Reference**

Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). *Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach* (8th ed.). Elsevier.

## Section 6: Explore: Energy Needs

A client with a burn injury has highly individualized energy needs. The most accurate method to determine specific energy needs is using indirect calorimetry.

> Indirect calorimetry (IC) is considered as 'the gold standard' to determine energy expenditure, by measuring pulmonary gas exchanges. It is a non-invasive technique that allows clinicians to personalize the prescription of nutrition support to the metabolic needs and promote a better clinical outcome. (Delsoglio et al., 2019, Abstract).

When indirect calorimetry is not available, then other predictive models can be used. One of the simplest ones to use is the Curreri formula.

(25 kcal x kg) + (40 kcal x % total body surface area) energy

Energy needs will vary according to the size of the burn. It is not unusual for a burn client to need 4,000–5,000 calories a day to meet energy needs (Grodner et al., 2023).

**References**

Delsoglio, M., Achamrah, N., Berger, M. M., & Pichard, C. (2019). *Indirect calorimetry in clinical practice*. *J Clin Med,* 8(9), 1387. doi: 10.3390/jcm8091387

Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). *Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach* (8th ed.). Elsevier.

## Section 8: Explore: Fluid Replacement—Nursing Care

Burns disrupt fluid distribution in the client, which can reduce circulatory volume and hinder recovery. Fluid replacement is critical to prevent dehydration, maintain circulation, and prevent further complications. Nursing care includes supporting the client's fluid replacement needs.

- Specific fluid replacement is individualized based on the client's age and weight and total surface area burned. The "Rule of Nines" is a commonly used assessment tool with percentage estimates of total body surface area (TBSA) burned (see the image below). Nursing care includes calculating fluid replacement needs based on an assessment tool. Identify in your clinical facility which assessment tool is used to calculate TBSA burned.
- Large water losses are also possible from vomiting, hemorrhage, fever, infection, or diuresis.
- Intravenous (IV) fluids are used initially.
- Provide oral fluids as soon as possible. Remember, older adults require special attention as they have depressed thirst mechanisms.

Once fluid resuscitation has been initiated, it is important to identify if appropriate perfusion, hydration, and renal function are present. Strict intake and output (I/O) is reported. It is also important to weigh the client daily because fluid loss from perspiration and/or wound drainage is difficult to calculate.

- Head and neck: 9%
- Upper limbs: 9% each
- Trunk: 36%
- Genitalia: 1%
- Lower limbs: 18% each

## Section 10: Explore: Intervention—Vitamin Replacement

Vitamins are critical for immune function, wound healing, clotting, and energy. Here are some examples:

- Vitamin C builds connective tissue and capillary walls and is important for collagen synthesis and immune function.
- Vitamin A is important for immune function and cellular repair.
- B vitamins metabolize protein and energy.
- B-complex vitamins build hemoglobin.
- Vitamin K promotes blood clotting.
- Vitamin B12 helps the body convert food into glucose, providing energy.

Exact supplemental requirements have not been identified with burn clients, however, they are commonly recommended due to the increase in energy demands.

Intravenous (IV) administration of high dose vitamin supplements improves vitamin levels. Nurses may administer medications as prescribed to support vitamin replacement needs.

## Section 12: Explore: Intervention—Mineral Replacement

Mineral replacement is equally important for burn nutritional therapy. Some examples include:

- Potassium & Phosphorus
  - Tissue catabolism may produce deficiencies.
- Sodium & Chloride
  - Fluid loss may affect these minerals.
  - Watch for hypernatremia as it is frequently reported due to extensive initial fluid resuscitation.
- Iron
  - Iron-deficiency anemia may develop from blood loss or inadequate absorption.
- Zinc & Selenium
  - Levels may be low because of inflammatory response.
  - Zinc has been found to improve the antioxidant status of burn clients and improve healing time (Wang et al., 2018).

**Reference**

Wang, X. X., Zhang, M. J., & Li, X. B. (2018). Advances in the research of zinc deficiency and zinc supplementation treatment in patients with severe burns. *Chinese Journal of Burns, 34*(1), 57–59. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29374929/

## Section 13: Explore: Interventions—Methods of Feeding

Routine intravenous (IV) fluids supply hydration and electrolytes, but do not meet energy and nutrient needs. The options for providing nutrition to a client with high energy needs to promote optimal healing, include oral, enteral, and parenteral. Read further for more information.

Methods of feeding:

- **Oral**: method of choice if it can be tolerated, if the burn is less than 20% total body surface area (TBSA), and the burn does not interfere with the oral site.
- **Enteral**: when oral feedings are not tolerated, next is the enteral route.
  - Correct placement of the gastrointestinal (GI) tube is verified by gastric content aspiration, pH testing, radiography (i.e., x-ray), and less often by auscultation.
- **Parenteral**: nourishment through peripheral veins or central veins, bypassing the GI tract.

## Section 15: Explore: Nursing Action—Enteral Feedings

Enteral feedings are prescribed by the primary care provider and the clinical registered dietitian and administered by the nurse.

There are a wide variety of commercial formulas available for enteral feeding use. Feedings are regulated by amount or rate. The rate can be continuous, usually measured in mL per hour, or by a specific amount immediately infused (bolus).

Nurses will monitor for enteral feeding-related complications: diarrhea is the most common complication. Several mechanisms have been proposed in explaining enteral-nutrition related diarrhea.

Adding fiber-rich formulas may improve bowel function and help reduce diarrhea.

**Reference**

Duncan, H. D. (2015). *Tube feeding associated diarrhea*. The Oley Foundation. https://oley.org/page/TF_assoc_diarrhea

## Section 16: Explore: Enteral Feeding Routes

There are many ways of feeding when the client requires an enteral route. These can include feeding tubes, gastronomy tubes, or nasogastric tubes. One tube used frequently is the percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG) tube. This gets its name by where it goes, and how it is inserted. Percutaneous means through the skin, an endoscope is a device with a camera, and gastronomy is a hole in the stomach.

## Section 17: Explore: Benefits of Early Enteral Nutrition

Early intervention with enteral nutrition aims to:

- Stimulate the body's mechanism for nutrition absorption through the gastrointestinal (GI) tract.
- Meet nutrient needs.
- Improve feeding tolerance.
- Decrease incidence of bacterial translocation.
- Decrease the number of infectious episodes.
- Decrease the need for antibiotic therapy.
- Improve nitrogen balance.
- Reduce urinary catecholamines.
- Diminish serum glucagon.
- Suppress hypermetabolic response.
- Enhance visceral protein status.

## Section 18: Explore: Parenteral Feedings

The parenteral method of feeding involves infusing nutrients and fluids directly into the venous system, bypassing the gastrointestinal (GI) tract completely. This is used when a client with a severe burn cannot tolerate food or formula through the GI tract.

- Peripheral parenteral nutrition is generally used for less than 14 days—for short-term nutritional support.
- Central parenteral nutrition is for large nutrient needs or full nutrition support for longer periods of time.

Remember, enteral feedings are preferred over parenteral feedings because they promote normal nutrient metabolism and maintain integrity of the gut. Parenteral nutrition bypasses the GI system.

A team of specialists including primary healthcare providers, registered dietitians, pharmacists, and registered nurses work closely together during the administration of parenteral nutrition therapy.

## Section 19: Explore: Nursing Care—Special Nutritional Therapy Needs

Consider these special nutritional need interventions while caring for a burned client.

- Clients with burns < 20% total body surface area (TBSA) can consume an oral meal plan adequate in nutrients, unless the burn site hinders eating.
- Clients require high energy intake, which includes:
  - Caloric needs that should be calculated precisely, ideally by indirect calorimetry
  - High portion of kilocalories from carbohydrates and moderate amount from fat
  - High protein intake to support wound healing
  - High vitamin and mineral intake to support nutrient deficits
  - Adequate fluid replacement to maintain hydration status
- The frequent recalculation of energy needs may be necessary if the client is gaining or losing weight.

## Section 20: Explore: Nutritional Challenges for Clients With Burns

There are tremendous nutritional challenges for clients with burn injuries or trauma.

A collaborative, interdisciplinary healthcare team approach should consider these important interventions.

- Plan of care influenced by:
  - location of burn
  - burn severity
  - comorbidities and other injuries
- Plan constantly adjusted to meet immunologic and metabolic needs.
- Critical attention is required for healing, which includes:
  - amino acids—amino acid needs are important at each stage for tissue rebuilding, fluid-electrolyte balance, and energy (kilocalorie) support
  - fluid and electrolyte balance
  - energy support
- Social support—The road to recovery may be long and stressful for both client and family members
