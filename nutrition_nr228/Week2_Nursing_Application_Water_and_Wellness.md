## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Water and Wellness

Water is vital for health and wellness. Unfortunately, access to clean water for cooking, cleaning, and bathing is not available to everyone. This lesson explores the role of the nurse in working with individuals to promote the adequate intake and use of water as part of their health and wellness routine.

By completing these learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:
- Model Chamberlain Care when communicating with clients, families, and other healthcare providers.
- Assess a client for altered fluid balance.
- Identify nursing diagnoses for clients who have altered fluid balance.
- Develop and implement a plan of care to address and intervene in clients with altered fluid balance.
- Evaluate the outcomes of clients with altered fluid balance.

## Section 3: Explore: Health History: Water Intake

When assessing a client's water intake, the nurse seeks cues related to adequate consumption of water, risk factors for fluid imbalance, and factors that contribute to fluid imbalance. For example, lactating women require approximately 700 ml of extra fluid daily to support water loss during breast milk production (Bardosono et al., 2016).

| Recommended Water Intake for Adults | | | |
|---|---|---|---|
| Male | 15 cups | 3.7 liters | 120 ounces |
| Female | 9 cups | 2.1 liters | 72 ounces |

Although fluid requirements remain the same through all stages of adulthood, physiologic changes—such as altered pituitary function and renal disease, level of activity, dietary intake, and body composition—can greatly impact fluid needs and balance.

One nutrient that influences fluid balance is sodium, which promotes fluid retention when consumed in high amounts. When obtaining the health history of a client with a fluid imbalance, the nurse should obtain information on potential sources of "hidden sodium" (processed foods, salting food prior to tasting, home water-softener). In addition to assessing sodium intake, the nurse should determine the client's knowledge of dietary sodium, its relationship to holding water in the body, and how to monitor their intake, incorporating education into the nursing plan of care as needed.

## Section 4: Explore: Nursing Care: Fluid Volume Excess (FVE)

Fluid volume excess (FVE), also referred to as fluid volume overload or edema, is a condition in which a person experiences increased fluid retention. This may result from excessive sodium or fluid intake, as well as physiological changes in normal body processes. Since increased sodium intake is a big factor in fluid volume overload, this section will review edema related to sodium intake. The American Heart Association recommends sodium intake should be about 1500 mg per day to maintain heart health (AHA, 2020).

### Tab: Contributing Factors

Fluid retention, or FVE, commonly occurs due to:
- Increased sodium levels in the blood (hypernatremia)
- Excessive fluid intake
- Excessive sodium intake
- Liver disease
- Malnutrition
- Kidney disease
- Heart failure

### Tab: Assessment Cues

- Weight gain
- Altered electrolytes
- Changes in blood pressure
- Changes in hemoglobin (Hgb) and hematocrit (Hct) values
- Edema
- Polyuria
- Shortness of breath, orthopnea, or dyspnea
- Tachycardia
- Bulging fontanelles in infants

### Tab: Nursing Actions

The primary nursing actions involve managing physiological changes (edema, shortness of breath, tachycardia) caused by the excess fluid. When FVE is severe, alterations in serum electrolytes may lead to emergent changes in respiratory, cardiovascular, or neurologic function.

Routine nursing actions include:
- Obtain daily weights
- Monitor fluid intake and output
- Implement fluid restrictions, if prescribed
- Monitor vital signs
- Assess extremities and areas of dependence for edema
- Monitor lung sounds and oxygenation status
- Administer medications, including diuretics and electrolytes, as prescribed
- Provide client education concerning the causes, symptoms, and treatment of FVE

### Tab: Evaluating Outcomes

During treatment for FVE, the nurse continues to monitor the client for cues related to over-hydration, anticipating that these will decrease as fluid balance is achieved. Client education regarding the risk factors for FVE and lifestyle changes (reducing sodium intake and adhering to prescribed therapies for known health alterations) is key to long-term control.

## Section 6: Explore: Sodium, Water, and Health

Goals for an individual with fluid volume excess (FVE) include educating the client on how to reduce sodium intake. It is important to mention a large percentage of dietary sodium comes from the consumption of processed foods while a smaller amount comes from seasoning with salt (American Heart Association, 2020).

## Section 8: Explore: Nursing Care: Fluid Volume Deficit (FVD)

Fluid volume deficit (FVD) occurs in the vascular and intracellular spaces when fluid output exceeds fluid intake (Wayne, 2022). Electrolytes within the fluid are lost in equal proportion, resulting in hypovolemia.

### Tab: Contributing Factors

Fluid loss commonly occurs through the:
- gastrointestinal tract (vomiting or diarrhea),
- renal system (polyuria)
- increased perspiration (fever, high environmental temperatures)

Decreased oral intake of water and other liquids also contributes to dehydration if the intake is less than output, particularly in infants and older adults. For example, older clients with memory impairment may forget to drink water, while those taking diuretics may purposely decrease fluid intake to decrease the number of times they urinate. People of all ages who are unable to get their own drinks (for example, infants or those confined to bed) rely on others to provide their fluids.

### Tab: Assessment Cues

| Mild to Moderate Dehydration | Severe Dehydration |
|---|---|
| Thirst | Concentrated or no urine output |
| Dry mouth | — Infants: no wet diaper for three hours |
| Listlessness | — Older children: no urination for eight hours |
| Cool, moist extremities | — Adults: no urination for eight hours |
| Reduced urine output | All ages: dark-colored urine |
| Weak pulse | Very dry skin |
| Sunken fontanel (soft spot on a baby's head) | Feeling dizzy |
| Mild irritability | Rapid heartbeat |
| | Rapid breathing |
| | Sunken eyes |
| | Sleepiness, lack of energy, confusion or irritability |
| | Fainting |

### Tab: Nursing Actions

The primary nursing action for clients in FVD is to provide fluids.
- For clients who can drink, the nurse provides fresh water and beverages of choice, balancing intake of sugar and/or caffeinated beverages that may contribute to dehydration with water.
- If the healthcare provider orders the client to "force fluids," the nurse educates the client on why it is important to increase water consumption (Wayne, 2022). A schedule that divides the fluid intake through the day should be created and shared with the client to increase their compliance with rehydration.

Other nursing actions include weighing the client daily, monitoring intake and output, reminding the client to drink, assisting the client to the toilet, as needed, and notifying the healthcare provider if the prescribed fluid intake is not being achieved through oral intake. If the client is unable to consume the required amount of fluid, intravenous fluids may be prescribed.

All clients at risk for dehydration should be educated on prevention. For clients without limitations due to health, this includes:
- Drinking mostly water
- Eating foods with high amounts of water like fruits and vegetables
- Avoiding or limiting drinks with caffeine like coffee, teas, and soft drinks
- Avoiding or limiting drinks with alcohol, which inhibits vasopressin, increasing urine production
- Avoiding or limiting sugary beverages, which can also increase urine production

### Tab: Evaluating Outcomes

During treatment for FVD, the nurse continues to monitor the client for cues related to dehydration, anticipating that these will decrease as fluid balance is achieved.

## Section 12: Explore: Nursing Care: Water Intoxication

The term "water intoxication" is generally used to refer to **hyponatremia** that occurs due to the consumption of excess water without adequate replacement of sodium, as may happen during strenuous exercise. Water intoxication is also possible when there is extensive loss of electrolytes because of dehydration, and rehydration is accomplished using only water without the addition of replacement electrolytes. Water intoxication results in physical changes in the body that, left untreated, can progress to behavioral changes, loss of consciousness, and death. Many of the behavioral changes result from hyponatremia in the brain.

### Tab: Contributing Factors

- Intense physical activity (e.g., running a marathon or military conditioning)
- Forced water consumption, often in cases of abuse
- Voluntary water consumption due to an underlying mental health condition
- Use of the drug MDMA (ecstasy or molly), often used at parties/raves where users dance, the environment is hot, and users consume a great deal of water
- Clients receiving total parenteral nutrition (TPN)

### Tab: Assessment Cues

- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Fluid overload: abdominal distention, periorbital edema, general swelling
- Head pain
- Muscle spasm, cramping, or weakness
- Hypotension
- Tachycardia
- Drowsiness
- Fatigue
- Anger, irritability
- Seizures
- Loss of consciousness

### Tab: Nursing Actions

The primary nursing actions involve managing physiological changes (edema, shortness of breath, tachycardia) caused by the excess fluid. When FVE is severe, alterations in serum electrolytes may lead to emergent changes in respiratory, cardiovascular, or neurologic function.

Routine nursing actions include:
- Obtain daily weights
- Monitor fluid intake and output
- Implement fluid restrictions, if prescribed
- Monitor vital signs
- Monitor labs: plasma and urine osmolarity, serum sodium
- Assess extremities and areas of dependence for edema
- Monitor lung sounds and oxygenation status
- Administer medications, including diuretics and electrolytes, as prescribed
- Provide client education concerning the causes, symptoms, and treatment of FVE

### Tab: Evaluation Outcomes

The goals of treatment for water intoxication include preventing brain hernia from swelling (severe cases), relieving the symptoms experienced by the client, preventing further declines in serum sodium level, and providing client education and support to prevent recurrence.
