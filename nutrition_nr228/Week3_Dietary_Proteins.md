## Section 1: Introduction to Dietary Proteins

Proteins are macronutrients needed to maintain good health. Proteins consist of amino acids and one nitrogen molecule. To maintain optimal health, the body digests proteins and restructures the amino acids to use for cell growth or energy. Like most foods, sources of protein can be both beneficial and harmful if not eaten as part of a balanced diet.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:
- Identify components and types of protein.
- Select sources of protein.
- Identify factors that impact the ingestion and digestion of protein.
- Discuss how proteins are used in the body.
- Describe the protein needs of individuals.

## Section 3: Explore: Protein Structure

Amino acids are the building blocks of protein. Proteins are the organic compounds formed when amino acids combine. The function of a protein depends on its structure and composition. Specifically, it is the way the amino acids are folded and the location of the amino acids that dictates their function in the body.

There are 20-22 amino acids that the body requires—the number reflects various ways amino acids are named. Nine (8 to 10, depending on how they are named) of the amino acids are considered essential because the body must obtain them from food. The liver can make the remaining amino acids using nutrients from a person's dietary intake. The liver can store every amino acid to ensure the body has a constant supply.

**Protein Structure:**
- Primary structure: Amino acid
- Secondary structure: α-Helixes
- Tertiary structure: Polypeptide chains
- Quaternary structure: Complex of protein molecule

## Section 4: Explore: Proteins in the Diet

Proteins are important in our diets because they help us grow, maintain our bodies, and create the multitudes of enzymes and hormones needed for the countless metabolic reactions in our bodies.

Not all proteins contain the same type or quantity of amino acids. There are two types of protein.

- **Complete proteins** contain all the essential amino acids for growth and maintenance.
  - Examples: fish, poultry, eggs, beef, pork, dairy, and soybeans.
  - Examples of plant-based complete proteins: chia, quinoa, buckwheat, and hemp seeds.
- **Incomplete proteins** lack some essential amino acids.
  - Examples: amaranth, beans, and lentils.

Foods that have high-quality proteins are expensive, such as meat (including poultry) and eggs. People in less-developed countries have fewer opportunities to eat these foods. Due to their high cost, many people cannot afford appropriate amounts of protein. Because plant-based proteins (nuts, seeds, and legumes) are less expensive and can be grown at home, plant proteins are the primary source of protein for many people and cultures worldwide. Those who eat a primarily plant-based diet can obtain all the essential amino acids by eating a variety of foods throughout the day. For example, both peanut butter and bread are incomplete proteins but they can combine to provide all essential acids. So, whether eaten together in a sandwich, or separately as toast in the morning and peanut butter on celery for an afternoon snack, they provide all elements of a complete protein.

## Section 6: Explore: Digestion and Absorption of Protein

### Digestion

Protein digestion starts in the mouth where two enzymes in the saliva, amylase and lipase, break down food. Once the protein reaches the stomach, hydrochloric acid and other enzymes called proteases break the food down further into smaller chains of amino acids. From the stomach, these smaller chains of amino acids move into the small intestine. The pancreas releases enzymes that reduce the acidity of the digested food, which allows more enzymes to work on further breaking down the amino acid chains into individual amino acids.

(Protein is first broken down into polypeptides then amino acids during digestion.)

### Absorption

Protein absorption happens in the small intestine, which contains microvilli. Microvilli are small, finger-like structures that increase the surface area for absorption in the small intestine. Once amino acids are absorbed, they are released into the bloodstream and taken to cells in the body where they start repairing tissues and building muscle.

(Amino acids are absorbed through the villi in the small intestine.)

## Section 7: Explore: Protein Metabolism—Catabolism

The liver is the principal site of amino acid metabolism, but other tissues, such as the kidney, the small intestine, muscles, and adipose tissue, take part. Generally, the first step in the breakdown of amino acids is the separation of the amino group from the carbon skeleton, usually by a transamination reaction. This breakdown of proteins is referred to as **catabolism**. Catabolism is enhanced by hormones secreted by the adrenal cortex.

Amino acids are removed from the blood by the liver cells and deaminated (the amino or nitrogen group is cleaved or taken off the molecule). The carbon chain remaining after this deamination process is a keto acid. Some keto acids are rearranged into the **glucose molecule** and can be used as a fuel source or stored as fat. Some other keto acids are rearranged chemically into **acetyl coenzymes** and can be used as a fuel. Still, other keto acids may be rearranged into **fatty acids** and stored as fat. However, the amino group (nitrogen section) is toxic and is usually excreted from the body in the form of urea. Urea diffuses into the blood and is then excreted by the kidneys.

**A healthy adult should have a zero-nitrogen balance**. This means a person will excrete nitrogen at the same rate of intake.

- A positive nitrogen balance occurs when more nitrogen is retained in the body than is excreted. A positive nitrogen balance occurs in growing children and pregnant women who are protein deficient.
- A negative nitrogen balance occurs when more nitrogen is excreted than retained from dietary foods. This occurs because of aging, physical illness, stress, or starvation.

## Section 9: Explore: Protein Metabolism—Anabolism

The major use of proteins is anabolism, the making of new cells. **Anabolism** centers around growth and building—the organization of molecules. In this process, small, simple molecules are built up into larger, more complex ones. An example of anabolism is gluconeogenesis. This is when the liver and kidneys produce glucose from noncarbohydrate sources.

Anabolism derives its energy from the **catabolism process**. Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is used in this process, fueled by anabolic hormones.

Other anabolic processes include bone mineralization and muscle mass growth. In some instances, synthetic hormones have been used as a shortcut to the body's own metabolism. Anabolic steroids are one example of this. Using anabolic steroids increases muscle mass by stimulating protein synthesis and muscle growth.

## Section 10: Explore: Function of Proteins

### How the Body Uses Protein

- **Growth and Maintenance:** The body requires a constant supply of amino acids to promote growth, and replace or repair cells.
- **Immune System Response:** The body uses protein to produce antibodies that can respond to bacteria and viruses.
- **Enzymes:** The body uses amino acids to make enzymes, such as trypsin, chymotrypsin, and carboxypeptidase, which are used to digest protein.
- **Fluid and Electrolyte Regulation:** In the body, proteins and minerals attract water, thus creating osmotic pressure. The pressure produced by proteins and minerals keeps water in a state of equilibrium between the compartments.
- **Acid-Base Balance:** Proteins are used by the body as a buffer, promoting a healthy internal pH.
- **Transportation:** Proteins transport nutrients in and out of the cells. For example, hemoglobin and myoglobin are proteins that store oxygen.

## Section 11: Explore: Protein Needs Across the Life Span

The recommended daily allowance (RDA) for protein for an adult is 0.8 grams per kilogram (grams/kg). For the average male adult, that is about 56–63 grams per day and 46–50 grams per day for the average female adult. Factors that influence a client's daily protein requirements include age, physiologic state, and level of physical activity.

- **Age:** Childhood protein requirements are higher (1 gram/kg) due to rapid growth. Current evidence (Hruby et al., 2020) supports that the decrease in total body weight that occurs with aging supports that increased protein intake (over 64 grams per day, or 1–1.5 grams/kg/day) helped middle-aged, high-functioning U.S. adults maintain their physical function better over two decades than those with normal or low protein intake.
- **Physiologic state:** Women who are pregnant or lactating require additional intake of protein. In addition, wound healing, fevers, and physical illness increase the body's need for extra proteins.
- **Athletes:** It is common for some athletes to consume up to 1.2–2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight (grams/kg). However, it is **not** recommended to exceed double the recommended daily allowance (or 1.6 grams/kg/day) of protein.

## Section 12: Explore: Protein Serving Sizes

The serving size for animal-based protein is 1 cup, which is about the size of a deck of playing cards or the palm of an average-sized adult's hand. Plant-based protein portions will vary by the food. Individuals consuming most of their protein from plant-based sources should be aware of the total protein content per serving.

Using the body is good for teaching portion control:
- A clinched fist is about a cup
- An open or cupped palm is used to estimate the size of a protein serving
- 2 palms are about 2 cups
- A thumb is used to indicate an ounce

## Section 14: Explore: Protein Excess

As good for the body as protein is, eating too much can be harmful, as well. Although high protein-based diets promote weight loss by eliminating a food group (carbohydrates), they also represent an unbalanced diet that may lack essential nutrients for health. As the body digests and metabolizes large amounts of protein, keto acids increase in the body, which may lead to ketoacidosis, a potentially dangerous acid-base imbalance.

Excessive protein intake is contraindicated in clients living with decreased renal function, including chronic kidney disease and renal impairment, as their bodies are unable to excrete the urea created by protein metabolism.

Even when an individual's total protein intake per day is not excessive, the quality of the protein can greatly impact their health. Because many animal-based proteins contain high amounts of saturated fat, the incidence of diseases such as atherosclerosis and hypertension may increase. For this reason, a variety of protein sources should be consumed in a well-balanced diet.

## Section 16: Explore: Protein Deficiency

Protein deficiencies can be seen in clients suffering from starvation. This means the client is lacking the intake of food for protein and/or energy. Protein deficiencies can be the result of poor diet or alterations in protein absorption. Both marasmus and kwashiorkor are diseases related to protein malnutrition that result in severe changes in the body.

Health conditions, medications, and physical and social factors can cause protein deficiencies. In America, populations at greatest risk for protein insufficiency are the low income, homeless, and elderly clients. In addition, clients with abnormal metabolism, cystic fibrosis, renal failure, cancer, heart failure, end-stage liver disease, and a fatty diet can be at risk for protein deficiencies (Marcel, 2017). The progression of physical changes related to protein malnutrition occurs along a continuum. The early signs continue as the lack of dietary protein continues.

Individuals with less severe decreases in protein intake may exhibit the following signs and symptoms:
- skin, hair, and nail problems
- loss of muscle mass
- increased risk of bone fractures
- bigger appetite and increased calorie intake
- risk of infections
- fatty liver
- slower growth in children

Globally, more than one third of childhood deaths are connected to undernutrition (LibreTexts, 2020). Marasmus (starvation) affects more than 50 million children under age 5 worldwide. Marasmus is characterized by an extreme emaciated appearance, poor skin health, hair loss, muscle loss, and growth retardation, with symptoms including acute fatigue, hunger, and diarrhea. Kwashiorkor is a disease brought on by a severe dietary protein deficiency. Its symptoms include edema (swelling) of the legs and feet, hair loss, anemia, emaciation (loss of muscle and fat) with a pot-belly, slow growth (children), and shiny skin.
