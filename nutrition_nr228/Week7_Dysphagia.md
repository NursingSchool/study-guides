## Section 1: Introduction to Dysphagia

When people think of gastrointestinal (GI) disorders, swallowing difficulties are not the first thing that comes to mind, but complications with swallowing can have great impacts on a client's nutritional status. Dysphagia is a swallowing disorder that causes difficulty or pain while swallowing or attempting to swallow.

This learning activity focuses on the causes, warning signs, and strategies to safely care for a client with dysphagia. By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Understand the causes of dysphagia.
- Identify the clinical manifestations of dysphagia.
- Develop feeding strategies to support the safety and nutritional intake of clients living with dysphagia.
- Identify possible causes of dysphagia.
- Identify warning signs of dysphagia.
- Identify feeding strategies for clients with dysphagia.

## Section 3: Swallowing Phases and Dysphagia

Swallowing occurs using a series of complex events or phases.

### Tab: Preparatory Phase (Not Pictured)

This phase involves the person getting the food to their mouth.

Clients with mobility issues in their upper extremities (e.g., tremors, weakness, decreased range of motion) may have difficulty getting food to their mouths and should be assessed by the nurse, even the client does not have dysphagia.

### Tab: Oral Phase

This phase occurs when the food enters the mouth and ends when the food bolus enters the pharynx. The primary occurrences during this phase are chewing the food, mixing the food with saliva, keeping the food in a bolus, and beginning to swallow.

Symptoms of chocking that require immediate follow-up by the nurse include coughing, choking, sensation of food getting stuck, persistent drooling of saliva, gurgled (or wet sounding) speech, and bringing food back up (regurgitation).

### Tab: Pharyngeal Phase

This phase begins as the bolus enters the oral pharynx and ends when it passes into the esophagus. Aspiration occurs during this phase due to the proximity of the openings to the trachea (anterior) and esophagus (posterior) in the neck, making this phase the least safe for the client. The epiglottis and other muscles involved with swallowing play a key role in preventing the food bolus from entering the trachea.

### Tab: Esophageal Phase

This phase begins as the bolus enters the esophagus and ends when it passes into the stomach. The esophageal muscles contract in a rhythmic manner, referred to as peristalsis, to propel the food through the esophagus to the stomach.

Learn more about the relationship between normal swallowing and the changes that happen with dysphagia by viewing the ["Swallowing Phases/Dysphagia Review"](https://youtu.be/uKkySqvA1II) [4:05] video.

## Section 4: Types of Dysphagia

Dysphagia is a swallowing disorder that causes difficulty or pain while swallowing or attempting to swallow. Difficulty in swallowing can be caused by:

- structural dysphagia
- esophageal motility

### Tab: Structural Dysphagia

Structural dysphagia results from changes to the actual structure of the esophagus can be caused by:

- obstruction
- inflammation
- edema
- surgery

### Tab: Esophageal Motility

Esophageal motility (movement) issues can be caused by:

- amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)
- myasthenia gravis
- cerebrovascular accident
- traumatic brain injury
- cerebral palsy
- Parkinson's disease
- multiple sclerosis

## Section 5: Dysphagia Diet

The loss of the ability to swallow can have a profound impact on a person's oral intake and nutritional status and greatly increase the risk of aspiration. The International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative (IDDSI, 2019) has an evidence-based ranking of both solid and liquid food preparations to use based on the needs of the clients.

Explore details on how IDDSI defines each phase of the dysphagia diet by reviewing the [IDDSI Framework](https://iddsi.org/Framework).

## Section 7: Individualized Care

Thin liquids may be thickened to facilitate swallowing and decrease the risk of aspiration. No two clients with dysphagia are alike. Therefore, the diet must be individualized according to the swallowing ability and personal food preferences of the client. Food must be served in a form that fits the specific anatomic and functional needs of the client.

## Section 9: Aspiration Precautions

When clients are diagnosed with dysphagia, there are specific recommendations for feeding to avoid aspiration.

- Position the client upright, bent slightly forward, with the chin tucked and head tilted forward.
- Eliminate distractions so the client can focus all their attention on the meal.
- The feeding assistant should sit at or below the client's eye level while feeding.
- Avoid asking them to talk while eating.
- Instruct the client not to use liquids to clear the mouth of foods. Liquids should be used only after they have cleared food from the mouth.
- Encourage frequent, dry swallows or coughing to help clear food from the mouth between bites.
- Encourage small bites (1 teaspoon of solid food or about 10–15 mL of liquid), especially if the client's ability to manage food is impaired.
- Allow adequate time to eat (for example, 20–30 minutes).
- Use spoons rather than cups; clients have less difficulty getting food and liquid this way.
- A straw may be used by some to drink liquids and thinned foods.
- While eating, check for voice quality. A wet or gurgled voice indicates food may be resting on the vocal cords.

During meals, supervision is necessary to prevent or minimize swallowing problems. Clients should be reevaluated regularly to determine whether any changes need to be made to the consistency of fluids or food. Sometimes clients eat too quickly and then choke when trying to swallow. Staff can observe and supervise while clients eat to remind them to complete the swallowing sequence before taking the next bite of food.

## Section 11: Feeding Techniques

Various feeding techniques may facilitate safe swallowing:

- Serve small, frequent meals to help maximize intake.
- Encourage clients with dysphagia to rest before mealtime. Postpone meals if the client is fatigued.
- Give mouth care immediately before meals to enhance the sense of taste.

Learn more techniques to assist clients with dysphagia to eat safely by watching the ["Guide to Feeding Someone With Dysphagia"](https://youtu.be/p-8SU4x9iIo) [1:58] video.

## Section 12: Living With Dysphagia

Living with dysphagia can be hard for the person with the diagnosis, but it can also be difficult for family and friends. Meals together might be missed as the individual with dysphagia is embarrassed to eat in front of others or is fearful of choking.

Creating meals that are nutritious, delicious, and easy to prepare can be a challenge. [Beyond the Blender](https://www.griffith.edu.au/__data/assets/pdf_file/0012/540012/Beyond-the-Blender-dysphagia-cookbook.pdf) is a cookbook developed with the special dietary needs of those with a diagnosis of dysphagia. Various recipes can be good recommendations for clients.
