## Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition Application: Nutrition, Health & Wellness

The digestive system, food labels, and many other factors influence a client's food selection and health maintenance. The digestive system plays an important role in the absorption of essential nutrients in order to maintain health. Food choice and food liking influence each client differently. Healthcare providers must assess all factors that affect a client's diet choices for education and interventions to be useful.

By completing these learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:
- Utilize information about the digestive system, food labels, and other nutritional factors to care for clients.
- Assist clients in selecting healthy foods that maintain good health.

## Section 3: Explore: Assessing the Social Determinants of Health and Nutrition

While providing care, the nurse must assess all factors of the client's environment in order to address any barriers the client and their family may encounter during the healing process. Social Determinants of Health—such as economic stability, neighborhood, education, food, community, and healthcare care systems—are factors that will affect this client's health and healing at home.

The nurse must analyze all factors and develop nursing interventions that are client-centered and measurable.

Let's review how a post-transplant client's assessment intersects with the Social Determinants of Health.

### The Importance of Assessment

It is important to note that nurses must assess the Social Determinants of Health on **all** client populations (pregnant, elderly, homeless, etc.) in order to address factors that will prevent the client from maintaining good health at home.

**Assessment Transcript:**
- Economic stability: The nurse must assess the family's financial stability since the client will not be able to work. The nurse may recommend social programs that may assist the family with healthy meals and medications.
- Neighborhood and physical environment: The nurse will assess the family's access to parks or walkways for physical activity. The nurse must also evaluate if the client has reliable transportation to follow-up appointments such as car, taxi, public transportation.
- Education-health literacy: The nurse will assess the clients' ability to process and understand basic health information. The nurse will continually assess the client's verbal and behavioral cues to indicate learning has taken place.
- Food: The nurse will assess if the client has access to nutrient-dense food. MyPlate recommends if clients cannot consume fresh whole foods, then frozen and canned foods are good options.
- Community and social context: The nurse will assess the client's community support structure, such as meals on wheels or transplant support groups.
- Health Care System: The nurse will assess the client's access to healthcare resources such as dietitians or nutritionists. It is essential for the nurse to remember that lifestyle and diet changes are incremental. This means the client will need constant follow-up to be successful.

## Section 4: Explore: Client Education: Healthy Eating

When providing diet and nutrition education, the nurse should include the client and the individual responsible for acquiring food and preparing meals. Consideration must be given to the ages, health, and nutritional needs of all members of the household, not just the client. The family is included because parents have the biggest influence on the diet of their children.

Not only is the family a vital support structure for the client, the family's food preferences influence what is available in the home. Parents can influence children's food choices and long-term health by including the children in meal planning from a young age.

## Section 5: Explore: Client Education: Healthy Eating Across the Lifespan

While teaching a family how to consume less processed foods, the nurse recommends the family consume fresh fish rather than hot dogs as their main protein for two meals a week. During the education session, the nurse is intentional in educating the family about the appropriate caloric intake for each family member and the serving sizes for each age group (U.S. Department of Agriculture, n.d.).
