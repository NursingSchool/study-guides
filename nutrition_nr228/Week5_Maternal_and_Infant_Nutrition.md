Aiden (pronouns: he/him/his) and Natalie (pronouns: she/her/hers) have decided to start a family. Let's follow them on their journey and learn about nutrition during preconception, pregnancy, and infant stages.

## Section 3: Explore: Preconception Maternal Nutrition

Almost half of all pregnancies in the United States are not planned. Therefore, pregnancy planning is a focus of Healthy People 2030 (n.d.). Natalie knows that preconception care is an important component of preparing for pregnancy. Nutrition and weight are major factors for a healthy fetus. Natalie is scheduled for an appointment with her healthcare provider to discuss her and Aiden's preconception health.

### Tab: Maternal Pre-Pregnancy Weight and Associated Risks

The healthcare provider tells Natalie that she has a body mass index (BMI) of 22.3, which is a normal weight. According to Eilender (2016), there is a relationship between maternal pre-pregnancy weight and birth outcomes. Referral to dietitians, peer support groups, and professional resources may be useful in helping women improve their nutritional status before pregnancy. Extreme weight-loss diets should be avoided. Review the risks associated with clients who are underweight and overweight.

**If a client is underweight:**

- Risks to the **client** include: infertility, nutritional deficiencies, heart irregularities, osteoporosis, amenorrhea
- Risks to the **newborn** include: preterm birth, low birth weight

**If a client is obese:**

- Risks to the **client** include: preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, cesarean delivery
- Risks to the **newborn** include: small-for-gestational-age, stillbirth, neonatal death, neural tube defect, congenital heart problems

### Tab: Vitamins and Minerals for Pregnancy

Natalie is concerned about her diet, as she views herself as a "picky eater." The healthcare provider replies that women of reproductive age generally do not get enough vitamins A, C, B6, and E, as well as folic acid, calcium, iron, zinc, and magnesium in their diets. Routine use of supplements improves maternal health and birth outcomes. This paired with a healthy diet can help both mother and child, so Natalie starts on a multivitamin supplement.

**Important Minerals and Vitamins for Pregnancy**

Minerals:

- Zinc — For organ formation and immune system development
- Iron — For red blood cell formation
- Calcium — To develop strong bones and teeth

Vitamins:

- Folic Acid/Folate — Helps prevent neural tube defects
- Vitamin A — Helps develop healthy eyesight, boost protein synthesis and cell differentiation, and maintain healthy skin
- Vitamin B1, B2, B3, and B6 — Used in energy metabolism
- Vitamin B12 — Helps form DNA and red blood cells, and maintains the central nervous system
- Vitamin C — Helps promote healthy bones, teeth, and gums
- Vitamin D — To absorb and use calcium
- Vitamin E — Acts as an antioxidant to protect cell structures, keeps the immune system strong, and helps the body use vitamin K

Eilender, E. (2016). *Nutrition throughout the lifecycle.* Monument Press. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/947086415

## Section 4: Explore: Preconception Paternal Nutrition

Preconception care for Aiden is just as important as it is for Natalie. The primary focus is on fertility and decreasing obesity.

The healthcare provider tells Aiden that his body mass index (BMI) is 27.4, which puts him in the overweight category. Increased weight can cause lower testosterone levels, poor sperm quality, and reduced fertility compared to men of normal weight. Maintaining a healthy weight for men is as important as it is for women in the preconception time, so diet was discussed.

The healthcare provider was also concerned about the fact that Aiden smokes and drinks alcohol occasionally. DNA in sperm can be damaged by tobacco, alcohol, drugs, caffeine, and poor diet. Certain medical conditions, such as diabetes, can also reduce sperm count and quality. Since new sperms are created every 42 to 76 days, damaged sperms can be replaced (Eilender, 2016). The healthcare provider encouraged Aiden to quit smoking and drinking. The provider gave him resources for both. Aiden was also encouraged to take a multivitamin because adequate levels of zinc and folate have properties that protect sperm against oxidative stress and DNA damage.

**Reference**

Eilender, E. (2016). *Nutrition throughout the lifecycle*. Monument Press. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/947086415

## Section 6: Explore: Nutrition During Pregnancy

Three months after their preconception care visit, Aiden and Natalie are expecting a baby! Select each tab to learn about important nutrients and foods to avoid during pregnancy.

### Tab: Nutrition During Pregnancy

Natalie will continue to take her multivitamin supplements during pregnancy. She plans to get plenty of fruits and vegetables, lean proteins, whole grains, and healthy fats. She knows she needs to focus on getting folic acid from leafy green vegetables and citrus fruits. Also, Natalie and the fetus need calcium and vitamin D, so she buys fortified juice and milk. Protein and iron are also important, so she plans to eat roast beef, chicken, and beans.

**Important!** If iron supplements are taken with orange juice, avoid the calcium-fortified versions. Although calcium is an essential nutrient during pregnancy, calcium can decrease iron absorption.

### Tab: Foods to Avoid

Natalie's obstetrician/gynecologist (OB/GYN) also tells her about foods to avoid to prevent foodborne illnesses and infections, including:

- sushi
- sashimi
- raw oysters
- raw clams
- raw scallops
- ceviche
- undercooked meat and poultry
- unpasteurized juice or cider
- cheese made with unpasteurized milk
- raw milk
- raw eggs
- raw sprouts
- raw dough or batter

## Section 7: Explore: Pregnancy and Weight Gain

Natalie has heard that "women need to 'eat for two' when they are pregnant." About 48% of women follow that misconception and gain too much weight during pregnancy (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2022).

Weight gained during pregnancy can impact the mother and baby long-term. A pregnant woman only requires 350 to 450 additional calories daily. Normal weight gain should be 2 to 5 pounds in the first trimester and then a pound each week until the end of pregnancy.

Natalie's obstetrician/gynecologist (OB/GYN) gave her the following chart and encouraged her to track her weight gain throughout pregnancy.

| If before pregnancy, you were: | You should gain: |
| --- | --- |
| Underweight: BMI < 18.5 | 28–40 pounds |
| Normal weight: BMI 18.5–24.9 | 25–35 pounds |
| Overweight: BMI 25.0–29.9 | 15–25 pounds |
| Obese: BMI ≥ 30.0 | 11–20 pounds |

**Note — You should keep track of your weight gain because:**

- If you gain less than the recommended amount of weight, you could deliver a baby that is too small.
- If you gain more than the recommended amount of weight, you could have a baby that is too large and have delivery complications or a caesarean delivery. Gaining more weight can also increase the amount of weight you have after pregnancy, which can lead to obesity.

**Reference**

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2022, June 13). *Weight gain during pregnancy*. https://www.cdc.gov/reproductivehealth/maternalinfanthealth/pregnancy-weight-gain.htm

## Section 9: Explore: Conditions That Affect Pregnancy Nutrition

In her first trimester, Natalie started to experience nausea with severe vomiting. She thought it was just morning sickness, but it seemed to be getting worse. She noticed an aversion to foods, weight loss, headaches, fatigue, and feeling faint. Her obstetrician/gynecologist (OB/GYN) diagnosed her with hyperemesis gravidarum. Natalie was hospitalized and given intravenous (IV) fluids. Although she did not want to take medication while pregnant, Natalie needed metoclopramide, an anti-reflux medication, to be able to eat food again. The benefits of the medications outweigh the risks of hyperemesis.

While she was in the hospital, Natalie's blood glucose level was checked for gestational diabetes and her iron levels were checked for anemia. In gestational diabetes, hormone changes cause the body to utilize insulin insufficiently. Since it can affect both maternal and fetal health, Natalie knows her blood glucose levels should be as close to normal as possible. She also knows she needs additional iron because she is producing blood for herself and the baby. Her labs came back normal but will be tested again later in her pregnancy.

**Distinguishing Between Morning Sickness and Hyperemesis**

**Morning Sickness:**

- Nausea **sometimes** accompanied by vomiting
- Nausea that **subsides at 12 weeks** or soon after
- Vomiting that **does not** cause severe dehydration
- Vomiting that **allows** you to keep some food down

**Hyperemesis Gravidarum:**

- Nausea accompanied by **severe** vomiting
- Nausea that **does not subside**
- Vomiting that causes **severe dehydration**
- Vomiting that **does not allow** you to keep any food down

## Section 11: Explore: Infant Nutrition

Natalie and Aiden welcomed a baby girl into their family! Miranda (pronouns: she/her/hers) was 8 pounds, 3 ounces. Typically, newborns will lose weight in the first couple of days after birth due to loss of fluid and meconium. They should then gain about 1 ounce per day. Nutrient requirements per pound are proportionally higher than at any other time in a newborn's life.

Natalie has decided to breastfeed Miranda. Although weight gain will be lower compared to formula-fed infants, breast milk is the best source of nutrients for infants.

## Section 12: Explore: Breastfeeding

The American Academy of Pediatrics (2012) continues to recommend exclusively breastfeeding infants for the first 6 months and then breastfeeding in combination with introducing foods until at least one year old. It is important for mothers to understand the benefits of breastfeeding, nutrition for breastfeeding, and proper ways of storing breast milk.

### Tab: Benefits of Breastfeeding

When Natalie first started feeding Miranda, the breast milk was yellowish and thick. This substance, colostrum, contains antibodies, white blood cells, vitamins, and a high level of protein. Although only 1–4 teaspoons are made a day, it provides enough nutrients until more mature milk comes in about 5 days after birth (American Pregnancy Association, n.d.).

Natalie is not only breastfeeding but also giving Miranda vitamin D and iron supplements, since babies need more than what breast milk provides. Vitamin D drops were started at birth and iron at 4 months (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2022b). A one-time vitamin K injection was given at birth since all newborns are low in vitamin K.

### Tab: Nutrition for Breastfeeding Mothers

The pediatrician gave Natalie information on diet considerations for breastfeeding mothers. Natalie should be consuming 2,300 to 2,500 kcal per day instead of 1,800 to 2,000 kcal per day when she was not pregnant (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2022a). She is continuing to take a multivitamin to ensure adequate nutrition. If Natalie was vegan or a lacto-ovo vegetarian, she would need to take vitamin B12 as well. She is also following the pediatrician's advice on limiting seafood due to the mercury content and limiting caffeine. Any food or beverage consumed is passed to the baby through breast milk. Caffeine can reduce the absorption of iron in the milk.

### Tab: Proper Storage of Breast Milk

After 3 months, Natalie returns to work and Miranda goes to daycare. Natalie pumps breast milk so that Miranda can have it at daycare. It is important for Natalie to know how to store breast milk safely to retain the quality of the milk.

| Type of Breast Milk | Countertop — 77°F (25°C) or colder (room temperature) | Refrigerator — 40°F (4°C) | Freezer — 0°F (-18°C) or colder |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Freshly Expressed or Pumped | Up to 4 hours | Up to 4 days | Within 6 months is best; up to 12 months is acceptable |
| Thawed, Previously Frozen | 1–2 hours | Up to 1 day (24 hours) | Never refreeze breast milk after it has been thawed. |
| Leftover From a Feeding (infant did not finish the bottle) | Use within 2 hours after the infant is finished feeding. | | |

## Section 13: Explore: Formula

Four months into breastfeeding, Miranda stopped showing interest in breastfeeding and more interest in bottles. Natalie continued to pump but developed mastitis, which is a breast infection requiring antibiotics. Natalie switched Miranda to a commercially-prepared, iron-fortified formula. She was surprised that infant formulas existed as ready-to-feed liquids, concentrated liquids, and powders.

When using the powder, Natalie used bottled water or boiled-then-cooled tap water. Formula should be discarded after one hour of serving. Prepared formula may be refrigerated for up to 24 hours.

**Note:** To save money, some parents dilute formula or give water to the infant in addition to breast milk or formula. This can lead to a dangerous condition called water intoxication. Extra water reduces the nutrients, slows growth and development, disturbs electrolyte balance, and may cause seizures. Always mix the formula as directed by the manufacturer.

At a pediatric appointment, Natalie asked the nurse why Miranda just couldn't have cow's milk. The nurse stated that infants cannot digest it completely. Also, cow's milk lacks the proper amounts of vitamins such as iron and vitamin C. Therefore, infants should not consume cow's milk in the first year of life.

## Section 15: Explore: Introducing Foods

At six months, Miranda started showing signs of readiness for solid foods. She could sit up, control her head, open her mouth when presented with food, attempt to grab food, and act hungry after formula feedings (Eilender, 2016). Aiden mixed a couple of spoonfuls of infant cereal with the formula into a thin consistency and fed it to Miranda with her usual formula feedings. He would never put the cereal in the bottle because he knew this presented a choking hazard.

Aiden and Natalie introduced one new, single-ingredient food per week. They tried pureed meats, vegetables, and fruits to see what Miranda liked. Eventually, Miranda would try to feed herself, so Aiden and Natalie offered her finger foods in small pieces (CDC, 2022).

Aiden and Natalie knew that they should avoid giving Miranda honey, unpasteurized drinks or foods, and cow's milk. They also knew not to add salt or sugar to Miranda's food. Foods high in sodium, such as canned foods and processed meats, should be avoided, and fruit juices should be limited.

**Introducing Foods (finger food examples)**

- Bananas, strawberries, and pears
- Scrambled eggs
- Soft, cooked pasta
- Crackers
- Finely chopped, cooked chicken and beef
- Cooked, small pieces of carrots, potatoes, and peas

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2022, May 17). *Foods & drinks for 6 to 24 month olds: Foods and drinks to limit.* https://www.cdc.gov/nutrition/InfantandToddlerNutrition/foods-and-drinks/foods-and-drinks-to-limit.html
