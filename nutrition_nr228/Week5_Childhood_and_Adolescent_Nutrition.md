## Section 1: Introduction to Childhood and Adolescent Nutrition

During childhood, children learn about healthy eating habits and form attitudes about food and food choices. There is incredible cognitive and physical development during this time.

This learning activity will cover the preschool years (ages 2-6), the school-aged years (ages 6-12), and adolescents (ages 12-18). You will follow a family with four children—Jake (aged 17), Josh (aged 15), Amanda (aged 12), and Karisa (aged 7).

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Describe nutrient requirements for early and middle childhood.
- Describe nutrient requirements for adolescents.
- Identify factors that influence nutrition in childhood.
- Discuss school and community programs available for children.

## Section 3: Explore: Nutrient Requirements of Early and Middle Childhood

Compared to infants, who triple their birth weight in the first year, young children have a slow down in growth. Children grow in spurts of height and weight followed by periods of little to no growth.

### Tab: Measuring Growth

Growth is measured by body mass index (BMI) charts that are age and gender-specific. The BMI is compared to that of other children of the same gender and age. The chart is divided into percentiles that indicate whether a child has a healthy weight, is underweight, overweight, or obese.

View these resources from the CDC, [Boys Stature Age Weight Growth Chart](https://cdn-media.adtalem.com/2dett4d/ATGE/BF/at/6xkt2vk7qpmrvrpjk7rjh4r/CDCBoysStatureAgeWeightGrowthChart.jpg) and [Girls Stature Age Weight Growth Chart](https://cdn-media.adtalem.com/2dett4d/ATGE/BF/at/pbr65fnccr867spf4sph7455/CDCGirlsStatureAgeWeightGrowthChart.jpg), as examples to understand how a child's growth is measured at each stage of life.

### Tab: Calorie Requirements for Growth

As children transition from toddlers to about age 10, their calorie requirements decrease due to slowing growth rates. After age 10 and through puberty, there are wider differences in calorie recommendations. Review the table shown here.

| Male | 1-3 | 1,046 |
| Male | 4-8 | 1,742 |
| Male | 9-13 | 2,279 |

### Tab: Healthy People 2030 Goal

Unfortunately, most children do not follow these recommendations. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2022), empty calories from sugars and fats contribute to 40% of daily calories for children, negatively affecting the quality of their diets. Almost half of these empty calories can be contributed to sodas, fruit drinks, desserts, pizza, and whole milk. In addition, most children do not consume the recommended amount of 4 to 6 cups of water daily. Childhood obesity rates have almost tripled in the last 30 years.

One of Healthy People 2030's main goals is to promote healthy eating. For people aged 2 years and over, the Healthy People 2030 objectives include:

**Increase:**

- fruit consumption
- vegetable consumption
- dark green vegetables, red and orange vegetables, beans and peas
- whole grains
- calcium
- potassium
- vitamin D

**Decrease:**

- added sugars
- saturated fat
- sodium
- iron deficiency

## Section 5: Explore: Nutrient Requirements of Adolescents

During adolescence, both boys and girls experience a rapid growth spurt associated with puberty.

### Tab: Calorie Requirements

The amount of calories a teenager requires varies greatly and is influenced by activity level, metabolic rate, and body mass (Eilender, 2016). Since boys have a greater increase in height, weight, and body mass compared to girls, their calorie requirements are higher.

| | Females (Ages 9-13) | Females (Ages 14-18) | Males (Ages 9-13) | Males (Ages 14-18) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| kcal | 2,071 | 2,368 | 2,279 | 3,152 |

### Tab: Nutritional Concerns

Teenagers require key micronutrients and macronutrients to support growth. However, research studies show that most teenagers do not get adequate amounts of calcium and vitamin D (Eilender, 2016). Calcium and vitamin D during this stage in life are critical to building bone mass. By the time they reach age 18, adolescents have developed almost all of their bone mass. After that, bones get denser with age. Adolescents also need more iron for growth, blood volume, immunity, and cognitive development.

Nutritional guidance is important to develop a healthy adult lifestyle, prevent weight problems, reduce disease risk, and ensure growth needs are met. The American Academy of Pediatrics (2009) encourages healthcare professionals to address five nutritional concerns with adolescents:

- low consumption of fruit and vegetables
- low consumption of whole grains
- low consumption of calcium and low-fat dairy foods
- high consumption of sweetened beverages
- frequent consumption of fast food

## Section 7: Explore: School and Community Food Programs

### Tab: School Breakfast Program

One of the Healthy People 2030 objectives is to increase the number of students taking part in the school breakfast program (SBP). Research has shown that eating breakfast every day increases academic performance and improves behavior in students (Adolphus et al., 2013). Since 1970, participation in SBP has grown. In 2017-2018, 35.4% of students in schools that were part of SBP participated in free, reduced-price, or paid meals (Healthy People 2030, n.d.).

**School Breakfast Program — Participation has slowly but steadily grown over the years:**

- 1970: 0.5 million children
- 1980: 3.6 million children
- 1990: 4.0 million children
- 2000: 7.5 million children
- 2010: 11.67 million children
- 2016: 14.57 million children

(U.S. Department of Agriculture, 2017)

## Section 9: Explore: Nutrition and Athletics

During childhood and adolescence, many children are involved in sports, in which weight and nutrition are emphasized.

### Tab: Weight Control and Concerns

Weight control, gain or loss, can be perceived as advantageous for the individual athlete and/or the team. This table lists the sports that have different emphases on weight control.

However, there are dangerous ways to reduce weight including "food restriction, vomiting, overexercising, diet-pill use, inappropriate use of prescribed stimulants or insulin, nicotine use, and voluntary dehydration," which can entail fluid restriction, spitting, and the use of laxatives or diuretics (Committee on Sports Medicine and Fitness, 2005, p. 1557). Since these practices jeopardize nutritional needs and hydration, they should be prohibited at all ages.

Weight loss techniques may result in physical complications, such as:

- delayed physical maturation
- oligomenorrhea and amenorrhea in females
- development of eating disorders
- potential permanent growth impairment
- increased risk of infectious diseases
- changes in the cardiovascular, endocrine, gastrointestinal, renal, and thermoregulatory systems
- depression

| Sports that emphasize leanness and/or competing at the lowest possible weight | Sports that emphasize increasing lean muscle mass |
| --- | --- |
| bodybuilding, cheerleading, dancing, distance running, cross-country skiing, diving, figure skating, gymnastics, martial arts, rowing, swimming, weight-class football, wrestling | football, rugby, basketball, power lifting |

### Tab: Calories Required for Athletes

The amount of calories an athlete needs is based on many factors such as body composition, age, state of growth, and level of fitness. Overall, athletes need to eat enough to cover the energy needed for the sport and daily living.

- Weight loss, when necessary, should be gradual at 1 to 2 pounds each week.
- To gain one pound of muscle, the calorie intake needs to be increased by about 2,000-2,500 kcal more than is being expended, the amount of protein consumed should be 1.5 g to 1.75 g of protein per kg of body weight per day, and strength training should be core fitness (Committee on Sports Medicine and Fitness, 2005).

It is important to emphasize to athletes and caregivers that nutritional needs for growth and development should be placed above athletic requirements.

**Reference**

Committee on Sports Medicine and Fitness. (2005). Promotion of healthy weight-control practices in young athletes. *Pediatrics, 116*(6), 1557–1564. https://doi.org/10.1542/peds.2005-2314

## Section 11: Explore: Body Image and Weight

Childhood and adolescence are times when the body image becomes defined. Select each tab to learn what research has found and what nursing interventions can help children with body image and weight concerns.

### Tab: Negative Body Image

Jankauskiene and Baceviciene (2019) surveyed 579 adolescents of both genders. They concluded that children who overestimate body weight and have a higher body mass index (BMI) demonstrate increased body image concerns, poor eating habits, and low self-esteem. Despite dissatisfaction with weight or image, these children did not use physical exercise or healthy eating to address the issues. They admitted to skipping meals, especially breakfast, which can lead to eating disorders.

The results "support the idea that teaching about positive body image and adequate body weight estimation must be a part of all evidence-based healthy nutrition, physical activity, and weight control-related health education programs" (Jankauskiene & Baceviciene, 2019, Conclusions section).

### Tab: Maternal Influences

It is essential that healthcare providers and educators do not shame a child who has body image or weight concerns. Many factors can play a role in these concerns. Rodgers et al. (2020) examined how the attitude, behaviors, and comments by mothers regarding body weight and eating impacted the thoughts of their children. They found that negative body and weight comments were associated with poorer child outcomes.

Their findings suggest that the family environment can play a role, and opinions about body image and nutrition are transferrable to children. Besides promoting positive body image, interventions should be directed at parent communication and the behaviors within the environment.

**References**

Jankauskiene, R., & Baceviciene, M. (2019). Body image concerns and body weight overestimation do not promote healthy behaviour: Evidence from adolescents in Lithuania. *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health, 16*(5), 864, 1–14. https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph16050864

Rodgers, R. F., Wertheim, E. H., Damiano, S. R., & Paxton, S. J. (2020). Maternal influences on body image and eating concerns among 7- and 8-year-old boys and girls: Cross-sectional and prospective relations. *International Journal of Eating Disorders, 53*(1), 79–84. https://doi.org/10.1002/eat.23166
