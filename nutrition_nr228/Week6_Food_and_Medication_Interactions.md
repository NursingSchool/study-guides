## Section 1: Introduction to Food and Medication Interactions

Since the introduction of computerized pharmacy systems, there has been a decrease in drug-drug interactions. However, food and medication interactions are also a common cause of adverse drug reactions and are more challenging. Food consumption is not normally documented in a client's chart. Clients sometimes don't disclose their use of herbal medicines and dietary supplements. Food, herbs, and supplements may interfere with a drug's absorption, metabolism, or excretion. This may increase the drug's toxicity, produce a new effect, or decrease effectiveness (Abdollahi et al., 2018).

This concept will look at the risk factors for food and medication interactions, the effects of food and medication, screening for food and medication interactions, and the importance of educating clients.

As you complete the concept, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Identify risk factors for food and medication interactions.
- Describe the effects of food, herbs, and supplements on medication.
- Describe the effects of medication on foods.
- Explain how to screen for food and medication interactions.
- Discuss the importance of educating clients about food and medication interactions.

## Section 3: Explore: Types of Interactions

### Pharmacokinetic Interactions

Pharmacokinetic interactions result in changes in drug absorption, distribution, metabolism, and elimination. The interactions lead to a decrease or increase in the drug's effect as a result of the concentration in the body. Pharmacokinetic interactions are the most common interactions and the cause of mild to severe side effects.

**Example:** Many drugs are metabolized with an enzyme system called the cytochrome P450 system in the liver. Grapefruit juice can block some of these enzymes, causing the drug to stay in the circulating bloodstream. This can lead to toxic levels and adverse effects of the drug.

### Pharmacodynamic Interactions

Pharmacodynamic interactions occur when food or herbs directly change the effect of the drug. It does not change the blood concentration, and adjusting the dose of the drug will not counter a pharmacodynamic interaction.

**Example:** Ingestion of tyramine-rich foods in conjunction with monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs) can cause a hypertensive crisis. MAOIs reduce the breakdown of tyramine thus helping relieve depression. However, if MAOIs and tyramine are taken together, the vasoconstriction effects are increased.

## Section 5: Explore: Risk Factors for Drug-Nutrient Interaction

Clients at increased risk for drug-food interactions include:

- Older adult clients
- Clients with chronic disease
- Clients on long-term drug therapy
- Clients with polypharmacy
- Clients who are malnourished
- Clients with increased nutritional needs

Older adult clients are the highest risk group. This population takes the majority of prescription medications, such as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, antihypertensives, and antidepressants, which have interactions with certain foods. Most older adults also have some type of underlying chronic conditions that require long-term therapies and nutritional interventions. The multiple therapies lead to polypharmacy.

In addition, the older adult population are more likely to suffer an adverse effect related to the interaction.

**Case Study:** A 77-year-old male client with a history of heart arrhythmias was placed on digoxin. The client was warned of decreased medication absorption and possible failure of medication associated with the consumption of calcium. The client remained adherent to taking the digoxin with water for several months and was showing improvement.

At a recent clinic visit, the client was told that his calcium level was low. He was encouraged to consume more calcium and a supplement was added to his medications. A medication review was not completed, so the link between calcium and digoxin was missed. Two weeks later, the client presented to the emergency department with a diagnosis of digoxin toxicity.

## Section 6: Explore: Effects of Drugs on Nutrition

Since drugs and nutrients use similar organs for absorption, metabolism, and excretion, drugs can interfere with nutritional status. Drugs can also impact the actual intake of food by influencing appetite, taste, and smell. The drugs that have the greatest impact on nutrition are those that are taken for prolonged periods of time, have dietary requirements or regulations, or compete with nutrient-binding sites.

**Appetite Changes**

- Drugs may suppress hunger, leading to weight loss. *Examples:* Some antibiotics, bulk agents, and narcotics like morphine decrease appetite.
- Drugs may stimulate hunger, leading to weight gain. *Examples:* Alcohol, corticosteroids, and megestrol acetate increase appetite.

**Taste and Smell Alterations**

- Some drugs may alter the ability to taste and smell certain foods which affects food intake. *Examples:* Allopurinol and lithium may cause a metallic taste, while aspirin and levodopa may cause a bitter taste. ACE inhibitors, beta blockers, and calcium channel blockers may cause altered smell.

**Nutrient Absorption**

- Certain drugs may increase, decrease, or prevent nutrient absorption in the gastrointestinal system. *Example:* Orlistat decreases the absorption of fats.

**Nutrient Metabolism**

- Drugs may increase or decrease the metabolism of certain nutrients. *Example:* Sulfonylureas and lithium decrease the uptake or release of iodine by the thyroid.

**Nutrient Excretion**

- Drugs can increase or decrease the excretion of nutrients. *Example:* Diuretics, especially loop and thiazides, can increase the urinary loss of potassium, magnesium, sodium, and calcium.

## Section 7: Explore: Drug-Food Interactions

The effect of a drug may be different than expected if it interacts with a food or beverage. It may also cause the drug to not work at all. One of the most common drug-food interactions is with grapefruit and grapefruit juice.

There are over 40 medications that can have an adverse effect with grapefruit. The video below from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration explains the details.

**Video:** "Don't Take This With That: Grapefruit, Drug Interaction" — FDA (YouTube: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FQJ4NO6SDqg)

### Video Transcript

Yep, that cheerful yellow and pink citrus fruit, full of vitamin C and a staple of a well balanced diet, can be dangerous. More than 50 drugs have been identified that may cause harm if you drink grapefruit juice while taking them. This can lead to potentially dangerous drug levels, causing serious side effects. Grapefruit can also interfere with transporters in the intestine that help absorb other drugs; so, less of the drug is absorbed and the drug may not be as effective when taken with grapefruit juice. An anxiety drug may make a person sleepy or even more anxious; a statin may cause heart muscle damage; and, an organ transplant patient may experience infections and kidney damage. It is possible for as little as 1 cup of juice or 2 grapefruit segments to change the way a drug works. The effects of grapefruit can last up to 24 hours. So, if your doctor or pharmacist says to avoid grapefruit juice… do it! Until all the medicine is gone. The U.S. Food & Drug Administration considers the potential for all prospective new drugs to interact with grapefruit juice. ## Section 9: Explore: Timing of Medication Administration

Some medications need to be taken before meals, with meals, or after meals. Researchers found that most clients, over 89%, take their medications at inappropriate times and without regard for meals. The following are examples of why the timing of medication administration is important:

### Before Meals

- **Help the body balance blood glucose:** Rapid-acting insulin should be injected within 15 min before a meal. Short-acting (regular) insulin can be given 30 minutes before a meal.
- **Ensure proper absorption:** Levothyroxine should be taken on an empty stomach 30-60 minutes before meals to avoid unpredictable absorption of the hormone. Bisphosphonates, such as alendronate and ibandronate, should be taken alone with a full glass of water.

### With or After Meals

- **Reduce nausea or vomiting:** Medications, like allopurinol, may cause nausea or vomiting. Take these after a meal to reduce these side effects.
- **Reduce stomach irritation:** Aspirin, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and steroid medications can cause stomach irritation, indigestion or ulcers. Food or milk can coat the stomach thus reducing these effects.
- **Treat reflux or indigestion:** Antacids are used to treat reflux after the consumption of food. It is most effective if taken immediately after a meal.
- **Ensure the medicine is not washed away:** Liquid nystatin is used for oral thrush and must be used after meals. The client cannot eat or drink anything for 20-30 minutes after application.
- **Ensure proper absorption:** HIV medicines, such as ritonavir, saquinavir and nelfinavir, require food in the gastrointestinal system in order for the body to absorb them properly.
- **Help the body process the meal:** Enzyme supplements should also be taken with food to help the body process the meal.

## Section 11: Explore: Herb and Drug Interaction

Rashrash et al. (2017) examined the prevalence of herbal use in over 26,000 Americans. Approximately 35% used herbal supplements. Of those that used supplements, almost 38% percent used prescription medications, and 42% used over-the-counter medication. The prevalence of use centered around people with chronic medical conditions such as stroke, cancer, and arthritis.

In a study by Alsanad et al. (2014), 806 clients with cancer were surveyed and 53.7% were reported to be taking herbal supplements. Drug combinations and herbal supplements taken were examined and potential herb-drug interactions were identified. It was discovered that 13.9% of clients were at risk of interaction. The most common herbs involved included garlic, green tea, mistletoe, Chinese herbal tea, iron, and Saint John's wort. Interactions included altered drug metabolism, decreased absorption, and hypersensitivity. It was also found that most of the clients did not discuss their herbal supplement use with healthcare professionals.

St. John's wort is most often used for depression but can be used for menopausal symptoms, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and topically for wound healing (Mayo Clinic Staff, 2017). However, it can interact with medications by increasing or decreasing the effects. Examples include:

- Antidepressants
- Birth control pills
- Barbiturates
- Cyclosporine
- Digoxin
- Certain immunosuppressive drugs
- Omeprazole
- Protease inhibitors
- Certain chemotherapy drugs
- Narcotics
- Certain statins
- Warfarin

## Section 13: Explore: Client Education

Educating clients about interactions is part of a healthcare provider's role. It is especially important if the client takes several different medications, sees more than one healthcare provider, and/or has multiple health conditions.

Here are some client education tips and suggestions:

- Screening for interactions can be done in all healthcare and home settings.
- Ask clients about all prescription drugs, over-the-counter drugs, and herbal supplements.
- Ask clients to describe how and when they take the medications and supplements.
- Remind clients that over-the-counter drugs may interact with other drugs, herbal supplements, and food as well.
- Teach clients how to read drug and supplement labels.
- Provide clients with educational materials and resources.
