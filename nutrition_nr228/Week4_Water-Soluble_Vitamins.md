## Section 1: Introduction to Water-Soluble Vitamins

Water-soluble vitamins are one of the two major categories of vitamins. Just like fat-soluble vitamins, they are essential nutrients for our bodies and, because we cannot produce them on our own, we must consume them.

After ingestion, water-soluble vitamins are dissolved in water and, as a result, are easily absorbed into the tissues of the body. Most vitamins are water-soluble and include the B complex group; thiamin (B1), riboflavin (B2), niacin (B3), pyridoxine (B6), folate, cobalamin (B12), biotin, and pantothenic acid. Other water-soluble vitamins include choline and vitamin C. We will explore each of these vitamins throughout this learning module.

Take the time to review the vitamins module and the functions of water-soluble vitamins prior to proceeding with this learning module. Also, think about the various deficiencies and differences between fat- versus water-soluble vitamins.

## Section 3: Explore: Water-Soluble Vitamins

As the name suggests, water-soluble vitamins dissolve in water. They metabolize quicker than fat-soluble vitamins. They are easily absorbed through the small intestine, and then travel through the blood to various tissues within the body. Any excess is excreted through urination. Here are the water-soluble vitamins.

- B-complex vitamins:
  - thiamin (B1)
  - riboflavin (B2)
  - niacin (B3)
  - pyridoxine (B6)
  - folate (B9)
  - cobalamin (B12)
  - biotin (B7)
  - pantothenic acid (B5)
- choline (B4)
- vitamin C

*(Image: Vitamin C supplement)*

A healthy, balanced diet from all the major food groups usually provides enough water-soluble vitamins for optimal health. Supplementation is not needed in healthy individuals. In general, if supplementation is needed, then the individual often has an underlying cause. Nutrient deficiencies are most often secondary to the effects of illness, treatment, socioeconomic factors, age, and/or lifestyle choices.

## Section 4: Explore: Water-Soluble Vitamins—A Closer Look

Let's take a closer look at the individual water-soluble vitamins. Use your learning resources as you review the nutrient, its function, deficiency, toxicity, and food sources for each vitamin-type. Note any special considerations for vitamin-types. Select each vitamin below to learn more.

### Tab: Thiamine (Vitamin B1)

- **Function:** Coenzyme (substance that activates an enzyme) needed for energy metabolism; important for nerve function related to the action of muscles.
- **Deficiency:** Muscle weakness, loss of coordination, and tachycardia. The deficiency alters the nervous system, muscle activity, gastrointestinal and cardiovascular systems, known as *Beriberi* that still occurs in some areas of the world (e.g., Asia) where white rice is the major food source. Rice is low in thiamin. Severe deficiency leads to *Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome*, which is a cerebral form of beriberi. Neurotoxic effects such as loss of memory, mental confusion, and ataxia occur and are often displayed by individuals with chronic alcohol use.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in urine. Toxicity is not common.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for individuals with underlying nutrient deficiency, chronic excessive alcohol consumption and those with thiamine deficiencies. Recommended for individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake.
- **Sources:** Lean pork, whole-grain or enriched bread and cereals, legumes, nuts, and seeds.

### Tab: Riboflavin (Vitamin B2)

- **Function:** Coenzyme needed for energy metabolism; important for normal vision and skin health.
- **Deficiency:** Swollen lips, cheilosis, glossitis, and seborrheic dermatitis. *Ariboflavinosis* is the group of symptoms listed above, associated with riboflavin deficiency.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. Toxicity is not common.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for individuals with underlying nutrient deficiency, chronic excessive alcohol consumption, some cancers, anorexia nervosa, and lactose intolerance. Recommended for individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake.
- **Sources:** Milk and milk products are major sources; plant and animal products; leafy green vegetables; whole-grain, enriched bread, and cereals. Milk is sold in nontransparent containers (e.g., cardboard and/or opaque plastic) to protect riboflavin. It is sensitive to sunlight's UV rays and artificial light. Loss of riboflavin occurs with use of glass or clear plastic containers.

### Tab: Niacin (Vitamin B3)

- **Function:** Coenzyme needed for energy metabolism; important for the nervous system, digestive system, and skin health. Critical for glycolysis and the tricarboxylic acid cycle.
- **Deficiency:** *Pellagra* is the deficiency disorder, which includes the Three D's: Dermatitis, Dementia, Diarrhea. Psychosis with severe deficiencies.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. *Flushing effect* throughout the body affects the vascular system.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for individuals with underlying nutrient deficiency, chronic excessive alcohol consumption, treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake. Medically administered to prevent liver damage, gout, and arthritic reactions. Often referred to as a "banana bag," as intravenous (IV) supplementation is yellow in color. Recommended for individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake.
- **Sources:** Meat, poultry, fish, whole-grain or enriched bread and cereals, vegetables (especially mushrooms, asparagus, and leafy green vegetables), peanut butter.

### Tab: Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6)

- **Function:** Coenzyme needed for amino acid, carbohydrate, and protein metabolism; essential for the nervous system function; helps make red blood cells.
- **Deficiency:** Dermatitis, altered nerve function, weakness, and poor growth and development. Normally, it occurs with other vitamin B deficiencies.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. Toxicity is not common. Megadoses may cause ataxia and sensory neuropathies; however rare.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for underlying nutrient deficiency, individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake.
- **Sources:** Meat, fish, poultry, vegetables, and fruits.

### Tab: Folate (Folic Acid, B9)

- **Function:** Coenzyme needed for making DNA, RNA, and new cells, especially red blood cells. Needed for formation of fetal neural tubes.
- **Deficiency:** Megaloblastic anemia, glossitis, diarrhea, irritability, depression, and anxiety; neural tube defects in children. Cells that require rapid cell growth and division are most vulnerable to deficiencies. Any condition that causes cells to speed up; infection, cancer, blood loss, burns, growth and development, & pregnancy may result in deficiencies. Numerous medications may decrease folate absorption; oral contraceptives, anticonvulsants, aspirin, chemotherapy, NSAIDS and antacids.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. Consuming amounts and effects more than the recommended daily amount have not been studied.
- **Supplementation:** Supplementation is recommended for women of childbearing age to reduce the risk of birth defects, as well as for women during pregnancy and lactation. Recommended for underlying nutrient deficiency, individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake.
- **Sources:** Leafy green vegetables and legumes, seeds, orange juice, and liver; now added to most refined grains.

### Tab: Cobalamin (Vitamin B12)

- **Function:** Coenzyme needed for making new cells; important to protect nerve fibers and nerve functions. Plays a role in folate metabolism.
- **Deficiency:** Neurological effects. Deficiencies are usually secondary to another underlying disease. Pernicious anemia (lack of intrinsic factor) or megaloblastic anemia (related folate dysfunction). Older adults are at higher risk due to reduction of the intrinsic factor by stomach mucosa. Relies on this intrinsic factor for absorption. Deficiency in this population results in psychiatric symptoms; delusions and hallucinations. Concerns for this deficiency in individuals who eat a vegan diet, as this vitamin is not found in plant foods.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. Toxicity has not been studied.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for underlying nutrient deficiency, individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake. Primary disease needs to be treated to prevent secondary cobalamin deficiency. Vitamin B12 injections are necessary for individuals who do not produce intrinsic factor, which is essential for absorption of vitamin B12 from the gut.
- **Sources:** Meat, poultry, fish, seafood, eggs, milk and milk products; not found in plant foods.

### Tab: Biotin (Vitamin B7)

- **Function:** Coenzyme needed for energy metabolism. Helps with transfer of carbon dioxide from one cell to another.
- **Deficiency:** Scaly, red rash, hair loss, and loss of appetite. Chronic use of antibiotics and long-term intravenous feedings (IV) are known to decrease the number of biotin-producing (i.e., good) bacteria.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. Toxicity is not common.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for underlying nutrient deficiency, individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake. Supplementation is needed with chronic antibiotic use and long-term IV feedings.
- **Sources:** Liver, salmon, peanut butter, egg yolks, yeast; also produced in the intestinal tract by bacteria.

### Tab: Pantothenic acid (Vitamin B5)

- **Function:** Coenzyme needed for energy metabolism.
- **Deficiency:** Does not occur in humans.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. Megadoses may lead to diarrhea or water retention, however, not common.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for underlying nutrient deficiency, individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake.
- **Sources:** Poultry, beef, seafood and organ meats; vegetables, whole grains, nuts, and seeds.

### Tab: Choline (Vitamin B4)

- **Function:** Needed for synthesis of acetylcholine (neurotransmitter) and lecithin (phospholipid).
- **Deficiency:** Rare.
- **Toxicity:** Excess is excreted in the urine. Megadoses may cause sweating, "fishy" body odor, vomiting, liver damage and hypotension.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for underlying nutrient deficiency, individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake.
- **Sources:** Poultry, fish, pork, eggs, beef, seafood, beans, and nuts.

### Tab: Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)

- **Function:** Antioxidant and coenzyme needed for protein metabolism; important for immune system health; aids in iron absorption and collagen formation for bone matrix, teeth, cartilage and connective tissues; aids in wound healing.
- **Deficiency:** *Scurvy*, disease that causes inflammation and weakening of connective tissue; potentially fatal. Chronic use of alcohol and drugs, smokers, and lack of obtaining and/or preparing fresh food (fruits and vegetables) can lead to deficiencies. At-risk groups generally have other underlying deficiencies as well.
- **Toxicity:** Extra vitamin C is stored in the liver to meet the demands of tissue infection and provides support for the immune system. Excess is excreted in the urine or the body adapts by destroying excess circulating vitamin C. The toxicity can cause a rebound effect if the person stops supplementation abruptly (and subsequent deficiency, as the body doesn't know when to stop destroying the vitamin). Megadose may cause cramps, diarrhea, kidney stone formation, and gout.
- **Supplementation:** Recommended for underlying nutrient deficiency, individuals during treatments or disorders that affect insufficient nutritional intake. Supplementation for chronic alcohol or drug use and cigarette smokers versus non-smokers. Cigarette smokers have lower levels of vitamin C (metabolic use is increased), therefore supplementation is recommended.
- **Sources:** Found only in fruits and vegetables, especially citrus fruits, vegetables in the cabbage family, cantaloupe, strawberries, peppers, tomatoes, potatoes, lettuce, papayas, mangoes, kiwifruit. Some foods and drinks are fortified with vitamin C (e.g., cereal and juice).

## Section 10: Explore: Water Versus Fat-Soluble Differences

**How are water-soluble vitamins different than fat-soluble vitamins?**

Let's compare! The main differences between water-soluble vitamins and fat-soluble vitamins is how they are absorbed and function within the body. One major benefit to water-soluble vitamins is that they are less likely to build up in the body, even in large amounts. Any excess will exit the body when you urinate. This also means that, because water-soluble vitamins are either used or excreted so quickly, you'll need to consistently get them into your body through nutrient-rich foods and supplements that help fill nutritional gaps.

Fat-soluble vitamins don't immediately leave the body and, instead, are stored in fatty tissue and in the liver. Because fat-soluble vitamins are stored in the body, excess is more likely—and that's not necessarily a good thing as toxicity can occur.

Toxicity is less likely with water-soluble vitamins. Regardless of the vitamin type, it is important to follow recommended dietary guidelines and collaborate with the healthcare team, including dietitians, for nutritional guidance and support.

## Section 12: Explore: Recommended Dietary Allowances

Maintaining a healthy body includes following the Dietary Reference Intake (DRI), which provides Recommended Dietary Allowances (RDAs) and adequate intakes for vitamins to meet nutrient requirements.

Following the DRI guidelines is used to prevent deficiencies and toxicity from overdoses, and accounting for the value of nutrient intake as a means of reducing disease risk.

Review the [Recommended Dietary Allowances and Adequate Intakes, Vitamins](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK56068/table/summarytables.t2/?report=objectonly) to learn more.

As a healthcare provider, you may need to provide client education on the daily recommendations for nutritional support. As you review the RDAs for water-soluble vitamins, pay special attention to any high-risk groups.

## Section 13: Explore: Life Span Considerations

Certain individuals may be at risk of having water-soluble vitamin deficiencies. Let's review these special considerations.

- Chronic alcohol consumption can cause a multitude of nutritional deficiencies. Nutritional support generally includes supplementation of B-complex vitamins and vitamin C.
- Folate supplementation is needed for childbearing women, during pregnancy and lactation.
- Older adults are at higher risk of cobalamin (vitamin B12) deficiency due to reduction of intrinsic factor by stomach mucosa. Deficiency in this population results in psychiatric symptoms including delusions and hallucinations. It could mimic a mental health disorder, when in fact the underlying problem is a vitamin deficiency.
- Chronic antibiotic use and long-term intravenous feedings (IV) are known to decrease the number of biotin-producing "good" bacteria to support gastrointestinal (GI) health. Supplementation of biotin may be needed.
