## Section 1: Introduction to the Nursing Application of Vitamins, Antioxidants, and Minerals

Vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants play an important role in maintaining good health. Teaching clients how to consume foods with ample amounts of these nutrients and eating a healthy, well-balanced diet is critical in meeting the physiological needs of the body.

This lesson will review major vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants. We will reflect on why consuming the recommended dietary allowances is important in keeping us healthy.

As you complete this activity, you will gain the nursing knowledge and skills needed to:

- Explain the use of vitamins, antioxidants, and minerals.
- Identify factors that affect nutrient absorption.
- Describe lifespan and cultural considerations.
- Differentiate food sources that contain the major vitamins, antioxidants, and minerals.

## Section 3: Explore: What Are Vitamins?

Vitamins are nutrients; essential, noncaloric, organic molecules needed in the diet. Often, they are referred to as micronutrients, since only small amounts are needed in the body to perform specific metabolic functions. Vitamins act as helpers to get chemical processes done. They are essential for our bodies and, because we cannot produce them on our own, we must consume them. After vitamins are ingested, they are considered precursors until the body turns them into active chemicals.

Vitamins are divided into two categories:

**Fat-Soluble Vitamins:** These are stored in fat cells, in the liver and near the liver, for 3 to 6 weeks.

**Water-Soluble Vitamins:** These pass through the body's water supply. The body cannot keep these vitamins, so they must be ingested for the body to maintain an appropriate level.

To promote health and wellness, vitamins and minerals are considered essential nutrients and, in acting together, they carry out hundreds of functional roles within the body.

## Section 4: Explore: Vitamin Deficiencies - Clinical Considerations

Certain subgroups are at risk for vitamin deficiencies.

According to the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), significantly higher deficiency risk was seen in women (37%), non-Hispanic blacks (55%), individuals from low-income households (40%), or without a high school diploma (42%), and underweight (42%) or obese individuals (39%) (Bird et al., 2017).

Subgroup populations at risk for vitamin deficiencies also include older adults because of decreased vitamin absorption, limited physical abilities, and decreased financial resources to purchase and prepare food.

Active male adults, which includes middle-aged males, generally consume enough foods to ensure adequate vitamin intake.

**Reference**

Bird, J. K., Murphy, R. A., Ciappio, E. D., & McBurney, M. I. (2017). Risk of deficiency in multiple concurrent micronutrients in children and adults in the United States. *Nutrients, 9*(7), 655. https://doi.org/10.3390/nu9070655

## Section 5: Explore: Vitamins - Clinical Considerations

Select each tab to view the clinical manifestations seen with fat-soluble vitamins.

### Tab: Vitamin A

**Deficiency causes:**

- night blindness (first indication of deficiency)
- xerophthalmia (dry, thickened condition of the conjunctiva)
- keratomalacia (deterioration of the cornea)
- blindness (severe deficiency)

**Toxicity:**

- weakness
- anorexia
- vomiting
- enlarged spleen and liver

However, toxicity is rare with healthy food consumption.

### Tab: Vitamin D

**Deficiency causes:**

- rickets in children
- osteomalacia in adults

**Toxicity:**

- hypercalcemia
- hypercalciuria

### Tab: Vitamin E

**Deficiency causes:**

- Primary deficiencies are rare.
- Secondary deficiencies cause metabolic and neurological deficits.

**Toxicity:** Toxicities are rare. However, supplement use is contraindicated with anticoagulation drugs (risk for bleeding).

### Tab: Vitamin K

**Deficiency causes:**

- bleeding

**Toxicity:** Toxic levels of vitamin K are most often from injection reactions in infants, causing hemolytic anemia and hyperbilirubinemia.

## Section 7: Explore: Vitamins - At-Risk Populations

Certain conditions may place clients at risk for vitamin deficiency.

- Chronic alcohol consumption can cause deficiencies in B-complex vitamins and vitamin C.
- Folate supplementation is needed for childbearing women during pregnancy and lactation.
- Older adults are at higher risk of cobalamin (vitamin B12) deficiency due to the reduction of intrinsic factor by stomach mucosa. Deficiencies can lead to cognitive and neurological symptoms.
- Chronic antibiotic use and long-term intravenous feedings (IV) are known to decrease the number of biotin-producing "good" bacteria to support gastrointestinal health. Supplementation of biotin may be needed.

## Section 8: Explore: Lifespan and Cultural Considerations

Any individual across the lifespan that has malabsorption issues is at risk of having a vitamin deficiency, particularly for fat-soluble vitamins in those clients who experience chronic fat malabsorption. Let's review other special considerations.

**Infants:** Premature infants and newborns are unable to immediately produce vitamin K as they do not have any intestinal flora "good gut bacteria" which helps produce vitamin K. Remember, they receive an injection after birth.

**Children & Adults:** A deficiency of vitamin D can lead to the disorders rickets (children) and osteomalacia (adults). A risk of rickets has increased among breastfed, dark-skinned infants and toddlers of African and Asian descent, especially in northern areas such as Canada and Alaska (Grodner et al., 2020).

**Older Adults:** Older adults need more vitamin D because their skin is less effective in synthesizing it during sun exposure to meet their nutritional needs. As we age, the skin manufactures less. Therefore, supplementation with vitamin D is often recommended for older populations. Be sure to educate on vitamin D rich foods as a primary wellness goal.

**Reference**

Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). *Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach* (8th ed.). Elsevier.

## Section 10: Explore: Antioxidant Review

Antioxidants are substances that can prevent or slow damage to cells caused by free radicals. Free radicals are waste substances produced by cells as the body processes food and reacts to internal forces and the environment. Antioxidants are known to help counteract free radicals in our bodies and are believed to help decrease the risk of certain diseases, enhance our health, and promote overall wellness.

These vitamins have antioxidant properties:

- vitamin A
- vitamin C
- vitamin E
- selenium

Antioxidants can protect against the cell damage that free radicals cause, known as oxidative stress.

## Section 12: Explore: Review of Minerals

Minerals are crucial for proper body development and health. Minerals are inorganic substances that are found naturally within the earth. They play a critical role by interacting synergistically with other elements and body systems to support and maintain good health. Minerals do not provide energy for the body. However, they are a catalyst for many body processes.

Let's look at some of the essential minerals and clinical considerations.

### Tab: Calcium

Calcium is an essential mineral. It can get too low (hypocalcemia) causing tremors, muscle cramps, paresthesia (numbness/tingling), and twitching. Muscles may even "lock up" (tetany). Osteoporosis is the long-term result of low absorption of calcium.

Calcium may get too high (hypercalcemia). The muscles get weak and lack reflexes, and the client may have confusion, fatigue, constipation, renal stones, and memory loss. Calcium may also reduce the absorption of iron, zinc, and other minerals.

Calcium carbonate is less expensive than calcium citrate. It is better absorbed with food due to the high acidity of the chyme. In addition, calcium carbonate is available at higher dosages. This means the client would take fewer pills to reach their recommended dose. Calcium citrate is less likely to cause constipation (NIH, 2020) and is best absorbed without food. Calcium citrate is available at lower doses, which means the client will have to take more pills to reach their recommended dose (NIH, 2020).

### Tab: Phosphorus

Phosphorus is another essential mineral for bone health. It is a crucial element of every cell in our bodies, and at least 85% of the phosphorus in the body is in the bones. Phosphate, which is a form of phosphorus, makes up at least 50% of our bone mineral mass.

Calcium alone is not sufficient to build strong bones. It is a composition of calcium and phosphorus, known as hydroxyapatite, that is needed. Hydroxyapatite is the vital ingredient that gives teeth and bone their rigidity or strength.

### Tab: Magnesium

Magnesium, like calcium and phosphorus, is found in our bones, providing structural and storage functions. When magnesium levels get too low (hypomagnesemia), it can lead to respiratory muscle paralysis, coma, and heart arrest. Symptoms aggravate the central nervous system (CNS) and can include confusion, reduced consciousness, muscle lock-up (tetany), muscle cramps, muscle weakness, and strong reflexes (nerve excitability).

When magnesium levels get too high (hypermagnesemia), it can depress the central nervous system (CNS). This can cause lethargy, weak muscles, loss of reflexes, low blood pressure, and heart rhythm problems.

Clients with alcoholism tend to have low magnesium levels. Magnesium is also used to slow preterm labor (high magnesium weakens the uterine muscle).

**Reference**

National Institute of Health. (2020). *Office of dietary supplements - calcium.* https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Calcium-HealthProfessional

## Section 13: Explore: Iron

Iron is a necessary component within the pigmented molecules of our red blood cells (RBCs). The pigment turns red when oxygen combines with it. We ingest iron by eating red meat, such as beef, venison, buffalo, and kangaroo. Iron is also found in spinach, broccoli, and dark green, leafy vegetables. Beans and chickpeas are also good sources of iron.

Be aware that caffeine blocks the absorption of iron and calcium. An iron deficiency can lead to anemia. It is important to mention that rice and flour are often fortified with iron as an effective community health intervention to decrease iron-deficiency diseases (CDC, 2020).

Iron-deficiency anemia can cause an individual to be cold, tired, and fatigued. Iron is needed to produce hemoglobin, which is essential in circulating oxygenated blood throughout the body.

**Reference**

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2020). *Micronutrient facts*. https://www.cdc.gov/nutrition/micronutrient-malnutrition/micronutrients/index.html

## Section 14: Explore: Sodium

The one mineral that is most present in the body is sodium. You may know that sodium is one-half of table salt (sodium chloride). Sodium helps fluid balance by being present in the blood to maintain a certain concentration (or osmolality). Water is drawn to sodium through osmosis. Therefore, wherever sodium goes, water follows.

When sodium levels are too low (hyponatremia), the kidneys and other organs get rid of extra water. Clinical manifestations of hyponatremia (low levels of sodium) include muscle cramps, loss of appetite, dizziness, fatigue, reduced ability to concentrate, and mental confusion. We can help by restricting someone's fluid intake.

When sodium levels are too high (hypernatremia), the kidneys and other organs retain water. This can lead to other complications (i.e., fluid overload, swelling, hypertension) depending on any underlying health problems.

## Section 16: Explore: Potassium

Potassium is one of the most important minerals in the body. Like all the other electrolytes, there is a certain amount of potassium inside and outside the cells of the body. Fluctuations in these levels can be caused by many things and can have disastrous consequences if the body is unable to achieve homeostasis with or without help.

If potassium gets too high or too low, it is life-threatening. Potassium is the main cation (positive ion) in intracellular fluid. Potassium regulates cell excitability. In addition, it moves out of the cell to let hydrogen in to help with acid-base balance. Potassium is also responsible for excitability and nerve impulse conduction, particularly in the heart. Levels that are too high or too low can cause cardiac (heart) arrest and death.

Potassium levels that are too high or too low can cause bad heart electrical rhythms. When potassium gets too low (hypokalemia), people get weakness, paresthesia (numbness and tingling), loss of reflexes, and (rarely) muscle weakness. The heart rate may go up and the heart rhythm will appear abnormal. Medications can cause low potassium. When potassium gets too high (hyperkalemia), similar symptoms occur in the muscles and reflexes. In addition, a slow heart rate and blood pressure may also occur.

## Section 17: Explore: Chloride

Chloride is the good neighbor of the body. Because it is a negatively charged ion, it combines with all the positively charged ions, including hydrogen, sodium, potassium, and calcium. There are high levels of chloride in cerebral spinal fluid as well as gastric and pancreatic juices.

The brain uses sodium and chloride together to attract water to make the cerebral spinal fluid. Hydrogen combines with chloride in the digestive process to make hydrochloric acid. Chloride even helps transport carbon dioxide (a waste product from the cells) through the blood to the lungs. Chloride is also important in acid-base balance by opposing bicarbonate (an alkaline).

Some facts about chloride:

- good neighbor except for bicarbonate
- helps regulate osmotic pressure and osmolality
- negatively charged to attract others
- helps maintain fluid balance (with sodium)
- helps regulate acid-base balance (scares bicarbonate)
- chloride and bicarbonate both anions (negatively charged)

Foods with lots of chloride:

- anything with salt

The Na+ ions attract the negative oxygen atoms of the water molecule. The Cl- ions attract the positive hydrogen atoms of the water molecule.

## Section 18: Explore: Calcium Absorption

The nurse must recognize factors that may alter the absorption of calcium. Insufficient calcium absorption can lead to osteoporosis, which is a preventable diet-related disease.

Factors that affect calcium absorption: lactose intolerance, the acidity of the digestive system, food binders, and high fiber intake:

- **Food binders:** Phytates (found in whole grains, legumes [dried beans]) and oxalic acids (found in beet greens and rhubarb) reduce the absorption of calcium.
- **Medications:** Diuretics can cause the excess excretion of calcium (National Institute of Health, 2020).

Genetic determinants that affect the absorption of calcium are:

- **Race:** White and Asian women are more susceptible to osteoporosis.
- **Gender:** Men have greater bone density than women.
- **Family history:** Predisposition to lower bone density may be genetically passed between generations.

**Reference**

National Institute of Health. (2020). *Office of dietary supplements - calcium*. https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/Calcium-HealthProfessional/

## Section 20: Explore: Client Teaching - Calcium

The nurse should carefully assess the client's diet, family history, and lifestyle for conditions that may alter nutrient absorption.

Client teaching includes:

- **Take calcium supplements with food** – Stomach acid produced while eating helps your body absorb calcium.
- **Vitamin D is required for the absorption of calcium** – Ensure the client's vitamin D intake is appropriate for their age.
- **Keep a food log** – Track and try to increase calcium intake from dietary food sources.
- **Calcium supplement forms** – Calcium citrate is more easily absorbed by the digestive tract but takes more pills to achieve the recommended daily allowance. Calcium carbonate is best absorbed when taken with food due to the high acidity content of the stomach (Cleveland Clinic, 2019).
- **Limit caffeine and sodium intake** – Sodium and caffeine can increase the loss of calcium in the urine (Brigham and Women's Hospital, 2020).

**References**

Brigham and Women's Hospital. (2020). *All about calcium supplements*. https://www.brighamandwomens.org/patients-and-families/meals-and-nutrition/bwh-nutrition-and-wellness-hub/special-topics/calcium-supplements

Cleveland Clinic. (2019). *Increasing dietary calcium*. https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/drugs/16297-increasing-calcium-in-your-diet

## Section 21: Explore: Client Teaching - Iron

Increased absorption of iron occurs when dietary sources are consumed with foods containing ascorbic acid (vitamin C). For example, drinking orange juice helps your body better absorb iron from other foods.

Educate clients to:

- Avoid taking calcium and iron supplements together (Cleveland Clinic, 2019). Milk, calcium, and antacids should not be taken at the same time as iron supplements. You should wait at least 2 hours after eating these foods before taking an iron supplement to prevent absorption issues.
- Consume iron from meat and plant sources to increase the absorption of iron.
- Cook food in a cast-iron skillet. Iron that leaches from the skillet will provide an extra source of iron for the food.

**Reference**

Cleveland Clinic. (2019). *Increasing dietary calcium*. https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/drugs/16297-increasing-calcium-in-your-diet

## Section 22: Explore: Client Teaching - Sodium

It is important for nurses to prevent clients' over-consumption of sodium. An increase in sodium intake can lead to hypertension and other health issues. Nurses must educate clients about foods that have hidden forms of sodium used to enhance the flavor.

Other forms of sodium used to flavor foods include:

- monosodium glutamate (MSG)
- sodium saccharin
- preservative (sodium sulfite)

Foods that contain high amounts of sodium include:

- pickled foods
- snack foods
- deli meat
- cheese
- condiments (Cleveland Clinic, 2020)

It is also important for nurses to teach clients how to read food labels for nutrient content. The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet specifically targets the reduction of high-sodium food items. It encourages the practice of seasoning food with natural herbs, such as lemon juice, thyme, or oregano. For diet changes to be successful, it is important for the nurse to carefully assess the spices and foods the client is familiar with.

Review the tips below for lowering sodium intake when shopping, cooking, and eating out.

**Shopping**

- Read food labels and choose items that are lower in sodium and salt, particularly for convenience foods and condiments.
- Choose fresh poultry, fish, and lean meats instead of cured food such as bacon and ham.
- Choose fresh or frozen versus canned fruits and vegetables.
- Avoid food with added salt, such as pickles, pickled vegetables, olives, and sauerkraut.
- Avoid instant or flavored rice and pasta.

**Cooking**

- Don't add salt when cooking rice, pasta, and hot cereals.
- Flavor your foods with salt-free seasoning blends, fresh or dried herbs and spices, or fresh lemon or lime juice.
- Rinse canned foods or foods soaked in brine before using to remove the sodium.
- Use less table salt to flavor food.

**Eating Out**

- Ask that foods be prepared without added salt or MSG, commonly used in Asian foods.
- Avoid choosing menu items that have salty ingredients such as bacon, pickles, olives, and cheese.
- Avoid choosing menu items that include foods that are pickled, cured, smoked, or made with soy sauce or broth.
- Choose fruit or vegetables as a side dish, instead of chips or fries.

**References**

Cleveland Clinic. (2020). Do you know where salt is hiding in your food? *Health Essentials from Cleveland Clinic*. https://health.clevelandclinic.org/know-salt-hiding-food

National Institute of Health. (n.d.). *Dash eating plan*. https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health-topics/dash-eating-plan

## Section 23: Explore: Why Vitamins, Antioxidants, and Minerals Matter

Good nutrition is crucial in keeping Americans healthy across the lifespan. People who eat a healthy diet live longer and are at lower risk for serious health problems such as:

- heart disease
- type 2 diabetes
- obesity

For people with chronic diseases, healthy eating can help manage these conditions and prevent complications (CDC, 2024).

Review this "[Making Healthy Eating Easier](https://www.cdc.gov/nutrition/php/resources/healthy-eating-benefits-for-adults.html)" fact sheet that outlines nutrition, physical activity, and obesity in America. It stresses why nutrition matters and efforts to help prevent vitamin and mineral malnutrition.

**Reference**

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2024, February 14). *Benefits of Healthy Eating for Adults.* https://www.cdc.gov/nutrition/php/resources/healthy-eating-benefits-for-adults.html
