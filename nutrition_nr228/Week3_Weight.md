## Section 3: Explore: Weight Explained

Weight is an essential component of healthcare when caring for a client. Today's focus is on promoting and assisting clients to maintain a healthy weight. This is defined as a client who can perform basic physical activities of daily living, move comfortably, maintain a weight without restrictive diet or excessive exercise by following healthy eating guidelines, and not experience weight-related disorders. Clients can struggle with weight issues such as being underweight, overweight, or unable to maintain a healthy weight throughout their life.

Weight alone does not determine a person's level of health. Someone can have a high body weight with significant amounts of lean muscle contributing to the weight. Likewise, someone can have a low body weight, yet a high percentage of fat to lean muscle tissue. Weight is used because of the ease and convenience of measurement. There are several methods to determine more accurate body composition (lean body mass plus fat).

In clients who have impaired renal function or premenstrual syndrome, fluid retention can cause a change in weight. Additionally, clients who take diuretics or visit the sauna can have a rapid reduction in fluid loss, which will also affect the number on the scale and not actual body composition.

## Section 4: Explore: Body Composition Measurement Methods

Besides weight, here are some ways to measure body size and composition:

### Tab: Densitometry or Hydrostatic Weight

Densitometry or hydrostatic weight involves underwater weighing of the client and is the standard to assess other measures. However, having clients submerged in water is not always practical or available. The apparatus is bulky, expensive, and not quick to use. Additionally, many clients do not want to be fully submerged underwater.

Video: Watch the video, "How to Perform Hydrostatic Weight" [3:23], to learn how this weight measuring method is performed.
PolyFit CP. (2012, October 23). *How to perform hydrostatic weight* [Video]. YouTube. https://youtu.be/NEUSzn1Jc88

### Tab: Bioelectric Impedance Analysis (BIA)

Bioelectric impedance analysis (BIA) is a more widely used and practical method for determining body composition. It is measured in one of two ways. One is a handheld monitor where the client's height, weight, and age are entered, and they grip the handles. Another method involves placing electrodes on the wrists and ankles.

Both methods use transmission of a very mild electrical current throughout the body. Body fat percentage is determined based on the ease of transmission of the current. The higher the fat percentage, the slower the transmission of the current.

### Tab: Body Mass Index (BMI)

Body mass index (BMI) is another way to interpret weight. It is measured by dividing the client's weight in kilograms by the square of the client's height in meters. As demonstrated in the image, there is a continuum that ranges from underweight to extremely (morbidly) obese.

While this calculation can apply equally well to both males and females, it does not take lean muscle mass into consideration. For example, someone who has very little muscle mass can be considered "normal," while someone who has a high muscle mass, like an athlete, can be categorized as obese or extremely obese.

## Section 5: Explore: Body Fat Functions

Body fat serves various vital functions in the body. The most well-known function is that of storage fat, which can be utilized as needed for energy. The minimal, protective layer under the skin is also essential.

In men, essential fat accounts for approximately 3–8% of body weight, and the appropriate total storage and essential fat range is 15–20% of body weight. Measuring the waist to hip circumference ratio is a good indicator of body fat distribution in males. This ratio should be less than 0.95 to 1. Additionally, waist circumference alone is a good indicator of fatness as well.

In females, there is a larger amount of gender-specific fat. Healthy totals of essential and storage fat range from 25–35% of total body weight. A healthy hip to waist circumference ratio should be 0.8 or less.

Athletes may have fat levels below these ranges, but they should be monitored. This is especially important in women, where menstrual irregularities and amenorrhea (no menstrual cycle) are correlated with osteoporotic bone loss and fractures.

## Section 7: Explore: Changes in Fatness

The shape of the body can influence characteristics of fat and potential risk factors. Here are some common body shapes and characteristics that affect health:

### Tab: Android Body Type (Apple)

Characteristics:
- It is largest around the waist, the middle.
- It is common in both men and women during menopause.
- Clients have a higher waist circumference than recommended.
- It leads to an increased risk for weight-related diseases and disorders.
- Visceral abdominal fat is dangerous, but it is also easier to lose.

### Tab: Gynoid Body Type (Pear)

Characteristics:
- Fat is primarily stored in the lower body (hips, thighs, buttocks).
- More common in women, especially before menopause.
- Clients have higher levels of estrogen, with a risk of insulin resistance.
- Fat is more difficult to lose.

Regardless of body type, genetics plays a role in body shape and fat distribution. While exercise and activity can decrease the amount of fat, the distribution is based on individual DNA. Fat is mostly stored in adipocytes, special cells that are designed to hold fat droplets. This fat is in the form of triglycerides, produced from glucose, glycerol, fatty acids, and amino acids. The amount of stored fat is dependent upon energy balance. If energy is needed, the fat is broken down for transport to cells; and if it is not needed, the fat becomes stored. At birth, adipocyte numbers are relatively low and grow during phases of development and growth.

A pound of body fat is approximately 3,500 kilocalories. This means that an excess intake and positive energy balance of 3,500 calories can cause a weight gain of 1 pound, and a negative energy balance or expenditure of 3,500 kilocalories can cause a weight loss of 1 pound. This energy balance is determined by the intake to energy expenditure ratio.

**Malnutrition Underweight**

While obesity is a global epidemic affecting 1.9 billion adults, malnutrition from being underweight is also a global problem.

According to the World Health Organization, "malnutrition, in all its forms, includes undernutrition (wasting, stunting, underweight), inadequate vitamins or minerals, overweight, obesity, and resulting diet-related noncommunicable diseases. . . . 462 million adults who are underweight" (WTO, 2021, Key Facts session).

## Section 9: Explore: Influences on Weight

Despite the size and amount of adipocytes, energy expenditure, and genetic makeup, a client's weight is significantly impacted by nutritional, psychological, economic, and social factors. All these factors should be considered when evaluating a client's weight.

### Slide 1: Nutritional and Financial Factors

The nutritional content and financial ability to buy food can impact the client's weight. If the client lives in a food desert, does not have financial resources, or does not have transportation to access a variety of foods to meet the recommended MyPlate Guidelines, the client's weight will be impacted. In addition, the convenience and low cost of prepackaged and fast foods are often appealing and much less expensive than foods that are rich in nutrients.

### Slide 2: Psychological Factors

Psychological factors also play a role in the weight of a client. It is important to monitor the emotional and behavioral status of clients to evaluate for possible eating disorders or traumas. Weight-related abuse can be detrimental to one's intake. Pregnant women are especially vulnerable to abuse due to weight changes. Altered body image and cultural views on weight can also impact the client's weight.

### Slide 3: Social Factors

Social factors are also large influences on weight change and intake. Food is often seen as a social event or incorporated into celebrations. Likewise, food can become solace for those who are depressed or even bored. Cultural implications can also be included in social factors, as food plays a large role in many cultures as well. The environment of the client is also important. If the client is in an unsafe area where exercise outside is restricted, it can be classified as an obesogenic environment, one that contributes to obesity.

## Section 10: Explore: Physical Activity Versus Sedentary Lifestyle

Physical activity is a core component of maintaining a healthy weight. Just as we promote ambulation for gastrointestinal (GI) motility and recovery from illness, getting moving is essential! When you choose a sedentary lifestyle, you are putting yourself at risk for serious health consequences. Being physically active provides a great deal of benefits for both your physical and emotional health.

**Benefits of Physical Activity**
- Increases muscle and bone strength
- Increases heart rate and respiratory effort
- Decreases depression
- Increases the ability to move around
- Increases self-esteem
- Promotes better sleep and rest
- Promotes healthy weight

**Increased Risks With Sedentary Lifestyle**
- Heart disease
- Type 2 diabetes
- Depression
- Stroke
- Hypertension
- Hyperlipidemia
- Weight gain
- Loss of strength

## Section 12: Explore: Wellness Approach to Weight

Although your readings, MyPlate, and Healthy People 2030 provide recommended daily food intake and energy expenditure, there is no "quick fix" or magic formula that changes everyone's size or shape to that desired. The overarching goal of these recommendations is not a diet but a lifestyle and attitude change. Weight can fluctuate, and no one eats and exercises perfectly all the time. Adopting a diet is often done for a short amount of time, and people return to their bad habits. Instead, wellness must be the focus for success. Fueling our bodies with the correct nutrients and being physically active provides benefits that are more than just externally visible.

Strategies for maintaining healthy weight success include:

### Tab: Adopt an Active Lifestyle

- Increase physical activity such as walking and gardening.
- Choose to do body movement that is produced by the skeletal muscles, requiring energy.
- Exercise does not have to be strenuous to be beneficial. Small changes have big impacts, like walking rather than driving, parking in a further parking space, increasing steps per day, or choosing the stairs rather than the elevator.

### Tab: Establishing Realistic Goals

- Identify individual needs.
- Examine the limit and control one has over the body and amount of stored fat, considering nonmodifiable factors such as genetics and fat distribution.

### Tab: Changing Behavior

- The most important goals to establish are related to changing behavior to find habits that can be lifelong and integrated into the lifestyle.
- Examples include planning meals, grocery lists, limiting access to junk foods, and so forth.

### Tab: Normalizing Eating

- Realize that eating is a normal and necessary process that is enjoyable.
- Establish a good relationship with all foods to learn balance and moderation.
- Use hunger cues to guide eating rather than "cleaning the plate" or eating because it's time.

### Tab: Minimizing Food for Emotional Needs

Address emotions and any emotional eating that is associated. Try journaling, doing exercise, eating without guilt, and arranging safe circumstances for eating.

### Tab: Eating Regularly and Frequently

Space meals and balance nutrients to fuel the body's energy needs all day long rather than one or two large meals per day.

## Section 15: Explore: Food and Athletic Performance

As with any physical activity, athletes need food as a source of fuel for energy. Just as a car cannot run without fuel before it stops working, the body needs the appropriate fuel for the task. These are important components of ensuring that all nutritional needs are met when planning for the athlete:

### Tab: Kilocalories

Minimum requirements: 1,800–2,000 kilocalories/day with the following criteria:
- Carbohydrates: 45–65% of kilocalories
- Protein: 10–35% of kilocalories
- Fat: 20–35% of kilocalories

Athletes should eat at least the minimum number of servings from each group above daily. Depending on body size, some athletes need more.

### Tab: Hydration

Requirement: water and/or electrolyte replacement drinks

Water is the most crucial nutrient for athletic performance. Lack of hydration can present itself in less than 1 hour. Loss of 1 pound of body weight is equivalent to 480 mL (2 cups) of water that needs to be replaced.

Sports with moderate-heavy exercise and continuous activity longer than 90 minutes can benefit from sports drinks with electrolytes to prevent hyponatremia from water alone.

### Tab: Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates provide glycogen that delays the onset of fatigue and allows for longer activity. Both simple and complex carbohydrates are needed.

Carbohydrate loading, increasing the number of carbs eaten, can be beneficial if the amount or length of training is increased, increasing glycogen stores for muscles.

### Tab: Protein

With athletic activity, protein use is increased. Many athletes eat more protein than recommended, so the excess protein can be broken down into amino acids then used for energy or stored as fat.

### Tab: Fat

The amount of fat used during exercise varies based on duration and intensity of the activity, prior level of training, and composition of diet.

### Tab: Vitamins and Minerals

A balanced diet provides the necessary vitamins and minerals. Many athletes use supplements, but it is not necessary or proven to be beneficial. Toxicity can occur if both the supplement and the proper diet are maintained.
