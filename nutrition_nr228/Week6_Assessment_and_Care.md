## Section 1: Introduction to Nutritional Assessment and Care

Nursing plays an essential role in all the stages of the nutritional care plan. The nurse can help identify risk factors, health-illness considerations, cultural, and religious needs, as well as counsel on genetic influences. Nurses work with others as part of a multidisciplinary healthcare team. The nurse's approach is holistic, thereby emphasizing health and wellness versus disease.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Discuss the nutrition care plan process.
- Describe the types of nutritional assessments.
- Explain the difference between enteral and parenteral nutrition.
- Describe the role of nutrition in complementary and alternative medicine practices.

## Section 3: Explore: Nutrition and the Nursing Process

The nursing process is widely used in nutritional health. The gathering and analysis of data are for the purpose of establishing nursing diagnoses related to nutritional needs.

An important step in the nursing assessment is knowing when to consult the registered dietitian (RD) in the process. Some of the main areas in the community where a registered nurse will be doing nutritional assessments are as a:

- school nurse
- home health nurse
- long-term care nurse
- postsurgical care nurse
- public health nurse

Review the case study shown here as a quick example to see the nursing process in action as it relates to nutrition. Think about how you will use the nursing process to incorporate nutrition into a client's overall plan of care.

### Case Study: Nursing Process and Diet Therapy

**Patient Profile:** Mrs. C is an 88-year-old woman brought to the hospital by her daughter. She is admitted with a diagnosis of dehydration, malnutrition, and electrolyte imbalance. On admission to the hospital, her daughter expresses frustration that her mother "just will not eat."

**Subjective Data:**

- Daughter states her mother has been eating less and less
- Daughter states she is very worried about the decreasing appetite and food intake
- Daughter states her mother has been losing weight

**Objective Data:**

- Appears pale, weak, tired
- Is confused about place and time but is able to state her name intermittently
- Does not recognize her daughter
- Recently diagnosed with dementia
- Lives with her daughter

Okay, so you have this information. What do you need to think about for each step of the nursing process?

**Step 1:** The nurse needs to collect more data regarding Mrs. C's nutritional status. A nutrition assessment is indicated. Nutrition assessment is a comprehensive approach to defining nutrition status that uses medical nutrition and medication histories, physical examination, anthropometric measurements, and laboratory data. The nurse needs to obtain a complete dietary history from the patient's daughter. The nurse needs to discuss Mrs. C's food preferences with the daughter.

**Step 2:** Other data to be collected include height and weight, BMI (body mass index), intake and output measurements, and daily weights (weight taken each day, at the same time, on the same scale, with the same type or amount of clothing, preferably with the bladder recently emptied). A calorie count and between-meal supplements should also be considered.

**Step 3:** A plan of care needs to be developed involving the nurse, dietitian, pharmacist, and physician. Discuss the impact of the medical diagnosis of dementia on Mrs. C's malnutrition. Review all medications she is taking for possible effects on nutrition and appetite. Review data obtained from calorie count. Review all data to determine the type of malnutrition and its cause.

**Step 4:** Appropriate nursing diagnoses include imbalanced nutrition; less than body requirements related to decreased ingestion; activity intolerance related to weakness, fatigue, and inadequate caloric intake; and deficient fluid volume related to decreased fluid intake.

## Section 4: Explore: Nutritional Assessment

Data sources for assessment information include anthropometric data, food and nutrition history, physical examination, laboratory tests, and client history (Charney, 2016). Screening may be triggered by a change in medical condition, diet, intake, or nutritional support. The criteria can be remembered using the ABCDs of a nutritional assessment.

### Tab: Anthropometric — Measurements of the Client

- weight (including percent of weight change over a given time period)
- height
- BMI (body mass index)
- head circumference (used in pediatrics)

### Tab: Biochemical — Used to Determine Nutritional Deficiencies

- liver function tests
- diabetes mellitus (e.g., glucose, HgbA1C)
- renal profile
- lipids
- anemia (e.g., iron, B12, thiamine)
- protein (e.g., albumin, pre-albumin)

### Tab: Clinical — Evaluation of Medical History and Current Status

- medical condition (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, kidney disease)
- treatment (e.g., dialysis, surgery)
- medication (e.g., chemotherapy, antibiotics)
- vital signs

### Tab: Dietary — Recall of Foods and Frequency Including Food Influences

- religious, ethnic, and cultural influences
- 3-day food diary
- 24-hour recall
- diet history

When taking a food history, some use a 24-hour recall. This is an interview to obtain information about all foods and beverages consumed in the past 24 hours. However, sometimes this recall is inaccurate due to a client's memory, cooperation, and communication ability. It may not reflect someone's true diet. Another option is to have the client complete a food diary to record intake over a longer period of time. This allows the healthcare provider to see dietary patterns.

**Reference**

Charney, P. (2016). *Nutrition assessment* (3rd ed.). Momentum Press. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/951075462

## Section 6: Explore: Diagnosis and Planning

The nutrition care process model is very similar to the nursing care process, including assessment, diagnosis, planning, implementation, and evaluation.

According to the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (2018), nutrition diagnoses fall into four categories: Intake, Clinical, Behavioral, and Environmental.

**Nutrition Diagnoses (categories):**

- **Intake:** More or less than body requirements
- **Clinical:** Problems from a medical condition
- **Behavioral:** Knowledge and beliefs
- **Environmental:** Food access and safety

Planning nutrition intervention involves reviewing and prioritizing the client's nutrition diagnoses. For each diagnosis, the client-focused expected outcomes need to be defined. The provider must work with the client and/or caregivers and develop interventions that fit with the client's lifestyle and culture. Resources needed to make the plan successful should be included.

**Reference**

Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. (2018). *NCP step 2: Nutrition diagnosis*. Nutrition Diagnosis Snapshot. https://www.andeal.org/files/Docs/IDNT_Snapshot_NDe4_S2.pdf

## Section 8: Explore: Nutritional Interventions

The purpose of a nutrition intervention is to resolve or improve the nutrition diagnosis by addressing the etiology. The intervention is a planned action intended to change nutritional behaviors, risk factors, nutrition-related knowledge, environmental conditions, or access to services. Interventions are customized to the client.

According to the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (2018), nutrition intervention can be classified into multiple categories:

- **Nutrition Education:** Instructing a client in a skill or providing knowledge to help clients modify food choices, preparation, and/or quantity.
- **Nutrition Counseling:** Providing a supportive environment with a counselor to establish nutrition-related goals to treat an existing condition and promote health.
- **Food and Nutrient Delivery:** Individualized provision of food and specialty diets.
- **Coordination with Other Providers:** Consultation with or referral to other healthcare providers, institutions, or agencies that can support the management of nutrition-related problems.
- **Population-Based Nutrition Action:** Interventions focused on the nutritional health of a community.

**Reference**

Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. (2018). *NCP step 3: Nutrition intervention*. Nutrition Intervention Snapshot. https://www.andeal.org/vault/2440/web/files/20140527-NI%20Snapshot.pdf

## Section 10: Explore: Enteral Nutrition

One clinical intervention is enteral nutrition or "tube feeding." The tube, called a nasogastric (NG) tube, is inserted through the nose down to the stomach or small intestine. Another option is to place a percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG) or jejunostomy tube (PEJ) which deposits nutrition directly into the stomach or small intestine.

This intervention is used when the upper gastrointestinal (GI) system needs to be bypassed. This can be used for the inability to consume enough nutrients, difficulties swallowing, or surgery that interferes with eating. The client must have at least a partially functioning gastrointestinal system.

Enteral nutrition can be used with people of all ages and there is a wide variety of enteral nutritional formulas. Feedings can be continuous or intermittent and are counted as intake in an I&O assessment. However, they are not without complications.

- **GI complications:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal distention, cramps, diarrhea, malabsorption/maldigestion
- **Mechanical complications:** Aspiration, tube malposition, tube clogged
- **Metabolic complications:** Hyponatremia, hypernatremia, dehydration, hyperglycemia, hypokalemia, hyperkalemia

Before administering any nutrition or medication through the tube, proper placement must be verified. If the tube is placed in the stomach, the amount of residual should be checked to prevent over-inflation of the stomach. The client should be in a semi-Fowler's position during feedings and afterward to prevent aspiration. Frequently, nasogastric feedings are used for a short duration, while PEG and PEJ feedings are longer term. When the condition has resolved and the client is able to consume enough by mouth, the tube is removed.

## Section 11: Explore: Parenteral Nutrition

Parenteral nutrition is delivering nutrients through the bloodstream via an intravenous solution. The solution "may include protein, carbohydrate, fat, minerals and electrolytes, vitamins and other trace elements for patients who cannot eat or absorb enough food through tube feeding formula or by mouth to maintain good nutrition status" (American Society for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition, n.d., para. 1). This type of nutrition can be total, supplying all nutrients, or partial, supplying a portion of nutrients.

Parenteral nutrition can be used as long as needed. It bypasses the gastrointestinal (GI) system completely and is used for patients who have a non-functioning GI system or require complete bowel rest. Some conditions include short bowel syndrome, cancer, bowel disorders, and severe pancreatitis.

Parenteral nutrition is usually administered through a central line, such as a peripherally-inserted central catheter (PICC) line. Infection of the catheter is the most common and serious complication of parenteral nutrition. Sterile technique is required with the insertion and maintenance of the central line. Other complications are blood clots and electrolyte imbalances.

American Society for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition. (n.d.). *What is parenteral nutrition*. https://www.nutritioncare.org/About_Clinical_Nutrition/What_is_Nutrition_Support_Therapy/

## Section 13: Explore: Complementary and Alternative Medicine Practices

Nutrition plays a large role in complementary and alternative medicine (CAM) practices. These practices involve healing approaches and therapies that are used together with, or instead of, conventional Western medicine.

Dietary supplements are the most used CAM therapy. They are readily available, relatively inexpensive, and can be bought without a prescribing healthcare provider. Dietary supplements can contain vitamins, minerals, herbs, amino acids, or hormones. Currently, manufacturers of dietary supplements do not have to prove product safety or efficacy (Shane-McWhorter, 2020).

Chinese medicine originated over 2,000 years ago and is centered around the philosophy that disease results from the improper movement of the life force, called "Qi." Qi is restored by balancing the opposing forces of yin and yang. This is done through medicinal herbs and acupuncture. Herbs can be used in a single form or as a mixture.

Clients with cancer have one of the highest rates of using nutritional CAM since it may improve their quality of life. Research has found that herbal CAM usage was 59% among clients receiving radiation and 22% among those receiving chemotherapy (Bayir & Guney, 2019). As a healthcare provider, it is important to ask clients if they are taking any dietary or herbal supplements. Despite their benefits, mixing certain supplements with prescriptions can cause interactions or treatments to be less effective.

**References**

Bayir, A. G., & Guney, M. (2019). Nutritional assessment and use of complementary and alternative medicine in cancer patients treated with radiotherapy and chemotherapy. *Alternative Therapies in Health & Medicine, 25*(6), 28–33.

Shane-McWhorter, L. (2020). *Overview of dietary supplements*. Merck Manual Professional Version. https://www.merckmanuals.com/professional/special-subjects/dietary-supplements/overview-of-dietary-supplements

## Section 14: Explore: Nutritional Evaluation

Nutrition interventions should be monitored and evaluated to determine the amount of progress made and whether the nutrition outcomes are being met. Subjective and objective information can be collected based on the outcome being evaluated.

**Food/Nutrition-Related History Outcomes:**

- Food and nutrient intake
- Food and nutrient administration
- Medications
- Complementary and alternative medicine practices
- Knowledge/beliefs
- Food and supplies availability
- Physical activity
- Patient perspective on nutrition quality of life

**Anthropometric Measurement Outcomes:**

- Height
- Weight
- Body mass index (BMI)
- Growth pattern indices/percentile ranks
- Weight history

**Biochemical Data, Medical Tests, and Procedure Outcomes:**

- Lab data (e.g., electrolytes, glucose)
- Tests (e.g., gastric emptying time, resting metabolic rate)

**Nutrition-Focused Physical Finding Outcomes:**

- Physical appearance
- Muscle and fat wasting
- Swallow function
- Appetite
- Affect

**Reference**

Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. (2018). *NCP step 4: Nutrition monitoring and evaluation*. Nutrition Monitoring and Evaluation Snapshot. https://www.ncpro.org/nutrition-monitoring-and-evaluation-snapshot
