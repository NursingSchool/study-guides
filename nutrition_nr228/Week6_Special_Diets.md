## Section 1: Introduction to Special Diets

Diets can be ordered by a healthcare provider if a client needs certain restrictions or has a health condition that requires a special diet. These diet orders can be found in the client's medical record. For clients with strict restrictions, nutrition counseling is sometimes ordered to assist the client in transitioning to a different diet and/or food preparation techniques.

As you complete the concept, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Identify common specialty diets used in healthcare.
- Explain the purpose of specialty diets.
- Identify what foods are restricted or allowed in specialty diets.

## Section 3: Explore: Regular Diet

A regular diet would be prescribed for any client that has no restrictions or health conditions that require a special diet. It is also referred to as a general diet. The purpose is to provide a well-balanced diet that provides adequate nutrition.

In most facilities, a client on a regular diet will be able to order meals off a menu independently or with assistance if needed.

## Section 4: Explore: Nothing By Mouth

Nothing by mouth (NPO), a Latin abbreviation for *nil per os*, is a diet order that means "nothing by mouth." This refers to a time, usually before a procedure or surgery, when the client **cannot eat or drink anything in order to prevent aspiration**. If the stomach is not empty, the contents can come up the esophagus and into the lungs. The length of NPO time varies based on the situation. Commonly, clients are NPO prior to and after surgery.

Clients may also be NPO in cases of reduced consciousness, gastrointestinal problems, or unsafe swallowing reflexes. In these cases, alternative hydration and nutrition is implemented.

NPO status must be documented and discussed with the client to improve compliance. If the client needs to take medications during this time, they should be reviewed with the healthcare provider. The healthcare provider will determine which medications should be taken and if sips of water are allowed.

## Section 5: Explore: Vegetarian and Vegan Diets

Vegetarianism is the exclusion of meat, poultry, and fish from the diet. Vegetarianism is regarded as a lifestyle rather than a diet, since there are many plant-based diets. People may choose vegetarianism due to religious beliefs, environmental reasons, or for animal rights. Benefits include a reduced body mass, lower cholesterol, and lower glucose levels. However, vegetarians may be deficient in calcium, vitamin B12, iron, and zinc.

According to Frey (2013), there are several types of vegetarians:

- **Vegans:** This is the strictest of the vegetarian lifestyles. Vegans exclude all animal products from the diet and use of all animal products. For example, vegans will not consume honey or dairy products and abstain from wearing leather and wool.
- **Ovolactovegetarians:** Vegetarians who will exclude meat, poultry, and fish but will consume eggs, milk, and other dairy products since these foods are not obtained by killing animals.
- **Ovovegetarians:** Vegetarians who include eggs in the diet but not milk products, meat, poultry, and fish.
- **Lactovegetarians:** Vegetarians who use milk and milk products but not eggs, meat, poultry, and fish.
- **Semivegetarians or Pesce/Pollo Vegetarians:** People who include fish or chicken in the diet but only minimally. They abstain from red meat.
- **Fruitarians:** Vegetarians who eat only fruits, nuts, seeds, and other plant matter that can be harvested without harming the plant.
- **Flexitarians:** People who prefer a vegetarian diet but are willing to eat meat, fish, or chicken on exceptional occasions.
- **Freegans:** Vegans who seek to avoid participating in any consumer practices they regard as abusive of humans or the environment. Freegans obtain their food by growing it themselves, bartering, or foraging in refuse bins and restaurant trash receptacles for discarded food. This practice is called "dumpster diving" in the United States.

Frey, R. J. (2013). Vegetarianism. In K. Key (Ed.), *The Gale encyclopedia of diets: A guide to health and nutrition*. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/828681996

## Section 7: Explore: Liquid Diets

Liquid diets can be used as a partial or full meal replacement option. They can be used after certain surgeries or procedures as well as for weight loss. Liquid diets for weight loss should only be used by clients with large amounts of weight to lose. They supply 500 to 800 calories per day, which is a very low-calorie diet. For this reason, clients using liquid for weight loss should be closely monitored by a healthcare provider (Davidson et al., 2013). The liquid supplements are usually in the form of a shake that provides a balance of vitamins and minerals. However, the longer the liquid diet, the higher the risk of deficiencies. The two most common are full liquid and clear liquid diets.

### Clear Liquid Diets

Clear liquid diets are used to maintain fluid balance and minimize digestive impact. They are used post-surgically and following acute vomiting or diarrhea. Clear liquid means the client can consume any liquid or a food that melts into a liquid that is clear. Allowed foods include water, fat-free broth, bouillon, coffee, tea, carbonated beverages, clear fruit juices, gelatin, fruit ices, and popsicles. Foods to avoid include milk and milk products, fruit juices with pulp, and cream soups. If you can see through it, it's a clear liquid.

### Full Liquid Diets

Full liquid diets are used to provide an adequate diet for clients who cannot chew or who are being progressed off of a clear liquid diet. All clear liquids are allowed as well as smooth-textured dairy products such as ice cream, strained or blended cream soups, custards, refined cooked cereals, vegetable juice, pureed vegetables, fruit juices, sherbets, puddings, and frozen yogurt. Foods not included are solids such as nuts, seeds, fruit, vegetables, and meat.

**Reference**

Davidson, H., & Cataldo, L. J. (2013). Liquid diets. In K. Key (Ed.), *The Gale encyclopedia of diets: A guide to health and nutrition*. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/828681996

## Section 8: Explore: Dysphagia Diets

Dysphagia diets, used for clients with impaired swallowing, were once based on a three-level scale — **Level 1:** Pureed, **Level 2:** Mechanically Altered, and **Level 3:** Advanced. However, these stages did not meet many clients' unique needs and clumped together foods with beverages.

In 2019, the International Dysphagia Diet Standardization Initiative (IDDSI) provided common terminology to describe food textures and drink thicknesses. To implement, the healthcare provider or dietitian would complete an assessment and make recommendations for foods or drinks for a client. The IDDSI framework consists of 8 levels (0-7). Drinks are measured from Levels 0–4, while foods are measured from Levels 3–7. Clients with a level 0-2 are on liquids with no form of solid foods. Only after level 3 can clients consume foods. The framework describes the progression from liquids to regular solid foods.

| Level | Name | Descriptor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | Thin | Flows like water |
| 1 | Slightly thick | Thicker than water; requires a little more effort |
| 2 | Mildly thick | Flows off a spoon; slower than thin drinks |
| 3 | Moderately thick | Can be drunk from a cup or eaten with a spoon |
| 4 | Pureed | Cannot be drunk from a cup; is eaten with a spoon; no chewing |
| 5 | Minced | Eaten with a fork or spoon; soft and moist |
| 6 | Soft and blenderized | Eaten with a fork or chopsticks; no knife required; needs chewing |
| 7 | Regular | Normal, everyday food |

**Reference**

The International Dysphagia Diet Standardization Initiative. (2019). *IDDSI framework*. https://iddsi.org

## Section 10: Explore: Gluten Free Diet

Gluten is a protein in grains such as wheat, oats, rye, and barley. A gluten-free diet is consumed by people with celiac disease and gluten intolerance. Celiac disease is an inflammatory immune response to gluten which damages the small intestine. Consuming gluten may cause a medical crisis in these clients. Some clients may have sensitivity to gluten, which causes gastrointestinal symptoms.

In addition to avoiding grains, there are a large variety of gluten-containing foods. It is important to check the food labels. Foods that could contain hidden gluten include:

- Soups
- Processed foods
- Candy
- Salad dressings
- Gravy
- Soy sauce
- Imitation bacon and seafood
- Beer

Gluten-free foods are available as substitutes, but they can cost 3-5 times as much. About 30% of Americans claim to follow a gluten-free diet (Sims et al., 2017).

**Reference**

Sims, J. L., & Frey, R. J. (2017). Gluten free. In Gale (Ed.), *The Gale encyclopedia of nutrition and food labels*. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/962776324

## Section 11: Explore: Diabetic Diet

The American Diabetes Association (2020) nutrition recommendations focus on the Diabetes Plate Method. Non-starchy vegetables, such as broccoli, carrots, green beans, and leafy greens, make up half of a 9-inch plate. Lean proteins, like chicken, turkey, and eggs, make up one quarter while limiting carbohydrates, like whole grains, fruits, and dairy, to the other quarter plate. Beverages should be water or a low-calorie drink.

Other diabetic plans include carbohydrate counting or carbohydrate measurement, since carbohydrates have the greatest impact on blood glucose levels. Sugars should also be limited.

**Nutritional Recommendations Per Plate (Diabetes Plate Method)**

- ½ Nonstarchy vegetables
- ¼ Protein foods
- ¼ Carbohydrate foods
- Water or low-calorie drink

**Reference**

American Diabetes Association. (February 2020). *What is the Diabetes Plate Method?* https://www.diabetesfoodhub.org/articles/what-is-the-diabetes-plate-method.html

## Section 13: Explore: Low Sodium Diet

A low sodium diet can be classified as a no added salt diet (4g sodium diet), low sodium diet (2g or 1g), or severe sodium restriction diet (500mg sodium diet). Clients with kidney disease, heart disease, and diabetes may be required to follow a low sodium diet. Since 77% of the average American's daily sodium intake comes from processed or prepared foods, it is important to read nutrition labels and have clients cut back on added salt (Tish, 2013).

Foods with high sodium include:

- Smoked meats (sausages, ham)
- Frozen dinners
- Canned entrees (ravioli, spam)
- Salted nuts
- Soy sauce

The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet is recommended by the National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute for lowering blood pressure and sometimes accompanies a low sodium diet. The DASH diet consists of fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy foods, and reduced saturated fats. Results of studies have shown that combining a lower sodium intake with the DASH diet had a greater benefit than just the DASH diet or a low salt diet alone in hypertensive clients (Parker et al., 2013).

**References**

Parker, T. J., & Chamberlin, S. J. (2013). DASH diet. In K. Key (Ed.), *The Gale encyclopedia of diets: A guide to health and nutrition*. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/828681996

## Section 14: Explore: Renal Diet

Clients with kidney disease or kidney failure have special dietary needs. They require a diet that limits sodium, potassium, phosphorus, protein, and fluid intake in order to decrease symptoms and reduce toxins. Since these nutrients are found in many foods, the diet can be challenging for clients. Client education about substitutes is key. See the table below for a list of substitutes from the National Kidney Foundation (2019).

| Restriction | Avoid | Substitute |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Salt and Sodium | Avoid salt and artificial salt substitutes since they contain potassium. | Herbs, spices |
| Protein | Due to high potassium and phosphorus levels, avoid peanut butter, nuts, seeds, dried beans, peas, and lentils. | Lean high-protein meat, fish, poultry, fresh pork, or eggs |
| Dairy | Most dairy foods are very high in phosphorus, so intake of milk, yogurt, and cheese needs to be limited. | Lower phosphorus options like butter, non-dairy creams, and sherbet. Depending on the brand, plant-based dairy substitutes for dairy may contain high amounts of added phosphorus, so check labels before using. |
| Fruit | Limit potassium containing fruit and juices such as oranges, bananas, melons, and prunes | Apples, berries, grapes, pineapple |
| Vegetable | Limit potassium containing vegetables like potatoes, tomatoes, and avocado | Broccoli, carrots, cucumber, and green beans |

**Reference**

National Kidney Foundation. (2019). *Dietary guidelines for adults starting on hemodialysis*. https://www.kidney.org/atoz/content/dietary_hemodialysis

## Section 15: Explore: Neutropenic Diet

A neutropenic diet is sometimes referred to as a low-bacterial diet. It is prescribed for clients with suppressed immunity such as cancer and HIV/AIDS. The diet protects them from acquiring foodborne illness. It should be noted that the neutropenic diet is controversial. Some researchers argue that limiting fresh fruit and vegetables as well as dairy and meat compromises nutritional intake. Also, research shows that adhering to safe food handling practices provides adequate protection (Wolfe et al., 2018).

Clients on this diet are to refrain from:

- All fresh fruits and vegetables
- Raw or rare meat, fish, and eggs
- Salads and deli meats
- Raw nuts and oats
- Unpasteurized dairy and soft cheeses
- Yogurt with live or active cultures
- Tap water

**Reference**

Wolfe, H., Sadeghi, N., Agrawal, D., Johnson, D., & Gupta, A. (2018). Things we do for no reason: Neutropenic diet. *Journal of Hospital Medicine, 8*, 573–576. https://doi.org/10.12788/jhm.2985

## Section 17: Explore: High Fiber Diet

Dietary fiber is defined as the indigestible carbohydrate component of plants. There are two types of fiber: **insoluble fiber** and **soluble fiber**.

- Insoluble fiber, which is in whole grains and raw vegetables, helps the body with peristalsis.
- Soluble fiber, which is in many fruits, slows digestion and promotes a sense of fullness. Soluble fiber also decreases cholesterol levels, lowering the risk for heart disease.

Besides improving heart health, a high fiber diet can prevent digestive issues such as constipation and hemorrhoids. Converting to a high fiber diet should be done gradually to prevent abdominal pain and diarrhea. It is also important to tell clients to consume at least eight glasses of water and other fluids daily. A high fiber diet is contraindicated for those on a fluid restriction (Tish, 2013).

**Reference**

Tish, D. A. (2013). High fiber diet. In K. Key (Ed.), *The Gale encyclopedia of diets: A guide to health and nutrition*. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/828681996

## Section 18: Explore: Low Fiber Diet

A low fiber diet, also known as a low residue diet or restricted fiber diet, consists of easily digested foods such as white flour pastas and breads, casseroles, moist tender meats, and canned cooked fruits and vegetables.

The purpose of a low fiber diet is to reduce the bulk of stool in the intestine for easier elimination. This diet may be prescribed for clients after bowel surgery or for an irritated, inflamed gastrointestinal tract. Depending on the condition of the client, the diet may be more or less restrictive.

When educating clients about a low fiber diet, learning how to read food labels is important. They should select foods with no more than 1 gram of fiber per serving.

## Section 19: Explore: Cholesterol Restricted Diet

Originating from the American Heart Association, a low cholesterol diet is designed to decrease the risk of cardiovascular disease by limiting cholesterol to less than 300 mg per day. There are a few ways to reduce cholesterol in the diet. One way is to read food labels.

Cholesterol free foods have fewer than 2 mg of cholesterol while low cholesterol foods have no more than 20 mg of cholesterol (Davidson et al., 2013). When preparing meals, clients can decrease cholesterol by choosing lean meats, baking or broiling meats, avoiding solid fats, and cooking with olive or canola oil.

**Reference**

Davidson, T., & Cataldo, L. J. (2013). Low cholesterol diet. In K. Key (Ed.), *The Gale encyclopedia of diets: A guide to health and nutrition*. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/828681996
