## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition for Gastrointestinal Health

Adequate nutrition consists of the ingestion and utilization of water, essential nutrients, vitamins, and minerals to maintain and sustain health and wellness. Gastrointestinal (GI) health affects all other body systems. As nurses, it is essential to assess clients' nutritional well-being, identify the factors that influence nutritional outcomes, and direct the care provided based on the findings. This module will guide you in the care of individuals facing nutritional deficits related to alterations in gastrointestinal health.

As you complete the concept, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Describe the nurse's role in assessing the impact of gastrointestinal disorders on the client's well-being.
- Differentiate nutritional deficits that occur as a result of GI disorders.
- Assess a client's level of distress based on GI symptoms.
- Explain nursing diagnoses related to GI disorders.
- Plan, implement, and evaluate care for clients with dietary needs related to GI disorders.

## Section 3: Explore: Six Dimensions of Gastrointestinal Health

When a client experiences a gastrointestinal (GI) disorder, it is essential that the nurse begin to recognize cues, identifying relevant data to the clinical situation. This is part of nursing assessment. It is also important to understand the profound, holistic impact from not only a GI perspective, but an alteration in elimination as well. When completing the assessment, identify data encompassing the following six dimensions of health (Grodner et al., 2020).

### Tab: Physical Health

The client's physical health is most affected when the disorder intensifies over time, leading to weight loss and nutrient deficiencies, in addition to the pathology of the disease itself.

### Tab: Intellectual Health

Since diet is so profoundly impactful on GI disorders, finding the right combination of foods or consistently restricting certain foods can be a challenge.

### Tab: Emotional Health

Having physical symptoms like diarrhea, gas, and vomiting can be difficult to manage. Clients may struggle with acceptance. Poorly managed symptoms lead to decreased self-esteem and well-being and even decreased quality of life.

### Tab: Social Health

It is necessary to assist clients in coping with changes that occur with some disorders like dumping syndrome and colostomies.

### Tab: Spiritual Health

It is important to see the client holistically and provide strategies such as meditation and yoga or other spiritual support.

### Tab: Environmental Health

Individuals living with an acute or chronic GI alteration often hesitate to be in public, fearing the loss of bowel control when they do not know the location of the closest restroom. This can impact both social and mental health.

**Reference:** Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). *Nutritional foundations and clinical application: A nursing approach* (8th ed.). Elsevier.

## Section 4: Explore: Social Determinants of Health

The five social determinants of health are important to consider when assessing gastrointestinal (GI) alterations and developing a holistic plan of care.

**Economic Stability**

1 in 10 live in poverty and cannot afford healthy food, healthcare, and housing.

**Education Access and Quality**

- Clients with higher levels of education have a longer life expectancy.
- People with decreased income, students with disabilities, and those who are bullied are more likely to struggle with math and reading and are less likely to go to college.
- Additionally, lower educational levels are more likely to have lower paying jobs, heart disease, diabetes, and depression.

**Healthcare Access and Quality**

- 1 in 10 do not have access to healthcare providers due to lack of insurance.
- These people do not get preventative care, screenings, and medications needed.
- Sometimes clients live too far away from healthcare and/or do not have transportation.

**Neighborhood and Built Environments**

- Many people live in unsafe situations, with a high rate of violence, unsafe air/water, and other health and safety risks.
- Low-income clients are more likely to be exposed to hazards at work such as secondhand smoke, noise, and toxins.

**Social and Community Context**

- Relationships with family, community, and co-workers impact a client's well-being.
- Positive relationships can help decrease negative impacts such as unsafe living situations, discrimination, access to food, and lack of available resources.

**Reference:** Healthy People 2030. (n.d.). *Social determinants of health*. U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion. https://health.gov/healthypeople/objectives-and-data/social-determinants-health

## Section 6: Explore: Nutritional Assessment

Many gastrointestinal (GI) disorders have significant nutritional implications. In many situations, diet is the cornerstone of therapy for GI complaints. GI disorders influence overall health by affecting how nutrients are absorbed by the different organs and body systems.

### Tab: Assessment Questions

During the assessment, ask specific questions regarding:

- the client's diet
- cultural and religious influences and restrictions on dietary intake
- foods that exacerbate or alleviate any symptoms that are reported

### Tab: Assessing Nutrient Intake

Gaining information to assess nutrient intake can be challenging, as evidenced in the link below. However, nurses can perform a detailed, holistic assessment that covers the social determinants of health outlined in the Healthy People program. Additional serum tests can provide detailed evidence of nutrient intake as well as electrolyte levels to evaluate hydration status.

Take time to review the "Nutrient Status Assessment in Individuals and Populations for Healthy Aging." Be sure to carefully review "Table 1—Limitations of Current Strategies to Assess Nutrient Intake."

Péter, S., Saris, W. H., Mathers, J. C., Feskens, E., Schols, A., Navis, G., Kuipers, F., Weber, P., & Eggersdorfer, M. (2015). Nutrient status assessment in individuals and populations for healthy aging-statement from an expert workshop. *Nutrients, 7*(12), 10491–10500. https://doi.org/10.3390/nu7125547

When GI disorders and nutritional deficiencies are identified, healthcare professionals often make recommendations for nutritional supplements, diets, and nutritional interventions to reduce additional variances in health. Early identification of clients at risk by nutritional assessment and early initiation of nutrition interventions have been associated with improved clinical outcomes.

## Section 9: Explore: Focused History—Recognizing Cues

Clients who have gastrointestinal problems often present with non-specific symptoms. Review each symptom below.

### Slide 1: Abdominal Pain

Pain is one of the body's ways to indicate that something isn't quite right. Use the PQRST mnemonic to evaluate. (P-Pain, Q-Quality, R-Radiation, S-Severity, and T-Time)

### Slide 2: Changes in Appetite

Has the client had a change in appetite? Some conditions cause the client to increase their intake while others result in decreased appetite and ingestion.

### Slide 3: Changes in Bowel Habits

Establish the client's normal bowel habits (frequency, consistency, urgency, constipation, etc.) and evaluate for changes.

### Slide 4: Swallowing Problems

Changes in age (older adults have decreased saliva) or other diagnoses (multiple sclerosis, cerebrovascular accident, etc.) can cause difficulty swallowing. An in-depth swallowing evaluation can be performed by a speech therapist if needed.

### Slide 5: Food Intolerance

Is the client able to eat all foods as desired? Do some foods lead to GI upset or discomfort? Has the client had a change in the ability to tolerate certain types of food or food in general?

### Slide 6: Nausea and/or Vomiting

GI symptoms that are often seen with many disorders include nausea and vomiting due to some sort of GI disturbance. Symptoms can range from mild, occasional nausea to chronic, constant nausea and vomiting.

### Slide 7: Changes in Weight

Has the client recently had a change in weight? An increase or decrease in weight can be related to GI illness.

## Section 12: Explore: Diagnosis of Client Needs—Prioritizing Hypotheses

Formulating nursing diagnoses (by analyzing cues and prioritizing hypotheses) depends on the information obtained in the assessment and aids in the clinical judgment and decision-making process.

Various reasons can cause the client to have gastrointestinal (GI) symptoms.

### Tab: Possible Nursing Diagnoses for GI Symptoms

- Constipation (or risk for) due to decreased gastrointestinal (GI) motility, medications, disease process, etc.
- Diarrhea due to increased GI motility, medications, disease process, etc.
- Bowel incontinence due to surgical intervention, disease process, etc.
- Fatigue related to nutritional deficits/chronic disease/current life stage (pregnancy, teenager, etc.)
- Imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements
- Nutritional deficiency due to lack of access to food, intake of nutrient-poor foods, etc.
- Fluid volume deficit due to nausea, vomiting, and/or diarrhea
- Ineffective individual coping strategies due to stressful situations, recent diagnoses, etc.
- Ineffective adolescent eating dynamics due to psychosocial complications
- Ineffective child eating dynamics due to lack of access to food, loss of appetite, medications, etc.
- Ineffective infant eating dynamics due to decreased breast milk, no access to formula, cleft palate, medications, prematurity, etc.

**Remember to prioritize care. You will need to evaluate and rank the diagnoses (hypotheses) according to priority. Which one is the most urgent or highest risk for your client?**

## Section 14: Explore: Planning for Gastrointestinal Alterations

Select the tabs below to learn about the nurse's role in planning care for clients with gastrointestinal alterations.

### Tab: Diet for Clients With GI Alterations

The client with gastrointestinal (GI) alterations benefits from a diet that is low in fiber and other substances that irritate the GI mucosa. One common diet is referred to as BRAT, for bananas, rice, applesauce, and toast.

- **B** (Banana)
- **R** (Rice)
- **A** (Applesauce)
- **T** (Toast)

### Tab: Interventions for Clients With GI Alterations

Here are some other solutions and planned interventions for clients with alterations in GI health:

**Nursing Actions**

- Advocate for client testing to determine cause of symptoms.
- Evaluate electrolytes and hydration status using labs, orthostatic vital signs, and other pertinent assessment data.
- Obtain a stool sample for culture and sensitivity and other parasites and digestive abnormalities.
- Determine client-specific needs based on age and developmental stage (e.g., infant—essential nutrients in formula and baby foods).
- Evaluate the effect of symptoms on physical, intellectual, emotional, social, and spiritual health.
- Establish assistance for stressful situations and life changes (e.g., counseling, have the client speak with their healthcare provider about medications).
- Establish a dietary plan for each client—detailed information on portion control, moderation, focusing on healthier alternatives, and so forth. It can be used for clients with excessive or inadequate intake.

**Client Education**

- Provide dietary strategies to prevent additional GI upset.
- Encourage strategies for hydration if unable to tolerate food.
- Teach strategies to deal with physical ramifications such as dumping syndrome and colostomies.
- Provide activities and establish a plan for clients who are unable to eat due to medical conditions (e.g., stretching, hand weights).
- Determine and educate the client on nutritional resources in the area for access to foods like food banks, church food pantries, and government assistance to meet dietary needs despite economic ability.
- Encourage adherence and explain the importance of any diet prescribed and consult a nutritional therapist if needed.
