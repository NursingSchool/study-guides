## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Dietary Proteins

The connection between a well-balanced diet and good health is clear. Eating a variety of foods, including proteins, provides the nutrients required for optimal health, energy, weight, and concentration. As a member of the healthcare team, the nurse uses the nursing process and critical thinking to promote optimal protein intake in others.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:
- Model Chamberlain Care® when communicating with clients, families and other healthcare providers.
- Assess a client for altered protein intake.
- Identify nursing diagnoses for clients who have altered protein intake.
- Develop and implement a plan of care to address and intervene in clients with altered protein intake.
- Evaluate the outcomes of clients with altered protein intake.

## Section 3: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Screening Tools

Most nutritional screenings are triggered by weight loss in clients experiencing physical symptoms of malnutrition that occur over time (chronic malnutrition) and include the physical signs common with protein deficiency. Chronic protein malnutrition is often accompanied by other nutritional imbalances that must also be addressed to provide the body with the nutrients needed to prevent long-term complications and restore health.

Acute weight gain is often caused by fluid retention associated with a known alteration in health, such as heart or renal failure, and is treated as such.

Every client experiencing sudden weight gain or loss should receive a nutrition screening. The Joint Commission (TJC), an organization that accredits healthcare facilities, recommends that all clients whose condition could be related to altered nutrition be screened within 24 hours of admission (TJC, 2022). Each care facility may define the conditions that trigger the screening and the information to be gathered.

To assist with nutrition screening, many facilities develop tools based on the requirements outlined in their policies. One example can be seen in the nutrition care algorithm (American Society for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition).

## Section 5: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Dietary Recall

There are several methods for collecting information regarding actual and habitual dietary intake. Most commonly, data is collected using **dietary recall methods** (historical/retrospective) or **documented food records** (current/prospective). Each method has its pros and cons, so it is important to choose a method suitable for the type of information needed.

Data is collected to provide information about calories (kcal), protein, carbohydrate, fat, vitamins, minerals, and fluid intake. Results can be calculated manually with food composition tables or analyzed by computer software. More than 100 computer programs are available to analyze dietary intake.

To complete the dietary recall assessment, the examiner asks the client to report all foods and beverages consumed during the past 1–3 days. Detailed descriptions of all foods, beverages, cooking methods, brand names, condiments, and supplements, along with portion sizes in common household measures, are included. Food models, measuring cups, life-size pictures, or abstract shapes (squares, circles, and rectangles) are used to assist the client in estimating the correct portion sizes of foods consumed. This method is useful in screening or during follow-up to evaluate adaptation of or compliance with dietary recommendations.

The advantages of dietary recall assessment are quick (only 15 to 20 minutes is needed) and applicable for most age groups. Because it is retrospective, the client does not modify their actual intake. The information can be obtained by face-to-face interview, telephone interview, or client self-reporting. However, the dietary recall relies on an individual's memory and awareness, so the information may not be accurate. And since dietary recall is assessed over the past 1 to 3 days, it may not provide a clear understanding of the client's current diet.

## Section 7: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Food Journals

Keeping a food journal has become an established means of tracking foods eaten. It is a listing of all that is consumed, including portion sizes and time of day. Studies indicate that more success occurs when someone attempts to normalize food consumption and keeps written records of their food intake. Food journals, when used daily, provide a more accurate record of nutritional intake than dietary recall.

Food journals can be a single page, written in a diary or notebook, or electronically kept on a computer or mobile device. When caring for an individual with protein malnutrition, recommending the client track both what they have eaten and how they are feeling physically, emotionally, and cognitively provides both the nurse and client with a better understanding of how well their diet is supporting their health.

The discussions that occur when helping a client start a food journal and when reviewing a completed journal provide the nurse with cues regarding the client's dietary intake and opportunities to provide the client with nutritional education.

## Section 9: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Laboratory Values

The three main proteins in serum plasma are albumins, globulins, and fibrinogen. Although globulins and fibrinogen are commonly reviewed as part of a nutritional assessment, decreased levels contribute to the physical changes associated with protein malnutrition, risk for infection (decreased serum globulins), and bleeding (decreased fibrinogen).

### Key Laboratory Findings

- **Albumin**: Maintains plasma oncotic pressure; transports small molecules
  - Normal: 3.5-5 g/dL (grams per deciliter)
  - Depletion: Mild 3.0-3.4 g/dL; Moderate 2.4-2.9 g/dL; Severe <2.4 g/dL
- **Prealbumin**: Carrier protein for thyroxin; transports vitamin A when combined with retinol-binding protein
  - Normal: 16-40 mg/dL (milligrams per deciliter)
  - Depletion: Mild 10-15 mg/dL; Moderate 5-9 mg/dL; Severe <5 mg/dL
- **Hemoglobin**: Carries oxygen to cells; makes blood cells red
  - Normal: Females 12.1-15.1 g/dL; Males 13.8-17.2 g/dL
  - Depletion in general: Mild anemia 10-12 g/dL; Moderate anemia 8-10 g/dL; Severe anemia <8 g/dL

## Section 11: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Cultural & Social Influences

People around the world associate food with social interaction. Whether dining with family, friends, colleagues, or others, most meals are shared with others. And the people with whom food is shared tend to influence an individual's dietary preferences and habits. The nurse is responsible for discovering how the client's cultural and societal influences impact their overall nutritional status, including consumption of healthy protein.

### Tab: Culture

It is important that the nurse understands how an individual's cultural knowledge and attitudes guide their food choices, particularly those customs with symbolic or religious implications. Since it is important to understand the food practices of diverse groups, asking the right questions is key.
- Which food practices are influenced by the client's culture?
- Who shops for and prepares the food?
- If fasting is required, what does that mean? Can any nutrients be consumed during the fast?
- Does the client abstain from certain foods (alcohol, meat, sweets)?

### Tab: Economics

Determine if the client has the resources to get healthy foods, either by purchasing or through community resources (food pantry, community kitchen, governmental assistance).

### Tab: Education

Assess the client's understanding of protein: How it supports health, sources of lean proteins, restriction of proteins related to current health status.

### Tab: Neighborhood / Environment

Ask the client if they feel safe when getting groceries and if they are accessible to the client. Some neighborhoods, known as food deserts, do not have grocery stores. For individuals without independent transportation, getting to the store to purchase food can be difficult.

### Tab: Social Context

Changes within a client's family or social group, such as loss of a loved one or going away to college, impact individuals differently, and may interfere with the desire to eat due to social isolation.

## Section 12: Explore: Nursing Process—Protein Imbalance

For clients with protein deficit, the main nursing diagnosis focuses on the alteration in nutrition, with actions and outcomes selected to promote and monitor their progress toward an optimal nutritional balance.

Additional nursing diagnoses may be added to address physical alterations, such as increased bleeding or infection, that result from the nutritional deficit.

**Nursing Diagnosis for a client with protein malnutrition following the death of a spouse.**

Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements related to inability to access protein, decreased appetite while grieving the loss of spouse as evidenced by 15-pound weight loss in the last month, hair loss, increased bruising, frequent urinary tract infections, and lethargy.

### Tab: Expected Outcomes

- No further weight loss
- Steady weight gain of 1-2 pounds per week until reaches ideal body weight
- Consumes 80-90% of all meals and snacks
- Agrees to plan for accessing quality foods, including protein, after discharge.

### Tab: Nursing Actions

- Provide companionship during meals.
  - Rationale: Attention to the social perspectives of eating is important in both hospital and home settings.
- Offer high protein supplements based on individual needs and capabilities.
  - Rationale: Such supplements can be used to increase calories and protein without conflict with voluntary food intake.
- Validate the client's feelings regarding the impact of their current lifestyle, finances, and transportation on their ability to obtain nutritious food.
  - Rationale: Validation lets the client know that the nurse has heard and understands what was said, and it promotes the nurse-client relationship.
- Help the client establish a written plan to obtain and consume a variety of foods.
  - Rationale: Grief can be overwhelming, leaving individuals without the ability to make even simple choices. Creating a written plan for nutrition provides the client with directions and eliminates the need to make decisions.
