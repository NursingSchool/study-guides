## Section 1: Introduction to Adult Nutrition

Nutrition plays a large role in successful aging. Consuming a healthy diet is critical in meeting physical and psychological needs as well as preventing disease.

George (pronouns: he/him/his), 77 years old, and Diane (pronouns: she/her/hers), 72 years old, struggle with nutrition. In this concept, you will follow this couple and learn about their nutritional requirements as well as factors that affect their nutrition.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Explain the nutrient requirements of adults.
- Discuss the influence of diet on reducing health risks.
- Describe how factors, including oral health, appetite, senses, swallowing, depression, finances, mobility, and culture, influence adult nutrition.

## Section 3: Explore: Adult Nutrient Requirements

According to the latest Dietary Guidelines for Americans (USDA & DHHS, 2020), small changes in daily eating habits are encouraged to improve health. The focus is on following healthy combinations of food and drink over time, increasing nutrient-dense foods, limiting calories from sugars and fats, and decreasing sodium.

Watch the video ["What's Your Healthy Eating Style"](https://youtu.be/j7CcaUZrUoE) [1:45] (external supplementary resource), which illustrates the above guidelines for ways of choosing healthy food and maintaining a healthy lifestyle.

George and Diane reflected on what they normally eat and their sedentary lifestyle. Like many Americans, they don't get enough servings of fruits and vegetables and eat above the recommended amount of grains. When estimating or calculating the amount of calories for themselves, George and Diane need to consider factors, such as age, sex, height, weight, and level of physical activity, that impact the total number of calories.

## Section 4: Explore: Nutrition and the Older Adult

As George and Diane get even older, their nutrient requirements remain the same. According to Volkert et al. (2018), the older adult's body will continue to decrease the ability to absorb and synthesize nutrients. The tabs below include risk factors that decrease older adults' nutrition intake and absorption. In their 80's and 90's, it will be important to make sure they do not become malnourished and dehydrated.

Risk factors attributed to older adults' nutrition health and the corresponding interventions:

**Chewing Problems** — Interventions:

- Oral care
- Dental treatment
- Texture-modified diet, if adequate

**Swallowing Problems (Dysphagia)** — Interventions:

- Professional swallowing evaluation
- Swallowing training
- Texture-modified diet, according to swallowing evaluation

**Impaired Upper Extremity Function** — Interventions:

- Physiotherapy, occupational therapy
- Adequate help with eating and drinking (e.g. cutting food, hand-feeding)
- Provision of adequate eating and drinking aids
- Finger foods
- Shopping/cooking aid, meals on wheels

**Restricting Mobility, Immobility** — Interventions:

- Physiotherapy
- Resistance training
- Group exercise
- Shopping/cooking aid, meals on wheels
- Family-style meals in institutions

**Depressive Mood, Depression** — Interventions:

- Eating and drinking with others/shared meals
- Pleasant meal ambiance/eating environment
- Group activities, occupational therapy

**Loneliness, Social Isolation** — Interventions:

- Eating and drinking with others/shared meals
- Group activities

**Poverty** — Intervention:

- Social programs

**Acute Disease, (Chronic) Pain** — Intervention:

- Adequate medical treatment

**Adverse Effects of Medication (e.g., xerostomia, apathy)** — Interventions:

- Check medication for potential side effects
- Reduce the dose of medication
- Replace or stop the medication

**Restricted Diets** — Intervention:

- Revision and liberalization of dietary restrictions

**Reference**

Volkert, D., Beck, A. M., Cederholm, T., Cruz-Jentoft, A., Goisser, S., Hooper, L., Kiesswetter, E., Maggio, M., Raynaud-Simon, A., Sieber, C. C., Sobotka, L., Van Asselt, D., Wirth, R., & Bischoff, S. C. (2018). ESPEN guideline on clinical nutrition and hydration in geriatrics. *Clinical Nutrition, 38*(1), 10–47. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2018.05.024

## Section 6: Explore: Oral Health Considerations

Oral health can have a large impact on nutrition and food consumption. According to Eilender (2016), in the population of older adults 65 and older:

- 40% suffer from periodontal disease.
- Only 22% have dental insurance.
- 25% do not have natural teeth.

George has difficulty chewing since he has some missing teeth. He cannot afford partial dentures or implants. Where he grew up, there was no water fluoridation or use of products with fluoride. George first visited a dentist when he was 19, since his family did not have money for dental visits. In addition, George takes a medication that causes dry mouth. Therefore, he tends to eat foods that are soft and drink more flavorful beverages like sweet tea. He knows he is at risk for choking and asphyxiation due to his reduced chewing ability.

To promote oral health, George's dentist suggested these oral health tips (CDC, 2021):

- Limit sugar intake.
- Drink fluoridated water and brush with fluoride toothpaste.
- Brush teeth twice a day and floss daily.
- Visit the dentist at least once a year.
- Do not use any tobacco products and limit alcohol consumption.

**References**

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2021, November 9). *What can adults do to maintain good oral health?* Retrieved November 3, 2022, from https://www.cdc.gov/oralhealth/basics/adult-oral-health/tips.html

Eilender, E. (2016). *Nutrition throughout the lifecycle.* Momentum Press. https://chamberlain-on-worldcat-org.chamberlainuniversity.idm.oclc.org/oclc/947086415

## Section 8: Explore: Physical Considerations

The senses of smell and taste play a key role in appetite, food choices, and food intake as it promotes appetite and allows recognition of flavors (Boyce & Shone, 2006). As the body ages, the sense of smell and the ability to discriminate between smells declines. The function of taste also declines and coincides with lack of smell. Some medical conditions and medications affect these senses. Medical conditions can also affect swallowing abilities or mobility, causing difficulties with eating. It can restrict the ability to prepare food as well as eat the food.

Diane has Parkinson's disease, which is a medical condition that can diminish smell and taste. The medications she takes also cause nausea if not taken on an empty stomach. She knows that most people with Parkinson's disease eat less and lose weight. Since the disease is progressive, she may eventually have problems with swallowing. The tremors may make it difficult for her to eat certain foods and drink liquids. Her healthcare provider suggested that she try spicier foods and herbs to stimulate her appetite. She should eat foods that are easy to swallow and high in water content to stay hydrated. A dietitian may be useful if her symptoms change (Parkinson's Foundation, n.d.).

**References**

Boyce, J. M., & Shone, G. R. (2006). Effects of ageing on smell and taste. *Postgraduate Medical Journal, 82*(966), 239–241. https://doi.org/10.1136/pgmj.2005.039453

Parkinson's Foundation. (n.d.). *Diet and nutrition.* https://www.parkinson.org/Living-with-Parkinsons/Managing-Parkinsons/Diet-and-Nutrition

## Section 10: Explore: Psychological Considerations

Ever since his wife has been diagnosed with Parkinson's disease, George has had depression. He has a loss of appetite, sadness, and decreased motivation to participate in activities. He consoles himself mainly with food. He knows that certain foods, like french fries and cookies, are not good for him, but when he is feeling down, it seems to cheer him up.

His healthcare provider started him on an antidepressant and supplements. Commonly, clients with mental disorders are low in omega–3 fatty acids, B vitamins, minerals, and amino acids, which are precursors to neurotransmitters such as serotonin (Rao et al., 2008).

**Reference**

Rao, T. S., Asha, M. R., Ramesh, B. N., & Rao, K. S. (2008). Understanding nutrition, depression and mental illnesses. *Indian Journal of Psychiatry, 50*(2), 77–82. https://doi.org/10.4103/0019-5545.42391

## Section 11: Explore: Financial Considerations

George and Diane are one of the 13.7 million households in the United States that deal with food insecurity (Economic Research Service, 2022). They live off Social Security benefits but are left with no extra money at the end of each month. Like 3 out of 5 adults over the age of 60, they qualify for the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) but are not enrolled (National Council on Aging, 2021). George believes that even with financial assistance to buy food, they would still have difficulties getting to the store, which would cost more money. He also doesn't want his family to know that they cannot afford the food they need.

Overall, seniors are more likely to suffer from food insecurity if they live in a southern state, have a disability, are younger than age 69, live with a grandchild, and are African American or Hispanic (National Council on Aging, 2021).

Economic Research Service. (2022, September 7). *Food security in the U.S.: Interactive charts and highlights*. U.S. Department of Agriculture. https://www.ers.usda.gov/topics/food-nutrition-assistance/food-security-in-the-us/interactive-charts-and-highlights/

## Section 13: Explore: Cultural Considerations

For many people, food is based on culture. Most people acquire nutritional habits from childhood learning and the environment in which they grew up (Reddy & Anitha, 2015). The way food is selected, prepared, and stored is also culturally influenced. Religion is also a factor in nutrition, as some practices incorporate fasting or eating of particular foods. Healthcare providers need to be aware of cultural influences and adapt diets appropriately. Most times there are combinations of foods that will give the same nutritional results.

Both George and Diane grew up in rural Louisiana eating Creole foods. Most Creole dishes, like Cajun, consist of "the Holy trinity" of onions, celery, and green bell peppers. Creole dishes also use cream, butter, seafood, tomatoes, and herbs. They are less spicy than Cajun dishes. Diane used to do most of the cooking and was quite popular for her homemade Shrimp Étouffée. However, with her change in medical conditions and their lack of income, George and Diane struggle to maintain their food culture.

**Reference**

Reddy, S., & Anitha, M. (2015). Culture and its influence on nutrition and oral health [Special Edition]. *Biomedical and Pharmacology Journal, 8*, 613–620. https://dx.doi.org/10.13005/bpj/757
