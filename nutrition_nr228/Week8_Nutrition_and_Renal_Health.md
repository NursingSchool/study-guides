## Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Renal Health

The kidneys play a key role in maintaining homeostasis by filtering out the toxins in our blood. When the kidneys are not functioning properly, it can lead to severe system complications.

There are various types of renal disorders and diseases and based upon the type, it is important for the nurse to understand the recommended nutritional support. Proteins, fluid, and electrolytes are very important to monitor for renal complications, and diets will be highly individualized.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Describe nutrition restrictions for someone with kidney failure.
- Explain ways to prevent kidney stones with nutrition.
- Describe dietary considerations for dialysis clients.

## Section 3: Explore: Importance of Nutrition and Kidney Health

The kidneys play an important role in the body. Diseases and conditions of kidneys, such as kidney disease, renal failure, and kidney stones, can affect other body systems and have serious consequences. These diseases can be caused or worsened by underlying conditions like hypertension and diabetes. Good nutrition can prevent or slow the progression.

For example, the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (2021) recommends that clients with chronic kidney disease in Stages 1–3 concentrate on weight management, control of diabetes and hypertension, and lowering cholesterol. It would be challenging for clients to deal with a new diagnosis and learn about nutritional requirements without professional assistance.

Clients should see a dietitian when there is a new diagnosis, a decline in kidney function, high potassium or phosphorus levels, hypertension, or if there are diet-specific questions. Research has shown that nutrition therapy can improve laboratory values, maintain kidney function, and increase knowledge of the condition. Surprisingly though, approximately 88% of clients with chronic kidney disease received no dietary consultation (Slinin et al., 2011). The same study showed that those clients that did see a dietitian had a 19% lower mortality rate.

**References**

National Institute for Health and Care Excellence. (2021, November 24). *Chronic kidney disease: Assessment and management*. https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ng203

Slinin, Y., Guo, H., Gilbertson, D., Mau, L., Ensrud, K., Collins, A. J., & Ishani, A. (2011). Prehemodialysis care by dietitians and first-year mortality after initiation of hemodialysis. *American Journal of Kidney Disease, 58*(4), 583–590. http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S027263861100850X

## Section 4: Explore: Kidney-Friendly Diets

Kidney-friendly diets are aimed at preventing damage from hypertension and diabetes. The following are diets recommended by the National Kidney Foundation (n.d.) to promote kidney health.

### Transcript Link: Kidney-Friendly Diets

**The DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) Diet**

You may know this as a heart-healthy diet, but since it helps lower blood pressure, it is also a kidney-friendly diet. This diet can slow the progression of kidney disease and kidney stone formation. It is rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish, poultry, and low-fat dairy products. It limits sodium to less than 2,300 mg per day and discourages added sugars.

**Mediterranean**

In a study, clients following a Mediterranean diet reduced the risk of developing kidney disease, prevented the decline of kidney function, and promoted better kidney transplant outcomes (Gomes-Neto et al., 2020). Even though this is also considered a cardiac diet, it has a positive effect on the kidneys. Like the DASH diet, it is high in fruit, vegetables, and whole grains with lower amounts of meat and dairy products. However, it emphasizes the consumption of monounsaturated fats like olive oil and nuts.

**Plant-Based Diet**

A plant-based diet is not the same as a vegetarian diet. On a plant-based diet, clients eat mostly whole grains, fresh fruits, and vegetables, but they can also consume small portions of animal products. On a vegetarian diet, in addition to plant-based products, processed and refined foods, fast foods, and salty snacks are allowed. A plant-based diet can have many health benefits for clients with kidney disease.

**References**

Gomes-Neto, A., Osté, M., Sotomayor, C., Van den Berg, E., Geleijnse, J., Berger, S., Gans, R., Bakker, S., & Navis, G. (2020). Mediterranean style diet and kidney function loss in kidney transplant recipients. *Clinical Journal of the American Society of Nephrology, 15*(2), 238-246. https://doi.org./10.2215/CJN.06710619

National Kidney Foundation. (n.d.). *Nutrition*. https://www.kidney.org/nutrition

## Section 6: Explore: Preventing Kidney Stones

It is estimated that one in ten people will have a kidney stone over their lifetime (National Kidney Foundation, n.d.). Most stones occur from calcium combining with oxalate or uric acid accumulation in the kidneys. If a stone occurs, it can block the flow of urine and cause pain. If a stone occurs and is removed, testing will show what type of stone it is and further guide recommendations.

### Tab: Drink Enough Fluids

One of the best ways to prevent a kidney stone is to drink enough water—about 2 liters per day. This dilutes substances that can lead to stones and keeps the excretion process. Citrus beverages, like orange juice, can help block stone formation as well. Episodes of dehydration can contribute to stone formation.

### Tab: Increase Calcium Intake

One would think cutting back on calcium would decrease stone formation. However, this is a common misconception. Calcium is needed to balance oxalate and excrete it through stool. Low amounts of calcium cause oxalate to collect in the kidneys.

### Tab: Reduce Sodium

Sodium can increase the amount of calcium in the urine. If the excess calcium does not bind to oxalate and excrete through stool, then it collects in the kidneys, thus increasing the chance of stone formation.

### Tab: Limit Animal Proteins

Uric acid is a byproduct of protein metabolism. Therefore, limiting red meat, poultry, eggs, and seafood will decrease the levels in the body.

### Tab: Avoid Foods That Form Stones

Food rich in oxalate, such as beets, chocolate, spinach, rhubarb, and nuts, can create kidney stones. If a client is prone to calcium oxalate stone formation, a dietitian or healthcare provider may recommend limiting or avoiding such foods. Food rich in oxalate should be eaten with a calcium-rich food, so binding occurs in the intestines and not in the kidneys.

**Reference**

National Kidney Foundation. (n.d.). *6 easy ways to prevent kidney stones*. https://www.kidney.org/atoz/content/kidneystones_prevent

## Section 7: Explore: Limiting Sodium

Consuming less sodium is important for kidney health, especially in clients with chronic kidney disease or kidney failure. The kidneys are responsible for the sodium-water balance in the body. When the kidneys fail, the sodium can build up, causing:

- increased thirst
- systemic edema
- high blood pressure
- heart failure
- shortness of breath

A low sodium diet is less than 2,400 mg per day. To limit sodium intake, do not use salt at the dinner table, reduce salt in recipes to half the amount stated, look for 5% or less sodium on food labels, and limit amounts of restaurant and packaged foods. Use herbs and spices to flavor food, but be cautious about salt substitutes. Many are high in potassium.

## Section 9: Explore: Limiting Protein

Adequate protein is essential for muscle growth and energy. Protein is found in animal and plant sources. Animal sources of protein are considered high quality protein since they provide all the essential amino acids. Plant sources are lower quality proteins because they lack one or more of the essential amino acids.

Consuming more protein than needed makes the kidneys work harder and may make kidney disease worse. When protein is broken down, it produces a waste product called urea. If the kidneys are not functioning appropriately, urea is not removed and will build up in the body. High urea levels can cause fatigue and a poor appetite. It can also cause itchy, white deposits on the skin.

Clients with chronic kidney disease need to figure out the right combination of proteins and the right amount of proteins to eat. High quality proteins will keep the body healthy with limited portions. With that in mind, some high-quality proteins, such as dairy products, are also high in phosphorus, which is bad for the kidneys. Therefore, lower quality protein sources can be incorporated into the diet. Due to the complexity of protein intake, clients should discuss the intake of proteins with a dietitian or healthcare provider.

## Section 11: Explore: Limiting Potassium

Potassium is needed for nerve and muscle health, including keeping a regular heart rate. The kidneys are used to maintain that potassium balance in the body. In kidney disease, toxins can build up in the blood and cause cardiac problems. Side effects of high potassium include weakness, numbness, and tingling. If the levels are too high, it can lead to an irregular heartbeat, heart attack, and even death.

According to the National Kidney Foundation (n.d.), a healthy adult consumes 3,500 mg to 4,500 mg of potassium per day. On a potassium-restricted diet, only 2,000 mg per day is allowed. There are many foods that are high in potassium, such as oranges, potatoes, and whole-grain bread. Foods like apples, carrots, and white bread are lower in potassium. The healthcare provider may also prescribe a potassium binder, which binds to potassium and is excreted through stool.

**Reference**

National Kidney Foundation. (n.d.). *Potassium and your CKD diet*. https://www.kidney.org/atoz/content/potassium

## Section 12: Explore: Limiting Phosphorus

Phosphorus is a mineral that can combine with calcium. In kidney failure, phosphorus is not removed and remains in the bloodstream. It then pulls calcium out of the bones, making them weak. It can also damage blood vessels, eyes, and the heart. Signs and symptoms of high phosphorus levels include joint pain, weakness, muscle pain, itching, and red eyes. Therefore, it is important to limit phosphorus intake through diet and medications.

Phosphorus is in many packaged and popular foods. Clients with renal failure should avoid packaged foods that contain phosphorus or labeled with the word "PHOS" in the ingredients. Foods such as meats, dairy, beans, whole-grains, and dark sodas are also high in phosphorus.

Phosphorus binding medications can be taken with meals to lower the levels of phosphorus in the blood. Most of these medications are calcium-based. However, there are newer ones on the market. Clients on a phosphorus binding medication will still have to lower their phosphorus intake and watch their diet.

## Section 14: Explore: Avoiding Supplements

According to the CDC (2021), clients with kidney disease should abstain from taking any herbal supplements because some can hurt the kidneys even more. Herbal supplements can contain the same electrolytes that clients with chronic kidney disease should avoid. Also, herbal supplements can interact with prescription medications. This is particularly important for clients who have received a kidney transplant.

**Herbal Supplements That Have Potassium**
- turmeric
- dandelion
- safflower
- lemongrass
- sassafras

**Herbal Supplements That Have Phosphorus**
- flaxseed
- turmeric
- sunflower
- horseradish

**Herbal Supplements That Interact With Prescription Drugs**
- St. Johns Wort, echinacea, ginkgo, garlic, ginseng, ginger, blue cohosh

**Reference**

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2021, August 10). *Diabetes and kidney disease: what to eat?* Retrieved November 17, 2022, from https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/managing/eat-well/what-to-eat.html

## Section 15: Explore: Vitamins and Minerals

Vitamins and minerals are essential to carry out certain functions in the body. In the case of kidney disease or failure, the body may not be getting enough of certain vitamins and minerals. Healthcare providers may recommend the following supplements:

- **B Complex** works with iron to prevent anemia and helps facilitate energy.
- **Iron** prevents anemia.
- **Vitamin C** helps heal wounds and prevent infections.
- **Vitamin D** facilitates calcium absorption into the bones.
- **Calcium** keeps bones healthy.

There are some vitamins and minerals that those with kidney disease need to avoid. These include vitamins A, E, and K, because they are fat soluble, so they are stored in the body and cause dizziness, nausea, and even death. In addition, other vitamins can cause complications as well. For example, some people may take a low dose of vitamin C since larger doses can cause a buildup of oxalate which puts them at risk for kidney stones and other issues over time. The healthcare provider will determine if vitamin supplements are needed and what dosage.

## Section 16: Explore: Fluids

Depending on the type and stage of kidney disease, the client may be under a fluid restriction. The more urine the kidney produces, the fewer restrictions there are on fluid consumption. When the kidneys cannot excrete water, the fluid will build up systemically, putting pressure on the heart and lungs. The client will have signs of fluid overload.

A client's fluid intake is calculated on an individual basis depending on urine output and dialysis settings. Fluids are considered any food that is liquid at room temperature. This includes ice cream, popsicles, gelatin, and soup.

Client education regarding fluid intake includes:

- drinking no more than what is allowed
- foods that are considered fluids
- hidden fluids such as those used in cooking
- how to manage thirst

For those clients that have large fluid restriction, thirst is a big issue. Encourage clients to limit sodium, suck on ice chips, sip fluids instead of gulping, take medications with applesauce, and maintain good oral hygiene. Clients should avoid mouthwash with alcohol since this will dry the mouth.
