## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Dietary Fat

Fats have an important role in maintaining good health. Unfortunately, not all fats promote health, especially when eaten in excess of the body's needs. This lesson will review the nurse's role in recognizing complications resulting from imbalanced fat intake and promoting health through a balanced diet including healthy fats.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the nursing knowledge and skills needed to:
- Recognize assessment cues indicating a nutritional imbalance of fats.
- Identify factors that influence a client's choice of dietary fats, including life span, cultural, and socioeconomic factors.
- Educate clients on improving their health through the selection of dietary fats.

## Section 3: Explore: The Deal With Fat

Fats are complex chemical structures that provide vital nutrients and energy to the body and make foods taste good. The downside of fats is that not all types of fat are good for the body.

Watch the video, The Deal With Fat [5:53], for a brief overview of the chemical facts of fats. (SciShow, 2015, December 21. The deal with fat [Video]. YouTube. https://youtu.be/mvvx2yQRbzQ)

**Transcript:**

In a saturated fat, every carbon is bonded to two hydrogens except the last, which is bonded to three. This chemical structure makes saturated fat tend to pack together easily, which can form plaques in the arteries and interact with cholesterol, making plaques build up in the bloodstream. This chemical structure also makes saturated fat tend to be solid (e.g., butter, coconut oil).

Unsaturated fat is not saturated because there is at least one double bond between two carbons in the chain. In nature, a double bond in a fat chain almost always locks it into a kink, which prevents the fat molecules from stacking into lattices and so don't form plaques, making them more likely to be liquid (e.g., olive oil, omega-3 acid).

## Section 4: Explore: Lipid Profile

A lipid profile is a blood test used to assess a person's risk of developing cardiovascular disease (screening) and to evaluate treatment of unhealthy lipid levels (monitoring). Although most lipid profiles include total cholesterol, high-density lipoproteins, low-density lipoproteins, and triglycerides, the exact fats measured on a lipid profile may vary by laboratory.

**Total Cholesterol Test**
It measures all the cholesterol in all the lipoprotein particles.
- Normal Range: < 200 mg/dL

**High-Density Lipoproteins (HDL) Test**
It measures the cholesterol in HDL particles. HDL is often called "good" cholesterol because HDL-cholesterol (HDL-C) takes up excess cholesterol and carries it to the liver for removal.
- Normal Range: Male: 45 mg/dL | Female: 55 mg/dL

**Low-Density Lipoproteins (LDL) Test**
It calculates or measures the cholesterol in LDL particles. Usually, the amount of LDL-cholesterol (LDL-C) is calculated using the results of total cholesterol, HDL-C, and triglycerides. LDL is often called "bad" cholesterol because it deposits excess cholesterol in walls of blood vessels, which can contribute to atherosclerosis.
- Normal Range: < 100 mg/dL

**Triglyceride Level Test**
It measures all the triglycerides in all the lipoprotein particles. Triglycerides are most in the very low-density lipoproteins (VLDL), which carry triglycerides in the blood.
- Normal Range: 40–180 mg/dL
- Note: Ranges and values may vary depending on laboratories, guidelines, the client's age, and comorbidities.

## Section 5: Explore: Nursing Process and Fats

It is imperative that nurses teach clients the health risk associated with consuming a high-fat diet. Often, clients will not have any early symptoms of hypercholesterolemia. Symptoms of hypercholesterolemia occur after the disease progresses and there is substantial plaque buildup in the arteries. Clients who have high cholesterol levels and do not modify their diets will be at risk of developing diet-related diseases such as heart disease and diabetes mellitus.

**Nursing Diagnosis**
Nursing diagnoses include:
- Risk for Altered Tissue Perfusion
- Nutrition Imbalance: More Than Body Requirements

**Planning**
The nurse understands that the lifestyle and diet changes occur incrementally. The plan of care should allow the client to adjust and make mistakes. During a family-diet education session, the nurse will:

### Tab: Establish Goals

The nurse is aware that the client's goals must be patient-centered for the client to reach their goals. Therefore, the nurse identifies positive outcomes the client wants to achieve. This allows the nurse to make goals incremental, measurable, and specific to the client.

### Tab: Identify Current Diets

Identify foods that the family consumes on a regular basis. This approach allows the nurse to discuss the nutrient values of foods currently in the diet. This will also allow the nurse to make healthier comparable food suggestions based on the economic stability of the client.

### Tab: Recommend Healthy Food Options

- Compare and contrast other food options that are comparable. This prevents the nurse from discussing foods as bad and good but instead focuses on food selection in reference to limiting the intake.
- Provide resources and recommendations on selecting healthy foods at restaurants and grocery stores. This empowers the client to make healthy food selection decisions that will maintain their health in their environment. Providing resources such as apps and websites addresses the Social Determinants of Health (health literacy). Providing clients with resources that will increase the client's knowledge also addresses the client's health literacy.

### Tab: Verify Learning

Verify learning has occurred. This step allows the nurse to assess if further education is required. The nurse will refrain from asking the client if they understand not to consume a high-fat diet. Rather, the nurse will ask specific, patient-centered questions that reflect the food items currently in the clients' diet of foods they will incorporate into their diet.

## Section 7: Explore: Lifestyle Changes to Reduce Fat Intake

Here are recommendations that nurses can make to help clients reduce saturated fats and increase healthy fats (CDC, n.d.).

### Tab: Tips for Decreasing Harmful Fats in a Client's Diet

- Read food labels and avoid trans fats and hydrogenated/partially hydrogenated oils. It is important to read the label for ingredients as well as fat content.
- Avoid fried products. Fried products contain more saturated fats.
- Avoid fast food. Fast food contains increased amounts of saturated fats. In addition, fried fast food contains a lot of empty calories.
- When eating out, ask that foods be prepared with olive oil. Olive oil is a monounsaturated fat and is healthier than saturated fat.

### Tab: Tips for Increasing Healthy Fats in a Client's Diet

- Cook with olive oil.
- Plan snacks of nuts or olives. Nuts have high amounts of monounsaturated fats.
- Eat more avocados. Avocados contain high amounts of monounsaturated fats. These fats are good to consume.
- Dress one's own salads instead of using commercial dressings. Use monounsaturated oils to make the salad dressing. Commercial salad dressings contain unhealthy fats and high amounts of sugar.

### Tab: Tips for Parents

Nurses can use the "Parent Tips: Cooking With Healthier Fats and Oils" handout to explain which oils are the healthiest for their family to consume. For example, canola oil is better than coconut oil because it contains less saturated fat. The less saturated fat oils a food contains, the healthier it is. (National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute. (n.d.). Parent tips: Cooking with healthier fats and oils [PDF]. National Institutes of Health. https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/educational/wecan/downloads/tip-fats-and-oils.pdf)

## Section 12: Explore: Client Education: Types of Fats

Nurses should include these important teaching points in client education.

### Tab: Types of Fats

Nurses must teach clients they should consume more polyunsaturated and monounsaturated fats. These types of fats are contained in products such as canola oil, peanut oil, and avocado oil. Saturated fats should be consumed sparingly, and trans fat should be removed from the diet (AHA, 2017).

**Love It — Unsaturated (Poly & Mono)**
- It lowers the rates of cardiovascular and all-cause mortality.
- It lowers bad cholesterol and triglyceride levels.
- It provides essential fats the body needs but can't produce itself.

**Limit It — Saturated**
- It increases the risk of cardiovascular disease.
- It raises bad cholesterol levels.
- Note: Saturated fats are found primarily in meat products, palm oil, and coconut oil. It is important to note that trans fats are processed by the body like they are saturated fats. This is why trans fats are unhealthy and can lead to heart disease.

**Lose It — Artificial Trans Fat, Hydrogenated Oils & Tropical Oils**
- They increase the risk of heart disease.
- They raise bad cholesterol levels.
- Note: It is important to note margarine can be consumed in moderation. Margarine is made from monounsaturated fats. It is important to mention that some margarines can contain high amounts of trans fat, so it is vital to read the food labels.

### Tab: Recommended Resources

There will be instances when clients may have difficulty determining how much fat is in a food product. This may occur in restaurants or even at the grocery store. Many phone apps and websites can identify the amount of fats and cholesterol in food. Nurses must be able to provide clients with resources that will help them make healthy diet and lifestyle decisions.

These websites provide nutrition information about whole foods and restaurant foods:
- Nutritionix Database: https://www.nutritionix.com/database
- Myfitnesspal: https://www.myfitnesspal.com/food/search
