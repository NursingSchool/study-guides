## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Energy

Nurses play a large role in client education. Promoting a healthy lifestyle, weight, and energy balance is no exception. Although there are so many diet myths today, nurses can positively influence a client's well-being.

Upon completion of this learning activity, you will gain the skills needed to:

- Describe the nurse's role in promoting a healthy lifestyle and energy balance.
- Differentiate positive and negative lifestyle behaviors that affect energy.
- Assess a client's energy level and the factors that contribute to it.
- Explain nursing diagnoses related to energy.
- Plan, implement, and evaluate care for clients with energy imbalances.

## Section 3: Explore: Client Energy Sources

Clients have multiple factors that influence energy levels. It is important for nurses to complete a thorough history to obtain both subjective and objective data to gain an overall picture of health. Because people get vital nutrients from the foods they eat, it is essential to evaluate the client's dietary intake and nutritional status.

Energy in the human body must be converted from nutritional sources into chemical energy. It is the progression of associated chemical reactions that are interdependent. There are two main energy pathways: anabolism and catabolism. These two processes together are called metabolism. They are interdependent and closely regulated in the body by hormones. Both energy pathways work together to produce energy for life-sustaining functions. The primary source of fuel for energy is food. The measurement of energy released from food is called kilocalories or calories.

In the human body, foods provide a great source of energy, but they must be transformed. After foods are ingested, the processes of digestion, absorption, and metabolism occur. The three energy-supplying macronutrients that we receive from food are carbohydrates, fats, and proteins.

## Section 5: Explore: Amount of Energy Needed

The amount of energy required by the body depends on various factors. Activity level, illness, injuries, and chronic diseases are important considerations for determining the amount of nutrients for a client.

The recommendations from MyPlate, Healthy People 2030, and the United States Department of Agriculture's Dietary Guidelines for Americans have suggested guidelines for intake across the lifespan for light-to-moderate activity levels. It is essential to consider the activity level of the client and the size of the client to determine the accurate intake for healthy weight and energy needs.

To formulate a plan for each client, three areas should be considered for energy needs. These include total energy expenditure, physical activity, and the thermic effect of food.

## Section 6: Explore: Nurses' Role in Client Education on Energy

Nurses can play a role in explaining the concept of fueling the body for energy purposes and focusing on health promotion and disease prevention. With the general population being bombarded with advertisements for multiple diets and "healthy foods," providing accurate information is essential.

Nurses can provide support and work as part of an interdisciplinary team of dietitians, healthcare providers, behavior therapists, and psychologists. Help clients understand that a healthy lifestyle can include enjoying food and making choices about how much, when, and where foods are eaten.

The traditional approach of exercising more and eating less is not the method that is currently promoted. In the past, dietitians have been consultants; now they are coaches and counselors, assisting clients with lifestyle changes. The focus of the long-term approach should be client-centered, rather than being on a "diet." Accepting genetics and the concept that wellness is the focus of health will also aid in the lifestyle change process.

## Section 8: Explore: Assessment of Energy

Energy requirements vary throughout the lifespan, among genders, and also with varying medical conditions. The nurse needs to use assessment skills to determine the appropriate energy needs for the client. Estimated calorie needs are determined by using the Estimated Energy Requirements formula involving heights and weights. For the most part, levels of activity vary from sedentary to very active, with different scales providing different classifications. One such scale is presented here.

Additionally, clients should be assessed to determine the following:

- **Typical dietary intake** — Identify protein-calorie malnutrition, vitamin deficiencies, or iron deficiencies.
- **Ability to perform activities of daily living** — Can the client meet his/her needs?
- **Perception of energy** — Does the client feel that they have adequate energy each day? Does sleep each night provide rest and energy?
- **Medical diagnoses** — Evaluate medical history to determine possible implications for change in energy. Conditions like heart failure, kidney disease, cancer, and pregnancy can alter energy. Evaluate labs such as blood glucose, electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, and oxygen saturation (resting and with activity).
- **Medications** — Certain medications can cause an increase or decrease in energy.
- **Sleep disorders** — Sleep apnea, insomnia, and night terrors can interfere with energy levels, the amount of sleep received each night, and the need for sleep aids.
- **Physical activity** — What is the client's activity status? What physical exercise is done and how often?
- **Emotional status** — Depression and other mental illnesses can cause alterations in energy.

## Section 9: Explore: Diagnosis of Client Needs

Formulating nursing diagnoses depends on the information obtained in the assessment. Various reasons can cause the client to not have the energy needed. Possible nursing diagnoses include:

- inability to adapt to changes in diet to promote health
- fatigue related to nutritional deficits/chronic disease/current life stage (pregnancy, teenager, etc.)
- ineffective individual coping strategies due to recommended dietary changes
- excess nutrients due to intake exceeding nutritional requirements
- nutritional deficiency due to lack of access to food or intake of nutrient-poor foods
- lack of knowledge related to a healthy lifestyle and energy needs

## Section 10: Explore: Planning and Interventions for Energy

Planning and interventions for clients with energy needs include the following:

### Tab: Nutritional Needs and Access

- Determine client-specific nutritional needs based on age and developmental stage (e.g., infants need essential nutrients in formula and baby foods).
- Explain nutritional needs and plan dietary consultations and healthy meal resources.
- Establish a dietary plan for each client including detailed information on portion control, moderation, focusing on healthier alternatives, etc. This can be used for clients with excessive or inadequate intake.
- Determine and educate clients on nutritional resources in the area for access to food, like food banks, church food pantries, and government assistance, to meet dietary needs despite economic ability.

### Tab: Activity and Exercise

- Implement energy-saving strategies for clients who are unable to be active due to medical conditions (e.g., delegating tasks to others and planning daily activities).
- Provide activities and establish a plan for clients who are unable to exercise due to medical conditions (e.g., stretching and hand weights).

### Tab: Sleep and Distress

- Develop and teach strategies to promote healthy sleep patterns (e.g., routine, no electronics at night, and resources for finding a safe place to rest).
- Establish assistance for stressful situations and life changes (e.g., counseling and having the client speak with their healthcare provider about antidepressants).

## Section 12: Explore: Specific Requirements for Energy

Clients will need specific nutrients in addition to the macronutrients for the production of energy in different stages of life. Review the changes in nutrients for each stage (Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine, 2020).

### Tab: Pregnancy*

- **Increased requirements:** energy, protein, essential fatty acids, vitamin A, vitamin C, B-vitamins (B1, B2, B3, B5, B6, B12, folate, choline), calcium, phosphorus, magnesium**, potassium, iron, zinc, copper, chromium, selenium, iodine, manganese, molybdenum

### Tab: Lactation*

- **Increased requirements:** energy, vitamins A, C, E, all B-vitamins, sodium, magnesium**
- **Decreased requirements:** iron

### Tab: Infancy, Childhood*

- **Increased requirements:** energy, protein, essential fatty acids

### Tab: Adolescence*

- **Increased requirements:** energy, protein, calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, zinc (females only)

### Tab: Early Adulthood (Ages 19-50)

- **Increased requirements for males, compared with females:** vitamins C, K, B1, B2, B3, choline, magnesium, zinc, chromium, manganese
- **Increased requirements for females, compared with males:** iron

### Tab: Middle Age (Ages 51-70)*

- **Increased requirements:** vitamin B6, vitamin D

### Tab: Older Adult (Age 70+)*

- **Increased requirements:** vitamin D
- **Decreased requirements:** energy; iron (females only)

**Note:**
*Relative to adult requirements for those 19-50 years of age (and on a per kg basis for macronutrients)
**Applies only to individuals under age 18.

**Reference:** Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine. (2020, December 9). *Nutritional requirements throughout the lifespan: A guide for clinicians.*

## Section 14: Explore: Evaluation for Energy

Clients' energy needs should be evaluated throughout their lives. The nurse should encourage yearly visits to monitor routine lab values, such as electrolytes, lipids, and cholesterol, and to check weight and vital signs.

Promoting healthy lifestyle modifications throughout the lifespan is essential to changing the mindset to focus on energy needs.
