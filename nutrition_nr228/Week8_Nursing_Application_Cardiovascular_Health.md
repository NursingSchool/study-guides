## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition and Cardiovascular Health

Cardiovascular disease is a group of diseases and conditions that affect the heart and blood vessels. It is currently the leading cause of death in men and women throughout the United States among all ethnic and racial groups. Diet is an important risk factor in coronary heart disease. Food-related risk factors include obesity, high blood pressure, uncontrolled diabetes, and diets high in saturated fats. A low-saturated fat, high-fiber, high plant-based diet can substantially reduce the risk of developing heart disease.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Utilize prior knowledge of theories and principles of nutrition and cardiovascular health.
- Implement the nursing process to determine appropriate nutrition for clients with cardiovascular disease.
- Demonstrate clinical decision making as it pertains to cardiovascular health.

## Section 3: Explore: Cardiovascular Disease

Cardiovascular disease remains one of the world's leading causes of death due to genetic and lifestyle influences. Selecting a heart-healthy diet and adopting an active lifestyle will help decrease the risk for development of cardiovascular disease that can lead to multiple health complications.

The nurse plays a large role in client teaching, promoting, and modeling a healthy lifestyle and preventative behaviors.

## Section 4: Explore: Assessment

Assessment of the client at risk for cardiovascular disease includes identification of risk factors through health history and physical examination. Understanding the pathophysiology of cardiovascular disease also assists the nurse in identifying the risk factors. Select the tabs below to learn more.

### Tab: Health History

- chest pain, heaviness, shortness of breath, weakness
- diet and exercise patterns
- smoking and alcohol intake
- history of heart disease, hypertension, diabetes, or other cardiovascular problems

### Tab: Physical Examination

- vital signs
- weight, body mass index, and weight to hip ratio
- peripheral pulse assessment
- heart sounds
- skin color and temperature
- presence of diaphoresis
- anxiety
- respiratory assessment—lung sounds, rate, and effort
- oxygen saturation
- electrocardiogram and cardiac rhythm

### Tab: Pathophysiology of Cardiovascular Disease

The model below explains the pathophysiology of cardiovascular disease. It is important to remember that foods and nutrients absorbed from the gastrointestinal (GI) tract enter the bloodstream first before being broken down in the liver. It is especially important to know about fats, as the fatty deposits collect along the arterial walls forming plaque. This leads to hypertension, coronary artery disease, and eventually other cardiovascular issues if left untreated or modified.

## Section 5: Explore: Nursing Diagnosis and Planning

Clients who are at risk for nutritional and cardiovascular alterations may have the following nursing diagnoses:

- Acute pain related to angina pectoris after eating a large meal
- Ineffective health maintenance due to unhealthy dietary choices and lack of physical activity
- Knowledge deficit related to cardiovascular disease and nutrition

Appropriate planning for clients with nursing diagnoses related to cardiovascular disease (CVD) and nutrition include the following:

- Provide teaching for modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors.
- Evaluate dietary intake and plan consultation with the dietitian for heart-healthy diets such as Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH).
- Identify lifestyle changes to decrease the risk for CVD (e.g., stopping smoking, changing diet, and increasing activity).
- Regulate blood pressure through modifiable behaviors and appropriate medication administration.

## Section 6: Explore: Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension Diet

DASH stands for Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension. The DASH diet is a lifelong approach to healthy eating that is designed to help treat or prevent high blood pressure (hypertension). The DASH diet plan was developed to lower blood pressure without medications.

### Tab: Reducing Sodium

The DASH diet encourages you to reduce the sodium in your diet and eat a variety of foods rich in nutrients that help lower blood pressure, such as:

- potassium
- calcium
- magnesium

### Tab: Prevention

The DASH diet is also in line with dietary recommendations to prevent:

- osteoporosis
- cancer
- heart disease
- stroke
- diabetes

### Tab: Versions of DASH

There are two versions of the DASH diet:

- Standard DASH diet—2,300 milligrams of sodium a day
- Lower-sodium DASH diet—1500 milligrams of sodium a day

## Section 7: Explore: Evaluation

Evaluating the nutritional needs of clients with cardiovascular disease (CVD) includes the following:

- adherence to medication
- dietary modifications
- increase in activity as physically able
- improvement of vital signs and decrease in cholesterol and lipid levels
- cessation of chest pain, improvement of tissue perfusion, and adequate circulation (PaO2 and PaCO2 normal levels)
- maintenance of systolic blood pressure, pulse pressure, and cardiac rhythm within normal parameters
- demonstration of decrease in or absence of complications from CVD

## Section 9: Explore: Hypertension

Complications of cardiovascular disease include both primary and secondary hypertension. Explore each further below.

### Tab: Primary Hypertension

Primary hypertension has no single known cause, but several factors are linked to variations in blood pressure control. These are:

- genetics factors
- diet especially increased salt (sodium chloride) intake
- obesity
- insulin resistance
- excess alcohol
- aging
- increased stress
- sedentary lifestyle

### Tab: Secondary Hypertension

In secondary hypertension, blood pressure is raised due to a known underlying cause. These are:

- renal disorders (e.g., chronic pyelonephritis, diabetic nephropathy).
- vascular disorders (e.g., coarctation of the aorta).
- endocrine disorders (e.g., primary hyperaldosteronism).
- drugs (e.g., alcohol, cocaine)
- miscellaneous causes (e.g., scleroderma, obstructive sleep apnea)

As a nurse, it is important to teach clients about ways to decrease cardiac disease risk, which includes eating and maintaining a healthy diet. Knowing factors that are linked to an increased risk of cardiovascular disease can help direct clients into making healthy choices to support heart health.

## Section 11: Explore: Dyslipidemia and Diet

Altered serum lipids lead to dyslipidemia. Dyslipidemia can refer to levels that are either higher or lower than the normal range for those blood fats.

- Dyslipidemia most often occurs as a result of diet and lifestyle.
- Dyslipidemia may also be triggered by intermittent hypoxemia from sleep apnea.

### Tab: Trans Fat

Trans fats increase low-density lipoprotein (LDL, "bad" cholesterol) levels and decrease high-density lipoprotein (HDL, "good" cholesterol) levels; therefore, they should be closely monitored and/or restricted. Encourage clients to read nutrition facts on labels of packaged foods which identify trans fat amounts.

Trans fats can be found in:

- some margarines (especially stick margarine) and shortening
- foods with hydrogenated or partially hydrogenated fat or oil

### Tab: Hydrogenated Oils

Many commercially made foods contain partially hydrogenated oils:

- bakery goods—cakes, pies, cookies, and crackers
- premixed products—pancake mix and chocolate drink mix
- fried foods—corn chips, french fries, fried chicken, and fried fish
- snack foods—chips (e.g., corn, tortilla, potato) and microwave popcorn

### Tab: Saturated Fat

Saturated fat is also a type of dietary fat. It is one of the unhealthy fats, along with trans fat. Foods like butter, palm and coconut oils, cheese, and red meat have high amounts of saturated fat.

## Section 14: Explore: Tips for Eating Healthy

Ten heart-healthy diet and lifestyle recommendations from the American Heart Association (n.d.) include:

- Start by knowing how many calories you should eat and drink to maintain your weight.
- Increase the amount and intensity of your physical activity to match the number of calories you take in. Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate physical activity or 75 minutes of vigorous physical activity—or an equal combination of both—each week.
- Follow the Mediterranean or DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) eating plan. Eat a variety of nutritious foods from all the food groups, and focus on the following:
  - a variety of fruits and vegetables
  - fiber-rich whole grains
  - low-fat dairy products
  - skinless poultry and fish, prepared in healthy ways
  - nuts and legumes
  - olive oil and non-tropical vegetable oils
- Eat fewer nutrient-poor foods: saturated fats, trans fat, sodium, red meat, sweets, alcohol, and sugar-sweetened beverages.
  - Alcohol can alter the effectiveness of some cardiovascular medications, such as beta-blockers (e.g., atenolol, esmolol, and metoprolol sulfate).
- Eat a variety of fresh, frozen, and canned vegetables and fruits without high-calorie sauces or added salt and sugars. Replace high-calorie foods with fruits and vegetables.
- Eat fish at least twice a week, especially sources of omega-3 fatty acids (salmon, etc.).
- Select fat-free (skim) and low-fat (1%) dairy products.
- Avoid foods containing partially hydrogenated vegetable oils.
- Choose low sodium foods and prepare them with little or no salt.
- Use alcohol in moderation: one drink per day (women) and no more than two drinks per day (men). Red wine contains resveratrol, a protective substance. (American Heart Association, n.d.)

## Section 16: Explore: Eating Away From Home

Once a person understands their dietary restrictions and can make healthy choices at home, their next challenge is often understanding how to select healthy items when dining outside their home, where they do not control which ingredients are used to prepare and cook the food. Even when people limit how often they eat out, a small amount of the wrong food could exacerbate their cardiovascular disease.

Fortunately, dietary restrictions do not need to interfere with eating healthy outside one's home when people take time to plan. Some tips that can be shared by the nurse or registered dietitian include:

- bringing a healthy dish to gatherings with friends or neighbors
- asking that high-sodium ingredients be left out of a dish
- removing high-fat items, such as cheese, from a salad
- going online to look at menus and nutritional information to know what foods are most aligned with their dietary needs

For the client's overall sense of well-being and being a part of a community, it is important for them to understand that staying at home for all meals can limit interactions with friends and relatives. The key to eating away from home is to create a plan that works for them.
