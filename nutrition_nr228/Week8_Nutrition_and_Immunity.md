## Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Immunity

Cancer, human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), and acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) involve not only dealing with the diagnosis and subsequent challenges of the actual disease processes but also the treatment of side effects that affect nutrition.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Describe the role of nutrition in prevention of cancer.
- Discuss how treatments of cancer, HIV, and AIDS impact nutrition.
- Explain nutritional considerations for clients with cancer, HIV, and AIDS

## Section 3: Nutrition and Cancer Prevention

According to the World Health Organization, it is estimated that between 30% and 50% of all cancers are preventable through modifiable risk factors (WHO, 2022, Reducing the Cancer Burden section). The World Cancer Research Fund International (WCRF, n.d.) makes recommendations based on strong evidence relating to diet, nutrition, and exercise. Studies have shown that adhering to the recommendations greatly reduces the risk of cancer.

### Transcript Link: World Cancer Research Fund & American Institute for Cancer Research Recommendations

**Avoid tobacco**
- The most important means of reducing cancer risk is to not use any form of tobacco and avoid all exposure to tobacco smoke.

**Be a healthy weight**
- There is strong evidence linking body fat and weight gain in adult life to 12 different cancers.

**Be physically active**
- Limit sedentary time and be moderately active to prevent weight gain

**Eat wholegrains, vegetables, fruits, and beans**
- Eat at least five servings of fruits and vegetables along with at least 30g of fiber each day

**Limit 'fast foods'**
- Diets high in fat, starches or sugars, and processed foods contribute to weight gain

**Limit red & processed meat**
- Limit portions to three times a week and avoid processed meats

**Limit sugary drinks**
- Drink water or unsweetened beverages. Sugar sweetened drinks should be avoided.

**Limit alcohol**
- There is strong evidence linking consumption of alcohol to over 7 cancers

**Don't rely on supplements**
- High dose dietary supplements should not be used to prevent cancer. Eating healthy is better protection.

**Breastfeed your baby**
- Breastfeeding benefits both mother and baby. Lactation protects against breast cancer and having been breastfed lowers the risk of asthma and type 2 diabetes.

**References**

World Cancer Research Fund International. (n.d.). *Cancer prevention recommendations*. https://www.wcrf.org/diet-activity-and-cancer/cancer-prevention-recommendations/

World Health Organization. (2022, February 3). *Cancer*. https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/cancer

## Section 4: Explore: Nutrition and Cancer Treatment Goals

Cancer treatment can cause side effects that affect nutrition. Eating well will help:

- Maintain strength and energy.
- Lower the risk of surgical complications.
- Help repair and rebuild normal tissues.
- Lower the risk of infection.
- Promote an increased tolerance to therapy side effects.
- Help enhance the quality of life.

### Meet Annette

Annette (pronouns: she/her/hers) was diagnosed with Stage II breast cancer. After getting over the shock of the diagnosis, Annette decided she was going to do her best to beat the cancer. Her healthcare provider informed her that the illness and treatments will change the way she eats. Prior to beginning treatment, she was encouraged to stock her pantry with a variety of foods and prepare meals ahead of time. She also reached out to a few family members and asked if they could assist with shopping and cooking. Her healthcare provider explained that cells in the gastrointestinal tract and bone marrow have a rapid turnover rate and some treatments damage them. She should be prepared for side effects and decreased immunity.

## Section 6: Explore: Anorexia and Cachexia as a Result of Cancer

Common causes of malnutrition in clients with cancer are anorexia and cachexia. Anorexia is the loss of appetite for food and occurs with almost all cancers. Cachexia is the weakness and wasting of the body with a weight loss of greater than 5% in 6 months. Most of the time, it is common with cancers that effect eating or digestion, but it can occur if the body is not storing fat or maintaining muscle due to tumor growth. Cancer causes the release of cytokines, which leads to hypermetabolism requiring increased nutritional requirements. Most clients with cancer do not have the nutritional base to compensate for this, so the body begins to break down fat and muscle. Cachexia can lead to death, so if needed, parenteral or enteral nutrition may be used. Clients can have both anorexia and cachexia at the same time.

Etiology is usually multifactorial and can include:

- side effects of treatment and/or medications
- changes in taste and smell
- decreased peristalsis
- infections
- psychological and emotional effects
- hypermetabolism

### Annette

Annette underwent a bilateral mastectomy and nearby lymph nodes were biopsied. Since the lymph nodes on the right side were abnormal, she was scheduled for radiation therapy. At first, she didn't feel like eating much because of the surgical pain, so she tried eating small amounts of fruits and vegetables throughout the day. She would add in protein-rich snacks like yogurt and peanut butter. The anorexia worsened after she had a few weeks of radiation. Her healthcare provider told her to eat something small every few hours and drink high-calorie, high-protein beverages like canned liquid supplements between the small meals instead of with the meals.

## Section 8: Explore: Nausea and Vomiting as a Result of Cancer

Nausea and vomiting are serious side effects of cancer and greatly impact nutrition. They can be caused by tumors, cancer treatments, slowing of bowels, electrolyte imbalance, infection, or anxiety (National Cancer Institute, 2018). Sometimes clients may have anticipatory nausea or vomiting due to an expectation of these symptoms related to treatment. This can cause clients to refuse or skip treatment. Nausea and vomiting can cause dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, fatigue, slow wound healing, and anorexia.

### Annette

Annette (pronouns: she/her/hers) received radiation therapy to the chest which caused nausea and vomiting in addition to the anorexia. Her healthcare provider ordered her antiemetic medication. She tried not to eat her favorite foods when sick to avoid linking them to feeling sick. She ate foods that were bland and easy on her stomach, like crackers, toast, yogurt, and broth if she had nausea. She would drink small amounts of clear liquids to help control the vomiting. She also kept a record of when she experienced these episodes to better prepare in the future.

## Section 9: Explore: Taste Abnormalities as a Result of Cancer Treatment

According to the American Society of Clinical Oncology (2020), clients with cancer can have taste changes during and after treatment. Common taste changes include foods tasting bland, metallic-like, chemical-like, or all foods having the same taste. This is caused by medications, nerve damage involved in taste, changes in saliva, or destruction of oral mucosa.

### Annette

Annette's (pronouns: she/her/hers) cancer was found to be hormone receptor negative, so she also started chemotherapy. The chemotherapy drug caused her to have a metallic taste in her mouth which made her anorexia and nausea worse. Her healthcare provider told her to use sugar-free gum or mints and plastic utensils. She found that those tips along with brushing her teeth or using mouthwash prior to eating really helped counteract the metallic taste. Cold foods tasted better than hot foods, plus they have fewer cooking odors, which helps the nausea. She was able to taste seasonings that were more tart, such as lemon and lime. Her healthcare provider stated it should stop about 3 to 4 weeks after treatment ends.

**Reference**

American Society of Clinical Oncology. (2020, January). *Taste changes*. Cancer.Net®. https://www.cancer.net/coping-with-cancer/physical-emotional-and-social-effects-cancer/managing-physical-side-effects/taste-changes

## Section 11: Explore: Nutrition and Human Immunodeficiency Virus—Treatment Goals

Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) causes HIV infection which can advance to the stage of acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS). HIV destroys the immune system, making the body susceptible to infections and cancer. Antiretroviral therapy is used to treat HIV infection by reducing the amount of virus in the body. Good nutrition helps maintain the immune system and body weight.

### Transcript Link

A healthy diet will:

- Prevent malnutrition
- Preserve body mass and function
- Minimize symptoms and complications
- Improve absorption of medications
- Improve quality of life

## Section 12: Explore: Malnutrition as a Result of Human Immunodeficiency Virus

Malnutrition and human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) is a vicious cycle. The HIV infection causes immune impairment leading to malnutrition that causes further immune deficiency and possible progression of the HIV infection to acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS). This is depicted in the image to the right.

Anorexia is a frequent symptom of HIV and AIDS. The causes are many, including opportunistic infections, such as oral thrush or side effects of medications. This causes a loss of body mass and fat stores. If treatment is not available or not used, wasting syndrome is common. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention define wasting syndrome as "the involuntary loss of more than 10% of one's body weight while having experienced diarrhea or weakness and fever for more than 30 days" (CDC, 2021, Wasting Syndrome Due to HIV section). The development of wasting syndrome is an important predictor of morbidity and mortality. Interventions must be initiated early, otherwise results may be fatal.

### Transcript Link: HIV/Malnutrition Cycle

The HIV infection causes immune impairment leading to malnutrition that causes further immune deficiency and possible progression of the HIV infection to acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS).

Decreased intake, digestion, and absorption lead to alterations in bowel activity and metabolism, which cause malnutrition and weight loss, leading to decreased immunity and increased risk of infection.

## Section 13: Explore: Physical Impairments—Human Immunodeficiency Virus

Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) and resulting opportunistic infections affect every body system, causing physical impairments especially if left untreated. For example, infections in the eyes can lead to bleeding in the retinas and possible blindness. Some HIV medications can damage the heart, liver, and kidneys, yet the benefits outweigh the risks. Nausea, vomiting, mouth and esophageal lesions, and impaired dentition are common symptoms of acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS). All these physical changes impact nutrition.

Determining causes is crucial to successful intervention. It is important for people with AIDS to keep their weight up. They may need to increase their intake of protein and calories. Drinking extra water can reduce some side effects of medications and prevent dehydration. Healthcare providers may recommend vitamin and mineral supplements as well. If needed, parenteral or enteral nutrition may be used.

## Section 14: Explore: Risk for Foodborne Illness While Immunosuppressed

Anytime the immune system is compromised, whether it be through cancer treatments or a condition like acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS), clients are more susceptible to food borne illnesses. It is important for this population to pay close attention to food safety rules. Developing a food borne illness can cause serious complications or lead to death.

Clients with suppressed immune systems should avoid:

- cold meats such as deli lunch meat, hot dogs, and salami
- unpasteurized dairy products and fruit juices
- smoked fish
- raw, unwashed fruits and vegetables
- undercooked meats and poultry
- raw or undercooked seafood and fish
- undercooked eggs or foods with raw egg
