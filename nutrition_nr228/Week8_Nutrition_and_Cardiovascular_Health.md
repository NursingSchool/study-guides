## Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Cardiovascular Health

Eating nutrition-rich food is essential to staying healthy. From the moment a person is born, a healthy diet supports growth while reducing the risk of chronic disease. The National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion (2022) reports that "adults who eat a healthy diet live longer and have a lower risk of obesity, heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers" (para. 1). In addition, healthy eating can help people manage their chronic problems and avoid complications.

Unfortunately, not everyone has access to healthy options and settles for foods that are higher in calories and lower in nutritional value. In addition, people in low-income communities and those with specific food preferences related to culture or dietary restrictions often lack access to convenient places that offer healthy, affordable foods.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Review nutritional habits and practices that support cardiovascular health.
- Apply nutritional principles for cardiovascular health in an evolving case study.

**Reference:** National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion. (2022, September 8). *Poor nutrition*. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. Retrieved November 18, 2022. https://www.cdc.gov/chronicdisease/resources/publications/factsheets/nutrition.htm

## Section 3: Explore: Nutrition and Cardiovascular Health

Two of the leading causes of heart disease and stroke are high blood pressure and high blood cholesterol (National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion, 2022). Consuming too much sodium can increase blood pressure and the risk for heart disease and stroke. The 2020–2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommend getting less than 2,300 mg a day, but Americans consume more than 3,400 mg a day on average (as cited in CDC, 2021, "Facts").

The average American consumes too much sodium, saturated fat, and sugar, increasing their risk of chronic diseases. For example:

- Fewer than 1 in 10 adolescents and adults eat enough fruits or vegetables.
- Six in ten young people aged 2 to 19 years and five in ten adults consume at least one sugary drink on any given day.
- Over 70% of the sodium that Americans eat comes from packaged, processed, store-bought, and restaurant foods (National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion, 2022, para. 3).

This over consumption of foods is linked to negative health outcomes and will have consequences throughout the person's lifetime. Eating foods low in saturated fats and high in fiber and increasing access to low-sodium foods, along with regular physical activity, can help prevent high blood cholesterol and high blood pressure.

## Section 5: Explore: Risk Factors

When looking at risk factors, it is important to identify which factors you can and cannot control. For example, if looking at cardiovascular disease, the risk factors are grouped into two categories: controllable and uncontrollable.

**Controllable** risk factors are those which you can take steps to change or influence:
- diet
- smoking
- excessive alcohol consumption
- inactivity

**Uncontrollable** risk factors are those which you cannot influence:
- age
- family history
- ethnicity
- gender

Early identification of risk factors as well as actions to change or improve your controllable risk factors will help ensure that you can enjoy a long and active future.

## Section 6: Explore: Lipids

Lipids perform three primary biological functions within the body: they serve as structural components of cell membranes, function as energy storehouses, and function as important signaling molecules.

Lipids are classified into 3 categories. These categories include:

### Tab: Triglycerides

- The largest class of fats makes up more than 95% of lipids in the diet.
- They are found in fried foods, vegetable oil, butter, whole milk, cheese, cream cheese, meats, avocados, olives, corn, and nuts.
- They can be semi-solid (fats are room temperature) or liquid (oils)

### Tab: Phospholipid (Lecithin)

- component of dietary lipids
- acting as an emulsifier (soluble in water and fat at the same time)
- found in plants (e.g., soybeans) and animals (e.g., egg yolks)
- crucial for building a protective barrier, or membrane, around your body's cells
- transporting lipids within the body
- not essential

### Tab: Sterol (Cholesterol)

- least common type of lipid
- necessary to bile, vitamin D, sex hormones, cells in the brain and nerve tissues
- not essential
- only found in animal products
- being considered bad for health, but cholesterol is essential for life

While it may seem that eliminating all triglycerides from the diet is in the best interests of one's health, that is not the case. Triglycerides are used to create cell walls, transporting lipids within the body, and synthesizing bile, vitamin D, and sex hormones. Following a balanced diet that increases the number of plant-based food sources and limits the number of fats and red meats, such as the DASH diet, provides enough triglycerides to maintain overall health and decrease cardiovascular disease risks.

## Section 7: Explore: Screening for Dyslipidemia

A diet high in "bad" fats contributes to dyslipidemia (increased serum triglycerides) and is associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular disease. For most individuals, changing their diets to include fewer "bad" and more "good" fats is a risk factor they can control. Because cardiovascular disease risk increases as people age, screening for dyslipidemia provides data that can motivate what may be an unwanted dietary change.

Triglyceride levels are divided as follows:
- Normal—less than 150 mg/dL (1.7 mmol/L)
- Mildly increased—150 to 499 mg/dL (1.7 to 5.6 mmol/L)
- Moderately increased—500 to 886 mg/dL (5.6 to 10.0 mmol/L)
- Very high—greater than 886 mg/dL (10.0 mmol/L)

**Triglycerides should be measured after fasting for at least nine hours.

| Men | Women |
|-----|-------|
| Screening should start at age 35 years (if there are no other risk factors). | Screening should start at age 45 years (if there are no other risk factors). |
| Screening should start at age 25 to 30 years if there are other risk factors, such as obesity, diabetes, high blood pressure, smoking, or family history of cardiovascular disease at a young age. | Screening should start at age 30 to 35 years if there are other risk factors, such as obesity, diabetes, high blood pressure, smoking, or family history of cardiovascular disease at a young age. |

## Section 9: Explore: Access to Healthy Food

Access to healthy food choices can be a challenge for many individuals. Those in low-income urban neighborhoods, rural areas, and tribal communities often face challenges with accessing healthy foods.

Availability of healthy food options, fruits and vegetables is often limited. When these healthy options are available, the cost is too expensive for those with budgetary constraints to purchase.

The Center for Disease Control has developed programs to eliminate these inequities. The Racial and Ethnic Approaches to Community Health program has been developed to provide funds for states and communities to improve food systems in the areas through food hubs, local stores, farmers' markets, and bodegas.

**Reference:** Center for Disease Control and Prevention. (2022, July 25). *Racial and ethnic approaches to community health*. Retrieved November 21, 2022 from https://www.cdc.gov/nccdphp/dnpao/state-local-programs/reach/index.htm

## Section 11: Explore: Nutritional Therapy

The dietary changes that support heart health focus on controlling existing cardiovascular problems by decreasing the rate of atherosclerosis, lowering the overall cholesterol triglyceride levels, and reaching or maintaining a healthy weight. This often includes altering the types and amounts of the fats, fiber, carbohydrates, and proteins.

**Fats**
- Replacement of saturated and trans fats with monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats can help decrease LDL cholesterol levels and increase HDL cholesterol levels.
- Use more olive and canola oils and nuts, such as walnuts, almonds, or pistachios.
- Fish oils may also reduce inflammation.

**Soluble Fiber**
- 5 to 10 g of soluble fiber per day
- 2 to 3 g of plant sterols or stanol esters per day

**Carbohydrate**
- Eating more complex carbohydrates (e.g., whole grains) increases soluble fiber intake, improves serum lipid levels, and helps control blood sugar levels.
- 45%-65% of daily calories should be consumed as complex carbohydrates.

**Protein**
- Substitute plant proteins for animal proteins.
- Use fish more often.

## Section 13: Explore: Nutritional Therapies and Disease Process

Because the risk factors and lifestyle choices that lead to atherosclerosis also contribute to cardiovascular disease, the focus on diet modifications varies slightly. Select each + sign to review key guidelines that promote disease specific cardiovascular health.

**Heart Disease**
- Reduce unhealthy cholesterol levels in dietary intake.
- Limit alcohol intake, especially if on medication.
- Avoid saturated fats.

**Hypertension**
- Limit sodium intake to an average intake of 1,500 to 2,300 mg.
- The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension **(DASH) diet is often recommended because:**
  - It is lower in sodium.
  - It is rich in potassium, magnesium, and calcium.
  - The nutrients help lower blood pressure.
  - It increases the consumption of fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy products.
  - It reduces the consumption of saturated and total fats.

**Heart Failure**
- Lessen the workload of the heart.
- Limit sodium intake in terms of these conditions:
  - Mild to moderate heart failure: 3000 mg/day
  - Unresponsive or severe heart failure: 2000 mg/day
  - Fluid restriction if the client is hyponatremic (1 to 2 L/day)
