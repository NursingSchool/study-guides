## Section 1: Introduction to Nutritional Factors

Many factors influence the foods our clients eat, such as culture, religion, and economics. Some factors are individualized, including dietary habits or the presence of chronic disease processes. External factors can include the type and amount of food availability. For example, food deserts, areas where nutritious food may be scarce, can significantly affect a client's nutritional status. Fortunately, there are many resources available to reduce food shortages and promote healthy food habits.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:
- Discuss personal and community factors that affect nutrition.
- Identify local and national resources that help improve nutrition.
- Review the nursing care of clients with nutritional imbalances.

## Section 3: Explore: Factors and Influences

The decisions that clients make about food choices determine their health. Nurses must have a basic understanding of how certain factors overlap and intersect to influence our clients' food choices.

Let's use broccoli as an example. How does a head of broccoli get to our clients' plates? Broccoli is affected by global factors such as climate, government, trade policies, and sociocultural norms like food preparation. ## Section 4: Explore: Organizations That Help

Many organizations fill a void by preventing malnutrition and improving health through better food choices. Some agencies take a global perspective (like the World Health Organization), while others focus on individual communities or states. These agencies may be funded by governments, private foundations, and/or local donations.

Here are some areas agencies address to affect food availability and choice.

### Tab: Globally

Various organizations funded by governments and private entities address food shortages and availability. Issues include working with governments and organizations that may be located in areas of war and famine. The primary goal is food supply and reduction in disease (addressing food storage, potable water, etc.).

### Tab: Nationally

The U.S. Institute of Medicine (IOM) makes nutrition recommendations. The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) incorporates these recommendations into a set of dietary guidelines, such as Healthy People. The Department of Health Services identifies goals through Healthy People 2030 that address food shortages and nutrition.

Other national organizations, like the American Dietetic Association, publish nutrition-related guidelines as well. Even some industry groups make food suggestions, especially if it helps sell their products (e.g., the National Milk Producer's Federation). Food producers fortify foods to help with nutritional deficits.

### Tab: Locally

In the community environment, we can observe how a community addresses issues such as food deserts and lack of access to fresh foods. For example, Meals on Wheels is a community intervention that addresses access to food. Various support organizations, churches, and local schools offer nutritional programs. Local food banks offer food to individuals and families in need.

## Section 5: Explore: Social Determinants of Health and Nutrition

Social determinants of health are conditions in the environments in which people are born, live, work, play, worship, and age that affect a wide range of health, including nutrition (Alliance for Strong Families and Communities, 2020). Nurses must assess all aspects of the client's environment to address any barriers the client may encounter while trying to maintain good health. Once the client's barriers to nutrition are identified, the nurse can implement interventions and recommendations that are client-centered.

Let's review how a client's assessment intersects with the social determinants of health.

**Transcript — Social Determinants of Health:**

- **Economic Stability**: Assess the client's employment and their ability to purchase food to maintain a healthy diet. The ability to consistently acquire food is the most critical social determinant influencing healthy eating.
- **Neighborhood and Physical Environment**: Assess the client's neighborhood for access to parks and activities for the client to stay active.
- **Education: Health Literacy**: Assess if the client has the capacity to obtain, process, and understand basic health information.
- **Community and Social Context**: Assess the client's community support structure, such as community gardens.
- **Food**: Assess the client's access to healthy food options.
- **Healthcare System**: Assess the client's access to healthcare resources such as dietitians, personal trainers, or nutritionists.

## Section 7: Explore: Community Nutrition

As healthcare professionals, when we think about community nutrition, we think about a group of people maintaining good health. Examples of groups of people are older adults in a living center, children at an elementary school, or all pregnant women in one state.

As an example, let's consider all the healthy individuals in the U.S. as one group. The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS) provide dietary guidelines and lifestyle recommendations for healthy individuals in the U.S. In addition, the USDA and DHHS provide education and resources that help guide individuals and communities in making healthy food selections. The USDA's and DHHS's overarching goals are to prevent diet-related diseases resulting from a nutrient deficiency or excess. Four major guidelines (USDA & DHHS, 2020) are provided for communities with a blueprint to follow.

As nurses, it is important to help clients incorporate these guidelines into their lifestyles. Nurses must perform thorough assessments to ensure diet planning, recommendations, and goals are client-centered.

**Transcript — Dietary Guidelines for Americans 2020-2025:**

- **Guideline 1**: Follow a healthy eating pattern at every life stage.
- **Guideline 2**: Customize and enjoy food and beverage choices to reflect personal preferences, cultural traditions, and budgetary considerations.
- **Guideline 3**: Focus on meeting food group needs with nutrient-dense foods and beverages, and stay within calorie limits.
- **Guideline 4**: Limit foods and beverages higher in added sugars, saturated fat, and sodium, and limit alcoholic beverages.

## Section 8: Explore: Family Education

Meet the Johnsons. The nurse is at their house to help with diet education. The nurse is incorporating the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) Healthy People recommendations into the Johnson family education session.

**Transcript — The Johnson Family Education Session:**

**Healthy Eating**
- **Follow a healthy eating pattern across the lifespan**

"Mr. Johnson, as you get older your energy requirements will decrease. Remember, you used to cut the grass and take the kids to daycare. Now your son cuts the grass and you babysit the grandkids in the backyard. It is important for you to consume the same amount of nutrients equal to your activity. It is important for you to read the food labels and focus on serving sizes to ensure you're not consuming too many nutrients. If you consume excess nutrients, you will gain weight."

**Variety of Foods**
- **Focus on a variety of nutrient density**

"Mr. Johnson, when grocery shopping, select foods that have the maximum amount of vitamins and nutrients. Mr. Johnson, which car does better in a race, the car with high-octane fuel or the car that uses low-budget fuel that's been fortified? Our bodies work the same way. For example, a whole apple contains more nutrients than apple juice."

**Limit Sugar, Fat, and Sodium**
- **Limit calories from added sugars and saturated fats and reduce sodium intake**

"Mr. Johnson, you're already doing this. Good job limiting your intake of those store-bought cookies several months ago. Only eating cookies during the holidays or once a month is a great treat for yourself."

**Make Healthy Choices**
- **Shift to healthier food and beverage choices**

"Mr. Johnson, I know you like carbonated sodas. Sodas contain a lot of sugar. Try to decrease your quantity of soda. A healthier beverage choice is mineral water with a slice of lemon or fruit puree."

**Focus on Family**
- **Support healthy eating patterns for all**

"We all have a role to play in the health of our communities. Mr. Johnson, the spring will be here soon, so during your next block party consider having healthier food options available. I know everyone likes hot dogs, hamburgers, and potato chips. However, consider offering salad and fresh fruit."

## Section 10: Explore: Food Portions

### Follow-Up Question

The nurse is incorporating the United States Department of Agriculture's (USDA) food guide recommendations into the Johnson family education session. Mr. Johnson (pronouns: he/him/his) asked his second question.

**Image Transcript:** Mr. Johnson asked: "So what do I say to my block party guests when they start asking about this pie chart with no desserts?"

### The Nurse's Response

Let's review a few of the important details.
- Fruits—Focus on whole fruits. Use canned or frozen when fresh fruit is not available. Fruits provide added fiber and minerals to your diet. Make half your plate with fruits and vegetables.
- Vegetables—Vegetables can be raw, cooked, canned, frozen, or 100% juice. Choose colorful vegetables (USDA, n.d.).
- Grains—Focus on whole grains. Whole grains provide more fiber, nutrients, and minerals. Look for whole grains that are first or second on the ingredient list.
- Dairy—Food items that retain calcium are in this group, but cream and butter are not in this category. Consume fat-free yogurt and soy beverages.
- Protein—Poultry, seafood, beans and peas, eggs, processed soy products, nuts, and seeds are protein source examples. Vary your protein source, such as fist (a way of measuring portion size using your hand) twice a week. Choose lean meat to reduce the amount of fat consumed.
- Drinks—Drink beverages with little to no sugar. Water and tea are good examples. Sweetened beverages provide no added nutrients.

Stay active! There is a website called Move Your Way® (https://health.gov/moveyourway) that provides activity resources. Also, check out the website MyPlate (https://www.choosemyplate.gov).

## Section 14: Explore: Setting Goals and Evaluation

After providing the Johnson family education on nutrition, how will the nurse know that interventions, such as diet education, will lead to effective change? What goals will the nurse set and how will the goals be measured?

### Tab: Nursing Actions

The nurse will continually assess and evaluate the client's attitudes, knowledge, and motivation to improve or maintain a healthy diet. In addition, the nurse will observe the client for positive behavioral changes, such as verbalizing an increase in activity.

Since diet has a direct effect on health, the nurse should also observe:
- improvements in health and healing
- signs of malnutrition or obesity
- normal body mass index (BMI) of 20-23
- waist circumference under 40 inches for men and under 35 inches for women

### Tab: Mr. Johnson's Follow-Up

Six months later, Mr. Johnson (pronouns: he/him/his) and his family returned to the clinic for their monthly check in. The nurse observed that Mr. Johnson had a BMI of 21, a waist circumference of 35, improvement in blood pressure, and a lower resting heart rate. His labs indicated his cholesterol was within normal limits.

In the client's electronic health record (EHR), the nurse documents Mr. Johnson's statements and actions taken that demonstrate his motivation and attitude toward improving his health.
