## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition and Renal Health

The kidneys play a key role in maintaining homeostasis by filtering out the toxins in the blood. When the kidneys are not functioning properly, it can lead to severe system complications.

There are various types of renal disorders and diseases and based upon the type, it is important for the nurse to understand the recommended nutritional support. Proteins, fluid, and electrolytes are very important to monitor for renal complications, and diets will be highly individualized.

This learning activity explores nutrition and immunity within application activities using the nursing process and clinical judgment. If you are not yet comfortable applying this knowledge, please return to the nutrition and renal health concept to review prior to completing this activity.

As you complete these application activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Utilize prior knowledge of theories and principles of nutrition and renal health.
- Utilize prior knowledge of theories and principles of nursing while implementing the nursing process.
- Demonstrate clinical decision making as it pertains to the concept.

## Section 3: Explore: Case Scenario—Chronic Kidney Disease

Viola Crandon (pronouns: she/her/hers) has had diabetes for over 20 years and hypertension for 15 years. As part of a routine check-up, she was asked to submit a urine sample and have labs drawn. The sample had a 60% glomerular filtration rate and there was a high level of protein in the urine. Her healthcare provider sent her to have an x-ray with contrast, which revealed evidence of minimal structural damage. She was diagnosed with Stage 2 chronic kidney disease (CKD).

The healthcare provider explained how kidney disease progresses through stages and the role of nutrition. Read the following progress note from the healthcare provider.

## Section 5: Explore: Chronic Kidney Disease Progression

Viola contacted her healthcare provider 6 months after her initial diagnosis of Stage 2 of chronic kidney disease (CKD), because she noticed swelling in her hands and feet. She is also having back pain that she had not had before, is always tired, and is not urinating as much.

Serum blood testing showed a decrease in her estimated glomerular filtration, rated from 60 at the time of diagnosis to 51 now, which means she is now in CKD Stage 3.

Based on her current condition, the healthcare provider (HCP) changed Viola's blood medication from a beta blocker to an angiotensin II receptor blocker, which will help reduce her serum protein levels. The HCP referred Viola to a registered dietitian for assistance with meal planning.

Viola loves to cook, and it is a challenge for her to abide by any diet restrictions. The dietitian stressed that healthy food choices could slow the progression of the disease and discussed ideas to overcome the challenges of adhering to a diabetic diet while also lowering sodium and protein intake. Here are the interventions:

- Avoid processed or packaged foods.
- Eat less potassium-rich foods.
- Limit animal proteins, including dairy products.
- Use herbs and spices to flavor foods, instead of salt.

## Section 8: Explore: Renal Failure

Viola was doing well by following her diet and health regimen until her mother passed away. Viola was close to her mother and found it difficult to complete the activities of daily living. Friends and neighbors, unaware of her medical history, brought over meals and groceries for her. Viola didn't want to disappoint or offend them, so she ate the food provided.

Her husband (pronouns: he/him/his) became worried about her. She had obvious weight loss and fatigue. One week later, he noticed that she was confused about simple things and wasn't eating. She said she was nauseated and had muscle cramps. He noticed she was itching her arms a lot. After some convincing, he took her to the emergency department (ED).

The healthcare provider diagnosed her with:

- renal failure
- uremia
- metabolic acidosis
- hyperglycemia
- hypertension

Viola has orders to be admitted and begin hemodialysis immediately.

## Section 10: Explore: Dialysis

Viola was upset because she felt like she couldn't eat anything. The dietitian stated that a plate should contain 1/2 vegetables, 1/4 carbohydrate-rich food, 1/4 high-protein food, and a piece of fruit. The biggest change is which fruit and vegetables Viola can eat.

Viola started on dialysis and met with a dietitian again. Although the dialysis filters out the toxins, it is vital that the meal plan is followed to avoid complications between dialysis sessions. Viola will have to continue her diabetic diet and consume:

- More high-quality proteins
- Less foods with high sodium
- Less potassium-rich foods
- Less foods containing phosphorus
- Less fluids
