## Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Trauma

Complicated stress can be caused by trauma, illness, disease, and various types of surgery. It leads not only to an increased risk of disease but impairs nutritional status and further decreases immunity. It is a vicious cycle.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Review energy requirements and other nutritional needs in times of severe stress.
- Identify the effects of complicated stress on metabolism.
- Discuss high-risk populations and the nutritional needs of clients with complicated stress.

---
## Section 3: Explore: Severe Stress

View the slideshow using the arrows for an explanation of severe stress.

### Slide 1 / 2

The severe stress caused by trauma, complex illness or disease, and surgery complicates the client's underlying health and recovery. Some examples of traumatic problems that lead to complicated stress are burns, fractures, malabsorption issues, and high-risk surgeries such as hip replacement, open-heart surgery, and bariatric surgery.

In an ideal world, all clients who are to undergo surgery will have optimal health and nutritional status. However, the client may have nutrient deficiencies secondary to the underlying health concern causing the need for surgery. These clinical manifestations include, but are not limited to:

- nausea
- vomiting
- fatigue
- anorexia
- fever
- decreased oral intake

### Slide 2 / 2

Severe stress may place the client in a hypermetabolic state. The road to recovery is to treat the underlying stress in collaboration with meeting their nutritional needs. This collaborative approach will support healing and decrease the risk of infection.

If the client has nutritional deficits prior to complicated stress, then this can further complicate the healing and recovery process. If the client does not have an immediate need for surgery, then meeting their nutritional needs will come first, prior to being a surgical candidate.

## Section 5: Explore: Ebb and Flow Phases

Let's review the two phases of a severe stress response and examples of clinical manifestations.

- **Ebb phase** (early phase)—immediately after injury
  - decreased oxygen consumption
  - hypotension
  - hypothermia
  - hyperglycemia
  - lethargy
  - increased circulating hormones (glucagon and cortisol)
- **Flow phase**—36 to 48 hours after injury
  - increased oxygen consumption
  - normal blood pressure (normotensive)
  - hyperthermia
  - hyperglycemia
  - increased nitrogen excretion
  - increased circulating hormones (glucagon and cortisol)

The flow (recovery) phase can last for days, weeks, or months until the injury heals.

If the client has nutritional deficiencies or is in poor nutritional status prior to severe stress, then they are at a greater risk of infection and poor wound healing.

## Section 7: Explore: Food and Nutritional Therapy

There is an increased need for protein, vitamins, nutrients, minerals, and fluids during a hypermetabolic state. Review the protein requirements and fluid needs shown here.

Pay close attention to the client's hydration/fluid status. Increased fluid losses are possible from fever, increased urine output, diarrhea, draining wounds, or diuretic therapy.

These specific formulas are estimates to help determine the energy and fluid requirements of clients experiencing hypermetabolic stress. It takes an interdisciplinary team approach, including primary care providers, registered nurses, and registered dietitians to calculate specific protein, fats, carbohydrates, and fluid needs within the plan of care.

**Protein requirements:**
- normal: 0.8 g/kg body weight
- moderate stress: 1.0 to 1.5 g/kg body weight
- severe stress: 1.5 to 2.0 g/kg body weight

**Fluid needs:**
- adults younger than 55 years: 35 to 40 mL/kg body weight
- adults 55 to 65 years: 30 mL/kg body weight
- adults older than 65 years: 25 mL/kg body weight

## Section 10: Explore: Effects of Stress on Carbohydrates, Fats, Vitamins, and Minerals

During times of complicated stress, carbohydrate (CHO), fat, vitamin, and mineral metabolism can be affected. Select each tab to look at the effects of stress on CHOs, fat, vitamin, and mineral metabolism.

### Tab: Carbohydrate Metabolism

- Hepatic glucose production increases.
- Insulin levels and glucose increase.

### Tab: Fat Metabolism

- Fat from adipose stores is mobilized for energy due to increased levels of catecholamines and a simultaneous decrease in insulin production.
- Fat stores are rapidly depleted if hypermetabolic clients are not fed during this time.

Note: Resulting malnutrition increases susceptibility to infection. Malnutrition may contribute to multiple organ dysfunction syndrome, sepsis, and even death.

### Tab: Vitamins and Minerals

- The need for most vitamins and minerals increases.
- If kilocalorie needs are met, the client is most likely to receive adequate amounts of most vitamins and minerals.
- Special attention should be given to vitamins A, C, and zinc because they play key roles in supporting skin integrity, wound healing, and immune system functioning.

#### Image Transcript: Essential Vitamins and Minerals

**Essential Vitamins and Minerals**
- **Eye function**: vitamins A, C, and E, Lutein, and zinc
- **Brain and memory**: vitamins B6 and B12, and folic acid
- **Heart and blood**: B complex, vitamins D and K, omega-3, and CoQ10
- **Digestion and detox**: vitamins A, B1, B3, B7, B12, C, and D
- **Muscular system**: B complex, vitamins C, D, and E, and omega-3
- **Reproductive health**: vitamins B6 and B12, folic acid
- **Energy release**: vitamin B12, magnesium, and CoQ10
- **Bone strength**: Vitamins D and K2, calcium, and magnesium

**Diet and Supplements**
- Add healthy nutrients to your diet.
- Eat more fruits and vegetables.
- It's better to consume a variety of foods than taking supplements.
- Ask your doctor how to get the nutrients you need and how to integrate supplements into your diet.

## Section 12: Explore: Nutrition Needs - General Surgery Client

Nutrition needs are increased in clients undergoing surgery because nutrition deficiencies can develop. The nurse should pay careful attention to nutritional status before surgery and individual nutrition needs after surgery. Under-nutrition can lead to decreased protein synthesis, weakness/fatigue, multiple organ dysfunction syndrome, and even death. Clients who are malnourished or in danger of malnutrition must be recognized early so corrective action can be taken prior to surgery. This includes a swallow evaluation to determine any oral feeding difficulties.

Proper nutrition after surgery is essential for wound healing and recovery. An increased need for protein is key for the surgical client. Some risks of protein deficiency after surgery include:

- poor wound healing
- rupture of the suture lines
- delayed healing of fractures
- depressed heart and lung function
- failure of gastrointestinal (GI) stomas
- anemia
- reduced resistance to infection
- liver damage
- extensive weight loss
- muscle wasting
- increased risk of death

## Section 14: Explore: High-Risk Clients

Clients who are at higher risk of nutritional deficiencies include:

- malnourished, starved, and obese clients
- children, older adults, and pregnant women
- clients with burns
- clients with broken bones, hip replacement, open-heart, and bariatric surgeries
- clients with multiple traumatic events
- clients with traumatic brain injury (TBI) that are severely hypermetabolic and catabolic
- clients with spinal cord injury (SCI) and those with multiple traumas in addition to SCI

A severe stress response stimulates the release of norepinephrine, epinephrine, and cortisol, resulting in a greater hypermetabolic response. Clients at high risk have significantly longer hospital stays and higher mortality rates than clients with an optimal nutritional status (Grodner et al., 2023).

**Reference**

Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). *Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach* (8th ed.). Elsevier.
