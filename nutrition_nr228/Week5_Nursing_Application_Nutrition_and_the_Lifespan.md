## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition and the Lifespan

Clinical decision making is important in identifying actual and potential health risks and providing interventions that promote health. The following application activities will focus on the nursing process and clinical judgment as they pertain to nutrition care across the lifespan.

If you are not yet comfortable applying this knowledge, please return to the nutrition across the lifespan concepts to review prior to completing this activity.

As you complete these application activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

- Utilize prior knowledge of theories and principles of nursing while implementing the nursing process.
- Recognize lifespan and cultural influences that impact nutrition and care.
- Demonstrate clinical decision making as it pertains to the concept.

**Nursing Process / Clinical Judgment (image transcript)**

- **Assessment (Recognize Cues):** Can students identify the most important information in a client scenario? — Recognize signs and symptoms; recognize normal vs. abnormal; identify history of.
- **Analysis (Analyze Cues, Prioritize Hypothesis):** Can students distinguish the most important information? — Connection between pathophysiology and client presentation; use findings/observations to determine client needs. Can students synthesize important information to determine client needs? — Prioritize client needs (likelihood, risk, etc.).
- **Planning (Generate Solutions):** Can students develop possible care options that align with client needs? — Things to address; things to avoid.
- **Implementation (Take Action):** Can students identify and perform the appropriate clinical actions? — Potential steps vs. appropriate steps; is potential action indicated, nonessential or contraindicated. Actions may be: request an order; administer a treatment or medication; perform (skill); document and/or communicate.
- **Evaluation (Evaluate Outcomes):** Can students evaluate outcomes and determine the effectiveness of actions they have deemed appropriate? — What findings indicate improvement or worsening of a condition.

## Section 3: Explore: Nutrition Scenario

The Satos and Tanakas are a three-generation Japanese family who live in the same house.

**Hiro Sato**

- 88 years old
- Married to Mari
- Father of Kaya
- Grandfather of Jo and Sara
- History of hypertension
- Wears full dentures

**Mari Sato**

- 82 years old
- Married to Hiro
- Mother of Kaya
- Grandmother of Jo and Sara
- Has moderate dementia

**Yoshio Tanaka**

- 34 years old
- Married to Kaya
- Father of Jo and Sara
- Healthy

**Kaya Tanaka**

- 33 years old
- Married to Yoshio
- Mother of Jo and Sara
- 9 weeks pregnant

**Sara Tanaka**

- 11 years old
- Daughter of Yoshio and Kaya
- Sibling of Jo
- Considers herself a "picky eater"

**Jo Tanaka**

- 8 years old
- Son of Yoshio and Kaya
- Sibling of Sara
- Does not like milk or milk products

## Section 5: Explore: Analyze Cues

Hiro does most of the cooking in the household. However, he is struggling to meet everyone's dietary needs and maintain their favorite cultural dishes. Here is what the home care nurse discovered:

- **Hiro** (grandfather) needs low sodium foods due to his hypertension, but he is not compliant with limiting sodium.
- **Mari** (grandmother) needs supervision and some prompting with meals due to her moderate dementia. Her foods cannot be complex.
- **Kaya**'s (mother) favorite foods are sushi, poke salad, and sashimi, but they contain raw fish and are contraindicated during pregnancy.
- **Jo** (son) doesn't drink or eat dairy products, but he needs his calcium intake.
- **Sara** (daughter) likes fruits but not vegetables, unless it is carrots.

The home care nurse confirms that everyone, except for Yoshio (father), has or is at risk for nutritional problems.

## Section 7: Explore: Prioritize Hypotheses/Generate Solutions

The nurse has decided to make the diagnoses—"Ineffective Therapeutic Regimen Management" for Hiro and "Ineffective Health Maintenance" for Kaya—as priority diagnoses since the two clients' conditions are current problems.

The next step, the nurse establishes the desired outcomes and identifies corresponding interventions to achieve the client goals.

**Diagnosis for Hiro: Ineffective Therapeutic Regimen Management**

| Desired Outcomes | Interventions |
| --- | --- |
| - Hiro will comply with a low sodium diet.<br>- Hiro will state the benefits of a low sodium diet. | - Teach Hiro about the advantages of adhering to a low sodium diet.<br>- Adjust diet to coordinate with Hiro's lifestyle and culture. |

**Diagnosis for Kaya: Ineffective Health Maintenance**

| Desired Outcomes | Interventions |
| --- | --- |
| - Kaya will avoid foods and beverages that may harm her or the fetus. | - Educate Kaya about foods to avoid during pregnancy. Include the benefits and possible risks if not compliant.<br>- Adjust diet to coordinate with Kaya's lifestyle and culture. |

## Section 9: Explore: Take Actions

After the client education, the Tanakas' family have adjusted how and what they eat.

- Hiro starts the Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet, which focuses on fish, poultry, whole grains, fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy products, and red meats, sweets, and fats are allowed in small amounts (National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute, n.d.).
- The family assists Mari with meals. They serve her only 1 to 2 foods at a time. They are being flexible with food preferences, and allowing her more time to eat with the family (Alzheimer's Association, n.d.).
- Kaya talked with the nurse and discussed food and beverages she should avoid. She read all the client handouts.
- Kaya and Yoshio buy Jo calcium-fortified foods, such as orange juice and bread, to increase his calcium intake.
- Kaya and Yoshio try to serve fruit at every meal, so Sara gets her vitamins. They are mixing vegetables in small amounts with foods that Sara does like. It disguises the flavors and Sara doesn't mind it.

**Take Action (clinical judgment transcript):** Can students identify and perform the appropriate clinical action? — Potential steps vs. appropriate steps; is potential action indicated, nonessential, or contraindicated. Actions may be: request an order, administer a treatment or medication, perform (skill), document, and/or communicate.

## Section 11: Explore: Evaluation

The home care nurse completes a follow-up visit 6 months later. The Tanakas family is doing very well.

- Hiro's (grandfather) sodium intake has decreased to the point that the dosage of his blood pressure medication was reduced. He had his dentures refitted and uses them when eating.
- Mari (grandmother) has an adequate intake. Her dementia has not worsened, and she is able to stay at home living with her family.
- Kaya (mother) is at the end of her pregnancy and is due next week. She and the baby are healthy. She gained 4 pounds during the first trimester, then gained about a pound per week for a total of 33 pounds, which is a normal weight gain from her pre-pregnancy weight.
- Sara (daughter) has expanded her choices for vegetables. She will now eat spinach, tomatoes, and cucumbers.
- Jo (son) has increased his calcium intake. Kaya and Yoshio are giving him vitamin D as well to assist with absorption.

The nurse has adjusted the diagnoses. Hiro and Kaya's problem diagnoses are resolved. Mari, Jo, and Sara are no longer at risk. The nurse will continue to follow up with the family.
