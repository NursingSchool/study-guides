Communicable Diseases and Immunizations

Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Communicable Diseases and Immunizations

This lesson provides an overview of communicable diseases and childhood immunizations. Communicable diseases are diseases that are transmitted from one person to another directly or indirectly. Many communicable diseases can be eradicated or lessened with immunizations. The nurse plays a critical role in health promotion and advocacy by educating about the importance of immunizations and following the recommended vaccination schedule.

"Vaccination" and "immunization" are terms that are used interchangeably.

Section 3: Explore: Communicable Diseases

The prevalence of most communicable diseases in children has decreased as immunizations have been developed and become part of routine pediatric care. The use of antibiotics has further reduced the incidence of complications from such infections. Some common childhood communicable diseases for which immunizations are available include chickenpox, measles, mumps, rubella, and whooping cough.

Communicable diseases have common patterns:

  • Incubation period: the initial stage after exposure before symptoms are apparent
  • Prodromal period: when the symptoms first become apparent; symptoms may be vague and not specified to the pathogen
  • Acute illness: as the pathogen replicates, more severe and specific symptoms are present
  • Decline stage: symptoms gradually improve
  • Convalescence: symptoms resolve

Section 4: Explore: Infection Control

Hospital-acquired infections are preventable if health care personnel practice meticulous handwashing and implement precautions to stop the spread of disease. Various precautions are implemented based on the mode of transmission of the disease.

Tab: Standard Precautions
  • Use barrier protection such as gloves, goggles, gowns, and masks.
  • The purpose is to prevent contamination from all body fluids, secretions, and excretions.

Transcript (isolation signage): Standard Precautions

Everyone must:

  • Clean hands when entering and leaving the room
  • Cover mouth and nose with arm or tissue when coughing or sneezing
  • Wear gown, glove, and face protection for any risk of body fluid contact

Care providers and staff must:

  • Wear appropriate mask, eye cover, gown, and gloves if contact with body fluids likely
Tab: Respiratory Hygiene
  • Cover mouth and turn head away when coughing and sneezing.
  • Offer surgical mask for persons who are coughing.
  • Use tissues to contain respiratory secretions.
  • Keep distance from others when coughing.
Tab: Airborne Precautions
  • For illnesses transmitted by airborne particles.

Transcript (isolation signage): Airborne Contact Precautions (in addition to standard precautions)

Restricted visitation, Enter only if immune

Everyone must:

  • Clean hands when entering and leaving the room

Care providers and staff must:

  • Wear gown and gloves before entering the room
  • Wear an N95 mask

Patient placement:

  • Airborne infection isolation room required (negative air pressure)
  • Keep door closed
Tab: Droplet Precautions
  • For illnesses transmitted by large particle droplets.

Transcript (isolation signage): Droplet Precautions (in addition to standard precautions)

Everyone must:

  • Clean hands when entering and leaving room

And:

  • Wear mask

Care providers and staff must:

  • Wear eye protection with respiratory symptoms and standard precautions when contact with secretions likely
Tab: Contact Precautions
  • For illnesses transmitted by direct contact or contact with items.

Transcript (isolation signage): Contact Precautions (in addition to standard precautions)

Everyone must:

  • Clean hands when entering and leaving room
  • Put on gown and gloves at door

Care providers and staff must:

  • Use patient-dedicated or disposable equipment
  • Clean and disinfect shared equipment

More information: CDC — Infection Control Basics

Section 5: Explore: Chickenpox

Chickenpox (varicella) is a highly contagious viral respiratory disease transmitted through the air, direct contact, and through contaminated objects. Contracting chickenpox during pregnancy can possibly cause birth defects or a life-threatening illness.

Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:

  • 24 hours of low-grade fever
  • Malaise
  • Anorexia

Acute phase:

  • Pruritic rash to face, trunk, extremities
  • Rash appears as papules (raised red/pink bumps), then forms into vesicles (small fluid-filled blisters), then the blisters break and leak and form crusts and scabs
  • Typically lasts about a week

(figure: chickenpox rash on forehead, Herrmann 1966)

Tab: Therapeutic Management
  • Antipruritic or antihistamine medications for itching
  • Acetaminophen for discomfort or fever
Tab: Nursing Management
  • For hospitalization, implement airborne contact precautions until lesions are crusted.
  • Educate parents about home care:
    • Isolate ill child from children at high risk and unvaccinated individuals until lesions are crusted.
    • Encourage skin care:
    • Apply pressure on itchy areas rather than scratching
    • Smooth and shorten nails
    • Place mittens on young children
    • Bathe daily without soap
    • Dress in lightweight, loose clothing to keep cool.
    • Encourage child to drink fluids.

Avoid administering aspirin (due to the risk of Reye's syndrome). (NIH NINDS — Reye's Syndrome)

Transcript (summary card):

Chickenpox (varicella)

  • Type: viral
  • Transmission Source: respiratory secretions
  • Isolation Precautions: airborne contact
  • Complications: secondary bacterial skin infections, encephalitis, pneumonia

Reference: Herrmann, K. L. (1966). [Chickenpox on forehead; Photograph]. CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL). https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=21510

Section 6: Explore: Measles

Measles (rubeola) is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by a virus. It is spread through the air and by direct contact of the droplets of infected persons.

Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:

  • Fever
  • Malaise
  • Cough
  • Conjunctivitis
  • Koplik's spots (pinpoint red spot with white specks in buccal cavity) lasting 3-4 days

Acute phase:

  • Irregular macular erythema rash appears beginning on face and behind ears and spreading to feet (lasts up to 7 days)
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Anorexia
  • Lymphadenopathy

(figure: measles rash, Partin 1976)

Tab: Therapeutic Management
  • Analgesics for pain
  • Vitamin A
Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:

  • Maintain isolation until fifth day of rash
  • Encourage rest
  • Administer antipyretics for fever
  • Dim lights for photophobia
  • Clean eyelids with warm saline if secretions are present
  • Use cool mist vaporizer

Transcript (summary card):

Measles (rubeola)

  • Type: viral
  • Transmission Source: respiratory secretions
  • Isolation Precautions: airborne and contact
  • Complications: otitis media, pneumonia, laryngotracheitis, encephalitis (rare)

Reference: Partin, B. G. (1976). [Measles; Photograph]. CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL). https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=17639

Section 7: Explore: Mumps

Mumps (parotitis) is a viral infection that can cause swelling in the salivary glands. It is spread through the air and by direct contact.

Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:

  • Fever
  • Malaise
  • Headache
  • Anorexia for 24 hours

Acute phase:

  • Unilateral or bilateral edema and tenderness of parotid gland (by day 3)
  • Earache that is aggravated by chewing

(figure: mumps parotid swelling, CDC 1975)

Tab: Therapeutic Management
  • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs to relieve pain, decrease inflammation, and reduce fever
  • Analgesics for pain
Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:

  • Apply warm or cool compress for edema
  • Encourage rest
  • Administer analgesics for pain
  • Encourage fluids and soft, bland foods

Transcript (summary card):

Mumps (parotitis)

  • Type: viral
  • Transmission Source: saliva
  • Isolation Precautions: droplet and contact
  • Complications: sensorineural deafness, encephalitis, myocarditis, arthritis, hepatitis, orchitis, oophoritis, pancreatitis, meningitis, sterility (rare)

Reference: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (1975). [Mumps; Photograph]. CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL). https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=14488

Section 8: Explore: German Measles

German measles (rubella) is a viral disease spread through direct contact with nasopharyngeal secretions of infected persons.

Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:

  • Young children have no symptoms besides rash
  • Adolescents have lymphadenopathy for 1 to 5 days
  • Low-grade fever
  • Headache
  • Sore throat
  • Anorexia
  • Clear nasal discharge
  • Cough

Acute phase:

  • Within 3 days a pinkish red maculopapular rash begins on face and spreads to trunk, then to extremities

(figure: German measles rash, CDC 1966)

Tab: Therapeutic Management
  • Analgesics for pain
  • Antipyretics for fever
Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:

  • Medicate for fever and comfort
  • Avoid exposure to pregnant women

Transcript (summary card):

German Measles (rubella)

  • Type: viral
  • Transmission Source: nasopharyngeal secretions
  • Isolation Precautions: droplet
  • Complications: miscarriage or birth defects, arthritis (rare), encephalitis (rare)

Reference: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (1966). [German measles; Photograph]. CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL). https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=22145

Section 9: Explore: Whooping Cough

Whooping cough (pertussis) is a bacterial respiratory disease. It is spread through the air.

Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:

  • 1-2 weeks of upper respiratory tract infection signs and symptoms
  • Cough
  • Sneezing
  • Little or no fever
  • Headache
  • Anorexia

Acute phase:

  • 4-6 weeks of dry, hacking cough followed by prolonged inspiration ("whoop" sound most often at night)
  • Vomiting of thick, stringy mucus

Convalescent stage:

  • 1-2 weeks of decrease in coughing and whooping

Link: Listen to a child with whooping cough

Tab: Therapeutic Management
  • Antibiotic therapy
Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:

  • Promote rest
  • Encourage fluids
  • Provide cool mist humidifier or humidified oxygen
  • Observe for signs of airway obstruction

Transcript (summary card):

Whooping cough (pertussis)

  • Type: bacterial
  • Transmission source: respiratory secretions
  • Isolation precautions: droplet and standard
  • Complications: pneumonia, atelectasis, otitis media, seizures, hemorrhage, weight loss, dehydration

Section 10: Explore: Nursing Management of Communicable Diseases

The nurse's role with communicable diseases is to help identify the infectious organism, prevent the spread, prevent complications, and promote comfort to the ill child.

Tab: Identify the Infectious Organism
  • History of recent known exposure
  • Evidence of clinical manifestations
  • Immunization history
  • History of having the disease
Tab: Prevent Spread
  • Advocate for immunization as primary prevention.
  • Report the incidence of communicable diseases to the local public health department.
  • Implement measures to prevent the spread of communicable diseases:
    • Follow isolation protocols.
    • Follow strict handwashing.
    • Use cough/respiratory etiquette.
Tab: Prevent Complications
  • Most children recover without difficulty from communicable diseases; however, certain groups of children are at risk for complications.
  • Children with immunodeficiency are at risk for viremia from the varicella-zoster virus; antivirals may be used to treat varicella infections in immunocompromised children.
  • Children with hemolytic disease are at risk for aplastic anemia from fifth disease.
  • An increased incidence of pertussis has prompted a recommendation for a booster vaccine.
Tab: Promote Comfort

Itching:

  • Cool baths
  • Refrigerated lotions (ex. calamine)
  • Lightweight clothes
  • Administer antipruritic medications
  • Keep nails short

Fever:

  • Antipyretic medication
  • Manipulate environment (fans)

Sore throat:

  • Lozenges and analgesics
  • Saline rinses

Section 14: Explore: Communicable Diseases and Vaccinations

Perhaps one of the most controversial topics in pediatrics is the topic of immunizations. Vaccines have single-handedly decreased the pediatric infectious disease rates over the years for many communicable diseases with high mortality and morbidity rates. As with all medications and treatments, vaccinations have side effects, and it is the question of side effects that brings vaccines under scrutiny. However, the reality remains that vaccinations protect children from very specific and dangerous communicable infectious diseases.

For example, the antivaccination movement was deemed responsible for the 2019 measles outbreak in America. In 2019, 1,282 measles cases were confirmed in over 31 states, which is the greatest outbreak of measles since 1992 (Patel et al., 2019). The cause was linked to unvaccinated people contracting and spreading measles.

Reference: Patel, M., Lee, A., Clemmons, N., Redd, S., Poser, S., Blog, D., Zucker, J., Leung, J., Link-Gelles, R., Pham, H., Arciulo, R., Rausch-Phung, E., Bankamp, B., Rota, P., Weinbaum, C., & Gastanduy, P. (2019). National update on measles cases and outbreaks: United States, January 1–October 1, 2019. Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report, 68(40), 893-896. https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/68/wr/mm6840e2.htm?s_cid=mm6840e2_w

Section 15: Explore: Immunization Schedules

In the United States, the immunization schedule recommendations are developed by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices and the American Academy of Pediatrics' Committee on Infectious Diseases. The typical immunization schedule begins during infancy to help provide immunity before children are exposed to potentially life-threatening diseases. Contraindications to receiving a vaccination include:

  • Severe febrile illness (note that minor illness is not a contraindication)
  • Immunocompromised children cannot receive live virus vaccines
  • Known allergic response to a previously administered vaccine or substance in the vaccine

A vaccine reaction may occur a few hours to days after injection. Localized tenderness, redness, swelling at the injection site, low-grade fever, and irritability can be expected side effects.

Section 16: Explore: Nursing Management of Vaccinations

Nurses have a responsibility to promote immunizations as a primary prevention strategy to protect the individual and the public from life-threatening and preventable diseases. Nurses are responsible for educating families about vaccines, recognizing contraindications to immunizations, and administering vaccines.

Tab: Parent Education
  • Provide accurate and user-friendly information including the disease the vaccine prevents and potential side effects such as fever, rash, and soreness at the injection site.
  • Provide the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's Vaccine Information Statement (VIS) as required by law.
  • Be flexible and offer options regarding vaccination schedules (for example, spread out vaccines over different visits so the child is not receiving so many at once).
  • Educate about ways to minimize unwanted effects of the vaccine (for example, topical anesthetic or acetaminophen before vaccine).
  • Document required vaccination administration information:
    • Day, month, year of administration
    • Manufacturer and lot number of vaccine
    • Name and title of person administering vaccine
    • Site and route of administration
    • Informed consent was obtained
  • Avoid judgmental language and respect the parent's wishes.
Tab: Contraindications
  • Administering a live virus to a severely immunocompromised child (live virus vaccines include measles, mumps, rubella [combined vaccine], rotavirus, and varicella)
  • Administering a live virus when recently receiving passive immunity (for example, after immunoglobulin administration or from maternal antibodies)
  • Administering measles, mumps, rubella, and varicella vaccines to a pregnant woman
  • Administering vaccines if the client has an allergy to components of the vaccine
Tab: Administration
  • Ensure proper storage of vaccine to protect potency.
  • Reconstitute per manufacturer's direction.
  • Rotate site of injections when several vaccines are needed at once.
  • Administer injections simultaneously when several are needed.

Croup and Bacterial Epiglottitis

Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care of the Child With Croup Syndromes

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients with croup. Croup is a general term applied to a group of symptoms characterized by hoarseness, a "barking" or "brassy" cough, varying degrees of inspiratory stridor, and varying degrees of respiratory distress from swelling or obstruction of the larynx.

Section 3: Explore: Types of Croup Syndromes

Infants and young children are more prone to acute upper airway infections than older children. Infants and young children have airways that are smaller in diameter than those of older children, which makes it more dangerous when swelling occurs.

Most types of croup, such as acute laryngotracheobronchitis, are benign and can be treated at home. However, acute epiglottitis is a medical emergency. Therefore, it is critical that the type of croup is identified quickly so it can be treated appropriately.

Section 4: Explore: Clinical Manifestations

Acute laryngotracheobronchitis and acute epiglottitis present with different clinical manifestations.

Tab: Acute Laryngotracheobronchitis

Acute laryngotracheobronchitis is the most common type of croup and primarily affects infants and children less than 5 years old. It is caused by viruses such as influenza types A and B, adenovirus, respiratory syncytial virus, and measles.

Clinical manifestations include:

  • Low-grade fever
  • Barky, brassy cough
  • Inspiratory stridor
  • Respiratory distress, which may include retractions, nasal flaring, and tachypnea

Tab: Acute Epiglottitis

Acute epiglottitis is a serious obstructive inflammatory process that can rapidly progress to complete respiratory obstruction, severe respiratory distress, and even sudden death. It typically affects children ages 2 to 5 years old and is often caused by haemophilus influenzae. The haemophilus influenza type B vaccine has dramatically reduced cases of acute epiglottitis in recent years.

Clinical manifestations include:

  • High fever
  • Sore throat
  • Sitting upright and leaning forward ("tripod" position) with mouth open
  • Drooling
  • Irritability
  • Cherry-red, edematous epiglottis

Section 6: Explore: Diagnostic Evaluation

Acute laryngotracheobronchitis and acute epiglottitis can be differentiated by assessing the history of illness. A definitive diagnosis for acute epiglottitis can also be confirmed by a lateral neck x-ray of the soft tissues.

Acute Laryngotracheobronchitis

The child has a history of illness:

  • Progressive onset
  • Usually preceded by an upper respiratory infection and low-grade fever
  • The child awakens in the night with a barking, brassy cough and at times inspiratory stridor.
  • Symptoms are typically worse at night, and agitation and crying tend to exacerbate the symptoms.
Acute Epiglottitis

The child has a history of illness:

  • Rapid onset
  • Often preceded by a sore throat

The child will often awaken in the middle of the night with a high fever and complain of a sore throat and painful swallowing:

  • Desire to sit upright and lean forward ("tripod" position) with mouth open
  • Drooling
  • Irritable and restless
  • Red and inflamed throat with cherry red, edematous epiglottis

Three classic signs of epiglottitis are:

  • Absence of spontaneous cough
  • Presence of drooling
  • Agitation

Section 8: Explore: Therapeutic Management

In the treatment of both acute laryngotracheobronchitis and acute epiglottitis, the primary objective is to maintain the airway. Airway management is based on the severity of respiratory distress.

Tab: Acute Laryngotracheobronchitis
  • Children with mild croup (no stridor at rest) are managed at home.
  • Cool mist constricts edematous blood vessels:
    • In the home: Parents can take the child outside to breathe in cool night air, use a cold-water vaporizer or humidifier, stand in front of the open freezer, or take the child to a cool basement or garage.
    • In the hospital: Cool mist will be administered by face mask or blow by.
  • For moderate to severe cases, nebulized racemic epinephrine is administered as quickly as possible.
  • Corticosteroids may be used to reduce edema early in treatment.
  • Supplemental oxygen is administered as needed. Intubation is implemented if airway obstruction is severe.
  • Fluid intake is encouraged for mild cases. NPO status is implemented for children with severe respiratory distress to prevent aspiration.
Tab: Acute Epiglottitis
  • Intensive observation of respiratory status is needed to ensure action is taken quickly. If the status deteriorates:
    • For less severe distress, mask or blow-by humidified oxygen is administered as needed.
    • For severe respiratory distress, children are intubated.
  • Administer intravenous antibiotics for children with suspected bacterial epiglottitis, followed by oral administration to complete a 7- to 10-day course.
  • Corticosteroids may be used to reduce edema early in treatment.
  • NPO status is implemented to prevent aspiration.
  • Epiglottal swelling usually decreases after 24 hours of antibiotic therapy, and the epiglottis is near normal by the third day.

Section 9: Explore: Nursing Care Management

There are several similarities between nursing care for children hospitalized with acute laryngotracheobronchitis or acute epiglottitis. Similarities include that the nurse should:

  • Continuously monitor respiratory status, including pulse oximetry, so that impending respiratory failure is recognized quickly.
  • Ensure resuscitation and suction equipment are available at the child's bedside.
  • Encourage the child to be held and comforted by parents to conserve energy and prevent exacerbation.

Some nursing actions that are specific to caring for a child with acute epiglottitis include:

  • Administer intravenous antibiotics.
  • Follow droplet isolation precautions for the first 24 hours of antibiotic therapy.

If epiglottitis is suspected, the child should be seen by the healthcare provider immediately. Do not attempt to visualize the epiglottis or take a throat culture, as this may cause complete airway obstruction.

Cystic Fibrosis

Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Cystic Fibrosis

Nursing Care of the Child with Cystic Fibrosis

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients with cystic fibrosis (CF).

Cystic fibrosis is a lethal, genetic condition characterized by mucus-producing gland dysfunction. The disease produces multi-system involvement including the lungs, small intestines, pancreatic ducts, and bile ducts. Advancements in antibiotics, nutrition, and respiratory management have improved the life expectancy of clients with cystic fibrosis in recent decades.

Section 3: Explore: Pathophysiology

Cystic fibrosis is an inherited, autosomal recessive disease caused by a mutation in the cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR) gene, which results in a disruption of the flow of chloride ions and water across cell membranes. In turn, this causes the lungs, pancreas, and other organs to produce thick and sticky mucus which clogs airways and ducts.

In the lungs, retained mucus serves as a medium for bacterial growth of organisms such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa, methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), and Klebsiella pneumoniae, which then causes respiratory infections.

In the pancreas, thick secretions block the ducts which prevent pancreatic enzymes (e.g., trypsin, chymotrypsin, amylase, lipase) from reaching the duodenum, causing impaired digestion and absorption of nutrients, particularly fats, proteins, and, to a lesser degree, carbohydrates.

Reference: Cystic Fibrosis Foundation. (December 29, 2014). Living today, adding tomorrow [Video]. Youtube. https://youtu.be/ApOf_MCiHtQ

Section 4: Explore: Clinical Manifestations

The clinical manifestations of cystic fibrosis vary widely and change as the disease progresses. The most common symptoms are respiratory, gastrointestinal, growth failure, and diabetes symptoms.

Tab: Respiratory System
  • Classic clinical manifestations include chronic coughing and wheezing.
  • Mucus impactions cause obstruction, hyperinflation, areas of atelectasis, and infection.
  • Advanced disease clinical manifestations include a barrel shaped chest, clubbing of fingers and toes, and repeated episodes of bronchitis and bronchopneumonia.

Tab: Gastrointestinal System
  • Some infants with cystic fibrosis will have a meconium ileus at birth.
  • Lack of digestive enzymes causes excessively large, bulky stools of undigested food that are frothy and foul smelling (steatorrhea).
  • Poor absorption of nutrients, vitamins, and fat and increased oxygen demands for pulmonary function results in children with cystic fibrosis having difficulty maintaining and gaining weight (failure to thrive).
Tab: Reproductive System
  • Females have delayed puberty and difficulty becoming pregnant and sustaining pregnancy.
  • Males are often sterile.
Tab: Integumentary System
  • A child with cystic fibrosis has sweat with abnormally high sodium and chloride concentrations, so they are at risk for abnormal salt loss, dehydration, and hypochloremic and hyponatremic alkalosis during hyperthermic conditions.
Tab: Endocrine System
  • Pancreatic insufficiency can cause diabetes symptoms of hyperglycemia, polyuria, glycosuria, and weight loss.

Section 5: Explore: Diagnostic Evaluation

Early detection of cystic fibrosis and early intervention are associated with better patient outcomes. Diagnosis is often early in infancy. Diagnostic evaluation of cystic fibrosis includes:

  • Newborn screening test: all 50 states screen newborns for cystic fibrosis
  • Sweat chloride test: sweat is stimulated, collected, and electrolytes are measured
  • Cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR) mutation: detection of this mutation provides a high degree of certainty of the disease, but does not guarantee the child will have cystic fibrosis

Section 6: Explore: Therapeutic Management

Therapeutic management of cystic fibrosis therapy includes minimizing pulmonary complications, ensuring adequate nutrition for growth, encouraging physical activity, and promoting a reasonable quality of life for the child and the family.

Tab: Management of Pulmonary Problems

Goals: prevent and treat pulmonary infection by improving ventilation, removing mucopurulent secretions, and administering antimicrobial agents.

Airway Clearance Therapy:

  • High-frequency chest compression:
    • A mechanical chest device (vest) is worn to assist with mucus breakdown and clearance
    • Nebulization therapies are generally administered during the vest therapy
  • Positive expiratory pressure (PEP):
    • Performed by breathing out with a moderate force through a device such as a PEP valve, flutter, or acapella
  • Chest physical therapy:
    • Percussion by another person to loosen mucus, then mucus is coughed out
  • Huff coughing:
    • A technique to move mucus from the lungs; used in combination with other therapy

Link: Video from the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation (2017) — airway clearance techniques.

  • Bronchodilators: opens bronchi for easier expectoration
  • Nebulized saline: improves airway hydration and increases mucus clearance
  • Physical exercise: any aerobic exercise that the patient enjoys should be encouraged to increase lung vital capacity, remove secretions, increase pulmonary blood flow, and maintain healthy lung tissue for effective ventilation
  • Isolation precautions:
    • Contact precautions are implemented for all clients with cystic fibrosis
    • Client should wear a mask in communal areas of the hospital
    • Individuals with cystic fibrosis should be separated from others with cystic fibrosis to reduce the chance of cross-infection

  1. Breathe in slowly through nose and hold 2-3 seconds. (Moves mucus from small airways to large airways.)
  2. Forcefully "huff" the air out. (Moves mucus from large airways and out of lungs.)
  3. Cough and spit out mucus. (To clear mucus from the airway.)
Tab: Management of Gastrointestinal Problems
  • Pancreatic enzymes are administered right before or with meals and snacks to ensure that digestive enzymes are mixed with food in the duodenum.
Tab: Management of Endocrine Problems
  • Cystic fibrosis related diabetes is a combination of insulin resistance and insulin deficiency.
  • Decreased nutrient absorption, anorexia, and severity of pulmonary illness cause increased risk of glucose management problems.

Section 9: Explore: Nursing Management

Nursing care of the child with cystic fibrosis involves comprehensive assessment and interventions to address all affected systems, whether in an ambulatory or hospital setting. Most patients with cystic fibrosis will require hospitalization for treatment of pulmonary infection, uncontrolled diabetes, or a coexisting medical problem that cannot be treated on an outpatient basis.

Tab: Assessment
  • Assess respiratory status: lung sounds, cough, presence of sputum, evidence of fatigue, and pulse oximetry.
  • Assess response to respiratory treatments.
  • Assess the frequency and nature of stools.
  • Assess for evidence of growth failure: weight loss, muscle wasting, pallor, anorexia, or decreased activity.
  • Monitor for signs of depression, anxiety, and disturbed self-image.
Tab: Medication and Enteral Feeding Administration
  • Administer medications: pancreatic enzymes (pancrelipase); vitamins A, D, E, and K; oral antifungals for Candida infection; antihistamines; anti-inflammatory agents; cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR) modulators; and antibiotics.
  • Administer oxygen as needed.
  • Administer enteral feedings as needed to meet needs for growth; administer feedings during the night to minimize the disruption of daily activities.
Tab: Collaboration in Care
  • Collaborate with respiratory therapy regarding respiratory treatments and respiratory medication administration.
  • Consult a dietitian for assistance in nutrition education and setting nutritional goals.
  • Involve child in planning care and modify care to meet the preferences of the child; it is not uncommon for children with cystic fibrosis to rebel against their treatment regimen and refuse some interventions.
Tab: Family Support
  • Support family in coping with a diagnosis of a chronic, life-threatening disease which will require lifelong and complex treatment.
  • Provide education for intravenous antibiotic administration at home.
  • Refer to reputable resources such as the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation for support and education.

Pediatric Respiratory Alterations

Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Pediatric Respiratory Alterations

Care of the Child with Respiratory Dysfunction

This lesson provides an overview of pediatric respiratory dysfunction. Respiratory dysfunction is of great concern among the pediatric population. Infants and small children have airways that are smaller than older children and adults, which causes respiratory illnesses to impact them differently. In this concept, common abnormal respiratory assessment findings, common respiratory diagnostic tests, nursing care management, respiratory syncytial virus, and asthma will be discussed.

Section 3: Explore: Common Abnormal Respiratory Assessment Findings

The respiratory assessment of pediatric clients includes both close visual observation and auscultation. Below are some abnormal findings that are common with the pediatric population.

  • Nasal flaring: enlargement of the nostrils to reduce resistance and maintain airway patency; sign of respiratory distress
  • Head bobbing: head bobs forward with each inspiration; sign of dyspnea
  • Stridor: high-pitched, noisy respiration; indication of narrowing of the upper airway due to edema, inflammation, or obstruction; associated with epiglottitis, foreign body, and tracheitis
  • Barking cough: caused by swelling of the trachea and bronchi; noise sounds similar to a seal barking; associated with croup syndromes
  • Grunting: sign of pain or respiratory distress; body's attempt to improve efficiency of respirations

Children will also display the abnormal respiratory findings associated with other age groups, including: tachypnea, retractions, wheezing, skin color changes, finger clubbing (chronic respiratory diseases, such as cystic fibrosis), coughing, and sputum.

Section 4: Explore: Additional Clinical Manifestations

In addition to respiratory clinical manifestations, infants and young children often have generalized symptoms associated with respiratory illnesses such as:

  • Fever
  • Anorexia
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Abdominal pain
  • Nasal blockage
  • Nasal discharge
  • Sore throat

Section 5: Explore: Common Respiratory Diagnostic Tests

(figure: peak flow meter used to evaluate pulmonary function)

Link: American Lung Association's (2020) video on how to use a peak flow meter

Several types of procedures and monitoring are available to assess respiratory function and to help diagnose respiratory disease.

  • Pulmonary function tests:
    • Noninvasive test using spirometry
    • Useful to evaluate severity of disease and effectiveness of treatment
  • Peak flow meter
  • X-rays
  • Blood gas
  • Pulse oximetry:
    • For infants, sensor placed on the toe or around foot
  • Transcutaneous monitoring:
    • Continuous monitoring of oxygen in blood
  • End-tidal carbon dioxide monitoring:
    • Exhaled carbon dioxide

Section 6: Explore: Factors Impacting Illness

Most acute illnesses in children involve the respiratory system. Differences in age, size, and susceptibility impact how respiratory illnesses present in infants and smaller children versus older children and adults.

Tab: Age
  • Infants and young children between 6 months and 3 years react more severely to acute respiratory tract infection and experience more generalized signs and symptoms than older children.
  • Maternal antibodies often protect infants younger than 3 months against respiratory infections.
  • Infants between 3 and 6 months are developing their own antibodies.
  • Some illnesses such as respiratory syncytial virus present as severe in infants and mild in older children.
Tab: Size
  • Infants and younger children have smaller airway diameters and shorter distances between structures in the respiratory tract can make illnesses that cause edema and increased secretions more severe than in older children.
  • Small nasal passages of infants are easily blocked by mucosal swelling and exudation, which can interfere with feeding.
  • Respiratory illnesses may contribute to the development of otitis media and sinusitis.
Tab: Susceptibility
  • Conditions that predispose a child to infection include allergies (e.g., allergic rhinitis), bronchopulmonary dysplasia, asthma, history of respiratory syncytial virus infection, cardiac anomalies, and cystic fibrosis.
  • Day care attendance and exposure to secondhand smoke also increase the likelihood of infection.

Section 9: Explore: Nursing Management

The nurse can provide much education to support families in caring for their children with respiratory infections at home.

Tab: Ease Respiratory Efforts
  • Warm or cool mist helps soothe inflamed membranes and is helpful for hoarseness or laryngeal involvement.
  • Instill saline drops.
  • Clear nasal passages using a nasal aspirator.
  • Topical vapor rubs can be used for children older than 2 years.
Tab: Promote Rest and Comfort
  • Most children with febrile illness will naturally limit activity when feverish and resume activity when fever subsides.
  • Antipyretics such as ibuprofen or acetaminophen should be used per the label instructions.
  • Caution parents about using over-the-counter combination "cold" remedies that include acetaminophen, which present a risk for overdose if acetaminophen is given in addition to the cold medication.
Tab: Prevent Spread of Infection
  • Promote handwashing.
  • Respiratory hygiene/cough etiquette includes:
    • Cover mouth and turn head away when coughing and sneezing.
    • Offer surgical mask for persons who are coughing.
    • Use tissues to contain respiratory secretions.
    • Keep distance from others when coughing.
  • Separate ill child from others.
Tab: Promote Hydration
  • Children are at risk for dehydration when they are febrile, anorexic, or have vomiting or diarrhea.
  • Because young infants breathe through their noses, a stuffy nose can interfere with feeding.
  • Encourage small amounts of fluid at frequent intervals for small children.
  • Observe frequency of wet diapers in a 24-hour period for infants; 6-7 in a 24-hour period is normal.
Tab: Observe for Deterioration

Observe for signs of worsening respiratory distress:

  • Increasing respiratory rate
  • Increasing work of breathing (chest retractions, nasal flaring, grunting)
  • Increasing heart rate
  • Worsening hypoxia
  • Poor perfusion
  • Reduced level of consciousness
  • Lethargy

Section 13: Explore: Respiratory Syncytial Virus

Bronchiolitis is an acute infection with bronchial involvement and is often caused by the respiratory syncytial virus (RSV).

Palivizumab is an injection given once a month throughout RSV season to help prevent infection for children at high risk for severe RSV disease, such as premature infants, infants with bronchopulmonary dysplasia, or those with certain heart defects.

Transcript (summary card): Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)

  • Occurs in the late fall through early spring.
  • In children under five:
    • #1 cause of bronchiolitis and pneumonia in pediatric patients
    • 2.1 million outpatient visits
    • 57,527 hospitalizations
  • Spread through droplets in the air and on surfaces.
  • Survives for several hours on surfaces.
  • Contagious 3-8 days:
    • Up to 4 weeks in infants and immunocompromised
    • Resolves on own 1-2 weeks
  • Symptoms include respiratory distress, retractions, tachypnea, nasal flaring, cough, wheezing, fever, runny nose, decreased appetite.
  • May progress to hypercapnia and hypoxemia.
  • Hospitalizations due to difficulty breathing and dehydration last 1-3 days.
  • Treatment:
    • Increase fluids (oral or IV), supplemental oxygen, bronchodilators.
    • Respiratory isolation with droplet and contact precautions

Section 15: Explore: Asthma

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterized by recurring symptoms, airway obstruction, and bronchial hyperresponsiveness. Asthma is the most common chronic disease of childhood which significantly contributes to childhood school absences and hospitalizations.

Tab: Clinical Manifestations
  • Recurrent episodes of wheezing, shortness of breath, chest tightness, and cough
  • Symptoms may be worse at night or during exercise
Tab: Diagnostic Evaluation
  • Pulmonary function tests
  • Peak expiratory flow rate

Tab: Therapeutic Management
  • Treatment plans include a stepwise approach to balance medications with symptoms and are based on the severity of disease: intermittent, mild persistent, moderate persistent, and severe persistent.
  • Prevention of exacerbations includes avoiding triggers, avoiding allergens, and adherence to prescribed treatment regimen.

Transcript (peak flow zones):

  • Green Zone: 80-100% of normal peak flow indicates good control of symptoms. Continue prescribed treatment plan.
  • Yellow Zone: 50-80% of normal peak flow indicates caution and worsening symptoms. Follow the prescribed yellow zone plan.
  • Red Zone: Less than 50% of normal peak flow indicates a medical emergency. Contact healthcare provider and follow prescribed red zone plan.
Tab: Nursing Care Management
  • Monitor respiratory status and response to treatment.
  • Provide education regarding triggers, medication administration, symptom monitoring, and developing asthma action plans.
