Child Abuse

Section 1: Adverse Childhood Experiences

Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) are potentially traumatic events that occur in childhood (0-17 years). ACEs are serious health problems that can have a long-term impact on health, safety, opportunity, and well-being.

ACEs include intentional physical abuse or neglect, emotional abuse or neglect, and/or sexual abuse against a child under the age of 18 by a parent, caregiver, or another person in a custodial role (i.e., religious leader, coach, teacher) that results in the harm, potential for harm, or threat of harm to a child.

By completing these learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Differentiate between the different types of child maltreatment.
  • Identify risk factors and key clinical manifestations related to child maltreatment.
  • Develop and implement a plan of care for children who are being abused.
  • Identify mandatory reporting requirements for health care providers suspecting child abuse.

Section 3: Explore: Child Maltreatment

The maltreatment of infants and children can be attributed to a variety of predisposing factors including characteristics of the parent, child, and environment. Child abuse can occur in any ethnic, racial, or religious group, regardless of economic or educational background.

A child can become a victim of maltreatment in one or more of the following categories.

Physical: The deliberate infliction of physical pain or harm to a child. Examples: Fractures; Shaken baby syndrome, abusive head trauma, or inflicted head injury (caregiver's frustrations with persistent crying can lead to this); Factitious disorder imposed on another or medical child abuse (also known as Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy)

Sexual: Occurs when any sexual contact takes place without consent regardless if the victim can give consent. Also, the employment, use, persuasion, or inducement of a child to engage in any sexually explicit conduct. Examples: Any sexual behavior toward a minor (rape, incest, molestation, exhibitionism, child pornography, child prostitution, pedophilia); Dating violence among adolescents

Emotional: The deliberate attempt to destroy or significantly impair a child's self-esteem or competence. Examples: Humiliation, Threatening behavior or words, Intimidation, Rejection, Isolation, Terrorizing, ignoring, or corrupting a child, Verbal assault, Over pressuring the child

Neglect: Failure to provide the following:

  • Physical care: The deprivation of food, clothing, shelter, supervision, medical and dental care, safety, education
  • Emotional care: Failing to meet the child's needs for affection, attention, or emotional growth and/or stimulation for normal growth and development

Section 5: Explore: Risk Factors of Child Abuse

Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) of infants and children can be attributed to a variety of predisposing factors. Let's explore these further.

Caregiver Characteristics: young age, social isolation, having a partner who is unrelated to the child, low income or socioeconomic status, low self-esteem, lack of education, substance use, limited or no parenting knowledge, history of abuse or having been abused themselves, no support system

Child Characteristics: children 1 year old or younger at greater risk due to increased caregiving demands and increase attention; infants and children who are unwanted, hyperactive, or have physical or mental disabilities are at risk due to increased caregiving needs; premature infants are at risk due to potential lack of parent-child bonding at birth

Environmental Characteristics: unemployment, inadequate housing, crowded living conditions, substitute caregivers, divorce, alcohol or substance use, poverty, chronic stress

Section 6: Explore: Warning Signs of Abuse

There are several warning signs of child abuse that a nurse must be aware of when completing a history and physical examination.

Warning signs of child abuse:

  • The child has physical evidence of abuse or neglect, including previous injuries.
  • History is incompatible with the pattern or degree of injury (i.e., caregiver report of the injury does not align with the diagnostic test results such as bilateral skull fracture after falling off a piece of furniture).
  • There is a vague explanation of the injury or caregiver reluctant to provide information.
  • Caregiver seeks treatment for a minor, unrelated complaint and significant trauma is found on exam.
  • Other injuries are discovered that are not related to the original client concern.
  • Different caregivers give contradictory information and history.
  • The mechanism of injury is not compatible with the age or developmental level of the child (i.e. 5-month-old turning on hot water).
  • Bruising or injury is present in a nonmobile client.
  • Child's affect is inappropriate given the extent of the injury.
  • There are inappropriate responses from the child or caregivers.
  • Evidence of abusive or neglectful caregiver-child interaction is present.
  • A history of repeated injuries requiring emergency treatment.
  • There is a delay in seeking care.
  • Multiple fractures or bruises at differing stages of healing are present.
  • A caregiver or child makes a statement of possible abuse.
  • Caregiver disappears after bringing in the child for trauma or a child with a suspicious injury is brought in for care by an unrelated adult.

Reference

Marino, B. S., & Fine, K. S. (2013). Blueprints pediatrics (6th ed.). Lippincott Williams & Wilkins.

Section 8: Explore: Clinical Manifestations of Physical Neglect

Physical neglect is defined as negligent treatment or the maltreatment of a child by a person responsible for the child's welfare, including both acts and/or omission of care. Physical neglect occurs when a caregiver does not provide the basic necessities that a child needs to survive and thrive. This includes basic physical needs such as food, clothing/shelter, and a safe environment in which to live.

Clinical manifestations of physical neglect:

  • Failure to thrive or growth failure
  • Malnutrition (i.e., thin extremities, abdominal distention, lack of subcutaneous fat)
  • Frequent injuries from lack of supervision
  • Delay in seeking care
  • Dull or inactive affect (i.e., excessively passive or sleepy)
  • Poor personal hygiene or lack of hygiene
  • Unclean or inappropriate dress
  • Excessive school absences
  • Self-stimulating behaviors (i.e., finger sucking or rocking)
  • Begging or stealing food
  • Substance abuse
  • Vandalism or shoplifting

Section 9: Explore: Clinical Manifestations of Shaken Baby Syndrome

Shaken baby syndrome is a form of child abuse that causes severe brain damage. Shaken baby syndrome usually occurs when a caregiver severely shakes a child out of frustration or anger, often because the infant won't stop crying. Babies have soft brains and neck muscles and delicate blood vessels. Shaking a baby or very young child can cause their brain to repeatedly hit the inside of their skull, causing significant injury in as little as 5 seconds of shaking. Permanent brain damage can result.

Shaken Baby Syndrome - clinical manifestations:

  • Can cause intracranial hemorrhage
  • Possibly no external manifestations of injury
  • Vomiting, poor feeding, listlessness
  • Respiratory distress
  • Bulging fontanels
  • Retinal hemorrhages
  • Seizures
  • Posturing
  • Alterations in level of consciousness
  • Apnea
  • Bradycardia
  • Blindness
  • Unresponsiveness
  • Bruising in an infant before 6 months of age is suspicious

Section 10: Explore: Clinical Manifestations of Physical Abuse

Physical abuse is one of the most common forms of child abuse. Physical abuse occurs when a caregiver or person close to the child deliberately commits an act that results in a physical injury to the child. These injuries can have serious, long-lasting effects and can sometimes lead to death.

Physical Abuse - clinical manifestations:

  • Bruises, welts in various stages of healing — Regular patterns descriptive of objects used (i.e., belt buckle, hand, wire hanger, wooden spoon, pinch marks)
  • Bruising in a non-mobile child
  • Burns on soles, palms, back, or buttocks — Patterns descriptive of objects used (i.e., round cigar or cigarette burns, marked areas from submersion in scalding water like a gloved hand appearance, rope burns on wrists or ankles, burns in the shape of an iron, radiator, or electric stove burner); Symmetrical burns; Stun gun injury: uniform, circular lesions evenly spaced about 5 cm apart
  • Fractures and dislocations of skull, nose, and facial predominate but can be anywhere on the child — Injury suggestive of the type of abuse (i.e., spiral fracture or dislocation from twisting of an extremity or whiplash from shaking the child); Multiple fractures at different stages of healing
  • Lacerations and abrasions on backs of arms, legs, torso, face, and external genitalia — Unusual manifestations such as abdominal swelling, pain, vomiting from punching; Descriptive marks (i.e., human bites or pulling out hair)
  • Chemical — Unexplained repeated poisoning especially drug overdose; Unexplained sudden illness (i.e., hypoglycemia from insulin administration)
  • Withdrawal
  • Aggression
  • Fear of caregivers
  • Lack of emotional response/reaction
  • Superficial relationships

Section 11: Explore: Clinical Manifestations of Emotional Neglect and Abuse

Emotional abuse (also known as psychological abuse) is a pattern of behavior that damages a child's sense of self-worth and negatively impacts their emotional development. In addition to withholding love and support, the person emotionally abusing the child may also reject, criticize, threaten, demean, or berate the child. They also may humiliate the child, engage in name-calling, and insult them.

Emotional abuse can occur in conjunction with physical abuse, sexual abuse, or neglect and is one of the hardest forms of abuse to recognize as it is often very subtle actions by the abuser. The abuser manipulates and controls the child by using words and actions that are emotionally hurtful and damaging.

Emotional Abuse - clinical manifestations:

  • Failure to thrive or growth failure
  • Enuresis
  • Eating or feeding disorder
  • Sleep disturbances or disorder
  • Withdrawal from environment and people
  • Unusual fearfulness
  • Self-stimulating behaviors (i.e., biting, sucking, rocking)
  • Antisocial behaviors (i.e., destructiveness, stealing, cruelty to animals or people)
  • Extremes of behavior (i.e., over compliant and passive or aggressive and demanding)
  • Lack of social smile and stranger anxiety during infancy
  • Developmentally delayed, especially language
  • Suicide attempts
  • Caregiver behaviors include isolating, rejecting, terrorizing, ignoring, verbally assaulting, over pressuring the child

Section 12: Explore: Clinical Manifestations of Sexual Abuse

Child sexual abuse is any interaction between a child (under 18 years old) and an adult (or another child) in which the child is used for the sexual stimulation of the perpetrator or an observer. Sexual abuse can include both physical contact and non-physical contact. Non-physical behaviors can include voyeurism (trying to look at a child's naked body), exhibitionism, or exposing the child to pornography. Laws surrounding the ages of victims, perpetrators, and consent vary by state, but generally, children under 18 years old cannot consent to any form of sexual activity.

Child sexual abuse often takes place within the family by a parent, stepparent, sibling or other relative; or outside the home, for example, by a friend, neighbor, childcare person, teacher, or stranger. When sexual abuse occurs, a child develops many distressing feelings, thoughts and behaviors. Children may be threatened by the abuser and be fearful to tell anyone else, especially if the abuser is someone they know well.

Sexual Abuse - clinical manifestations:

  • Bruises, lacerations, bleeding or irritation of external genitalia, anus, mouth, or throat
  • Sexually transmitted infection
  • Difficulty walking, sitting, or standing
  • Pain on urination or pain, swelling, and itching of genital area
  • Penile discharge
  • Recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  • Presence of sperm
  • Pregnancy in young adolescent
  • Withdrawal behavior, excessive daydreaming
  • Regressive behaviors (i.e., bedwetting, thumb sucking)
  • Profound and rapid personality changes (i.e., extreme depression, hostility, aggression, social withdrawal)
  • Unusual body odor
  • Bloody, torn, or stained underwear
  • Poor relationships with peers
  • Rapidly declining school performance
  • Sudden changes (i.e., anxiety, weight gain/loss, clinging behavior)
  • Sudden onset of phobias or fears (i.e., fears of the dark, men, strangers, or of settings or situations like undue fear of leaving the house or staying at a particular house)
  • Running away from home
  • Substance abuse

Section 13: Explore: Important Note

A thorough history and physical exam along with assessment findings are needed to create an "index of suspicion" of child abuse. A nurse must examine the totality of circumstances in each case and with each client.

No one assessment finding can automatically conclude child abuse. For example, urinary tract infections (UTIs) may be attributed to poor hygiene or urinary reflux and do not necessarily indicate child abuse with UTIs being the only symptom.

Nurses should remain objective but carry an index of suspicion for suspected child abuse when common assessment findings (i.e., warning signs) of abuse are objectively found.

Section 15: Explore: Nursing Implications and Actions

Nursing care management of a child who has experienced any form of maltreatment includes:

Tab: Protecting the Child
  • The priority is to remove the child from the abusive situation to prevent further injury. Assess safety and reduce danger for the victim.
  • Identify abuse as soon as possible by conducting a thorough history and physical exam.
    • Interview caregivers separately.
    • Use age and developmentally appropriate language that the child can understand.
    • Use open-ended questions that require a descriptive response. Open-ended questions are less threatening and elicit more relevant information.
    • Assess for unusual bruising on the abdomen, back, and buttocks.
    • Document thoroughly the size, shape, and color. Use diagrams to represent the location.
    • Assess the mechanism of injury, which might not be congruent with the physical appearance of the injury. Many bruises at different stages of healing can indicate ongoing beatings.
    • Observe for bruises or welts in the shape of familiar objects like belt buckles, cords, or thin tree branches.
    • Observe for burns that appear glove-like on hands or feet, which can indicate forced immersion into scalding water. Small, round burns can be caused by cigarettes or cigars. Document detailed descriptions of all findings.
    • Note fractures that have unusual features (forearm spiral fracture) which can be caused by forceful twisting of the extremity. The presence of multiple fractures is suspicious.
    • Assess for head injuries by checking the level of consciousness, noting equal and reactive pupils. Monitor for nausea and vomiting.
  • Clearly and objectively document all information obtained in the interview with caregivers and during the physical assessment. If possible, photograph and detail all injuries.

All 50 states have mandatory reporting requirements for healthcare providers. Reporting includes maltreatment and suspected cases of child abuse. There are civil and criminal penalties for not reporting. Check your state's reporting requirements and have the emergency number readily available.

Tab: Supporting the Child
  • Treat the child the same as any other hospitalized child and not as a victim. Facilitate caring for their physical needs, developmental tasks, and play interests.
  • Provide support and consistent care for the child.
  • Use age and developmentally appropriate language that the child can understand.
  • Promote self-esteem.
  • Assist with alleviating feelings of shame and guilt.
  • Avoid asking probing questions of the child.
Tab: Supporting the Family
  • Be direct, honest, and professional.
  • Be understanding and supportive.
  • Model appropriate behaviors for child-rearing, helping the caregivers relate positively to their child and fostering a therapeutic environment.
  • Circumstances surrounding abuse and neglect are case-sensitive, and referrals to protective agencies and services are made to keep the child safe. If a referral is made, explain the process of child protective services to the caregiver.

Section 17: Explore: Preventing Adverse Childhood Experiences

Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) are preventable. Creating safe, stable, and nurturing relationships and environments for all children and families can prevent ACEs and help children reach their full potential.

According to the CDC (2026), some strategies for preventing ACEs include:

  • Conduct early childhood home visits based on identified risk factors (young mothers, unmarried, low socioeconomic status).
  • Strengthen economic support for families by strengthening household financial security and family-friendly work policies.
  • Promote social norms that protect against violence and adversity through public education campaigns and advocacy efforts.
  • Teach skills of social-emotional learning, safe dating and healthy relationship skills programs, and parenting skills and family relationship approaches.
  • Connect youth to caring adults and activities through mentoring programs and after-school programs.
  • Intervene to lessen immediate and long-term harms via enhanced primary care, victim-centered services, treatment to lessen the harms of ACEs, treatment to prevent problem behavior and future involvement in violence, and family-centered treatment for substance misuse disorders.

Reference

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). (2026). About adverse childhood experiences. U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. https://www.cdc.gov/aces/about/?CDC_AAref_Val=https://www.cdc.gov/violenceprevention/aces/fastfact.html

Family Centered Care

Section 1: Introduction to Pediatric Concepts

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients. In this lesson, you will learn about how to incorporate family-centered care into your nursing practice through health promotion and anticipatory guidance.

Section 3: Explore: Family-Centered Nursing Care

Families are complex and dynamic institutions and there is no one-size fits all nursing approach. Each child and family must be cared for using an open, accepting mindset and each pediatric client will have a unique set of living and environmental circumstances to which the nurse must be acceptable.

A family is defined as whatever an individual considers it to be. Families can include many or very few individuals, with varying roles and responsibilities, who have a strong emotional bond and commitment to one another. The term caregiver is used to identify the primary person(s) caring for a pediatric client. This person may or may not always be the biological parent, but one who has been deemed legally responsible for the child's care and wellbeing. Due to the expanding concepts of family, the term household is sometimes used. Positive family relationships are characterized by caregiver-child interactions that demonstrate mutual warmth and respect.

In pediatrics, a family is viewed as a whole unit, instead of individual members. A change to one member affects the entire family unit. Too much or too little change can lead to dysfunction within the family. Families undergo stress which can be expected or unexpected, such as an unexpected illness or an expected, scheduled procedure. Both instances can add stress to the family unit, which can cause individual members to react to the stress in ways they would not normally.

Section 4: Explore: Components of Family-Centered Care

Family-centered care applies to the planning, delivery, and evaluation of health care for children of all ages in any setting and includes the following components:

  • mutually agreed upon partnerships between families, nurses, and health care providers through which the families and children benefit
  • respecting cultural, spiritual, and social diversity and including family-specific views in the plan of care
  • understanding the growth and developmental needs of children and their families or caregivers
  • treating both children and their families as clients
  • working with all types of families, family structures or compositions (i.e., traditional, nuclear, single-parent, blended, extended, foster, binuclear, communal family), and parenting styles (i.e., dictatorial/authoritarian, permissive, democratic/authoritative, or passive)
  • collaborating with families and caregivers regarding hospitalization, home, and community resources
  • allowing families and caregivers to serve as experts regarding their children's health conditions, usual and unusual behaviors in different environments and situations, and routine needs

Section 6: Explore: Anticipatory Guidance

One component of providing family-centered nursing care is anticipatory guidance. Anticipatory guidance is the process of understanding upcoming developmental needs and then teaching caregivers to meet those needs.

Nurses should provide information about what caregivers can expect from children in each developmental stage. As children grow and develop, their basic needs evolve over time. Nurses should be prepared to discuss the following developmentally-appropriate health promotion items with caregivers:

  • physical, cognitive, and psychosocial development, immunizations, health screenings, and age-appropriate activities
  • injury and illness prevention
  • nutrition
  • sleep
  • dental care
  • sexuality

Health promotion through anticipatory guidance is vital in providing services proactively, with the goal of optimizing the child's level of functioning. It ensures the child is growing and developing appropriately and it promotes the best possible health of the child by teaching caregivers and children about upcoming developmental needs and expectations.

Introduction to Pediatric Nursing

Section 1: Introduction to Pediatric Nursing

Pediatric nursing involves providing care for infants, children, and adolescents on a continuum from health to illness. The primary goal in pediatric nursing is to improve the quality of health for children and their families.

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients and how culture, communication, hospitalization, and play all influence the quality of care that is provided.

Section 3: Explore: Health and Wellness - Pediatric Stages

Framing Health and Wellness Across the Continuum

Life span development influences our overall health and well-being. Nurses seek to understand how age, risk-behavior, and wellness contribute to optimal health outcomes.

Click below to review age-related differences, health risks, health promotion concepts, and how the nursing process is used across the life span development continuum.

Infant (1 month to 1 year)
DomainDetails
PhysicalBirth weight doubles by 5 months. Height increases about 2.5 cm monthly during the first 6 months and then about 1.2 cm monthly until 1 year. Vision and hearing develop. Some newborn reflexes disappear (grasping, sucking, etc.). By 4 months, an infant can hold their head up. Learning to sit, stand and walk.
CognitiveDevelop in the sensorimotor period by Piaget. Explore through senses and learn through trial and error. Shake, throw, and put things in their mouth. Important milestone: Object permanence develops between 7-9 months (knowing things still exist even if not seen). Language develops from crying, cooing, and laughing to imitating sounds, comprehending the meaning of simple commands, and repeating words.
PsychosocialExperience Erikson''s period of trust vs. mistrust (learning to trust self and others through caregiver interactions). Begin to separate self from others. By 8 months, differentiates strangers from familiar people and close attachment to caregiver occurs. By 9 months, able to play simple games, such as peekaboo.
Health RisksInjury: motor vehicle, aspiration, suffocation, falls and poisoning. Physical or emotional abuse or neglect. Sexual abuse.
Health PromotionQuality and quantity of nutrition: Breastfeeding is recommended for the first year of life. Iron-fortified commercially prepared infant formula as an alternative. No solid foods until 6 months. After 6 months, cereals, fruits, vegetables and meats can be introduced one new food at a time. Ensure adequate iron and fluoride intake. The total daily sleep time is 15 hours with 1-2 daytime naps. Immunizations: Influenza (age 6 months and older annually); Hepatitis B (HepB): 3 dose series at birth, 1-2 months, and 6 to 18 months; Rotavirus: 2-dose series at 2 and 4 months, or 3-dose series at 2, 4, and 6 months; Diphtheria, tetanus, and acellular pertussis (DTap): 4-dose series at 2, 4, 6, 15 months; Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib): 4-dose series at 2, 4, 6, 12-15 months, or 3-dose series at 2, 4, 12-15 months; Pneumococcal conjugate (PCV13): 4-dose series at 2, 4, 6, 12-15 months; Inactivated poliovirus (IPV): 3-dose series at 2, 4, and 6-15 months.
Nursing ProcessAssessment-opportunities for developing and using senses, developmental delays, parents'' knowledge, abuse, and growth. Intervention-use stimulation strategies to optimize development and use of senses while conserving energy during hospitalizations, limit negative experiences, encourage parents to provide care during hospitalization, limit the number of caregivers, anticipatory parental guidance (nutrition, back to sleep, car seat safety, and immunizations).
Toddler (12 to 36 months)
DomainDetails
PhysicalGrows about 3 inches in height and 4-6 pounds in weight each year. Height at 2 years (24 months) is approximately 50% of their final adult height. Starts to participate in self-care such as eating, dressing, and toileting. Gross motor skills develop from walking to running, jumping, climbing, and riding a tricycle. Fine motor skills develop in drawing, stacking blocks, and feeding self.
CognitiveStart of the Preoperational Stage of Development according to Piaget (covers the age span from 2 to 7 years). Recognize self as a separate being, but unable to consider other''s views (egocentric). Imitate others'' behavior. Language develops to include an ability to speak in two-word sentences and vocabulary up to 300 words by the age of 24 months. Reading to the toddler enhances their language development.
PsychosocialExperience Erikson''s period of Autonomy versus Shame and Doubt. Strive for independence. Explore the power of temper tantrums and negativism. Solitary play continues, but begin to engage in parallel play (playing next to someone instead of with someone).
Health RisksInjury: poisoning, drowning, or motor vehicle accidents.
Health PromotionNutrition: Childhood obesity; limit milk to 2-3 cups/day; grains, fruits, vegetables, dairy and protein; may desire to eat one type of food repeatedly; serve healthy finger foods. Toilet training. Immunizations: Influenza (1 dose annually); Diphtheria, tetanus, & acellular pertussis (DTap): 4th dose at 15 months; Measles, mumps, rubella (MMR): 1st dose (12-15 months); Varicella: 1st dose at 12-15 months; Hepatitis A: 2 dose series 12-24 months; Hepatitis B (HepB): 3rd dose; Inactivated poliovirus: 3rd dose at 18 months.
Nursing ProcessAssessment-safety of the environment. Intervention-Encourage parents to stay with the child and participate in care during hospitalizations, establish trust with the parents, provide anticipatory parental guidance (safety precautions including car seat safety, temper tantrums, negativism, reading, toileting, and eating).
Preschoolers (3-5 years)
DomainDetails
PhysicalGain about 5lb/year. Grow about 2.5 to 3 inches/year. Toilet training is completed for most. By 5 years old, preschoolers are able to skip on one foot, jump rope, skate and swim. Visual acuity approaching 20/20.
CognitivePiaget''s cognitive theory does not include a period specifically for children 3 to 5 years old. The preoperational is divided into two stages: the preconceptual (2 to 4 years), and intuitive thought (4 to 7 years). The main transition is a shift from totally egocentric thought to social awareness and the ability to consider other viewpoints. Thinking is more complex, and children are able to classify objects based on size, color, and by questioning. Aware of cause and effect. More social interaction. Magical thinking: Believe in lifelike qualities in inanimate objects and that thoughts have power. Fear of bodily harm. By age 6, children have a vocabulary of 8,000 to 140,000. "Why" questions are common.
PsychosocialExperience Erikson''s period of Initiative Versus Guilt. Acquiring a sense of initiative is the major developmental task. Develop new skills and explore their environment. Guilt arises when children attempt activities that are beyond their abilities. Allowing preschoolers to do things on their own, yet setting limits and providing guidance assists with this stage of development. Stress may revert preschoolers to a previous stage of development, such as bed wetting, thumb sucking or wanting assistance from parents on activities they usually are independent in, such as feeding, eating, or dressing. Play becomes more social and shifts from the parallel play of a toddler to an associative play of playing together. Pretend play is common and children may have imaginary friends.
Health RisksSame as toddlers. Setting good parental examples is important.
Health PromotionNutrition: Consume about half of adult portions. The finicky eating of a 4-year-old changes to an interest in trying new foods at age 5. Sleep: average 12 hours at night. Regular vision screening, especially the presence of strabismus. Immunizations: Influenza (1 dose annually); Tetanus, diphtheria & acellular pertussis (Ddap): 5th dose (4-6 years); Inactivated poliovirus: 4th dose (4-6 years); Measles, mumps, rubella (MMR): 2nd dose (4-6 years); Varicella: 2nd dose (4-6 years).
Nursing ProcessAssessment-opportunity to practice and learn new skills, the parents'' knowledge. Intervention-Involve the child in care (for example through manipulation of the equipment), encourage expression of feelings, and provide anticipatory parental guidance (injury prevention, physical and developmental milestones).
School Age (approx. 6 years - 12 years)
DomainDetails
PhysicalGain about 4-7 lb/year. Grow about 2 inches/year. Most girls exceed boys in height and weight by the end of the school age years. Large muscle coordination improves and strength doubles. Fine motor skills improve also. Children are able to draw in detail. Body: Modest and sensitive about being exposed.
CognitiveAccording to Piaget, the transition to the concrete-operational period is around 7 years old. Around age 11, transition to the fourth and final stage, formal operations, beginning to understand abstract relationships, formulate hypothesis, and consider different options. Start to demonstrate logical and more concrete thinking. Able to see other''s viewpoints: Are less egocentric and able to understand that others may not share the same thoughts or feelings. Concentration improves. Able to place objects in order according to their size. Are less likely to interpret words literally and able to understand their meaning within a context (understand jokes and riddles).
PsychosocialExperience Erikson''s period of Industry Versus Inferiority. Acquire more skills and competencies. When recognized for success, children develop a sense of worth. When faced with failure, children develop a sense of unworthiness. Develop self-concept and self-esteem. Group play, and group and personal achievements are important. Learn to collaborate and work towards a common goal. Prefer same-sex peers.
Health RisksAccidents and injuries with bicycles and motor vehicles are the most common. Infections, such as the common cold. Poverty and access to health care.
Health PromotionScreening: visual, auditory, and growth; scoliosis. Health education in schools to promote good health practices. Dental care. Safety, such as wearing bicycle helmets. Nutrition: Obesity. Immunizations: Influenza (1 dose annually); Meningococcal: 1st dose (11-12 years); Tetanus, diphtheria & acellular pertussis (Ddap): 11-12 years; Human papillomavirus (HPV): recommended for all adolescents ages 11-12 years (can start at age 9 years) and through age 18 years if not previously adequately vaccinated - age 9 through 14 years at initial vaccination: 2-dose series.
Nursing ProcessAssessment-growth, visual and auditory status, posture for scoliosis, and identify stressors. Intervention-minimize stress and the child''s stress response, provide for privacy for the child and anticipatory parental guidance for the parents; annual health visits.
Adolescence (usually 13-20 years)
DomainDetails

Section 6: Explore: Growth and Development

With five pediatric life span groups (infant, toddler, preschool, school-age, and adolescent), it is often hard to imagine how anyone can learn everything for each age group. However, it is actually easy!

Think About It

Growth and development follow an orderly, yet individual, sequence guided by theoretical frameworks. If you learn the sequence (developmental milestones) and the theories, you will be able to apply that knowledge to the pediatric client you are caring for.

Knowledge of pediatric clients will allow you to adapt your teaching approach to the developmental level of the pediatric client. For instance, consider you are caring for a preschool child in a pediatric medical-surgical unit and you have an order for an intravenous (IV) catheter. Based on the fact that preschool children are egocentric, you would want to explain in simple terms that the IV is not a form of punishment, but something to help them feel better. In addition, knowing a child's developmental level allows you to tailor your nursing interventions to meet their needs.

Section 8: Explore: Health Promotion

When you think about pediatric health promotion, think about the concept of wellness. Pediatric health promotion involves activities that enhance physical, psychological, and emotional wellness. In addition, disease and injury prevention is the main focus. Due to concerted global efforts focused on child health promotion, mortality and morbidity among children have significantly decreased over the years. In fact, the immunization of children against disease has been deemed one of the greatest accomplishments in modern medicine.

Check out the CDC immunization schedule (n.d.). Be sure to figure out which vaccine is given first and when live vaccines are given.

As front-line healthcare providers, nurses play a critical role in advocating for public health and educating caregivers on primary prevention strategies (i.e., immunizations). According to the American Nurses Association (ANA, 2015) policy statement supporting immunizations, "Current evidence and research show that immunizations are essential to the primary prevention of disease from infancy through adulthood" (p. 2). Nurses also have a responsibility to provide accurate information related to the safety and efficiency of vaccines, to include recommended immunization schedules, throughout an individual's healthcare continuum.

While we may not persuade all of our patients to get vaccinated, we can, and we must do our part to spread truth about the importance of protecting ourselves and our children from disease and counteract misinformation. Every patient we vaccinate is a step toward stopping the spread of diseases we thought were left behind. (Cox, 2019, para. 8)

Encourage your patients and families to ask questions and seek clarification of information, address their concerns regarding vaccine safety, and educate them on recommended immunization schedules. A crucial part of pediatric nursing is the use of anticipatory guidance. Anticipatory guidance begins before the child is born and continues throughout the pediatric life span. The aim is to prepare the parents ahead of time to avoid disease or injury.

Section 10: Explore: Cultural Inclusivity

Cultural inclusivity in nursing addresses and supports the needs of people from diverse cultures and values their unique contributions to their care. It acknowledges the ethnic and cultural differences found in people of varied races or religious beliefs. Understanding what preferences are acceptable, preferred, or forbidden can assist the nurse in planning care that is most appropriate for their client's ethnic and cultural background.

Fact: Many cultural preferences and rituals do not impact medical care or pose harm to the client.

The most effective way to determine appropriate care is to ask the client or family about their preferences and taboos. Never make assumptions about client or family preferences, as some may not follow the traditional norms of their culture. Some nursing considerations when planning care for cultural inclusivity are to inquire about communication, physical touch, diet, and ritual preferences.

Fact: Supporting a client's or family's cultural norms can increase comfort and decrease anxiety.

Section 12: Explore: Therapeutic Play

Play serves a vital function in the life of a child. Therapeutic play helps in the development of many types of skills such as intellectual, sensorimotor, social, creativity, self-awareness, and therapeutic awareness.

  • Play allows children to express feelings, emotions, and fears.
  • Play facilitates mastery of development stages.
  • Play assists in the development of problem-solving abilities and coping strategies in a safe environment.
  • Play allows children to learn socially acceptable behaviors.
  • Play can be used to teach children, especially in a hospital, pediatrician's office, or other acute care facility, and assists in gaining cooperation for medical treatments.
  • Play activities should be tailored and appropriate to each child's stage of development.

Section 13: Explore: Therapeutic Play - Nursing Considerations

Utilizing therapeutic play with a pediatric client is one of the most effective ways to engage the child and gain compliance when performing procedures.

Some nursing considerations when using play to engage a child are:

  • Select toys and activities that are age appropriate, safe for the child, and that enhance development.
  • Consider isolation precautions and the child's specific illness in relation to toy selection, as those toys may not be allowed to leave the child's room once they have been introduced.
  • Encourage caregivers to bring one favorite toy from home to help ease anxiety and fears.
  • Allow the child to go to the playroom if possible and it is not contraindicated based on the child's activity level, mobility, and illness.
  • Encourage peers to visit an adolescent client using appropriate precautions when indicated.
  • Involve a child life specialist in planning activities or procedures.
  • Utilize dolls or stuffed animals to demonstrate procedures before they are performed on the client. This is most effective for younger children who exhibit stranger danger or are fearful of health care personnel.
  • Allow the child to manipulate and play with medical equipment.

Section 16: Explore: Illness and Hospitalization

An illness and hospitalization can be traumatic and disruptive for a child and their family. A nurse needs to understand how an illness can impact a family and incorporate interventions to help the child and family cope. The level or degree of impact is directly proportional to the child's level of development.

Tab: Infant

At this age, the child is unable to describe their illness, follow directions, or understand the need for therapeutic procedures. Therefore, stranger and separation anxiety develop, leading to feelings of protest (appears sad, agitated, inconsolable, or watching desperately for caregivers to return), despair (appears sad, hopeless, withdrawn, or acts ambivalent when caregivers return), and detachment (appears happy, interested in the environment, becomes attached to healthcare members, or may ignore caregivers).

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:

  • Preserve the child's trust.
  • Reassure the child that the caregivers will return.
  • Encourage the caregivers to "room in" to facilitate caregiver-child attachment.
  • Maintain normal routines and rituals when possible.
  • Allow choices, when possible, to facilitate feelings of control with the child's caregiver.
Tab: Toddler

At this age, the child has a limited ability to describe illness, a poorly developed sense of body image and boundaries, limited understanding of the need for therapeutic procedures, and limited ability to follow directions. Therefore, they will experience separation anxiety and exhibit a strong reaction to any type of procedure related to the intrusion of boundaries. Because of the toddlers' perception of poorly defined body boundaries, they fear that their body will be mutilated by common medical procedures (e.g. a blood draw). Simply telling them that the bleeding will stop after the procedure will not calm their fears, but applying a band-aid is much more reassuring to them. Toddlers are most likely to demonstrate regression behaviors (i.e., thumb sucking, bottle feeding, or elimination accidents in an already toilet-trained child) that are more appropriate to an earlier stage of development, often to cope with stress or anxiety. Caregivers should be educated on the changes in behavior and punishment should be avoided.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:

  • Preserve the child's trust.
  • Reassure the child that the caregivers will return.
  • Encourage the caregivers to "room in" to facilitate caregiver-child attachment.
  • Maintain normal routines and rituals when possible.
  • Allow choices, when possible, to facilitate feelings of control with the child's caregiver.
Tab: Preschool

At this age, the child has a limited understanding of the cause of their illness but knows what illness "feels" like. They have a limited ability to describe the clinical manifestations of their illness but can understand the cause and effect which can be inhibited by concrete thinking. Fears in this age group are related to magical thinking. Therefore, invasive procedures are interpreted as body mutilation. Illness is perceived as a punishment for their bad behavior. Some degree of separation anxiety exists, but the child may become uncooperative, withdrawn or aggressive, or develop nightmares.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:

  • Encourage caregivers to participate in the child's care and play time.
  • Allow children to express their feelings.
  • Give simple explanations and avoid medical terminology (with pediatric clients and caregivers).
  • Allow time for questions and maintain a trusting relationship with caregivers and the child.
  • Continually praise the child, focus on desired behavior, and give rewards (e.g., stickers).
Tab: School-age

At this age, the child has a beginning awareness of body functioning and can describe pain. They have an increasing ability to understand cause and effect. They have a major fear of losing control, pain, and bodily injury and will seek out information as a way to maintain a sense of control. They can sense when they are not being told the truth. They experience stress when separated from peers and their regular routine is disrupted.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:

  • Communicate openly and honestly.
  • Clarify misconceptions.
  • Encourage participation in care to maintain a sense of control and independence.
  • Facilitate visitation for siblings and peers.
  • Provide explanations using visual aids (e.g., diagrams, models, and body outlines).
  • Praise the child and focus on the desired behaviors.
Tab: Adolescent

At this age, the child mostly understands cause and effect and their perceptions of illness severity are based on the degree of body image changes. Their major fears surround loss of independence, loss of identity, body image disturbance, and rejection by others. Separation from peers is a source of anxiety and they experience feelings of isolation. Physical appearance and peer acceptance have major importance to how adolescents perceive themselves, and they might not adhere to treatments or medication regimens due to body image concerns or peer influence.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:

  • Involve adolescents in the plan of care.
  • Support relationships with family and peers.
  • Provide consistent and factual explanations.
  • Be supportive of emotional expressions and outbursts.
  • Facilitate communication between the adolescent and their caregivers.

Section 5: Explore: Pain Assessment by Age

In pediatrics, most of our clients cannot talk and articulate what they are feeling. So assessment of pain depends on the child's cognitive, emotional, and physical development. Expected clinical manifestations of pain by age include:

Slide 1: Infant
  • Harsh, tense, loud cry
  • Rigid body, thrashing of extremities
  • Local, deliberate withdrawal from painful stimulus
  • Facial grimacing and expressions of pain (eyes tightly closed, mouth open in a square shape, eyebrows drawn together and lowered)
  • No association between stimulus and pain
  • Generalized restlessness, clings to family member
  • Increases in blood pressure and heart rate; decreases in arterial oxygen saturation
Slide 2: Toddler
  • Harsh, tense, loud cry or screaming
  • Verbal expressions of pain or discomfort ("no," "ouch," "it hurts")
  • Facial grimacing, flinching, thrashing of extremities
  • Attempt to push away or avoid stimulus
  • Generalized restlessness, uncooperative, clings to family member, and requests physical comfort
  • Will demonstrate behavior (tensing, pulling away, crying) in anticipation of painful stimulus; the anticipatory phase is more intense than the painful stimulus
Slide 3: Preschool
  • Able to describe the location and intensity of the pain
  • Will deny pain to avoid an injection
  • Restlessness, irritability, crying, kicking
Slide 4: School-age
  • Able to describe pain, concerned with disability and death
  • Girls express pain more than boys
  • Stalling behaviors
  • Overt behaviors - biting, kicking, crying, bargaining
  • Muscular rigidity
  • Less intense anticipatory phase, more intense with the painful stimulus
  • Cues to pain: facial expression, silence, false sense of being "okay"
Slide 5: Adolescent
  • Able to describe pain; understands cause and effect
  • More verbal expression of pain with less protest
  • Increased muscle tension with body control
  • Associates pain with being different from peers
  • May exhibit projected confidence, conceited attitude, withdrawal, rejecting others

Section 9: Explore: Pain Management

Pain in the pediatric population is managed by atraumatic, pharmacological, and non-pharmacological interventions.

Tab: Atraumatic Measures
  • Use a treatment room for painful procedures instead of the child's hospital room or playroom. This will protect the "safe spaces" where the child feels protected and safe (i.e., the child's hospital bed).
  • Use developmentally appropriate language when explaining procedures.
  • Offer choices when appropriate (i.e., pink or blue bandage).
  • Offer trinkets from the toy box (if available) as a reward for cooperating and being brave.
  • Allow caregivers to stay with the child during painful procedures.
  • Use play therapy to explain procedures, demonstrating on a doll, or allow the child to perform the procedure on a doll or toy.
  • Ask a child life specialist to play with the child.
Tab: Pharmacological Measures
  • Select the least traumatic method for administration of pain medications.
  • For mild pain, begin with a non-opioid (i.e., NSAIDs).
  • For moderate or severe pain, administer an opioid (i.e., morphine).
  • Combining non-opioids with opioids treats pain peripherally and centrally, which provides for greater pain management with fewer adverse effects such as nausea, respiratory depression, and constipation.
Tab: Non-pharmacological Measures
  • Distraction: play, television, game on a tablet, movie, music
  • Relaxation: hold or rock the infant, assist with breathing techniques for an older child
  • Guided imagery: facilitate the child in having an imaginary, pleasant experience with the child describing the details
  • Positive self-talk: child says positive things or words of affirmation during a procedure or painful experience
  • Behavioral contracting: rewards (i.e., stickers, tokens), time limits for the child to cooperate, reinforce cooperation with a reward
  • Containment: swaddling an infant, maintaining proper positioning, placing rolled blankets around the child
  • Nonnutritive sucking: pacifier with sucrose before, during, and after painful procedures
  • Complementary and alternative medicine: foods, massage, homeopathy or naturopathy

Section 13: Explore: Death and Dying

A nurse is charged with meeting the physical, psychological, spiritual, and emotional needs of their clients during illness. The same quality of care needs to be extended to clients and their families leading up to and after the time of death. Grief and mourning in children are directly related to their current stage of development and understanding. Children at each age react very differently to death and dying and nurses should be familiar with these reactions.

Hospital visitation by extended family and friends should be determined on a case by case basis and should be at the caregiver's discretion. Caregivers may need the additional outside support that extended family and friends can offer. However, the child is the priority and if too many visitors are causing undue stress to the child, then visitation may need to be limited.

Tab: Infants & Toddlers
  • They lack an understanding of the concept of death.
  • Infants react to the loss of a caregiver by crying, sleeping more, and eating less.
  • They may become aware that someone is missing and may experience separation anxiety.
  • Toddlers have egocentric thinking which prevents their understanding of death.
  • They will mimic parental emotions (i.e., sadness, anger, depression, anxiety).
  • They may regress to an earlier stage of behavior (i.e., cease walking and talking).
  • Toddlers may develop fearfulness and become more attached to the remaining caregiver.
  • They may react to the changes brought about by being hospitalized (i.e., change in routine, immobilization, painful procedures, less independence, separation from family).
Tab: Preschoolers
  • Characterized by egocentric thinking, they interpret death or separation from a caregiver as a punishment for bad behavior.
  • They view death as temporary and reversible because they don't understand the concept of time and the dead person may appear to be "sleeping."
  • They use magical thinking, believing that the dead person will come back or that thoughts can cause an event like harm or death.
  • Death may cause guilt and shame.
  • They may ask a lot of questions and may display a fascination with death.
  • Common behaviors include nightmares, bowel and bladder problems, crying, anger, or out-of-control behaviors.
Tab: School-Age
  • They respond to logical, factual explanations of death.
  • They may believe that death serves as a punishment for wrongdoing.
  • They may deny sadness and attempt to act like an adult.
  • They experience feelings of fear of the disease process, the death process, the unknown, and loss of control.
  • They can be curious about funeral services and what happens to the body after death.
  • Older school-aged children (ages 9-12) begin to have an adult concept of death as being inevitable, irreversible, and universal.
  • Common behaviors include fear (displayed through uncooperative or acting out behaviors), difficulty with concentration in school, or psychosomatic complaints.
Tab: Adolescents
  • Adolescents have an adult-like concept of death.
  • Death may be seen as a personal but distant event.
  • They can have difficulty accepting death because they are starting to develop a sense of identity and dealing with puberty.
  • They have a sense of invincibility, as if no harm can come to them.
  • This group develops a better understanding of illness and death.
  • They rely mostly on peers rather than the influence of caregivers, which can cause the adolescent to feel isolated during a serious illness.
  • They may be unable to relate to peers or communicate with caregivers.
  • They may become more concerned and stressed related to changes in physical appearance due to medication or illness than the prospect of death.
  • They may experience guilt and shame.
  • Common behaviors include feelings of loneliness, sadness, fear, or depression. Acting out behaviors may include risk-taking, delinquency, suicide attempts, or promiscuity.
