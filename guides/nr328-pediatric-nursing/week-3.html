Section 1: Introduction to Cardiovascular Alterations in Children

Cardiovascular Alterations in Children

In this lesson, we will discuss cardiovascular functions and alterations. The field of pediatric cardiology has experienced an evolution over the past half century. There have been advances in diagnostic techniques, procedures, and pediatric anesthesia. Consequently, the pediatric nurse is required to be more sophisticated in assessment, planning, implementation, and evaluation of care when it comes to pediatric cardiovascular pathology.

By completing these learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Describe hemodynamics, risk factors, clinical manifestations, and diagnostic evaluation of children with cardiovascular alterations.
  • Conduct assessment of cardiac function when caring for children with cardiovascular alterations.

Section 3: Explore: Cardiovascular Alterations

Cardiovascular Alterations

Cardiovascular disorders are classified as congenital or acquired.

  • Congenital heart defects (CHD) are those heart anomalies that develop in utero and manifest at birth or shortly after. CHDs arise at birth and are most often anatomical in nature, resulting in abnormal cardiac function that leads to heart failure and hypoxia.
  • Acquired cardiac disorders are those processes or abnormalities that occur after birth and can be found in the normal heart or in the presence of congenital heart defects. Acquired cardiac disorders can result from a variety of factors including familial tendencies, infection, autoimmune responses, and environmental factors.

Anatomic defects of the heart prevent normal blood flow to the pulmonary and/or systemic system. Defects are categorized into four blood flow patterns in the heart:

  • Increased pulmonary blood flow
  • Decreased pulmonary blood flow
  • Obstruction to blood flow
  • Mixed blood flow

These will be covered in more detail, but it is important to understand the normal fetal to newborn circulation at birth.

Section 4: Explore: Altered Circulation - Increased Pulmonary Blood Flow

Altered Circulation: Increased Pulmonary Blood Flow

Some of the most common cardiac defects fall into the category of increased pulmonary blood flow. Each of these defects causes an increase in the flow of blood back to the lungs instead of forward through the body. These defects include:

  • Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)
  • Atrial septal defect (ASD)
  • Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
Interactive Transcript: The Heart

Septal Defects

Normally, the heart's two main pumping chambers, the right and left ventricles, are separated from one another by a muscular wall called the ventricular septum. A ventricular septal defect is an abnormal opening in the ventricular septum, essentially a hole. This opening allows blood to flow directly from one ventricle into the other. Similarly, an abnormal opening in the atrial septum is known as an atrial septal defect.

Patent Ductus Arteriosus

occurs when the lumen of the duct between the aorta and the pulmonary artery remains open (patent) after birth. Remember, this opening is needed in utero for the developing fetus since the lungs are non-functional. Once the infant is born, this opening should close spontaneously. When it fails to close, then this passageway will remain open, disrupting normal blood flow. This disruption of blood flow sounds like a "machinery" murmur which can be heard during auscultation.

Section 5: Explore: Cardiac Defect Outcomes

Cardiac Defect Outcomes

Depending on the severity of the cardiac defect, two clinical outcomes can occur: Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) or hypoxemia.

Defects that result in increased pulmonary blood flow (or in a left-to-right shift shunting of blood) cause symptoms of CHF. Defects that result in decreased pulmonary blood flow cause cyanosis. The conditions can occur alone or together.

Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) is the inability of the heart to pump an adequate amount of blood to systemic circulation to meet the body's metabolic demands.

Because these hemodynamic changes occur from different causes and at different times, the clinical presentation may vary among children.

Section 8: Explore: Altered Circulation - Decreased Pulmonary Blood Flow

Altered Circulation: Decreased Pulmonary Blood Flow

Some of the common cardiac conditions with decreased pulmonary blood flow include Tetralogy of Fallot and tricuspid atresia.

Although there may be multiple disease processes that could occur based on the type of pulmonary obstruction, let's take a moment to consider Tetralogy of Fallot.

Tetralogy of Fallot is a congenital heart defect (CHD) made up of four defects within the heart.

  • There is a hole in the heart's septum between the two ventricles called a ventricular septal defect (VSD). This causes deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to mix with oxygenated blood in the left ventricle.
  • As a result of pulmonary stenosis, deoxygenated blood is restricted from moving from the right ventricle and unable to become oxygenated in the lungs.
  • The aorta is overriding and opens to both the left and right ventricles, rather than just the left ventricle.
  • The wall of the right ventricle is thicker than normal, resulting in ventricular hypertrophy.

For additional information about tricuspid atresia, click here: https://www.lhm.org.uk/tricuspid-atresia/#hlhs

Interactive Transcript: The Heart

Tetralogy of Fallot

is one of the most common congenital heart defects. This condition is characterized by four main features or defects - ventricular septal defect, right ventricular hypertrophy, pulmonary stenosis, and an overriding aorta. In Tetralogy of Fallot, unoxygenated blood passes through the ventricular septal defect to the left ventricle. This causes the outflow from the right ventricle to be obstructed, limiting blood flow to the lungs, which is known as pulmonary stenosis. As a result, the aortic valve becomes enlarged and overrides the ventricular septal defect, causing deoxygenated blood to enter systemic circulation. The right ventricular wall hypertrophies, or thickens, as a result of the obstruction. This combination of abnormalities can vary in severity, but usually result in decreased oxygen in the systemic circulation causing cyanosis.

Tricuspid Atresia

is a failure of the tricuspid valve to develop, which prevents blood from entering the right ventricle from the right atrium. Deoxygenated blood from the right atrium passes through the atrial septal defect, where it mixes with the oxygenated blood in the left atrium. The mixed blood then travels to the left ventricle where it goes to the aorta into systemic circulation or passes to the left ventricle and through a ventricular septal defect to the right ventricle, pulmonary artery, and lungs. As a result, these infants will be cyanotic.

Section 10: Explore: Tetralogy of Fallot

Tetralogy of Fallot

Children experiencing Tetralogy of Fallot may have inadequate blood flow throughout the body. As a result, they may experience a variety of symptoms, such as:

  • Cyanosis
  • Difficulty feeding
  • Restlessness
  • Behavioral changes
  • Seizures

Very often, children with Tetralogy of Fallot will have sudden hypercyanotic episodes, called "tet spells."

There are very specific nursing interventions that accompany these hypercyanotic episodes in order to help the child recover. Nursing interventions include:

  • Place the child in a knee-chest position.
  • Administer 100% oxygen by face mask.
  • Use a calm, comforting approach.
  • Administer morphine subcutaneously or through an existing intravenous (IV) line.
  • Begin intravenous (IV) fluid replacement and volume expansion if needed.

In children who can walk, those diagnosed with Tetralogy of Fallot may assume a squatting position (knee-chest) when experiencing a hypercyanotic spell in order to relieve the discomfort associated with the tet spell.

Section 14: Explore: Altered Circulation - Obstructed Blood Flow

Altered Circulation: Obstructed Blood Flow

Obstructive defects include those defects where blood flow exiting the heart is stenosed or restricted causing an obstruction of blood flow. The blood pressure before the defect is increased and the pressure that occurs after the defect is decreased. This results in decreased cardiac output.

Some of the common cardiac conditions with obstruction to blood flow include:

  • Aortic stenosis
  • Pulmonary stenosis
  • Coarctation of the aorta

These children have clinical manifestations of heart failure.

Interactive Transcript: The Heart

Pulmonary Stenosis

is a narrowing or fusing of the pulmonic valve leaflets at the entrance of the pulmonary artery that obstructs blood flow from the right ventricle to the lungs. Since the right ventricle cannot eject sufficient blood flow to the pulmonary artery to maintain normal blood oxygen levels, this presents an emergent situation. Deoxygenated blood is forced through the foramen ovale, bypassing the right ventricle to the left side of the heart where it is pumped through systemic circulation. As a result, these infants will be cyanotic. Surgery, or less invasive balloon dilation, is needed to restore blood flow to the lungs. A similar issue involving the aortic valve is known as aortic stenosis.

Coarctation of the Aorta

refers to a narrowing of the main blood vessel (the aorta) which carries oxygen-rich blood from the heart to the body. As a result, there is higher blood pressure before the coarctation, which can be detected by differences in blood pressure between the upper extremities and lower extremities. The degree, or severity, of narrowing dictates how soon an intervention is needed. Severe coarctation is often found very early within the first few weeks of life. However, a less severe coarctation may remain undiagnosed for years as there are no clinical manifestations of a deformity.

Section 15: Explore: Altered Circulation - Mixed Blood Flow

Altered Circulation: Mixed Blood Flow

Mixed defects occur when there is a mixing of blood from the pulmonary and systemic circulations within the heart chambers. When fully saturated systemic blood mixes with desaturated pulmonary blood, there is a relative desaturation in systemic blood. These children experience pulmonary congestion and decreased cardiac output. They have clinical manifestations of congestive heart failure (CHF) although cyanosis is not always visible. These children often require multiple surgical interventions beginning in the first week of life.

Some of the common cardiac conditions with mixed defects include:

  • Transposition of the great arteries (or transposition of the great vessels)
  • Truncus arteriosus
  • Hypoplastic left heart
Interactive Transcript: The Heart

Transposition

of the great arteries occurs when the aorta and the pulmonary artery are reversed (or transposed). In transposition, deoxygenated blood returns to the body through the right atrium, enters the right ventricle, and goes to the body via the aorta, bypassing the lungs. Oxygenated blood is pumped back into the lungs via the pulmonary artery without circulating through the body. As a result, these infants will be cyanotic. In transposition of the great arteries, a patent foramen ovale or atrial septal defect needs to be present so that there is a connection between the right and left sides of the heart, allowing oxygenated blood to reach the body. To establish normal blood flow, surgery is required in the first few weeks of life to switch the aorta and pulmonary artery to their correct anatomical position.

Truncus Arteriosus

refers to the failure of the common great vessel to divide into the pulmonary artery and aorta, resulting in one major vessel (or "trunk") arising from the left and right ventricles. A ventricular septal defect allows blood to mix before entering the common trunk. The mixed blood then goes to the body via the aorta and to the lungs via the pulmonary artery. As the infant grows, the blood pressure will drop, with too much blood to go to the lungs, causing congestive heart failure. Surgery is needed to separate the pulmonary artery from the aorta to restore normal blood circulation.

Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome

refers to the underdevelopment of the left side of the heart. The mitral valve is also underdeveloped, either severely narrowed or missing. The abnormal (or absent) mitral valve prevents blood from entering the left ventricle. Blood flow is diverted through the atrial septal defect into the right atrium where it combines with blood from the lungs and flows into the right ventricle. The right ventricle then pumps blood to both the lungs and the body. For this reason, this defect is sometimes referred to as a single ventricle defect.

Section 16: Explore: Congenital Heart Defects Nursing Care

Congenital Heart Defects: Nursing Care

Children with congenital heart defects (CHD) tire easily due to oxygenation problems and poor nutrition. For this reason, priority nursing care focuses on hydration, oxygenation, and pain management.

A great mnemonic for this is HOP (hydrate, oxygenate, pain management).

Hydrate

  • Keep the child well hydrated.
  • Maintain fluid and electrolyte balance.

Oxygenate

Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Kawasaki Disease & Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome

Pediatric Hematologic Alterations: Kawasaki Disease and Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients with Kawasaki disease and multisystem inflammatory syndrome.

Kawasaki disease is an acute systemic vasculitis with an unknown cause. This illness is self-limiting and resolves in 6 to 8 weeks. Without treatment, approximately 20% to 25% of children develop cardiac sequelae.

Multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children (MIS-C) is a serious inflammatory condition of organs and tissues that appears to be linked to coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19).

Section 3: Explore: Kawasaki Disease

Kawasaki Disease

Kawasaki disease (KD) is a multisystem disorder involving vasculitis (inflammation of the inner lining of arteries and veins). It is also referred to as mucocutaneous lymph node syndrome. It is the leading cause of acquired heart disease in children. KD is seen in children of most racial and ethnic backgrounds. The estimated incidence rate is 112 cases per 100,000 children less than 5 years of age.

The cause of KD is unknown but generally affects young children under 5 years old. KD does not spread from person-to-person, but is often seen in geographic and seasonal outbreaks with more cases reported in the late winter and early spring.

Pathophysiology

KD involves widespread inflammation of the small and medium-sized blood vessels with the coronary arteries being the most susceptible to damage. During the acute stage of the illness, there is progressive inflammation of the small vessels (capillaries, venules, arterioles) along with myocarditis. The vasculitis can progress to the medium-sized muscular arteries, potentially damaging the walls of the vessels and leading to coronary artery aneurysms in some children. With treatment, the systemic inflammation gradually subsides within 6 to 8 weeks. Almost all the mortality and morbidity resulting from KD is due to cardiac complications with children who have had giant aneurysms.

Visit the CDC website About Kawasaki Disease.

Kawasaki Disease: Clinical Manifestations

Image Transcript: Clinical Manifestations of Kawasaki Disease
  • irritability
  • fever for 5 days
  • bilateral conjunctivitis, no discharge

  • swollen cervical lymph nodes
  • rash on body

Stages

Children with Kawasaki disease progress through 3 stages of the disease process.

Acute stage - one to two weeks

  • abrupt onset of high fever lasting longer than 5 days that is unresponsive to antipyretics or antibiotics
  • conjunctivitis without exudate (drainage)
  • bright red, chapped lips
  • strawberry tongue with white coating or red bumps on the posterior aspect
  • red, oral mucous membranes with inflammation
  • edema of the hands and feet
  • erythema of the palms and soles
  • exanthem - non blistering rash
  • cervical lymphadenopathy
  • irritability
  • cardiac manifestations: myocarditis, decreased left ventricular function, pericardial effusion, and mitral regurgitation

Subacute stage - two to four weeks

  • fever and other manifestations gradually subside
  • irritability
  • anorexia
  • desquamation of hands and feet
  • temporary arthritis and arthralgia
  • cardiovascular manifestations

Convalescent stage - six to eight weeks

  • clinical symptoms have mostly resolved
  • laboratory values may still be abnormal

Section 6: Explore: Kawasaki Disease: Diagnostics

Kawasaki Disease: Diagnostics

Echocardiogram

  • Monitors myocardial function and coronary artery dimensions.
  • Common (baseline) findings during the acute phase include pericardial effusions, mitral regurgitation, decreased ventricular function.
  • Follow-up echocardiograms (echos) should be performed at 1-2 weeks after diagnosis and at 4-6 weeks after treatment. More frequent echos may be needed depending on the client's response to treatment.

Laboratory Tests

  • Complete blood count (CBC)
    • to assess white blood cells (WBC), red blood cells (RBCs), and platelets for infection
    • results will show elevated WBCs and platelets, and low RBCs, indicating anemia
  • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
    • indirect measurement of the degree of inflammation in the body
    • will be elevated
  • C-reactive protein (CRP)
    • non-specific indicator of inflammation in the body due to acute conditions
    • will be elevated
  • Elevated liver enzymes
  • Decreased albumin levels

Section 7: Explore: Kawasaki Disease: Therapeutic Management

Kawasaki Disease: Therapeutic Management

High dose gamma globulin (IVIG)

  • Reduces duration of fever and incidence of coronary artery abnormalities when administered within the first 7-10 days of the illness.
  • A single, large intravenous (IV) infusion of 2 g/kg is given over 8-12 hours.
  • May be repeated if the child remains febrile after 36 hours of the initial infusion.
  • Nurses should monitor vital signs and assess for an allergic reaction during infusion.

Salicylate (Aspirin)

  • Administered to control fever and inflammation and as an antiplatelet agent.
  • Administer high dose, 80-100 mg/kg/day divided every 6 hours.
  • Once afebrile, dose is reduced to 3-5 mg/kg/day until platelet count returns to normal range which can be approximately 6-8 weeks. Child is weaned over time to decrease the risk of bleeding.
  • If a child develops coronary abnormalities, then salicylate therapy may continue indefinitely.

Section 8: Explore: Kawasaki Disease: Nursing Care

Kawasaki Disease: Nursing Care

Nursing care and considerations are primarily focused on symptomatic relief.

  • Monitor vital signs and cardiac status. Client should remain on continuous cardiac monitoring.
  • Monitor for heart failure during initial stage of the illness and during intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) infusion because of the large fluid load being placed on the heart.
  • Monitor ins and outs (I&Os).
  • Document daily weights.
  • Administer intravenous (IV) fluids to prevent dehydration from high fever and increased metabolic needs.
  • Administer IVIG per protocol.
  • Administer aspirin as prescribed.
  • Passive range of motion (ROM) to extremities to maintain mobility and reduce arthritis.
  • Promote comfort and provide care.
    • Perform oral hygiene. Apply lip balm as needed for mucosal inflammation.
    • Offer clear liquids and soft foods.
    • Perform cool baths or apply cool compresses to skin.
    • Apply emollients to skin as ordered to maintain hydration. Avoid soap as this will dry out the skin and increase skin breakdown.
    • Provide a calm, quiet environment.
    • Cluster care to promote rest.
  • Monitor for complications.
    • Aneurysms
    • Side effects of aspirin therapy (bleeding, gastrointestinal upset)
    • Side effects of IVIG therapy (elevated blood pressure, facial flushing, tightness in chest)

Section 11: Explore: Kawasaki Disease: Family Education

Kawasaki Disease: Family Education

Caring for a child with Kawasaki disease requires a multidisciplinary approach and the family and caregivers are a large part of the health care team. The nurse should provide education that is evidence-based and appropriate for both the child's needs and the general health literacy of the caregivers.

Slide 1

Family should understand disease progression.

Slide 2

Maintain all follow-up appointments for monitoring of symptoms which may persist for months.

Slide 3

Child's irritability can last for up to 2 months.

Slide 4

Skin manifestations are painless, but the skin could be tender. Keep skin clean and avoid harsh soaps.

Slide 5

Perform passive range of motion (ROM) exercises in the bathtub to assist with joint inflammation from arthritis, which can be severe and may interfere with walking. Notify the provider if the child refuses to walk.

Slide 6

Avoid live immunizations (i.e., MMR or varicella vaccines) for 11 months after intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) administration as the body may not produce the appropriate number of antibodies.

Slide 7

Monitor child's temperature daily and prior to giving salicylate (Aspirin). Notify the provider if the child has a fever.

Slide 8

Maintain a heart healthy diet (low-cholesterol).

Slide 9

As the child ages, screening for heart disease is recommended (blood cholesterol testing, blood pressure monitoring, periodic imaging of the heart) to monitor for long-term complications or sequelae.

Slide 10

Monitor for clinical manifestation of aspirin toxicity.

Section 13: Explore: Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children

Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children

Multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children (MIS-C) is a serious condition that appears to be linked to coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19). Most children who become infected with the COVID-19 virus have only a mild illness, but some children go on to develop MIS-C.

In MIS-C, multiple body systems and tissues become inflamed, including the heart, lungs, kidneys, brain, skin, eyes, or gastrointestinal organs. MIS-C is considered a syndrome (a group of signs and symptoms), not a disease, and clinical manifestations depend on which areas of the body are affected.

The etiology and risk factors are still unknown, but it appears to be an excessive immune response related to COVID-19. MIS-C is rare and most children who have it will eventually get better with medical intervention, but it has been known to cause death in some children.

Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children: Manifestations and Management

Multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children (MIS-C) clinical manifestations include some of the following, but not all children have the same symptoms. These clinical manifestations closely resemble Kawasaki disease.

  • persistent fever lasting longer than 24 hours

    • skin rash
    • mucocutaneous lesions

Early warning signs that need emergency care and intervention are:

  • difficulty breathing
  • pain or pressure in the chest
  • new onset confusion
  • decreasing level of consciousness - inability to wake or stay awake
  • pallor and/or cyanosis to skin, lips, or nail beds
  • severe abdominal pain

Treatment is tailored to client-specific clinical manifestations to reduce inflammation in any affected vital organs to protect them from permanent damage, and includes primarily supportive care such as:

  • respiratory support to assist with oxygenation

  • medications
  • anticoagulation medications (i.e., aspirin or heparin) to reduce the risk of blood clots
  • anti-inflammatory medications (i.e., steroids, IVIG, and antibiotics) aimed at reducing inflammatory effects

Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children: Nursing Management

Caring for a child with multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children (MIS-C) requires a multidisciplinary approach and the family and caregivers are a large part of the health care team. The nurse should provide education that is evidence-based and appropriate for both the child's needs and the general health literacy of the caregivers. Nursing care and considerations closely resemble caring for a child with Kawasaki disease.

Monitor for complications

  • Aneurysms
  • Side effects of aspirin therapy (bleeding, GI upset)
  • Aspirin toxicity — headache, confusion, dizziness, or tinnitus. If noted, report immediately to the healthcare provider.
  • Side effects of intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) therapy (elevated blood pressure, facial flushing, tightness in chest)

Nursing care

  • Focused primarily on symptomatic relief.
  • Monitor vital signs and cardiac status.
    • Client should remain on continuous cardiac monitoring.
  • Assess for heart failure.
    • Decreased urine output, gallop heart rhythm, tachycardia, respiratory distress
  • Monitor ins and outs (I&Os).
  • Document daily weights.
  • Administer intravenous (IV) fluids to prevent dehydration from high fever and increased metabolic needs.
  • Administer medications as prescribed.
  • Promote comfort and provide care.
    • Perform oral hygiene. Apply lip balm as needed for mucosal inflammation.
    • Offer clear liquids and soft foods.
    • Perform cool baths or apply cool compresses to skin.
    • Apply skin lotions to maintain hydration.
    • Provide a calm, quiet environment.
    • Cluster care to promote rest.

Pediatric Assessment: Developmental Considerations

Assessing children is different from assessing adults. Before you learn about specific cardiovascular alterations, here are some things to remember when assessing children with cardiac defects.

Physical assessment techniques vary depending on the age of the child. To achieve the most accurate assessment, it is recommended to follow this sequence:

Tab: Infant
  • Assess supine or sitting, preferably in caregiver's lap.
  • Auscultate heart, lungs, abdomen.
    • Record respirations first, and then apical heart rate/pulse.
  • Perform traumatic and invasive procedures last (i.e., blood pressure and rectal temperature).
  • Infants' respirations are primarily diaphragmatic, so count the abdominal movements instead of watching for the chest movement.
  • Address the child by name and explain each step to the caregiver as it is done.
Tab: Toddler
  • Assess supine, sitting, or standing near the caregiver.
  • Inspect body through play (i.e., count fingers, tickle toes) using minimal contact initially.
  • Introduce equipment slowly.
  • Auscultate, percuss, palpate whenever quiet.
  • Perform traumatic procedures last.
Tab: Preschooler
  • Assess supine, sitting, or standing with caregiver in close proximity.
  • If cooperative, proceed in a head-to-toe direction.
  • If uncooperative, follow toddler procedures.
  • Speak to the child using developmentally-appropriate language.
Tab: School-age
  • Assess sitting; presence of caregiver based on age.
  • Proceed in head-to-toe direction, examining genitalia last.
  • Include the child in all parts of the assessment and speak to the caregiver before and after the examination.
  • Speak to the child using developmentally-appropriate language and appeal to his or her desire for self-care.
Tab: Adolescent
  • Assess same as school-age child, offer option of caregiver's presence.
  • Proceed in head-to-toe direction, examining genitalia last.
  • Speak to the adolescent using mature language and appeal to his or her desire for self-care.

Section 10: Explore: Pediatric Physical Assessment

Pediatric Physical Assessment

The physical examination should include the following:

Accordion: Growth Curve

The child's weight and height should be compared to normal values on the growth curve. Children with cardiac alterations tend to be small for their age. Many of these children fail to thrive.

Accordion: Vital Signs

Vital signs should be assessed and may vary with age. Heart rates will decrease as the child ages. Blood pressure differences in extremities can indicate a defect known as coarctation of the aorta.

Accordion: Heart Sounds

Auscultation of heart sounds should be completed in both a sitting and reclining position. S1 and S2 heart sounds should be clear and crisp. S1 is louder at the apex of the heart while S2 is louder near the base of the heart. Sinus arrhythmias that are associated with respirations are common.

Accordion: Circulation

Assessing for cyanosis, capillary refill time, neck veins, edema, and clubbing of fingers should be performed. In a child with cardiovascular alterations, cyanosis will first appear as circumoral, in nailbeds, buccal, and around the eyes. Clubbing will appear only after the child has had long-term cardiac alterations resulting from long-term hypoxia.

Accordion: Pulses

Palpation of pulses should be bilaterally equal. Pulse locations and expected findings in children and adolescents are the same as those in the adult population.

  • The radial pulse is difficult to palpate accurately in children younger than 2 years of age because the blood vessels lie close to the skin surface and are easily obliterated.
  • Infants and young children are often nervous or fearful, causing the heart rate to elevate; therefore, the nurse should listen to the heart a few minutes before counting the pulse.
  • For infants and children, auscultate the apical pulse (4th intercostal space at the left midclavicular line) with the stethoscope for a full minute.
  • In infants, brachial, temporal, and femoral pulses should be palpable, full, localized, and compared bilaterally at the same time while assessing strength and regularity.
  • Pulses that are weaker in the legs and bounding in the arms can suggest coarctation of the aorta.
Accordion: Chest

Inspection and palpation of the chest should include observing for an active precordium and palpating for heaves or thrills.

Accordion: Lungs

Auscultation of lung sounds should be performed to listen for any fluid caused from increased pulmonary flow.

Section 1: Introduction to Pediatric Hematologic Alterations

Pediatric Hematologic Alterations

Just like the heart, blood is part of the circulatory system. In addition, due to the vast number of components in the blood, hematologic alterations can be numerous in a pediatric patient. One of the most common red blood cell disorders in children is anemia. Anemia occurs when the hemoglobin content of the blood is insufficient or inadequate to satisfy the body's needs for oxygen.

There are many types of anemia, including iron deficiency anemia, sickle cell anemia, and aplastic anemia. Although all these anemias may involve a dysfunction of hemoglobin, clinical manifestations may vary.

By completing these learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Describe hemodynamics, risk factors, clinical manifestations, and diagnostic evaluation of children with hematologic alterations
  • Discuss pharmacologic and non-pharmacologic strategies when caring for children with hematologic alterations
  • Determine nursing care for children with hematologic alterations

Section 3: Explore: Anemia

Anemia

Anemia is a reduction in red blood cells (RBCs) mass and/or hemoglobin concentration compared with normal values for age.

Slide 1: Pathophysiology

The pathology of anemia is a decrease in the oxygen-carrying capacity of blood and consequently a reduction in the amount of oxygen available to the cells. In some cases, when anemia develops slowly, the child usually adapts to the declining hemoglobin level and the clinical manifestations may not be as easily recognized.

Slide 2: Clinical Manifestations of Anemia

When the hemoglobin level falls enough to produce clinical manifestations, the signs and symptoms are due to tissue hypoxia. Anemia is caused by decreased levels of hemoglobin or RBCs, not inadequate oxygen saturation of existing hemoglobin (in other words, there are oxygen saturated RBCs, but just not enough of them in circulation). Cyanosis is typically not seen as it results from an increased quantity of deoxygenated hemoglobin in arterial blood (in other words, there is a lack of circulating oxygenated blood).

These are the signs/symptoms that you will see in a client with anemia:

  • dizziness/lightheadedness
  • tachycardia
  • pallor
  • fatigue, irritability, muscle weakness, headache
  • slowed thought process
  • decreased attention span
  • systolic heart murmur
  • dark urine
  • splenomegaly
  • hepatomegaly
  • icteric sclera, jaundice
  • low blood pressure (late sign of shock)
  • cool skin
  • decreased peripheral pulses
  • circulatory collapse and pain (found in sickle cell anemia)
  • frontal bossing (found in beta-thalassemia)
Slide 3: Causes

The main causes of anemia are:

  • Decreased production of red blood cells (RBCs) or RBC components
  • Increased destruction of RBCs
  • Excessive loss of RBCs through hemorrhage
  • Sequestration (found in sickle cell anemia)

Section 4: Explore: Nursing Care - Sickle Cell Anemia

Nursing Care: Sickle Cell Anemia

Nursing care management of sickle cell anemia includes:

Slide 1

Prepare the child and family for laboratory tests. Several blood tests will be ordered to determine the cause, current blood status, and treatment plan.

Slide 2

Decrease tissue oxygen needs by continual assessment of the child's energy level and tolerance for play and activities of daily living (ADLs), and adjust to allow as much self-care as possible without overexertion.

  • Plan age-appropriate diversional activities (i.e., watching TV, playing video games, reading, coloring or drawing, board or card games, etc.) to promote rest.
  • Crying places greater stress demands on the body which increases oxygen needs, so parental presence is important to minimize crying in younger children.
Slide 3

History and physical examination: This can point to the severity of the anemia and some indication of the possible cause. The nurse should ask about:

  • Nutrition, especially if the child is lactose intolerant or has inadequate intake of iron. Pay attention to statements like, "my child drinks a lot of milk" or "my child is on a liquid or vegetarian diet," as these could be cues to possible iron deficiency.
  • History of chronic, recurrent infection.
  • Eating habits, especially pica (which is the consumption of non-nutritive substances such as dirt, starch, lead-based paint chips, paper).
  • Bowel habits, as the presence of frank blood in stools or dark, tarry stools indicate chronic blood loss.
  • Familial history of hereditary diseases, such as sickle cell or thalassemia.
Slide 4

Prevent complications.

  • Children with anemia are prone to infection because tissue hypoxia causes cellular dysfunction which weakens the immune system. Infection also worsens anemia by increasing the metabolic needs of the body.
  • Minimize multiple blood sample draws and only retrieve the amount needed for lab tests.
  • Cardiac decompensation can result from excessive demands of the heart. Observe for clinical manifestations of heart failure (i.e., tachycardia, dyspnea, rales, moist respirations, cough, and sweating).

Section 6: Explore: Iron Deficiency Anemia

Iron Deficiency Anemia

Iron deficiency anemia is the most prevalent anemia worldwide. As mentioned previously, the production of hemoglobin (or red blood cells, RBCs) requires iron, and iron deficiency will result in decreased hemoglobin levels. Iron deficiency anemia is preventable and usually results from an inadequate dietary supply of iron.

Adolescents tend to be at the highest risk for iron deficiency anemia because of poor dietary habits, rapid growth, menses, strenuous physical activities, and obesity.

Slide 1: Causes
  • Impaired iron absorption
    • malabsorption disorders (i.e., lactose intolerance, inflammatory bowel disease)
  • Inadequate supply of iron, deficient dietary intake
    • rapid growth rate
    • excessive cow's milk intake, especially in toddlers; delayed addition of solid foods
    • poor general eating habits
    • exclusive breastfeeding of an infant after 6 months of age
  • Inadequate iron stores at birth
    • low birth weight, prematurity, multiple births
    • severe iron deficiency in the mother (hemoglobin <9 g/dl)
    • fetal blood loss at or before delivery
  • Increased iron requirements from blood loss (i.e., acute or chronic hemorrhage, parasitic infestation)
  • Excessive demands for iron required for growth
    • prematurity
    • adolescence
    • pregnancy
Slide 2: Clinical Manifestations
  • Tachycardia
  • Pallor
  • Fatigue, irritability, muscle weakness
  • Systolic heart murmur
  • Brittle, spoon-shaped fingernails
  • Cravings for non-nutritive substances (pica) like ice, dirt, paper
Slide 3: Diagnostic Evaluation
  • History and physical exam to determine sources of nutritional intake
  • Blood laboratory tests
    • complete blood count (CBC): decreased red blood cell (RBC) count, decreased hemoglobin (Hgb), and decreased hematocrit (Hct)
    • Hgb levels vary with age
    • RBC indices: decreased
    • reticulocyte count: can be decreased
    • total iron-binding capacity (TIBC): elevated represents the body's compensatory mechanism to absorb more iron from alternative sources than normal during states of deficiency
  • Stool analysis: guaiac test for the presence of blood in the stool
Slide 4: Therapeutic Management

Prevention is the primary goal. This can be achieved through optimal nutrition and appropriate iron supplementation.

  • Iron supplements
  • IV ferrous sulfate administration is very painful and requires close monitoring of the client during administration
  • Blood transfusion - packed red blood cells (PRBCs)
  • Supplemental oxygen

  • Modify the infant's diet to include high iron and vitamin C.

Section 10: Explore: Hemophilia

Hemophilia

Hemophilia is a group of bleeding disorders characterized by difficulty controlling bleeding. Bleeding can be internal or external and bleeding time is extended because of a lack of factors required for the blood to clot. In most cases, hemophilia is inherited and is considered X-linked recessive, which will affect boys. However, if there is a familial tendency, females should be tested to determine if they carry that trait. This is important as hemophilia can be deadly without swift, decisive interventions.

Hemophilia can vary in severity based upon the percentage of clotting factor a child's body may have. For example, a child who has mild hemophilia can have up to 40% of the normal factor VIII in his body, while a child who has severe hemophilia has very little or no factor VIII.

Slide 1: Causes
  • Congenital deficiency
  • Dysfunction in vascular or plasma clotting mechanisms
  • Absence of specific clotting factor
Slide 2: Clinical Manifestations

Expected findings include bleeding episodes, reports of joint pain and stiffness, impaired mobility, easy bruising, and activity intolerance. Bleeding tendencies are sometimes discovered in infancy after a circumcision. However, sometimes hemophilia is not manifested until an infant becomes more active and prone to injuries during the toddler years.

  • Active bleeding (i.e., bleeding gums, epistaxis, hematuria, or tarry stools)
  • Hematomas and/or bruising, even with minor injuries
  • Hemarthrosis, bleeding into the joint cavities (i.e., knees, elbows, ankles) as evidenced by joint pain, stiffness, warmth, swelling, redness, loss of range of motion, deformities
  • Headache
  • Slurred speech
  • Decreased level of consciousness
Slide 3: Types of Hemophilia

Hemophilia A

  • deficiency of factor VIII
  • referred to as classic hemophilia
  • accounts for 80% of cases
  • X-linked recessive disorder

Hemophilia B

  • deficiency of factor IX
  • referred to as Christmas disease
  • X-linked recessive disorder

Von Willebrand

  • inherited lack of the Von Willebrand factor protein
  • inability of platelets to aggregate
Slide 4: Therapeutic Management
  • 1-diamino-8-d-arginine vasopressin (DDAVP) - synthetic form of vasopressin that increases plasma factor VIII
    • effective for mild hemophilia, not severe
    • not effective for hemophilia B (factor IX deficiency)
    • caregivers taught to administer at home via venipuncture for children > 2 years
    • give prior to dental or surgical procedures where bleeding loss is expected
  • Factor VIII or factor IX (depending on the type of hemophilia)
    • prevent and treat hemorrhage
    • IV infusion
    • can require numerous doses
  • Corticosteroids
    • treat hematuria, acute episodes of hemarthrosis, and chronic synovitis
    • monitor for infection and bleeding
    • maintain good hygiene and avoid individuals with colds, viruses or infections
  • Aspirin and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory agents (NSAIDs)
    • avoid as they inhibit platelet function
    • Ibuprofen and other non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs are used occasionally to treat inflammatory musculoskeletal and join pain, under close supervision by the primary care provider.
Slide 5: Diagnostic Evaluation
  • DNA testing to detect classic hemophilia trait in females
  • Blood laboratory tests
    • prolonged partial thromboplastin time (aPTT)
    • factor-specific assays
    • platelets and prothrombin time are within expected ranges
    • whole blood clotting time is within expected ranges or can be prolonged

Section 11: Explore: Hemophilia - Nursing Care Management

Nursing care management of hemophilia includes:

  • Recognize and treat bleeding early.
  • Administer injections via subcutaneous route instead of intramuscular when possible.
  • Avoid unnecessary skin punctures and use venipuncture for blood samples instead of finger or heel sticks.
  • Use surgical aseptic technique.
  • Monitor for headache, slurred speech, or loss of consciousness as these could be signs of cerebral bleeding.
  • Monitor urine and stool for occult blood or signs of a gastrointestinal (GI) bleed (black and tarry stools).
  • Avoid aspirin and aspirin products and use acetaminophen as an acceptable substitute.
  • Control localized bleeding.
    • Apply pressure if open wound.
    • Administer factor replacement.
    • Encourage the client to rest and immobilize affected joints.
    • Elevate affected joints and apply ice.

Hemophilia: Client Education

Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Sickle Cell Anemia

Pediatric Hematologic Alterations: Sickle Cell

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients with sickle cell anemia. Sickle cell disease is a group of diseases in which abnormal sickle hemoglobin S (Hgb S) replaces normal adult hemoglobin (Hgb A).

Sickle cell anemia is the homozygous and most common form of sickle cell disease. Sickle cell anemia is an inherited, autosomal-recessive genetic disorder that affects the red blood cells (RBCs), which become acutely sickle-shaped, occluding small blood vessels (blocking blood flow) causing pain and decreased function. The child must receive the autosomal-recessive gene from both parents in order to have sickle cell anemia. If a child has sickle cell trait, then the child inherited the gene from one parent but is asymptomatic.

Section 3: Explore: Sickle Cell Anemia

Sickle Cell Anemia

Sickle cell anemia is a congenital hemolytic disease resulting from a defective hemoglobin molecule (Hgb S) that causes red blood cells (RBCs) to become sickle-shaped. Sickle cell anemia is a chronic illness with distress resulting from blocked and inadequate circulation and tissue/organ damage that causes pain over time, organ failure, and death.

Pathophysiology

The pathology of sickle cell anemia is that the RBCs become rigid, rough, and elongated, forming a crescent or sickle shape. This process is called sickling, which can produce hemolysis (cell destruction). The altered cells accumulate in capillaries and smaller blood vessels, making the blood more viscous. Normal circulation is impaired, causing pain, tissue ischemia and infarctions, and swelling.

Risk Factors

Sickle cell anemia is an autosomal recessive condition requiring genes from both parents. When both parents are carriers, each pregnancy presents a 25% chance that a child will be born with the disease and a 50% chance that the child will have the sickle cell trait.

Sickle cell anemia primarily affects African-Americans, but may also be seen in those of Mediterranean, Indian, Middle Eastern or Hispanic descent.

Section 4: Explore: Sickle Cell Crisis

Sickle Cell Crisis

Children with sickle cell disease have three types of problems or crisis episodes. A crisis episode can be the result of:

Accordion: Vaso-Occlusive Crisis
  • This is the most common, non-life-threatening crisis related to blocked blood flow (ischemia) of tissues and bone from sickling.
  • Blood stasis causes ischemia and infarction. Seriousness of sequalae depends upon the site of the occlusive crisis.
  • Acute (related to dehydration and decreased oxygen) vaso-occlusive crisis:
    • Severe pain, usually in bones, joints, and abdomen but can be located anywhere on the body (e.g. low back pain)
    • Hematuria
    • Obstructive jaundice
    • Visual disturbances
    • Tissue engorgement
Accordion: Sequestration
  • This is a potentially life-threatening crisis where there is excessive pooling of blood primarily in the spleen (splenomegaly) and sometimes in the liver (hepatomegaly) resulting in lethargy, pallor, and hypovolemia which can progress to shock.
  • Hypovolemic shock signs include irritability, tachycardia, pallor, decreased urinary output, tachypnea, cool extremities, thready pulse, and hypotension.
Accordion: Aplastic Crisis
  • This results from an extreme drop/reduction in red blood cells (RBCs; bone marrow suppression– often a vital trigger) leading to anemia, pallor, lethargy, sleepiness, and dyspnea.
  • Typically triggered by a viral infection.

Section 8: Explore: Sickle Cell Anemia: Nursing Care

Sickle Cell Anemia: Nursing Care

Nursing care and considerations for clients with sickle cell anemia include:

Slide 1: Pain control
  • Administer sufficient pain medication to promote comfort.
  • Administer analgesics (oral or intravenous) around the clock (ATC) when in crisis. Utilize patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) if appropriate.
  • Apply comfort measures (i.e., warm packs to painful joints to promote vasodilation of vessels).
    • Do not apply ice as this increases the vasoconstriction of the vessels thereby exacerbating the symptoms.
  • Remember: Opioids administered for pain may cause constipation, which can also cause pain. When administering opioids, the nurse should monitor bowel movements and implement interventions to prevent constipation.
Slide 2: Blood Products
  • Administer blood products (usually packed red blood cells [RBCs]) and exchange transfusions per facility protocol for severe anemia (replaces the sickled cells with normal blood cells).
  • Observe for hypervolemia and transfusion reaction.
Slide 3: Hydration
  • Decreases blood viscosity, sickling and vascular blockage.
  • Intense hydration therapy (intravenous [IV] fluids and oral intake) to prevent or correct dehydration while maintaining fluid and electrolyte balance.
  • Monitor intake and output to avoid fluid overload and monitor kidney function.
Slide 4: Oxygen
  • Administer oxygen as prescribed if hypoxia is present to increase tissue perfusion and decrease tissue ischemia.
Slide 5: Comfort and Mobility
  • Promote and encourage rest to decrease oxygen consumption and avoid fatigue.
  • Position for comfort and to decrease stress on painful joints.
  • Mild range of motion to retain mobility.
Slide 6: Infections
  • Prevent infection if possible.
  • Administer antibiotics.
  • Perform frequent hand hygiene.
  • Give oral prophylactic penicillin.
  • Administer vaccines to avoid pneumonia (pneumococcal conjugate vaccine), meningitis (meningococcal vaccine), and Haemophilius influenzae type B vaccine (Hib).

Sickle Cell Anemia: Family Education

Keeping a child with sickle cell anemia healthy and happy takes a coordinated effort from the child, their family, and their health care team. The nurse should provide education that is evidence-based and appropriate for both the child's needs and the general health literacy of the caregivers.
