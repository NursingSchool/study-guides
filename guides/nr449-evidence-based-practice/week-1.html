Ethics in Research

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Research and Evidence-Based Practice

Ethical principles for nursing research are in many ways the natural extension of ethical principles for professional nursing practice. Ethics is often defined by what is right versus what is wrong. Whether planning research or reading research, understanding and applying ethical principles is an important element of ensuring the highest quality client care.

This concept will review some key ethical situations in history and discuss how nurses support nursing research and uphold these ethical principles.

Section 3: Explore: Ethical Research Considerations

All research should be implemented with ethical care and the protection of the study participants. Researchers must adhere to ethical standards throughout all steps of the research process. They must decide what choices are right or wrong and make value judgments to ensure ethical treatment of everyone.

We must appreciate and value the current ethical and legal principles and, to do so, we need to remind ourselves of past research events.

In previous concepts, you learned about Nazi research in the 1930s and 1940s. The Nazis performed sterilizations and experiments in efforts to annihilate European Jews.

You also read about The Tuskegee Study that went on for forty years where African-Americans suffered from a lack of treatment for syphilis. Men, women, and children were negatively impacted.

Additionally, the Stanford University Prison Experiment in 1971 was a study conducted in a psychology class attempting to measure the effect of role-playing, labeling, and social expectations on behavior over two weeks. The study had to be stopped in the first week after students (either in the role of prisoners or guards) broke out into physical altercations. Depending on their role, students experienced a variety of emotions and behaviors, causing some to become cruel, depressed, or disoriented.

Section 4: Explore: Codes of Ethics

In response to human rights violations, various codes of ethics have been developed. Some originated overseas and some are based in the United States.

Currently, the most cited issues deal with informed consent, respect for confidentiality and privacy, and beneficence.

The ethical standards known as the Nuremberg Code were developed in 1949. These aimed to protect human subjects from enduring the kind of cruelty and exploitation the prisoners endured at Nazi concentration camps.

10 Elements of the Nuremberg Code:

  1. Voluntary consent is essential—to this day, studies require the participants to consent.
  2. The results of any experiment must be for the greater good of society.
  3. Human experiments should be based on previous animal experimentation.
  4. Experiments should be conducted by avoiding physical/mental suffering and injury.
  5. No experiment should be conducted if it is believed to cause death/disability.
  6. The risks should never exceed the benefits.
  7. Adequate facilities should be used to protect subjects.
  8. Experiments should be conducted only by qualified scientists.
  9. Subjects should be able to end their participation at any time.
  10. The scientist in charge must be prepared to terminate the experiment when injury, disability, or death is likely to occur.

(NIH, n.d.)

Several other international standards have been developed, including the Declaration of Helsinki, which was adopted in 1964 by the World Medical Association and most recently revised in 2013. It has over 35 different principles that must be followed by anyone conducting research.

Both the American Nurses Association and the International Council for Nurses have developed codes of ethics that establish nursing behavior expected while participating in research activities.

Section 5: Explore: Ethical Dilemmas in Research

Research that violates ethical principles typically occurs because a researcher believes that the knowledge obtained will be beneficial in the long run. When participants' rights and study quality are put in direct conflict, ethical dilemmas arise for researchers. The goal is to develop high-quality evidence for practice, but researchers must also adhere to rules for protecting human rights. Another type of dilemma may arise if nurse researchers face conflict-of-interest situations, in which their expected behavior as nurses conflicts with standard research behavior (e.g., deviating from a research protocol to assist a patient). It is precisely because of such dilemmas that codes of ethics are needed to guide researchers' efforts.

For examples of ethical dilemmas in research where the desire for rigor conflicts with ethical considerations:

1. Research question: Does a new medication prolong life in patients with cancer?

Ethical dilemma: The best way to test the effectiveness of an intervention is to administer the intervention to some participants but withhold it from others to see if the groups have different outcomes. However, if the intervention is untested (e.g., a new drug), the group receiving the intervention may be exposed to potentially hazardous side effects. On the other hand, the group not receiving the drug may be denied a beneficial treatment.

2. Research question: Are nurses equally empathic in their treatment of male and female patients in the operating room?

Ethical dilemma: Ethics require that participants be aware of their role in a study. Yet, if the researcher informs nurse participants that their empathy in treating male and female surgical patients will be scrutinized, will their behavior be "normal?" If the nurses' usual behavior is altered because of the known presence of research observers, then the findings will be inaccurate.

3. Research question: How do parents cope when their children have a terminal illness?

Ethical dilemma: To answer this question, the researcher may need to probe into the parents' emotional states at a vulnerable time, yet an understanding of the parents' coping mechanisms might help to design effective ways of addressing parents' grief and stress.

4. Research question: What is the process by which adult children adapt to the day-to-day stress of caring for a parent with dementia?

Ethical dilemma: Sometimes, especially in qualitative studies, a researcher may get so close to participants that they become willing to share "secrets" and privileged information. Interviews can become confessions—sometimes illegal behavior. In this example, suppose a woman admitted to physically abusing her mother. How does the researcher respond to that information without ignoring a pledge of confidentiality? And, if the researcher divulges the information to authorities, how can a pledge of confidentiality be given in good faith to other participants?

Section 6: Explore: Ethical Principles: Beneficence

The Belmont Report articulated three primary ethical principles on which standards of ethical research conduct are based: 1) beneficence; 2) respect for human dignity; and 3) justice.

Beneficence imposes a duty on researchers to minimize harm and maximize benefits. Human research should be intended to produce benefits for participants or, more typically, to generalize to a larger population. This principle covers multiple aspects, including the right to freedom from harm and discomfort and the right to protection from exploitation.

Tab: Right to Freedom from Harm and Discomfort

Researchers have an obligation to prevent or minimize harm in studies with humans. Participants must not be subjected to unnecessary risks of harm or discomfort, and their participation in research must be necessary for achieving societally important aims. In research with humans, harm and discomfort can be physical (e.g., injury), emotional (e.g., stress), social (e.g., loss of social support), or financial (e.g., loss of wages). Ethical researchers must use strategies to minimize all types of harm and discomfort, even if temporary.

Protecting human beings from physical harm is often straightforward, but it may be more difficult to address psychological issues. For example, participants may be asked questions about their personal lives. Such queries might lead people to reveal deeply personal information. The need for sensitivity may be greater in qualitative studies, which often involve in-depth exploration into highly personal areas. Researchers need to be aware of the nature of the intrusion on people's psyches.

Tab: Right to Protection from Exploitation

Involvement in a study should not place participants at a disadvantage. Participants need to be assured that their participation, or information they provide, will not be used against them. For example, people describing their economic situation should not risk the loss of public health benefits, nor should people reporting drug abuse fear being reported for a crime.

Study participants enter a special relationship with researchers and this relationship should not be exploited. Because nurse researchers may have a nurse-client relationship (in addition to a researcher-participant relationship), special care may be needed to avoid exploiting that bond. A client's consent to participate in a study may result from their understanding of the researcher's role as a nurse, not as a researcher.

In qualitative research, the psychological distance between researchers and participants often declines as the study progresses. The emergence of a pseudotherapeutic relationship is not uncommon, which could create additional risks for exploitation. On the other hand, qualitative researchers often are in a better position than quantitative researchers to do good, rather than just to avoid doing harm, because of the close relationships they develop with participants.

Section 7: Explore: Ethical Principles: Respect for Human Dignity

The second ethical principle in the Belmont Report is respect for human dignity. This principle includes the right to self-determination and the right to full disclosure.

Tab: The Right to Self-Determination

The principle of self-determination means that prospective participants have the right to decide voluntarily whether to participate in a study, without risking prejudicial treatment. It also means that people have the right to ask questions, to refuse to answer questions, and to drop out of the study.

A person's right to self-determination includes freedom from coercion. Coercion involves explicit or implicit threats of penalty for failing to participate in a study or excessive rewards for agreeing to participate.

Coercion can be subtle. For example, a generous monetary incentive (or stipend) to encourage the participation of a low-income group (e.g., the homeless) might be considered mildly coercive, because such incentives may be seen as a form of pressure.

Tab: The Right to Full Disclosure

Respect for human dignity encompasses people's rights to make informed decisions about study participation, which requires full disclosure. Full disclosure means that the researcher has fully described the study, the person's right to refuse participation, and potential risks and benefits. The right to self-determination and the right to full disclosure are the two elements on which informed consent is based.

Suppose we were testing the hypothesis that high school students with a high absentee rate are more likely to have lower grades than students with good attendance. If we approached potential participants and fully explained the study's purpose, some students might refuse to participate and nonparticipation would be selective; students who had lower grades, the group of primary interest, might be least likely to participate. Moreover, by knowing the study purpose, those who participate might not give candid responses. In such a situation, full disclosure could undermine the study.

In such situations, researchers sometimes use covert data collection (concealment), which is collecting data without participants' knowledge and thus without their consent. This might happen if a researcher wanted to observe people's behavior and was worried that doing so openly would change the outcome. Researchers might choose to obtain needed information through concealed methods, such as observing while pretending to be engaged in other activities.

A more controversial technique is the use of deception, which can involve deliberately withholding information about the study or providing participants with false information. For example, in studying high school students' use of drugs, we might describe the research as a study of student health practices, which is a mild form of misinformation.

Deception and concealment are ethically problematic because they interfere with people's rights to make truly informed decisions about personal costs and the benefits of participation. Some people think that deception is never justified, but others believe that if the study involves minimal risk yet offers benefits to society, then deception may be acceptable.

Full disclosure has emerged as a concern in connection with data collected over the internet. There is disagreement on what is considered public information and what is not.

Section 9: Explore: Ethical Principles: Justice

The third principle articulated in the Belmont Report concerns justice, which includes participants' rights to fair treatment and their rights to privacy.

Tab: The Right to Fair Treatment

One aspect of justice concerns the equitable distribution of benefits and burdens of research. The selection of participants should be based on research requirements and not on people's vulnerabilities. For example, groups with lower social standing (e.g., prisoners) have sometimes been selected as study participants, raising ethical concerns.

Potential discrimination is another aspect of distributive justice. During the 1990s, it was found that women and minorities were being excluded from many clinical studies. In the United States, this has led to regulations requiring that researchers who seek funding from the National Institutes of Health (including NINR) include women and minorities as study participants.

The right to fair treatment encompasses other obligations. For example, researchers must treat people who decline to participate in a study in a nonprejudicial manner, they must honor all agreements made with participants, they must show respect for the beliefs of people from different backgrounds, and they must always treat participants courteously and tactfully.

Tab: The Right to Privacy

Research with humans involves intrusions into people's lives. Researchers should ensure that their research is not more intrusive than it needs to be and that privacy is maintained. Participants have the right to expect that any data they provide will be kept in strict confidence.

Privacy issues have become even more salient in the U.S. healthcare community since the passage of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA), which articulates federal standards to protect patients' medical records and health information. For healthcare providers who transmit health information electronically, compliance with HIPAA regulations (the Privacy Rule) has been required since 2003.

Historical Events in Research

Section 1: Introduction to Historical Events in Research

When humans participate in research studies, special care must be taken to protect their rights. However, breaches in ethical conduct have occurred throughout history.

This lesson will focus on three egregious examples of research misconduct:

  • Nazi medical experimentation
  • The Tuskegee Study
  • The Willowbrook Study

Though the primary researchers were physicians in each study, evidence suggests that nurses were aware of the misconduct.

Additionally, this lesson will review several important documents that provide guidelines for human subject research. As nurses, our responsibility to society mandates that we learn from the past to protect the future.

Section 3: Explore: Ethics in Human Research

Research is defined as a systematic investigation to develop or contribute to generalizable knowledge (USDHHS, 2021).

Ethics are the principles that determine if one's conduct is either right or wrong and distinguish between acceptable and unacceptable behavior. Ethical considerations provide guidelines for how research is to be conducted. Of primary importance is that research promotes knowledge, truth, and avoids error. Therefore, researchers must not fabricate, falsify, or misrepresent data. When research involves human subjects, significant risks are possible. Despite a researcher's best intentions, mishaps may occur.

Unfortunately, in the past, some researchers have deliberately violated human participants' rights. Before the mid-1900s, there were no existing research regulations or policies to protect human subjects; therefore, no oversight or safeguards existed.

Section 4: Explore: Nazi Medical Experimentation

From 1933 until 1945, German scientists conducted heinous experiments on World War II concentration camp detainees. This research was intended to enhance soldiers' performance. Some of the studies performed included:

  • submersion in cold water for days to test the length of survival for pilots who parachuted into the North Sea
  • resuscitation using various methods on subjects who survived experimentation
  • burning the skin with phosphorus to track wound healing
  • performing surgery without anesthesia to gauge pain levels
  • opening the uterine cavity during pregnancy to learn about fetal growth and development
  • gender reassignment surgery

Not surprisingly, many of the subjects did not survive experimentation. However, once the international community discovered these atrocities, it became clear that oversight of research involving human participants was necessary.

Section 5: Explore: The Nuremberg Code

Eventually, the German scientists were prosecuted for war crimes against the concentration camp victims in the Nuremberg Trials. The Nuremberg Code, established in 1949, was used by American military judges to prosecute the scientists and is considered one of the most important documents in history related to medical research (Shuster, 1997).

Though the code was not enforced by law, it was the first set of international guidelines that advocated for subjects' voluntary participation and informed consent (Mandal et al., 2011). Of primary importance, the code stated that "voluntary consent of the human subject is absolutely essential" (NIH, n.d., p.1).

The code's ten principles remain in use today to protect the rights of human subjects in medical research.

Ten Guiding Principles:

  1. Voluntary consent is required.
  2. Any experimentation must be for the greater good of society.
  3. Human experiments should be based on previous animal experimentation.
  4. Experiments should not inflict unnecessary physical and mental suffering and injury.
  5. If death or disability is likely, experimentation should not occur.
  6. The risks of experimentation should never exceed the benefits.
  7. Adequate facilities should be provided to protect subjects from injury, disability, and death.
  8. Only qualified professionals should conduct experiments and should oversee all stages of the process.
  9. At any time, a subject may end participation in an experiment.
  10. The lead investigator must be prepared to stop the experiment if injury, disability, or death is likely to occur.

(NIH, n.d.)

Section 7: Explore: The Declaration of Helsinki

The Declaration of Helsinki, developed in 1964 as an extension of The Nuremberg Code, was adopted by the World Medical Association and has been revised several times, most recently in 2013 (Resnik, 2020). The goal of the declaration was to distinguish between therapeutic and non-therapeutic research.

  • Therapeutic research is experimentation that offers a subject the opportunity to receive beneficial treatment.
  • Non-therapeutic research seeks to gain knowledge, while not providing benefit to the study subject.

The most recent Declaration of Helsinki, released in 2013, reinforces the necessity of informed consent by research subjects while allowing for surrogate consent if the subject is a minor or cognitively impaired. However, it states that studying vulnerable populations should only be conducted when necessary to promote the health of these specific populations and only when non-therapeutic research cannot be undertaken on competent adults.

Reference

Resnik, D. B. (2020, August 25). Research ethics timeline. https://www.niehs.nih.gov/research/resources/bioethics/timeline/index.cfm

Section 9: Explore: The Willowbrook Study

Unfortunately, the United States has also committed egregious experimentation on human subjects. The Willowbrook Study, conducted from 1963 to 1966 in Staten Island, New York, involved children as research subjects. Researchers studied residents of a state-run school for children with learning disabilities to determine the effects of untreated hepatitis. This misconduct was exceedingly egregious because the subjects were members of a vulnerable population: cognitively impaired children. To make matters worse, despite closed enrollment in the school, children whose parents agreed to allow their participation in the study were admitted, introducing an added element of influence.

Allegedly, Willowbrook researchers purposely exposed subjects to hepatitis. After observation, during which infected children were untreated, a vaccine was tested on the subjects. The research community continues to debate the value of this study as it resulted in the hepatitis vaccine currently in use today; however, the researchers purposely harmed a vulnerable student population and parental consent was questionable.

Section 10: Explore: The Tuskegee Study

Between 1932 and 1972, the Public Health Service conducted a study investigating the effects of untreated syphilis on African American men in Tuskegee, Alabama. However, the researchers did not tell the subjects that they were involved in an experiment. In addition, researchers withheld treatment from subjects who tested positive for the disease, even though an effective antibiotic (penicillin) was available. The study ended in 1972 when a reporter alerted the public and Congress to the ethical dilemmas with the study. Eventually, the participants and their families filed a lawsuit, which the U.S. government settled.

As a result of wide-scale public outrage, the National Research Act in 1974 created the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavior Research in the United States. This commission drafted The Belmont Report, which became a foundational document for ethical practices involving human subjects in the United States (Mandal et al., 2011).

Reference

Mandal, J., Acharya, S., & Parija, S. C. (2011). Ethics in human research. Tropical Parasitology, 1(1), 2-3. https://doi.org/10.4103/2229-5070.72105

Section 11: Explore: The Belmont Report

In 1978, the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research produced The Belmont Report (USDHHS, 2016). The report's primary purpose was to identify basic ethical principles that should underlie the conduct of research involving human subjects. The three principles in the report form the cornerstones of human subject research: (1) beneficence, (2) justice, and (3) respect for persons.

Tab: Beneficence

Beneficence dictates the researcher's obligation not to harm the research participants but also to maximize the possible benefits. Subjects must be made aware of potential risks of study participation (Miracle, 2016). One measure defined in The Belmont Report to protect subjects from harm is the Institutional Review Board (IRB). The primary purpose of the IRB is to review research proposals, protect subjects from injury, and ensure risk mitigation measures are in place (Miracle, 2016).

Tab: Justice

Justice demands equal treatment and fairness for all research subjects. All clients require proper care and treatment even if they decline to participate in a research study (Miracle, 2016). No subject can receive less than an acceptable standard of care. Finally, all subjects have the right to know the outcome of the study (Miracle, 2016).

Tab: Respect for Persons

Respect for persons is based on two ethical concepts: autonomy and protection of vulnerable populations (Miracle, 2016).

  • Autonomy allows people to make their own decisions and choices. Research subjects must consent to voluntary participation. Informed consent consists of providing information, ensuring comprehension of that information, and the act of voluntary participation without coercion.
  • Vulnerable populations with diminished autonomy require increased protection and special consideration before experimentation. Specific vulnerable groups include children, prisoners, pregnant women, fetuses, mentally disabled persons, and economically and educationally disadvantaged persons (Gordon, 2020).

Review the complete Belmont Report for more information.

Section 13: Explore: The Common Rule

In 1981, the Federal Policy for the Protection of Human Subjects or the "Common Rule" was published and adopted into law (USDHHS, 2016). The main elements of this rule are:

  • research institutions must comply with the Common Rule
  • researchers must obtain and document informed consent
  • all human research must be approved by an Institutional Review Board (IRB)
  • vulnerable populations (pregnant women, prisoners, and children) must be protected (Mandel et al., 2011)

Additionally, the Common Rule established requirements for the IRB, including membership, function, operations, review of research, and record keeping (Mandal et al., 2011).

References

Mandal, J., Acharya, S., & Parija, S. C. (2011). Ethics in human research. Tropical Parasitology, 1(1), 2-3. https://doi.org/10.4103/2229-5070.72105

Section 15: Explore: Institutional Review Board

Under the Food and Drug Administration's (FDA) authority, the Institutional Review Board (IRB) is a group that is formally designated to review and monitor research involving human subjects. To protect the rights and well-being of human research subjects, the IRB has the authority to approve, require modifications, or disapprove research (U.S. Food & Drug Administration, 1998).

Every institution is not required to have its own IRB. However, FDA regulations permit an institution without an IRB to arrange for an "outside" IRB to be responsible for the initial and ongoing approval of research studies conducted at a non-IRB institution (U.S. Food & Drug Administration, 1998).

The primary purpose of the IRB is to protect human research subjects.

An IRB must review all research studies involving human subjects. There are three categories of research review:

  • An exempt review is limited to research studies that pose no risk for the subjects. For example, the researcher may survey participants or collect data from medical records. Though the study does not need full IRB approval, the researcher must still adhere to ethical principles.
  • An expedited review is used when a study poses minimal risk for the subjects. The probability of physical or emotional discomfort from participation is no greater than that experienced during daily life. These studies are reviewed by one or two IRB members who will approve or deny the research proposal.
  • A full review is necessary when research poses more than minimal risk to subjects. These studies must be reviewed by the entire committee. Federal funds are often received and publication of research findings is anticipated. Methods of data collection, the consent process, and selection of subjects are closely inspected.

Reference

U.S. Food & Drug Administration. (1998, January). Institutional review boards frequently asked questions. https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/institutional-review-boards-frequently-asked-questions

Section 17: Explore: An Ongoing Issue

Long after the enactment of numerous rules and regulations surrounding medical research, ethical breaches have occurred. The Gelsinger case at the University of Pennsylvania is a recent example of a severe violation of ethics.

In 1995, an experienced and respected researcher designed a study that used a genetically engineered cold virus that had previously only been tested in animals to deliver genes to correct a genetic liver disorder in humans. After receiving Institutional Review Board approval, the study commenced. The subjects were stable adult clients with the specific, identified genetic liver disorder.

Jesse Gelsinger, an 18-year-old client, was the next-to-last subject enrolled in the study despite reports expressing concern about the safety of the cold virus. Unfortunately, Gelsinger received a dose of the virus that was significantly larger than the first client enrolled in the study and subsequently died from a massive immune system response to gene therapy.

As a result, the university immediately stopped all gene therapy research. The Gelsinger case questioned whether high-risk research should be conducted on healthy, stable clients and whether adverse events were ignored or misinterpreted.

Since the Gelsinger case, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the National Institutes of Health (NIH) have received almost 1,000 adverse events related to gene therapy, leading experts to ponder whether these protocols are ready for human trials. The Gelsinger case, and others that are similar, highlight the need for careful consideration when medical research includes human subjects.

Introduction to Evidence Based Practice

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Research and Evidence-Based Practice

Evidence-based practice (EBP) is the process nurses and other clinicians use to inform client care and delivery decisions. EBP is the foundation for safe and effective healthcare, and ensures that evidence is the basis for practice decisions rather than eloquence or tradition. In addition, there is a direct connection between research and evidence-based practice.

This concept reviews the integration of evidence-based practice and research into one's daily work while considering the newest trends and research.

Section 3: Explore: Nursing Research & Its Evolution

The word research includes the stem search, which means "to seek", and the prefix "re-", which means "again." Therefore, research signifies a replication of a previous search.

Research is a systematic process of inquiry that uses rigorous guidelines to produce unbiased, trustworthy answers to questions about nursing practice.

Timeline: Nursing Research Evolution

1854-1856: When you think about nursing research, the story starts with Florence Nightingale. Florence was a nurse during the Crimean War, where she showed her calling and devotion as a nurse. In two years (1854-1856), Nightingale's critical thinking and problem analyses; implementation of interventions and positive outcomes; advancement of nursing practice based on evidence; detailed documentation and statistical analysis; and tenacious political advocacy provided the foundation for how to reform healthcare systems.

1950s: Fast forward about one hundred years, and nurses are more fully involved in research.

1970s-1980s: Publication opportunities increased, now spreading the information to a larger group of providers.

1986: The National Center for Research for Nursing (NCRN) opened as part of the National Institutes of Health (NIH).

1993: The NCRN became an institute to support clinical and basic research with a new name, the National Institute of Nursing Research (NINR).

Section 7: Explore: Quality Improvement

Research must be broad and neutral so it can be published and generalized to impact a larger group of clients after peer review. Research subjects must be informed of their participation and provide informed consent. In a healthcare organization, research provides information that helps quality improvement (QI) projects generate more evidence to support care. Quality improvement is different from the research process because quality improvement projects are beneficial to specific organizations. Quality improvement is an internal process where data and evidence help direct practice changes for a specific organization.

For example, if a nursing unit has a high medication error rate, nurses can look at what research recommends to reduce errors and compare it to current practices. The facility may modify the medication room setup, documentation, or technology (computers/pyxis/medication storage) that is used. Staff should then receive education and training on practice changes.

The Johns Hopkins Nursing Evidence-based Practice Model

Let's explore some common vocabulary used in research. Select each term below for more information.

Tab: Peer Review

Practice experts review and evaluate the quality of the research. Peer review is how nurses become published or present at nursing conferences so that other nurses can learn about the new findings.

Tab: Blinded Review

The experts evaluating the research are blinded to or do not see the researcher's name to prevent bias.

Tab: Replication

When a repeated study has similar results as a prior study, nurses feel more confident in the findings and possibly consider making practice changes based on the evidence.

Tab: Magnet Status

Organizations who contribute to new knowledge, use evidence-based practice, and strive for nursing excellence can obtain a Magnet designation. Achieving Magnet status is a priority for many organizations and includes involvement by bedside nurses.

  • Advancing practice
  • Improving health promotion
  • Highlighting gaps in the science of nursing

Section 12: Explore: Sources of Knowledge and Credibility

Nurses must ensure that they are accessing credible information as there are many sources that are not evidence-based. Current practice may be based upon the components listed in the CASTLE infographic below.

Just because nurses have always done something a certain way is not enough. Nurses need to verify that practice is supported by the most recent research and recommendations. Client safety should always be at the core of clinical practice decisions.

CASTLE:

  • Customs and tradition: "We've always done it that way." This may not be best for the client.
  • Assembled information (quality improvement data): Data collected from client charts.
  • Scientific research: The most objective source of nursing knowledge; research papers and articles.
  • Trial and error: "If it works, we'll use it." This may not always be the safest or best practice.
  • Logical reasoning (inductive and deductive): This is basing a decision on current clinical signs.
  • Experts or authorities: Who conducted the research or wrote the article? A nurse or other provider should be the author versus lay people without nursing education and experience.

Research and the Law

Section 1: Introduction to Research and the Law

The duality of law and ethics exists in research. Law is considered the minimum standard with ethics being above the minimum behavior. Most researchers and providers provide ethical care but, if not, the legal system is there to address the situation.

This lesson will describe how the nurse can maximize the legal and ethical protections afforded to the subjects.

Section 3: Explore: Four Categories of Law

Law is divided into four broad categories: Common, Administrative, Statutory, and Tort.

Common Law

Common law is the part of English law that is derived from custom and judicial precedent, rather than statutes.

Example: Medical Malpractice Cases

Administrative Law

Administrative law generally refers to the laws and legal principles governing the creation, administration, and regulation of government agencies at the federal, state, and local levels.

Example: (HIPAA) Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

Statutory Law

Statutory law in the United States consists of laws passed by the legislature. For the federal government, statutory laws are acts passed by the United States Congress. These acts are designated as Public Laws or Private Laws.

Example: Denying a client based on financial status - Congress passed broad rights to healthcare for the elderly, disabled, people living in poverty, and children by establishing Medicare and Medicaid.

Tort Law

A tort is an act or omission that gives rise to injury or harm to another and amounts to a civil wrong for which courts impose liability.

Example: Using an instrument that is known to be defective.

Section 5: Explore: Legal Issues

There are a variety of potential legal issues to navigate during the nursing process.

Tab: Communication

A researcher needs to be aware of what they share with others related to the study and its participants.

  • Defamation is a false and harmful oral statement.
  • Libel is a false and published (or written) statement.

The intent should be to distribute correct information.

Tab: Emotional Safety

A researcher must protect subjects against inflicting mental or emotional distress. Sometimes a statement about a risk of emotional distress is added to consent forms to reduce the legal risk of this occurring.

Tab: Client Cells/Tissue

There is ongoing discussion about whether consent is required to use an individual's tissues. Clients may not even be aware that they have contributed their cells to research. If a profit is made by selling tissues, some researchers don't feel that the client should be compensated.

Tab: Privacy

Subjects have the right to privacy. Subjects have the right to have their health information kept confidential, released only to authorized individuals, and to have their bodies protected from public view.

Tab: Consent

Researchers should ensure that subjects who are underage, disabled, or members of a vulnerable population are represented by their legal guardians. Additional examples of vulnerable populations include low-income, homeless, LGBTQ, and the elderly. Additionally, some clients live in more rural locations and do not have access to jobs and healthcare.

  • The language must be simple enough to understand.
  • The consent must not be coerced.
  • Consent must be documented.
  • The subject must have had time to review the document.

Section 7: Explore: Institutional Review Board (IRB)

Institutional Review Boards (IRB) are required by law in any organization that allows research involving people.

This requirement evolved from early protection programs such as the Belmont Report and the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research.

Goals of an IRB include:

  • Safeguarding against the inhumane treatment of individuals
  • Ensuring that research is ethically acceptable
  • Ensuring that the welfare and rights of research participants are protected

The U.S. Food & Drug Administration (FDA) has a detailed website about IRBs which provides additional information.

There are three categories of research review by an IRB:

  • An exempt review is limited to research studies that pose no risk for the subjects. For example, the researcher may survey participants or collect data from medical records. Though the study does not need full IRB approval, the researcher must still adhere to ethical principles.
  • An expedited review is used when a study poses minimal risk for the subjects. The probability of physical or emotional discomfort from participation is no greater than that experienced during daily life. These studies are reviewed by one or two IRB members who will approve or deny the research proposal.
  • A full review is necessary when research poses more than minimal risk to subjects. These studies must be reviewed by the entire committee. Federal funds are often received and publication of research findings is anticipated. Methods of data collection, the consent process, and selection of subjects are closely inspected.

Section 10: Explore: Animal Testing

Research that has benefited humans often began with research conducted on animals. There are many animal advocacy organizations that have pushed for better treatment of animals and have campaigned for humane treatment.

Multiple guidelines now include:

  • Injuries should be limited to unavoidable levels
  • Analgesic and other pain relief agents should be used during testing
  • Replace animal subjects with non-animal alternatives
  • Reduce the number of animals used in experimentation

Experimentation raises concerns about necessity, purpose, risks, benefits, as well as the subject's capacity for suffering.

Some states have laws against animal testing and consider it abuse. Researchers must identify the parameters they must follow and uphold ethical practices.

Section 12: Explore: Research and the Internet

Gathering data on the internet requires additional standards, including ethical standards and attention to avoid legal repercussions.

  • Over the internet, the ability to observe the potential participant while they are reading the informed consent document is absent, so it is difficult to know with certainty that the participant read the document. This inability to observe the process might allow more room for the participant to later claim that their consent was not informed.
  • Other steps might need to be taken by the internet researcher to increase confidence that the participant has read and understands the material related to the research. One possibility is to have an e-mail conversation with the participant after they submit the informed consent document to verify that they have read the document and to answer any questions or clarify any concerns.

Nurse researchers must:

  • Identify why online is more effective and more beneficial than in person.
  • Ensure subjects have a safe, private space to respond.
  • Support the ethical acquisition of web addresses; ensure that the list owner has endorsed the study.
  • Evaluate the vulnerability of subjects.
  • Implement a process to ensure subjects are of legal age.
  • Provide contact information so participants can easily communicate with researchers as needed.

The possibility of a lawsuit brought by a participant outside the researcher's home state opens the possibility of multiple jurisdictions in which a lawsuit could be filed. Because it is state-regulated, malpractice is generally a state court issue. However, if the participant is in one state and the researcher/provider is in another state, the lawsuit could be brought in the participant's home state or, if the participant and the researcher are from different states, the case could be brought to federal court.

Research as Evidence in Nursing Practice

Section 1: Introduction to Research in Nursing Practice

The definition of evidence-based nursing is a rigorous methodology in which all the research data for a particular problem or issue is analyzed together, then combined with patients' values and preferences, as well as professional expertise. This professional expertise includes a nurse's accumulated experience, education, and skills.

This concept will review how nurses can participate in the research process.

Section 3: Explore: Triad of Evidence-Based Practice

Let's look at the makeup of evidence-based practice.

  • Scientific evidence is discovered through various experiments and studies.
  • Experts share best practices and client stories.
  • Client preferences are reviewed.

Triad of Evidence-Based Practice (widget transcript):

Best scientific evidence

  • Results of experiments and quantitative studies

Clinical experience

  • Expert panels
  • Practice groups
  • Consensus statements

Patient preferences

  • Satisfaction
  • Quality of life
  • Treatment burden
  • Qualitative studies

Section 4: Explore: What is Research?

The first leg of evidence-based practice (EBP) focuses on finding the best scientific evidence: research. What exactly is research?

There are many definitions in the literature and a movement to increase nursing research volume is in progress. It involves collecting, organizing, and analyzing information to advance understanding of a topic or issue.

  • Research has the broad intent of improving nursing practice. We cannot rely on doing things the same way year after year without ensuring there is not a better way or treatment.
  • Research benefits large groups of people. Often findings support treatment options for certain genders or specific age groups.
  • Participants are fully informed about study risks and sign consent. Research studies are regulated and must follow specific protocols.
  • Research results are peer-reviewed and can be replicated. Additional researchers and colleagues ensure the study's validity before it is shared with the public as recommended changes.

In order to apply research into nursing practice, it must meet the patients' needs, be carefully assessed for credibility, and have external validity (the ability to generalize the findings from a research study to other groups of people, locations, and situations).

Section 6: Explore: Nursing Research - Problem Identification

Applying nursing research to practice starts with problem identification.

The inspiration for identifying a research problem is all around us. As we reflect on our practice, we find ourselves asking questions that lead us to ideas for researchable problems:

  • Why are we doing this?
  • Should we be doing this?
  • How could we do this better?

Nursing is a practice profession and nurses can identify problems from various sources in patient care, education, and leadership. Research helps us solve practice problems by determining the best answer. The textbook describes sources of researchable nursing problems in detail. As a baccalaureate-prepared nurse, research-based evidence is expected to be utilized for solving practice problems.

Through research, evidence, and synthesis of findings into practice, we now have evidence-based practice (EBP) protocols and guidelines. Below are a few examples of EBP:

  • Catheterization Associated Urinary Tract Infection (CAUTI) protocols
  • Intramuscular (IM) injections
  • Medication errors

Section 7: Explore: Nursing Research: Identifying a Need

Before gathering research to review and to incorporate into practice, the nurse needs to identify the need for evidence based practice (EBP). There are three categories of factors that may be used to uncover a need when choosing what to study:

  • Problem-focused
  • Nursing knowledge
  • New equipment, technology, and products

See how the three categories apply to various situations.

  • Medication error reports
  • Client concerns
  • Nurse observations

Section 9: Explore: Nursing Research - Credibility

Once the nurse has identified the need for change in practice, the next step is to gather research evidence to be included in the review. When selecting articles to review, it is important to negate any possible bias, which can be avoided by a careful appraisal of the literature. The appraisal process begins by determining whether the journal, authors, and publication process are credible.

Credibility can be determined by the following:

Tab: Is the source credible?

Researchers must disclose their professional degrees and what type of experience they have. They are often considered subject matter experts.

Tab: Is there a conflict of interest or bias?

Reviewers must confirm that the researcher has nothing to gain, such as financial compensation or other benefits. Additionally, confirm that the editorial board that regulates and reviews the studies is unbiased and does not have any financial ties to the study.

Tab: Has the research undergone a blind peer review?

A blind peer review is a neutral party who is not connected with the authors of the study who could complete the review.

Tab: Has the study been published within a reasonable time frame?

Is the information still relevant? Evidence should be conducted and published within the last 3-5 years.

Tab: Has the study been replicated?

If a researcher can replicate a study's results, it is more likely that those results can be generalized to the larger population. It increases its validity.

Section 10: Explore: Nursing Research - Level of Evidence

Research to be included in the review can be graded based on the strength of the evidence. There are several rating scales that can be used to evaluate the strength of the research. One rating system includes the levels of evidence, with each level corresponding with the type of study conducted.

  • Level I = multiple randomized control trials (RCT), large sample sizes and large effect sizes, that support a specific treatment
  • Level II = randomized or non-randomized control studies that support a specific treatment
  • Level III = evidence from case studies, studies of intact groups, non-randomized
  • Level IV = chart reviews, reviews of other studies, expert opinions

RCTs or Reports of Multiple RCTsStrong recommendation to use or not use the research findings
RCTs with limitationsRecommend to use or not use the research findings
