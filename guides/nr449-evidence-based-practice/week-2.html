Identifying Practice Problems

Section 1: Introduction to Identifying Practice Problems

Curiosity drives the identification of practice problems. This process begins with observation of a practice problem, which then becomes a question. These questions lead to nursing research that provides the evidence to solve the problem.

In this lesson, you will learn to identify practice problems and translate a problem into a researchable question.

Section 3: Explore: Finding Problems

The beginning step in implementing evidence-based nursing practice is to identify the need for change to improve client outcomes. The need for change may be evident because of emerging problems, poor results, or new knowledge. Often, the nurse is frustrated due to inefficiencies or ineffective care and searches for a "better way."

The search for a problem is the easiest part of the research process as issues surround the nurse every day. Other sources of researchable issues include:

  • Clinical practice observations
  • Client feedback
  • Personal experiences
  • Social issues

Once the general topic of research has been selected, the nurse must consider preliminary questions. Keywords in these questions will drive the search for relevant literature. Issues that are too broad increase the complexity of the research. Narrow questions are easier to research and address.

As the research problem moves from a general topic to a narrow, researchable question, measurable outcomes become evidence.

Transcript:

  • Why are we doing it this way?
  • Should we be doing it at all?
  • Is there a better way of doing it?

Section 4: Explore: Nurse-Sensitive Concepts

Since nursing research aims to improve nursing care, many nurse researchers focus on concepts within the nursing span of control. Nursing-sensitive indicators aim to determine the impact nurses have on client care.

For example, a nurse interested in preventing central line-associated bloodstream infections will focus on sterile dressing changes (nurse-sensitive concept) instead of venous access methods (medically-sensitive concept). Other examples of nurse-sensitive concepts include:

  • Prevention of burnout
  • Prevention of lower-back injuries
  • Prevention of medication errors
  • Effective non-pharmacologic pain management techniques
  • Prevention of client falls
  • Reduction of restraint prevalence

Link: National Database of Nursing Quality Indicators (NDNQI)

Section 7: Explore: Research Problems and Researchable Questions

Finding significant problems in nursing care is critical to improving outcomes for clients, nurses, communities, and organizations. The evolution of a problem from a topic of interest to a research problem will narrow the focus to a researchable question.

The progression from an abstract concept to a measurable concept follows a specific path. The goal is to narrow the focus of the research problem so that a researchable question emerges.

If the research problem has not been narrowed, the question generates too many concepts and relationships to test.

Transcript — Evolution of the Research Process (worked example):

  1. General topic of interest: Wound management
  2. Identification of a problem: Wound management for clients with pressure injuries
  3. Exploration of the gap in knowledge: Literature review
  4. Development of the problem statement: Few studies exist to direct and/or support wound management activities in clients with pressure injury.
  5. Development of the purpose statement: The purpose of this study is to compare two wound management techniques: manuka honey and normal saline dressing, in clients with pressure injury.
  6. Development of the research question: Is there a significant difference in outcomes between two wound management techniques: manuka honey and normal saline dressing, in clients with pressure injury?

Section 8: Explore: Research Problem Statements

Research problem statements are declarations of disparity, or the difference (gap) between what is known and what needs to be known about a topic. The discrepancy is what needs to be addressed by the research process.

Two examples of problem statements:

"Fall prevention research has defined the risks that contribute to in-hospital falls and established valid and reliable fall risk assessment tools. However, there was a dearth of research regarding fall prevention protocols that link fall risk factors to evidence-based interventions. After identifying this gap, our team interviewed patients who had fallen and their care team members to determine perceptions of why hospitalized patients fall and interventions that could be effective and feasible in the hospital setting" (Duckworth et al., 2019, para. 2).

"Recent research has documented the correlation between nurse burnout and health care-associated infections, patient falls, medication errors, and other patient adverse events. Based on the findings of landmark studies, it is clear that working conditions for nurses (including nurse-patient ratio, shift hours, and role strain/overload) must be improved to effectively prevent burnout" (Alexander et al., 2015, para. 1).

Section 9: Explore: Research Purpose Statements

Research purpose statements are declarations of intent by the researcher and indicate the goal of the study. They are clear, concise, and easily identified.

Two examples of purpose statements:

"The purpose of this study is to assess the effectiveness for engaging patients and family in the 3-step fall prevention process (as defined by patient/family knowledge of their personalized fall risk factors and prevention plan) of each of the Fall TIPS modalities" (Duckworth et al., 2019, para. 7).

"The purpose of the present study is to examine the efficacy of yoga to improve self-care and reduce burnout among nurses practicing at an urban, tax-supported health care network" (Alexander et al., 2015, para. 5).

When developing purpose statements, the researcher should use unbiased verbs such as compare, describe, develop, discover, explore, test, and understand.

  • Example: The purpose of the study was to explore the effects of art therapy on speech recovery in adult stroke patients in a rehabilitation facility.

Biased verbs include demonstrate, prove, and show.

  • Example: The purpose of the study was to prove that art therapy improves speech recovery in adult stroke patients in a rehabilitation facility.

Section 12: Explore: Research Questions

Using the arrows below the images, go through the presentation below to learn more about research questions and problem statements.

Slide 1:

The problem statement is a general statement of why a research study is needed. The purpose statement gives an overview of the intent of the research study. Neither is specific enough to guide the research study design and methodology. Writing a research question is the final step before beginning research design. It outlines the primary variables that will be studied.

Two examples of research questions:

Quantitative randomized controlled trial: In adult surgical clients in the operating room, does the pre-incision use of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) compared to the use of povidone-iodine impact surgical site infections?

Qualitative study: What are the perceptions of pregnant adolescents regarding accessing prenatal care?

Slide 2:

The researcher should ask:

  • Does the question make sense?
  • Is the question important to clinical care?
  • Is the question logical?
  • Could this research yield practical benefits?

Slide 3:

The research question guides research design and methodology. The time spent carefully developing a research question is essential to constructing the rest of the research process.

Literature Review

Section 1: Introduction to Literature Review

Conducting a literature review is both an art and a science. Each step requires methodical attention to an established process with creativity when selecting and combining search terms. Whether beginning a research project or searching for evidence to improve client care, the nurse's ability to locate current evidence on the topic of interest is the key to success. This learning activity focuses on the literature search.

By completing the interactive learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Describe the rationale for conducting a thorough review of the literature.
  • Explain the components of a successful literature review.
  • Select appropriate search terms when given a Patient/Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, Time (PICOT) question.
  • Choose inclusion and exclusion criteria to narrow the scope of a literature search.
  • Document the literature search.

For more information on this topic, review the How Do I…Learn to Search? topic in the Chamberlain University Library.

Section 3: Explore: Literature Review: Purpose

A critical step of the research process is locating and evaluating all current evidence relevant to a specific clinical question or area of interest. Next, a researcher uses that evidence to discover information gaps that a researcher can fill. Finally, clinicians evaluate the evidence to determine if the evidence can improve client care.

Today's body of health-related research is vast and continues to grow daily. However, because quality research findings are available in various publications, locating the correct information is challenging. Therefore, nurses must master the knowledge and skills needed to search databases efficiently.

Before the researcher can write the literature review, they must do a literature search.

Link: Chamberlain Library's guide on learning to search

Section 4: Explore: Steps of the Literature Review

A successful literature search requires an organized approach, pre-planning, and documentation of steps taken. The eight steps outlined below start after the nurse has identified a clinical problem and written the PICOT (short for patient/population, intervention, comparison, outcome, and time) question and ends when the findings have been summarized and synthesized (Houser, 2023).

Transcript — Steps of the Literature Review:

  1. Identify the research problem and PICOT question
  2. Select the resources to be searched
  3. Set inclusion and exclusion criteria
  4. Build the search strategy and conduct the search
  5. Screen and organize the initial list
  6. Retrieve full text and summarize applicable articles
  7. Critically review the study quality and findings
  8. Summarize and synthesize the findings

Reference: Houser, J. (2023). Nursing research: Reading, using, and creating evidence (5th ed.). Jones & Bartlett.

Section 5: Explore: Step 1: The Problem and Question

Although most nurses have identified a problem and written the PICOT question before starting the literature search, how the question is stated determines how their search will progress. The search terms are derived from the research question based on the population of interest, intervention being evaluated, comparison intervention/control, the outcome being measured, and time differentiating the data being sought from related findings.

Review this example PICOT question, "In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?"

Transcript (PICOT Example):

  • P (population) – In post-menopausal women
  • I (intervention) – does hormone replacement therapy
  • C (comparison) – compared to no hormone replacement therapy
  • O (outcome) – impact the incidence of breast cancer?
  • T (time) – [no time component]

Section 6: Explore: Step 2: Select Resources

The resources used for the literature search vary from a single research article with a lengthy reference list to online databases with millions of published research findings. The bibliographic database indexes published works, making them readily searchable by keywords, subject, names of authors or journals, and a variety of other categories.

Nurse researchers and clinicians focus their searches on databases that contain nursing, medical, and allied health resources in their collections. One such database is CINAHL (Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature). CINAHL's content includes numerous active, indexed, peer-reviewed, and abstracted journals that can be searched in a matter of moments. There is a collection of open access (OA) journals as well.

Additional databases with research findings relevant to nursing include:

  • MEDLINE, a free biomedical database available courtesy of the National Library of Medicine
  • Cochrane Library, which houses systematic review and clinical trial information
  • ProQuest, with a variety of resources focused on health and medicine, science and technology, and the social sciences
  • Latin America & Iberia Database houses academic journals locally published in Latin American countries
  • Google Scholar, an academic database curated by Google

Note: Limiting the number of databases searched can result in publication bias resulting in loss of findings, such as negative results, that influence how the literature review impacts the research study or nursing practice.

To search the Chamberlain University library databases, visit the library homepage and look for Database Shortcuts, which includes a link to the full database holdings.

Section 7: Explore: Step 3: Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

To get the most useful studies, the researcher must consider what should be included or excluded from the search. While all criteria will have inclusion terms, not all will have exclusion terms.

Common criteria include:

  • Study design
  • Year of publication
  • Population of interest
  • Interventions
  • Outcomes

Inclusion criteria should be as specific as possible. If the researcher wants to know the effects of hormone replacement therapy on the increased incidence of breast cancer, omitting the word "increased" will impact the search.

With the PICOT question, "In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer," the criteria might look like this:

Inclusion CriteriaExclusion Criteria
Study designQuantitativeQualitative
Year of publicationWithin the last five yearsNone
Population of interestPost-menopausal womenNone
InterventionsHormone replacement therapy (HRT)No HRT, mastectomy, lumpectomy
OutcomesIncreased incidence of breast cancerNone

Section 9: Explore: Step 4a: Search Strategy

Before entering a search term, the nurse should create a search plan or strategy. The time spent creating a search strategy will improve the effectiveness of the literature search by determining the major concepts of the PICOT question, which terms will be searched for each concept, how the terms will be combined, and which limits will be set. See how a research question becomes a search strategy below.

Question: "In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?"

Transcript (PICOT Example):

  • P (population) – In post-menopausal women
  • I (intervention) – does hormone replacement therapy
  • C (comparison) – compared to no hormone replacement therapy
  • O (outcome) – impact the incidence of breast cancer?
  • T (time) – [no time component]
Tab: Search Concepts

Search concepts are the major themes or focuses of the research question. Typically, these broad terms will yield many results when searched and many may not align with your interest. The concepts are often the exact words and phrases used in the PICOT question.

Literature Search Strategy Template

Question: In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?

Concepts: post-menopausal women | hormone replacement therapy | breast cancer

Tab: Search Terms

Search terms are the exact words or phrases used when searching. Terms may include variables in the research question, characteristics of the target population, or the theoretical framework of the research problem. Searching for similar terms helps avoid missing research studies that address the research question using slightly different terms.

Literature Search Strategy Template

Question: In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?

Concepts: post-menopausal women | hormone replacement therapy | breast cancer

Terms: post-menopausal women / older women | hormone replacement therapy / HRT | breast cancer / cancer

Tab: Connectors

Boolean operators, such as "AND," "OR," and "NOT," are considered connector words and will make the database search for your keywords in a specific way. Entering them in all capital letters lets the database know they are connectors, not search terms.

Boolean operators impact the search in the following ways:

  • AND will reduce your results and only show sources that contain both keywords it connects.
  • OR will expand your results and show sources that have either keyword or both keywords.
  • NOT will reduce your results by excluding sources that contain the keyword to its right.

Example: Adding the connector "AND" in between the keywords picked out from your assignment will make the database search for sources that contain both keywords. Common mistakes with Boolean operators include:

Placing the Connecting Word in the Wrong Place:

  • Correct: post-menopausal women AND hormone replacement therapy AND breast cancer
  • Incorrect: post-menopausal women OR hormone replacement therapy AND breast cancer

Using Lower Case:

  • Correct: post-menopausal women AND hormone replacement therapy AND breast cancer
  • Incorrect: post-menopausal women and hormone replacement therapy and breast cancer

Literature Search Strategy Template

Question: In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?

Concepts: post-menopausal women | hormone replacement therapy | breast cancer

Terms: post-menopausal women / older women | hormone replacement therapy / HRT | breast cancer / cancer

Connectors: post-menopausal women AND hormone replacement therapy AND breast cancer

Section 11: Explore: Step 4b: Conduct the Search

Most databases provide filters and symbols that help narrow down or change the search altogether without changing the search terms and connectors. It is best to apply no more than one to two limiters initially. If the search yields too many articles, limiters can be adjusted.

Tab: Search Filters

Search filters limit the search results based on parameters set by the researcher and by skipping articles that will not be useful.

  • Date Range – limits the search to findings published within the set dates. Used to locate the most current research, typically within the last five years.
  • Language – limits the search findings to those published in the language specified.
  • Peer-Review – qualified professionals have evaluated these articles to determine the accuracy of the information and the methods used. Peer reviews increase the credibility of the research article.
  • Full text only – setting the search to only articles that are available in full text only may limit the return and introduce bias to the review.
Tab: Quotation Marks

Adding quotation marks to a phrase of two or more words lets the database search for those words together. For example, searching for "post-menopausal women" will prevent articles focused on all women from being included in the results.

Tab: Parentheses

Grouping similar search terms within parentheses instructs the database to search for them as a single topic. For example, grouping (hormone replacement therapy OR HRT) tells the database to look for either term.

Tab: Truncation

Truncation is used to shorten a search term with an asterisk () allowing the database to locate all word forms and is useful when search terms include different forms of the same word. For example, using the search term children may exclude relevant articles. By truncating it to child, the database will search for child, children, childhood, and other words that start with child.

LanguageEnglish
Peer reviewedYes, or checked
Quotation marks"post-menopausal women"; "hormone replacement therapy"
Parentheses("hormone replacement therapy" OR HRT)
TruncationNone

Section 14: Explore: Step 5: Screen and Organize

The first search usually returns numerous results, many of which are not related to the research question. To avoid downloading and reading off-topic articles, use the inclusion and exclusion criteria set earlier in the planning process.

Inclusion CriteriaExclusion Criteria
Study designQuantitativeQualitative
Year of publicationWithin the last five yearsNone
Population of interestPost-menopausal womenNone
InterventionsHormone replacement therapy (HRT)No HRT, mastectomy, lumpectomy
OutcomesIncreased incidence of breast cancerNone

To refine the number of results after the first search, consider changing one or two limiters slightly by changing the range of dates or marking that only peer-reviewed publications should be used. Changing more than one or two limiters at a time may result in a drastic reduction of relevant articles.

Once the number of studies is manageable, save the search if that option is available. Then read the title of the article, looking for the inclusion and exclusion criteria. If the title is not helpful, read the abstract or skim the article. Finally, delete articles with exclusions from the search results list.

Section 15: Explore: Step 6: Retrieve and Summarize

The searcher should use full-text versions of articles for the literature review and most articles in the Chamberlain University Library are available as full-text. However, for relevant articles not available as full-text, the searcher can request an interlibrary loan. This request is as simple as completing a form so the librarian can get access to the article for you to review.

Organizing and summarizing the texts in the literature review is like taking notes during a lecture, allowing the researcher to have essential information available in a format that can be sorted and referenced. For example, many researchers include the following details in summaries:

  • Full APA citation (for creating the reference list)
  • Purpose of the study
  • Research design
  • Sample size
  • Methodology and measurements
  • Treatment or treatments
  • Results
  • Findings
  • Discussion
  • Implications for practice

Section 17: Explore: Step 7: Critically Review

Once the relevant articles are identified and obtained, the next step is to evaluate the author's quality of design and methodology. The researcher should note the answers to these questions and other information gathered during the evaluation of that article.

Transcript: All articles should be evaluated using the same criteria, often in the form of questions, like:

  • Is the study relevant?
  • How do the findings increase the body of knowledge?
  • Is bias present?
  • Is the conclusion aligned with the results?
  • How does this study contribute to client care?

Section 19: Explore: Step 8: Summarize and Synthesize

The final step of the literature review is to write a summary that presents the studies' key findings logically. Rather than reviewing each study, the researcher should organize the information to show the relationships or differences between the articles. The summary should show the significance of the reviewed research and link it to the research question, setting the stage for further study or implementation into practice.

At this time, the researcher is ready to design their study and the clinician is ready to disseminate their findings to stakeholders in the organization.

Sources and Levels of Evidence

Section 1: Introduction to Sources and Levels of Evidence

Research produces evidence. It is essential to understand the strength of that evidence to decide the quality of research and the need for further study. By understanding, grading, and identifying strong sources of evidence, the nurse can make better decisions about the quality of research and the need for more answers.

After completing this module, you will be able to:

  • Describe different sources of evidence.
  • Determine the strength of evidence.
  • Apply a grade or strength to examples of evidence.

Section 3: Explore: Evidence

Evidence is a set of available facts or information that supports the answer to a question. The entire purpose of research is to produce evidence. Once enough evidence exists to answer a research question, researchers can be confident that the question is answered. However, in answering the question, other questions often arise which require additional research.

Not all evidence is equal. While some forms of evidence can answer a question, others leave doubts. This uncertainty is considered the strength of the evidence. There are two ways to ensure enough evidence exists. The first is to produce more evidence. The second is to grade the evidence.

Identifying the levels of evidence involves creating a ranking system that can identify the strength of evidence and better identify how likely the evidence answers the research question.

Section 5: Explore: Evidence Strength

The strength of evidence can indicate the accuracy of the research question. The strength of evidence comes from the source used to produce that evidence. It is important to get a large and/or strong amount of evidence before deciding to implement research in a healthcare practice environment.

There are various ways of deciding the level or strength of evidence. Some use scoring and grading techniques to determine the strength of research. This divides the different types of evidence and ranks them based on their factual accuracy.

Just because the evidence is weak or the source is questionable does not mean that the research is invalid. It does mean that more information may be needed to better support the research findings. The risks and benefits of the facts may determine whether the evidence is useful in certain situations.

Section 7: Explore: Sources and Uses of Evidence

We use evidence in many different aspects of nursing. There are many tools used for patient care management that are based on evidence. Tools like the Braden Rating Scale or the Hamilton Depression Scale are based on different forms of evidence. Other tools, including critical pathways, care maps, or protocols are often based on evidence.

Transcript:

Nursing Process: Within the nursing process we use evidence when assessing a client condition, diagnosing a client problem, and planning a client intervention. This is evidence that we will usually collect but may also be research we use when planning or intervening.

Public Policy: We use evidence in public policy to guide law changes, establish policies and procedures for care, or to determine the cost and feasibility of implementation of evidence-based care.

Section 8: Explore: Grading Evidence

In determining strength, a list of evidence can be created and categorized from the strongest to the weakest form.

Aggregated (pulled together) evidence is among the strongest, using a specific process of criteria for selecting a combination of research. Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and clinical practice guidelines are all forms of aggregated evidence analysis. These are usually comprehensive and will grade the quality of the individual evidence used as part of the aggregate.

Individual research that specifically studies one question or item can be strong or weak depending on the study design and variables used. Because these studies are not replicated if used alone, they are weaker than the aggregated evidence.

Even with the individual research, some studies are considered stronger (randomized clinical trials) than others (case-controlled studies).

Transcript — Grading Evidence (strongest to weakest):

  1. Clinical practice guidelines
  2. Meta-analyses
  3. Systematic reviews
  4. Randomized clinical trials
  5. Cohort studies
  6. Outcome research
  7. Case-controlled studies
  8. Expert opinion

Section 10: Explore: Aggregated Evidence

Here are some forms of aggregated evidence.

Tab: Clinical Practice Guidelines

This is a process of evidence collection and analysis that uses many different sources to summarize and create a guide for the practicing clinician. Usually, evidence is graded based on the quality, potential risks, study weaknesses, cost, and barriers to implementation. This is the most comprehensive quality and highest strength evidence.

Tab: Meta-Analyses

This is a process of collection and analysis of evidence related to a specific question or problem. Statistical methods are used to summarize the results of the collected evidence. It is a very strong form of evidence.

Tab: Systematic Reviews

This answers a specific research question by collecting and summarizing all evidence available that fits specific criteria. While this is like a meta-analysis, it does not use statistical methods to come to a conclusion.

Section 11: Explore: Non-Aggregated Evidence

Here are some forms of non-aggregated evidence.

Transcript:

Randomized Clinical Trials: This is a study design that randomizes treatment and control groups to remove any type of bias in obtaining results. This type of research uses statistical methods to measure results. It is a strong form of research, but is limited to study design, sample size, and statistical methods used. To be stronger evidence, this study needs replication.

Cohort Studies: These studies follow participants across a point in time. They may measure a specific group or demographic and measure risk factors or other characteristics to match outcome. Because there is less control over variables, samples, and other criteria, this evidence is not as strong as other types of evidence.

Outcome Research: This measures the results of a specific population. There may be minimal control over treatment groups, and this may measure data after the fact. It cannot control for environmental factors that would skew the results.

Case Controlled Studies: This is an observational study that will look at two groups; one with, and one without a disease or condition. There is no control over environment or variables, and often this is done retrospectively. This is a lower grade of evidence because of all the variables and challenges in completing it.

Expert Opinion: Clinicians have experience, and they frequently will maintain their practice level. Expert opinion occurs within research when several people that specialize in the same topic or problem, as a community, decides an intervention to solve a problem. This is the weakest form of evidence.

Section 13: Explore: Source Credibility

Several things can make a source credible. Here are a few examples:

  • Source authors have strong clinical and/or educational credentials in the field of study.
  • Source authors do not have a conflict of interest (personal or financial) with the study or the way the study is designed.
  • The source is peer-reviewed.
  • The publisher has no positive or negative financial connection to the outcome of the study.
  • The source has a reasonable publication timeframe (reasonable time from date of study to date of publication).

Section 15: Explore: Levels of Evidence

When research is collected for practice guidelines, a grading process is used to better categorize the strength of research. This is usually a four to five level rating scale that identifies the source type and the strength of evidence. This helps quantify for the reader how strong the research may be to help determine its translation to practice.

Good research should offer strong evidence. Most researchers will not accept popular media or social media as evidence except under specific conditions. For example:

Frieda is a verified user on Twitter who identifies herself as a registered nurse working at a Baywood hospital. She states in a tweet that the intensive care unit (ICU) at the hospital is at 70% capacity with influenza pneumonia patients. She could be considered a primary source of this statistic. Though this may be considered "level 5" on the chart below, the source needs significant verification before accepting her as a valid source.

Most of the time, nothing should be accepted as an appropriate source through social media unless it is linked to an appropriate website and is verifiable. Social media have a high risk of disinformation; therefore, they are not a credible source.

The PICOT Question

Section 1: Introduction to the PICOT Question

The research question guides research design and methodology. Therefore, carefully developing a research question is essential to constructing the rest of the research process.

The PICOT model helps the researcher develop a specific clinical question. PICOT stands for:

  • Population/Patient Problem
  • Intervention
  • Comparison
  • Outcome
  • Time

This lesson will teach you how to formulate a research question to be used in evidence-based practice.

Section 3: Explore: Research Questions: Where Do I Start?

Establishing a well-written research question is the most essential step in the research process. How the question is stated determines which measures will be used, which data will be collected, which analysis is appropriate, and which conclusions may be drawn.

Transcript (presentation, 5 slides):

  1. Answer the why question first. When the researcher understands the reason for the study, the specifics of the question are apparent.
  2. Review the literature. Replication of previous studies is essential. Do not finalize the research question without an extensive literature review.
  3. Write, refine, and refine again. Write the research question. Then think about it and refine it. Ponder it some more, then refine it again. The effort spent to get the question right is worth the time.
  4. Do not wait for perfection. The research question is a work in progress. As the design of the study becomes clear, so will the exact research question.
  5. Keep the research question focused. Do not include more than one major concept or outcome per question. Multiple questions should be used instead of multiple parts to a single question.

Section 5: Explore: Elements of a Research Question

A research question differs from a purpose statement in that it is stated as a question and gives specificity to what will be measured in the study.

The acronym PICOT outlines the elements of a good quantitative research question.

Sample PICOT Question: In adult surgical clients in the operating room (population), does the pre-incision use of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) (intervention) compared to the use of povidone-iodine (comparison) impact surgical site infections (outcome) in 30 days (time)?

Population (P), Intervention (I), and Outcome (O) are always present. Comparison (C) and Time (T) may or may not be present, depending on the question.

Example of an exploratory study without a Comparison (C) and Time (T): For terminally ill cancer patients (population), does massage therapy at bedtime (intervention) impact quality of sleep (outcome)?

Transcript (interactive hotspots for the sample PICOT question):

P: Population

  • Who is the client?
  • What is the problem?
  • Consider age, sex, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, etc.
  • In adult surgical clients in the operating room

I: Intervention

  • What do you want to do for the client (treat, diagnose, observe)?
  • What is the client exposed to?
  • Therapeutic measure, medication, diagnostic test, etc.
  • does the pre-incision use of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG)

C: Comparison

  • Is there a standard or alternative intervention to compare with the planned intervention?
  • Another intervention, placebo, standard of care, diagnostic gold standard.
  • A comparison is not always present.
  • compared to the use of povidone-iodine

O: Outcome

  • What do you hope to accomplish, measure, improve, or affect?
  • The desired outcome should be measurable.
  • impact surgical site infections

T: Time

  • What is the time period?
  • The specific time period must be relevant to the question. Note the period over which the population is observed, or the outcome is measured.
  • Many questions do not include a time.
  • in 30 days

Section 6: Explore: PICOT Question Example

As another example: In post-menopausal women (population), does hormone replacement therapy (intervention) compared to no hormone replacement therapy (comparison) impact the incidence of breast cancer (outcome)?

Transcript (PICOT Example):

  • Population – In post-menopausal women...
  • Intervention – ...does hormone replacement therapy...
  • Comparison – ...compared to no hormone replacement therapy...
  • Outcome – ...impact the incidence of breast cancer?
  • Time – [No time component]

Section 7: Explore: Qualitative Research Questions

A qualitative research question is less prescriptive and more general. It outlines the phenomena to be studied and who can best inform the inquiry. Measurements, interventions, and comparison groups are irrelevant in qualitative research, so they are not a part of the question.

Examples of qualitative research questions:

  • What are the perceptions of pregnant adolescents (population) regarding accessing prenatal care (outcome)?
  • How do pregnant women newly diagnosed with diabetes (population) perceive reporting blood glucose levels (outcome) to their healthcare providers during pregnancy?

Section 8: Explore: PICOT and Purpose Statements

You will notice that the elements of the PICOT question are very similar to those in the purpose statement. However, many research articles will not explicitly state the PICOT question. When the research question is not specifically identified, the reader will need to infer the elements from the study design, methods, and sampling.

Let's review a research study conducted by de Avila Meinberg et al. (2012).

The purpose statement reads, "The aim of the present study was to evaluate the effects of an oral hygiene protocol (consisting of 2% chlorhexidine and manual brushing) in a heterogeneous population of ICU patients on the VAP incidence, duration of mechanical ventilation, and lengths of the patients' ICU and hospital stays" (de Avila Meinberg et al., 2012, para. 4).

Inline practice question (drag-and-drop PICOT builder): "In (population), does the use of (intervention) compared to (comparison) impact the rate of (outcome)?" — build the PICOT question from this purpose statement.

Answer: In ICU patients (population), does the use of 2% chlorhexidine with toothbrushing (intervention) compared to placebo gel with toothbrushing (comparison) impact the rate of ventilator-associated pneumonia (outcome)?

Reference: de Avila Meinberg, M. C., de Fatima Meinberg Cheade, M., Miranda, A. L., Fachini, M. M., & Lobo, S. M. (2012). The use of 2% chlorhexidine gel and toothbrushing for oral hygiene of patients receiving mechanical ventilation: Effects on ventilator-associated pneumonia. Clinical Research, 24(4). https://doi.org/10.1590/S

Section 12: Explore: Research Question Appraisal

Now that you have learned the elements of a PICOT question, it is important to determine a good research question from one that lacks desirable characteristics. Unfortunately, creating questions and finding answers is seldom straightforward.

The acronym FINER provides a guide for evaluating the desirable characteristics of a research question. Researchers use this framework to consider the philosophical, logical, and scientific implications of writing research questions.

Transcript — FINER:

  • Feasibility: Are there enough participants to power the study? Are the participants needed available? Is the scope narrow enough for the study?
  • Interesting: Is the question interesting?
  • Novel: Does the study confirm or refute previous findings or provide new findings?
  • Ethical: Does the study cause unacceptable risk to participants? Does it invade privacy?
  • Relevant: Is the question relevant to scientific knowledge, clinical and health policy, or future research directions?

Section 14: Explore: Research Question and Research Design

A focused research question guides how that question will be answered. This is the research design.

Transcript — Research Question and Research Design:

  • Who is the client population? → Sampling Strategy
  • What treatment is being tested? → Intervention Protocol
  • How will the effect be demonstrated? → Outcomes Measured

Section 15: Explore: From Research Question to Hypothesis

The research question guides the design of the study and the hypothesis suggests the statistical analysis. A research hypothesis is a specific statement that predicts the direction and nature of the results of a study. The way it is written determines which statistical tests are used and which outcome is expected.

Question: What is the relationship between nurse burnout and healthcare-associated infections?

Hypothesis: There is no relationship between nurse burnout and healthcare-associated infections.

Although stating that no relationship exists may seem counterintuitive, it is the only way to measure statistical significance. The researcher cannot be certain that a relationship exists but can calculate the probability that it does not.

Tab: Elements of a Good Hypothesis
  • Statement of an expected relationship (or lack of relationship)
  • Identified direction of interest
  • Includes the population, variables, and comparison
  • A null hypothesis states that there is no difference between groups as a result of receiving the treatment or not receiving the treatment.
  • An alternative hypothesis specifies an expected difference between treatment groups. There are two types of alternative hypotheses:
    • A nondirectional hypothesis is one in which the researcher is interested in a change in any direction.
    • A directional hypothesis is one in which the researcher is interested in only one direction of change.

Worked example:

Question: In adult surgical clients in the operating room (population), does the pre-incision use of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) (intervention) compared to the use of povidone-iodine (comparison) impact surgical site infections (outcome) in 30 days (time)?

Null Hypothesis: In adult surgical clients in the operating room, there is no difference in the rate of surgical site infections with the use of CHG as compared to the use of povidone-iodine.

Directional Alternative Hypothesis: In adult surgical clients in the operating room, there is a reduction in the rate of surgical site infections with the use of CHG as compared to the use of povidone-iodine.

The Research Process

Section 1: Introduction to the Research Process

Research involves the search for truth. The goal of the research process is to produce knowledge that is relevant and applicable to the body of nursing knowledge and that becomes evidence for practice. However, there are multiple ways to determine and describe the truth. Therefore, the researcher must carefully consider the research question, design, and philosophical foundation before moving forward.

This lesson will describe the research process, introduce you to two paradigms of nursing research (quantitative and qualitative design), and describe research classifications. Lastly, you will begin to learn how to read and critique research studies.

Section 3: Explore: Nursing Knowledge: Ways of Knowing

The nursing profession is defined by the knowledge possessed and sought. Carper (1978) identified four patterns of knowing that form the structure of nursing knowledge: personal, empirical, ethical, and aesthetic. The practice of nursing is a holistic, human experience.

The ways of knowing allow nurses to develop a deep personal understanding of themselves and the practice of nursing.

Transcript (slide presentation):

Personal Knowing refers to knowledge obtained through observation, reflection, and experience. This knowledge of ourselves enables us to establish authentic, therapeutic relationships with others (Brugger & Madison, 2015).

Empirical Knowing is knowledge gained through research and objective facts. This knowledge is used through the employment of evidence-based nursing practice and is often called the "science" of nursing (Brugger & Madison, 2015).

Ethical Knowing is the sense of right and wrong that we use to develop our moral values. In the nursing profession, personal ethics are based on the obligation to protect and respect human life (Brugger & Madison, 2015). Nurses are guided in developing professional morals by the American Nurses Association's Code of Ethics for Nurses (2015).

Aesthetic Knowing is how we characterize the moment when we uncover something new, such as perspective. This "aha" moment is often called the "art" of nursing.

References:

  • American Nurses Association. (2015). Code of ethics for nurses with interpretive statements. American Nurses Association.
  • Brugger, S. B., & Madison, H. E. (2015). Nursing knowledge: The ways of knowing. https://www.excelsior.edu/article/nursing-the-ways-of-knowing/
  • Carper, B. A. (1978). Fundamental patterns of knowing in nursing. Advances in Nursing Science, 1(1), 13-24. https://journals.lww.com/advancesinnursingscience/Citation/1978/10000/Fundamental_Patterns_of_Knowing_in_Nursing.4.aspx

Section 4: Explore: Phases of the Research Process

Research is systematic and rigorous, meaning that decisions are carefully made that form the foundation of the study. However, some characteristics are universal in all research studies, which make up the phases of the study.

Though this process may appear as steps, with the end of one phase leading directly to the next, research is a fluid process. Several things happen within the same time frame, such as searching for a theoretical framework, the literature review, and the development of the research question. Often the researcher will move on to design the study, but occasionally, new literature will be published that requires the research question to be redeveloped. This question revision is an example of when phases may be conducted out of sequence, or the researcher may move back and forth between steps.

Due to the variety of research, the process must be used as a general guide adapted to each unique situation.

Transcript (phases diagram):

  1. Define Problem
    • Reflect on practice
    • Identify a gap in knowledge
  2. Scan Literature
    • Review literature for basic knowledge, relevant evidence, and theoretical framework
  3. Determine Design
    • Design should work with philosophical assumptions, nature of question, intent of researcher, and time dimension
  4. Sampling Strategy
    • How subjects will be recruited and assigned to groups
    • How many subjects are needed
  5. Collect Data
    • Use reliable and valid methods
  6. Analyze Data
    • Use techniques appropriate for type of data
  7. Communicate Findings
    • Disseminate to appropriate audiences
  8. Support Practice
    • Link research to nursing practice guidelines

Once the process is complete, we must communicate our findings. Whether with the interprofessional team to enhance client care or with the medical community to improve quality outcomes, communication is the final step in both the nursing and research process.

Section 7: Explore: Research Paradigms

A paradigm is an overall belief system that strives to make sense of reality and is the basis of knowledge. Two broad research paradigms reflect research methods: qualitative and quantitative studies.

Both types of research have similar characteristics, including:

  • Disciplined, rigorous approach based on external evidence
  • Methods that require samples and the cooperation of individuals
  • Focus on the rights of human subjects and ethical guidelines
  • Ultimate aim of discovering new knowledge used to improve nursing practice
Tab: Quantitative Research

Quantitative research is the traditional approach to scientific research that assumes an objective reality and that events are not random but linked to an associated cause. It involves identifying the variables that represent characteristics of interest and then measuring them in a reliable, valid way.

The phases of the research process are tightly controlled, from the way subjects are selected to the study design to eliminate bias. Statistical analysis is used to establish confidence in the results and rule out the effects of random error.

The study of cause and effect in nursing is essential to evidence-based practice. Therefore, quantitative research is helpful to answer questions about how nursing actions can influence outcomes.

Tab: Qualitative Research

Qualitative research is a naturalist approach to inquiry that represents a view of reality constructed by the individual. Reality is not fixed but exists based on participant beliefs, resulting in multiple views of reality. Thus, the researcher develops a relationship with the subject and bias is inherent.

Qualitative methods focus on understanding the meaning of an experience from an individual's perspective. Data is collected through observations, interviews, case studies, or social interactions. Data is analyzed based on verbal descriptions and observable behaviors from which themes are identified.

Examples of qualitative research include perceptions of quality of life, the meaning of a life event, and the willingness to endure side effects for a therapeutic result. The goal is to incorporate client preferences and values as a guide for nursing practice.

Section 9: Explore: Mixed Methods Research

Mixed methods research is when researchers collect and analyze both quantitative and qualitative data within the same study. By drawing upon the strengths of both types of research, the researcher can explore diverse perspectives.

The quantitative data enables the researcher to identify and measure relationships, while the qualitative data provides a deeper understanding of human experience. Both are essential to support quality client care.

Examples of studies using mixed methods:

  • Describe the rate of hand washing on a nursing unit (quantitative) and explore the nurses' perceptions of the importance of hand washing (qualitative).
  • Measure the bacteria on hands after washing (quantitative) and observe hand washing technique (qualitative).

Study example: mixed methods study

Section 10: Explore: Classifications of Research: Intent of the Researcher

Research is classified in several different ways. The first type of classification is based on the intent of the researcher.

Both basic and applied research may be conducted using quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods.

Provide new knowledge for the foundation of nursing (basic research). Basic research is commonly called theoretical, pure, fundamental, or bench research. This type of research builds knowledge that forms the foundation for practice but does not directly apply to the practice setting.

  • Example: Studying the effects of circulatory volume on neonatal cardiac function.

Provide knowledge that practitioners can immediately apply to the practice of nursing (applied research). Applied research has a single goal of improving nursing practice. The findings are intended to contribute to the modification of nursing practice.

  • Example: Determining the efficacy of a specific counseling technique after the death of a spouse.

Section 11: Explore: Classifications of Research: Nature of the Design

The nature of the design can also classify research.

Experimental research refers to studies of cause and effect, which usually explore the effectiveness of an intervention. Participants are chosen and assigned to groups randomly, then the researcher manipulates a treatment and compares the outcomes to a group that received no treatment. If the outcomes are different, researchers can assume that the difference is due to the treatment, since all other variables have been controlled.

Quasi-experimental research is like an experimental design, but the selection and assignment of subjects are not randomized. Instead, convenience samples are often used.

  • Example: The population studied is residents in two nursing homes. One group will receive treatment and the other group will not receive treatment. However, the subjects are not randomly assigned to the nursing homes and they already reside in one of the two homes, creating a convenience sample.

Nonexperimental research cannot test cause and effect.

  • Descriptive research involves the study of a particular situation or event that already exists. No variables are manipulated.
  • Correlation research focuses on existing relationships between variables.
    • Example: Do teenagers with mothers who had a teenage pregnancy have a higher adolescent pregnancy rate themselves?
  • Predictive research searches for relationships in which the values of one variable can be used to predict the values of another. For example, predictive research involves determining whether a risk factor will lead to a particular health condition.
    • Example: Do specific family characteristics predict the risk of adolescent pregnancy?

Section 12: Explore: Classifications of Research: Time Dimension

A final way that research can be classified is by the time dimension chosen for the study.

Tab: Retrospective

Retrospective studies focus on the past because the data used has already been collected about events that have already happened. This type of data is termed secondary.

  • Example: Using medical record data to determine differences in the rate of ventilator-associated pneumonia between clients who received oral care every 4 hours and those who did not.
Tab: Prospective

Prospective studies focus on the future, with data being collected by the researcher.

  • Example: A study involving oral care and ventilator-associated pneumonia by experimenting with different time periods, methods, and durations of oral care.

Prospective studies are more reliable since the researcher has greater control over the data collected. However, when using retrospective data, the researcher must rely on the accuracy and completeness of the originally collected data.

Tab: Longitudinal

Longitudinal studies are conducted over time, usually over very long periods, typically to study the emergence of disease or the long-term effects of treatments. Data collection occurs at prescribed intervals during the study. A significant disadvantage of longitudinal studies is subject attrition.

  • Example: Monitoring the children of smokers over time to measure the emergence of pulmonary disease.
Tab: Cross-sectional

Cross-sectional studies collect data at a single point in time, often across multiple populations, to identify the existence of a phenomenon in the present, not on how it will look in the future. Though able to be completed quickly, the effects of time are not analyzed, leading to the primary disadvantage of this type of study.

  • Example: Determining the distribution of pulmonary disease in children who have a caregiver who is a smoker in the home at a given point in time.

Section 16: Explore: Reading Research for Evidence-Based Practice

The ability to read and interpret research is an essential task for nurses. The American Nurses Association Code of Ethics (2015) states in Provision 7 that "all nurses must participate in the advancement of the profession through knowledge development, evaluation, dissemination, and application to practice" (p. 27).

By engaging in scholarly inquiry and reading or participating in research, nurses expand the body of knowledge that advances the theory and practice of nursing. However, reading and interpreting a research study can be challenging. Learning to critique research studies is a foundational task necessary to translate evidence into practice (Kaplan, 2012).

Before deciding to read a research article, determine if the research report addresses a question of interest or can be used in your nursing practice. Then begin to answer the questions listed below (adapted from Kaplan, 2012):

Tab: Does the title of the article accurately describe the research?

An informative title conveys key concepts and perhaps provides clues to the methods used or variables explored.

Tab: Is the abstract representative of the article?

Start with reading the abstract, which provides a brief overview of the study's purpose, research question, methods, results, and conclusions.

Tab: Does the article introduction make the purpose of the study clear?

A good introduction provides the basis for the article. It should include a statement of the problem, the rationale for the study, and the research question.

Tab: Is the literature review relevant to the study and comprehensive? Does it include current research?

The literature review provides the context for the research study. It establishes what is currently known about the problem and what gaps in knowledge may exist.

Tab: Does the methods section explain how researchers addressed the research question?

The methods section tells you how the study was conducted and should provide enough detail to allow the study to be replicated. Use this information to identify the independent and dependent variables. The researcher should describe how subjects were recruited and selected for participation. Additionally, any data collection instruments should be described, including how reliability and validity were accounted for during data collection.

Tab: Is data analysis consistent with the study question and research design?

Data analysis must relate to the research design. For example, a quantitative research study may use descriptive statistics to summarize data and other tests, such as t-tests or regression analysis, to compare or evaluate data. A qualitative study may use coding or content analysis to interpret data.

Tab: Are the results presented clearly in the article?

Results should be presented in a manner that is easy to read, such as tables or figures, then summarized in the text.

Tab: Are the limitations presented and their implications discussed?

Researchers must state the limitations of the study. These factors explain why the results may not be relevant in all situations, may only be generalized to specific situations, or may be less robust than anticipated. Examples of limitations include low survey response rates or having a high attrition rate during a longitudinal study.

Tab: Does the discussion explain the results in relation to the research question and study significance?

The discussion provides an opportunity to link study results back to the original research question, theoretical framework, and literature review. The results should be interpreted and explained. A discussion is also a place for the researcher to make recommendations for nursing practice and future research. The reader must determine if the recommendations are supported by the data or seem to be the researcher's opinions.

Tab: After reading the article, how can you use this research to improve nursing practice?

Consider what you have learned from the article. Then, if the information is relevant to your practice, determine how you can implement the findings into client care at your institution.
