Section 1: Introduction to Research Design

Understanding research design is an important step for the nurse researcher. The research design is a framework for the research study. The research design is selected to produce data that answers a credible research question.

Section 3: Explore: Design Selection

Nurse researchers should have a well-thought-out research question before determining the study design. A research question is matched with a specific design. Review the factors that influence design selection.

Interactive Transcript: Factors influencing design selection

Variables: Protecting participants is an important ethical role of the nurse researcher. Manipulating the research environment or putting research participants in danger are reasons not to implement a research design.

Sampling and measurements: Is the sampling instrument valid and reliable? Is the population available during the research time frame?

Researcher: What are the strengths and weaknesses of the researcher which may produce limitations for the study?

Time frame: The researcher must be aware of the length of time they require from start to finish of the research project.

Amount of control: Studies such as vaccine trials will need high levels of structure to control threats to validity.

Audience: The audience of the research results may expect specific types of research. It is important to understand the audience when choosing research designs.

Resources: What resources are available to the researcher? This can range from money, use of a copier for research surveys, and even participant pools for the researcher to utilize as participants.

Section 4: Explore: Design Decisions

All nurse researchers should understand the decisions that go into choosing a research design. There are always tradeoffs as no research design is without flaws. The nurse researcher must understand the flaws of each type of design and the impact on research credibility. The nurse should ensure that the chosen design is rigorous and systematic.

Infographic Transcript: The Three Phases of Decision Making
  1. Identify assumptions: What assumptions are made about the knowledge to be gained from the study? This will help the researcher understand if a quantitative, qualitative, or a mixed-method design is required.
  2. Select a design: Which design is appropriate for the purpose of the study?
  3. Implementation: The researcher must develop detailed plans of how they will implement the study.

Section 5: Explore: Design Approach

When a researcher examines the type of research design to use, they must understand how the design type will fit their research question. This is often based on the type of data and measurement they are looking to analyze.

Tab: Quantitative Design

A quantitative design measures data and analyzes numbers related to the research question. For example, the researcher may seek to measure the blood pressure of clients who have had a myocardial infarction (MI) and completed cardiac rehabilitation versus clients who have had a MI and not done any rehabilitation.

Tab: Qualitative Design

A qualitative design seeks to understand the lived experiences of research participants or the meaning of a phenomenon. For example, the researcher may be seeking to understand the lived experiences of nursing students who also work 20 hours or more a week.

Tab: Mixed-Method Design

A mixed-method design is used when both qualitative and quantitative data are available. For example, the researcher may include the lived experiences of MI clients in the quantitative study. Adding qualitative data in addition to the quantitative data makes the design method a mixed-method design.

The aim of the study depends on whether the study is exploratory or confirmatory.

  • Exploratory studies are usually qualitative or mixed-method and are descriptive, used to learn more and describe a phenomenon.
  • Confirmatory studies are based on testing a relationship between variables and are used to determine the relationship between the variables or examine causality.

Section 6: Explore: Variables

The concept that will be studied must be measurable. This measurable characteristic is called a variable, and can be a characteristic, event, or response to a research question. Variables are not used in qualitative research, but are the basis of quantitative research. Review each type of variable below.

Tab: Descriptive Variables

Descriptive or measurable characteristics are not influenced by the study or researcher. Examples include:

  • Participant information, such as age or gender
  • Participant specific characteristics, such as height
  • Measurable characteristics, such as blood pressure
  • Attitudinal data, such as client satisfaction

For example, research that explores the influence of exposure to blue light on hours of sleep may not be influenced by descriptive variables, such as the gender of the participant.

Tab: Research Variables

Research variables are introduced into a study to measure an expected effect. They may be independent or dependent.

  • An independent variable is the cause in cause and effect. It is added to a study to measure an effect.
  • A dependent variable is the effect in cause and effect. It is the outcome.

An example of a design with independent and dependent variables is research that explores the influence of exposure to blue light on hours of sleep. The independent variable is the exposure to blue light and the dependent variable is the hours of sleep.

Tab: Extraneous Variables

Extraneous variables are not part of the planned research or experiment, but are factors that influence the outcome. These may confuse the interpretation of the research outcome.

For example, in research that explores the influence of exposure to blue light on hours of sleep, an extraneous variable could include blue light exposure outside of the research framework. This may not be part of the planned experiment and may influence outcomes of the research.

Section 7: Explore: Descriptive Study Designs

Nurse researchers should understand study designs and how they influence research. Review the following types of descriptive study designs to increase your understanding of the variety of design choices in nursing research.

Tab: Survey Design

Survey design uses a single point in time or a single sample characteristic. For example, when diagnosed with cancer, what do teen participants describe as their top information need? The single point in time is at diagnosis.

Tab: Cross-Sectional Design

Cross-sectional design, single point in time, sample characteristics differ on a key characteristic. For example, when diagnosed with cancer, what do participants (aged 5-50 years old) describe as their top information need? The key characteristic difference is the age of the participants. The single point in time is at diagnosis.

Tab: Longitudinal Study

Longitudinal study data is collected at select points over time to describe changes in characteristics or events. For example, a study of teen cancer clients at diagnosis, first treatment, and 6-month treatment to see what participants describe as their top information need.

Tab: Case Study

Case study is a collection of detailed information using varied techniques to explore a single individual, program, event, or action. For example, a case study using participants from the Chamberlain University Tinley Park campus nursing program graduating class to explore what they perceive as their priority needs after graduation.

Tab: Single Subject Design

Single subject design interventions are studied on individual's responses, either at baseline or ongoing. For example, a researcher does a repeated review of a small participant group's behavior over time regarding adherence to their blood pressure medication.

Tab: Phenomenology

Phenomenology is the lived experience of a phenomenon among participants who have experienced the phenomenon. For example, the researcher may use a phenomenological study of nursing students who work 20 hours or more a week to investigate the meaning of their lived experiences as nursing students.

Tab: Ethnography

Ethnography is a cultural study in which the researcher immerses themselves in the culture over a period of time. For example, a researcher may live within a tribe for years to understand their view of the environment.

Section 8: Explore: Relationship Study Designs

Review the following types of relationship study designs to increase your understanding of the variety of design choices in nursing research.

Tab: Correlation

A correlation is a relationship between two variables in a single population or one variable in two populations. For example, researching the relationship between maternal age and anxiety in mothers who participate in planned caesarean sections. The two variables are age and anxiety in a population of pregnant mothers with planned c-sections.

Tab: Predictive Study

A predictive study relationship is between a predictor variable and an outcome variable. For example, research on medication adherence related to the age of the participant. The predictor variable is the participant's age and the outcome variable is medication adherence.

Tab: Grounded Theory

Grounded theory is the development of a theory of process, action, or interaction based on analysis of participants' words. For example, nursing research that enables nurses to see patterns of health in groups, such as teen participants and medication adherence. This research helps guide practice in teaching teens about medication adherence.

Tab: Test of Model Fit

Test of model fit uses a preconceived model to test theories of causal relationships between variables. For example, a researcher may study whether the introduction of a support group affects the coping skills of teens diagnosed with anxiety.

Section 12: Explore: Causality

Causality study design aims to examine the effectiveness of nursing interventions. Causality must be established and quantified.

Tab: Causality Study Criteria

There are three criteria that must be considered in a causality study.

  • Temporality: The time sequence between the independent and dependent variables must support causation.
  • Influence: The statistical soundness of the effect that the independent variable has on the dependent variable.
  • Specificity: The elimination or control of other explanations for the relationship between the dependent and independent variables.
Tab: Research Designs Exploring Causality

Review the research designs that explore causality:

  • Experimental design: Introduces an intervention to the treatment group and compares the outcome to the group that has not had intervention (control group); subjects are randomly assigned to either group.
  • Quasi-experimental design: Introduces a treatment to a group. There is no random assignment or control group used.
  • Causal-comparative design/case control: Groups are matched by independent variables; nonexperimental study groups are selected by characteristics of interest and dependent variables are analyzed for differences.
  • Time series analysis: Measures baseline, implements treatment, collects data about outcome over time at specified dates or periods of time.

Section 14: Explore: Evaluating Evidence-Based Research Articles

The nurse researcher must be able to evaluate research articles to gain a better understanding of their research design and to see research that has been done already about their research question. The following should be considered when evaluating a research article.

Interactive Transcript

Identify the design and the standard language used to describe the design choice.

  • The research may describe the steps used within a phenomenological study of nursing students who work 20 hours or more a week to investigate the meaning of their lived experiences as nursing students. The reader would identify that a phenomenological descriptive design was used and clarify that standard research language was used in the study.

The rationale is provided for the design choice.

  • The reader would identify the rationale the researcher had in the design, which is to gain a better understanding of the lived experiences of nursing students who also work while in school.

The design selected can be clearly identified and linked to the research question.

  • The reader should be able to identify the design used and this design should be a phenomenological design to provide the best understanding of the nursing students' lived experiences.

Design variables are identified and defined.

  • Are the students also parents? Do they pay their own rent and bills? How old are the students? These are all variables that may influence outcomes.

The study can be replicated by others.

  • Is the study explained in a way that others can reproduce the study?

In qualitative design, the researcher has explained decisions made as the design has emerged. Qualitative design produces themes as the researcher examines feedback from participants. The reader should be able to understand decisions that have been made as the researcher moves through the research process.

Section 1: Introduction to Theory and Frameworks

Theory, which comes from the Greek word meaning "vision," is an attempt to explain the world of healthcare. Think of it as a road map! Theories map complex processes of human actions and interactions that affect client care.

Theories are not facts but are an attempt to explain reality. Researchers use theory as a framework, providing a conceptual foundation that is the backbone of the research study. Using a strong theoretical framework brings credibility to the study results and allows the study to be replicated.

During this lesson, you will learn to review published research studies and recognize various theoretical frameworks and how their use guides the research process.

Section 3: Explore: Theoretical Frameworks

Research theory explains the meaning, nature, and challenges associated with an observed phenomenon, so that readers may use that knowledge to act in a more informed and effective way (USC Libraries, 2021).

Theories attempt to explain, predict, and understand phenomena and are often used to challenge existing knowledge.

The theoretical framework is the structure that supports the research study. It introduces and describes the theory that explains why the research problem exists and justifies the need for the study. It is how the researcher conceptualizes the nature of the research problem and determines how data is perceived and interpreted.

The theoretical framework strengthens the research study in several ways. Click on the + icons in the interactive to learn more.

Interactive Transcript: Theoretical Framework

Evaluation

  • A distinct statement of theoretical assumptions enables the reader to critically evaluate the research.

Connection

  • The theoretical framework connects the reader to existing knowledge. Additionally, it connects the research hypothesis to the research methods.

Generalization

  • Articulating theoretical assumptions allows the researcher to progress from simply describing a phenomenon to generalizing aspects of the phenomenon.

Limitations

  • The theoretical framework helps to identify limits to generalizations. It specifies which variables influence a phenomenon and highlights how those variables might differ and under what circumstances.

Reference

USC Libraries. (2021, November 3). Theoretical frameworks. https://libguides.usc.edu/writingguide/theoreticalframework

Section 4: Explore: Choosing a Theoretical Framework

A theoretical framework is used to limit the scope of the relevant data by focusing on specific variables and defining how the researcher will analyze and interpret the data collected. How do researchers choose the appropriate theoretical framework?

Infographic Transcript: Choosing a Theoretical Framework
  1. Examine the research problem. The research problem anchors the entire study.
  2. Determine the key variables in the research. What is the anticipated effect that the variables have on the outcome? What factors contribute to the outcome?
  3. Review related literature. Determine how other researchers have addressed similar research problems.
  4. Identify the independent and dependent variables. The independent variable is manipulated or changed by the researcher. The dependent variable is the variable being tested and measured.
  5. Review several relevant theories. Choose a theory that best explains the relationships between the key variables in the study.
  6. Determine the relevance of these theories to the research. Be sure you can explain how the chosen theory is relevant to the current research problem.

Section 6: Explore: Theoretical Framework and Research Design

Theory may influence research design. The researcher begins by deciding the topic of interest and then forms a hypothesis regarding how the concepts interact. After reviewing the literature, a theory is used as the framework to focus on the specific variables of interest, thereby forming a road map for the research design. Theoretical frameworks may be used in both quantitative and qualitative research.

Review this research study by Gallegos & Sortedahl (2015).

Can you answer these questions?

  • What is the research problem?
  • What are the variables of interest?
  • What is the theory chosen to frame the research?
  • Is the theory relevant to the problem?
  • How will the data be collected?
  • Does the collection tool chosen seem related to the theory?

Reference

Gallegos, C., & Sortedahl, C. (2015). An exploration of professional values held by nurses at a large freestanding pediatric hospital. Pediatric Nursing, 41(4), 187-195. http://www.pediatricnursing.net/index.html

Section 9: Explore: Theories Used in Nursing Research

Nurse researchers use both nursing and non-nursing theories to provide structure to their studies. Some examples of nursing theories that have been used to support research include:

  • Benner's Novice-to-Expert Framework
  • Roy's Adaptation Model
  • Beck's Theory of Postpartum Depression
  • Kolcaba's Comfort Theory
  • Pender's Health Promotion Model

Some theories that support nursing research but come from other disciplines include:

  • Bandura's Social Cognitive Theory
  • Prochaska's Transtheoretical Change Theory
  • Becker's Health Belief Model
  • Ajzen's Theory of Planned Behavior

Here are some examples:

  • Alvarado-Garcia and Salazar Maya (2015) used Roy's Adaptation Model to study the experiences of elderly adult clients living with chronic benign pain.
  • Cole and Gaspar (2015) used Pender's Health Promotion Model to guide the development of a self-management protocol for clients with epilepsy.

References

Alvarado-Garcia, A. M., & Salazar Maya, A. M. (2015). Adaptation to chronic benign pain in elderly adults. Invest Educ Enferm, 33(1), 128-137. https://doi.org/10.17533/udea.iee.v33n1a16

Cole, K. A., & Gaspar, P. M. (2015). Implementation of an epilepsy self-management protocol. Journal of Neuroscience Nursing, 47(1), 3-9. https://doi.org/10.1097/JNN.0000000000000105

Section 10: Explore: Finding the Theoretical Framework in a Research Study

A research study's theoretical framework is not always formally acknowledged or described, making it difficult for the reader to clarify the foundation of the study. Quantitative researchers are less likely to identify the theoretical framework, but often use what is already known about a problem based on previous research. Qualitative research reflects on a specific theory and always uses a theoretical framework.

A theoretical framework can be found in one or several places in the article.

  • The theory will be mentioned in the introduction.
  • The theory may be detailed in a separate section within the article.
  • The concepts of the theory will relate to what outcome is studied.
  • The theory will guide the way the study variables are viewed.
  • The findings of the study will be considered in relation to the theory.
  • The theory may be included in the title of the research article.

Section 11: Explore: Critiquing the Use of Theory in Nursing Research

When critiquing the use of a theory in nursing research, several questions must be considered.

  • Is the theoretical framework clearly identified in the research study?
  • Is the theoretical framework consistent with the practice of nursing?
  • Is the theoretical framework the best fit for the research design?
  • Does the researcher clearly describe the variables studied and how they relate to a measurable outcome?
  • Does the literature review support the choice of variables?
  • Once data is collected and analyzed, is the theoretical framework used as a basis for comparison?
  • Did the study findings correlate with the theoretical framework?
  • Did the findings support or refute the findings of other researchers who used the same theoretical framework?
  • If discrepancies in findings exist, can they be explained using the theoretical framework?
  • Does the researcher discuss the findings as they relate to the theory used in the framework?

Section 1: Introduction to Types of Research

In this lesson, you will learn about the differences between descriptive research, qualitative research, and quantitative research and how important these types of research are to the nursing profession.

Section 3: Explore: Descriptive Research

Descriptive research is a type of research that is used to describe the characteristics of a population. It collects data that is used to answer a wide range of what, when, and how questions pertaining to a particular population or group. The primary focus of descriptive research is the exploration and description of phenomena in real-life situations. Descriptive research does not answer questions about why a certain phenomenon occurs or what the causes are.

Descriptive research plays an important role in nursing and can be invaluable in documenting the prevalence, nature, and intensity of health-related conditions and behaviors. The results of descriptive research studies are critical in the development of effective interventions. In nursing, the only way to understand the beliefs and values of different individuals and groups is to describe them. This descriptive knowledge helps nurses develop interventions that will benefit individuals, families, or groups and enable them to obtain desirable and predictable outcomes.

Infographic Transcript: Descriptive Design

In nursing practice, a descriptive design can be used to:

  • Develop theory
  • Identify problems
  • Make decisions
  • Determine what others in similar situations are doing

Section 4: Explore: Descriptive Research Strengths & Limitations

A variety of data collection methods may be used alone or in combination to answer the types of questions guiding descriptive research. Some of the more common methods include surveys, interviews, observations, case studies, and portfolios. The data collected through these methods can be either quantitative or qualitative.

Each of the methods used to collect descriptive data has its own strengths and limitations. The following are some of the strengths and limitations of descriptive research studies in general.

Tab: Strengths
  • It provides high-quality data since the research is conducted in the respondent's natural environment. Study participants are questioned or observed in a natural setting (e.g., their homes, childcare, or educational settings).
  • Study data can be used to identify the prevalence of particular problems and the need for new or additional services to address these problems.
  • Descriptive research may identify areas in need of additional research and relationships between variables that require future study. Descriptive research is often referred to as "hypothesis-generating research."
  • Depending on the data collection method used, descriptive studies can provide high-quality data and generate rich datasets on large and diverse samples.
  • It provides thorough information since the data collected is qualitative and quantitative and gives a holistic understanding of a research topic.
  • Data collected from descriptive research is robust and represents a larger population (or sample size), which makes it easier to analyze and use for decision making.
Tab: Limitations
  • Descriptive studies cannot be used to establish cause and effect relationships.
  • Respondents may not be truthful when answering survey questions or may give socially desirable responses.
  • The choice and wording of questions on a questionnaire may influence the descriptive findings.
  • Depending on the type and size of the sample, the findings may not be generalizable or produce an accurate description of the population of interest.

Section 5: Explore: Types of Descriptive Research Studies

Descriptive research studies can be conducted using a variety of study types. The researcher must ensure that they select the best type of study for their research to maximize the study results.

Click on each "+" sign in the interactive to learn more.

Interactive Transcript: Types of Descriptive Research Studies

Observational

Researchers collect data based on their view of the behavior and characteristics of the respondent, with the respondents themselves not directly having an input. It is often used in market research, psychology, and some other social science research to understand human behavior.

Survey

Respondents answer through surveys or questionnaires or polls. They are a popular market research tool to collect feedback from respondents. Can be conducted online or offline, making it the go-to option for descriptive research where the sample size is extremely large.

Cross-Sectional

Cross-sectional studies can be thought of as providing a "snapshot" of the frequency and characteristics of a disease in a population at a particular point in time. These are very good for measuring the prevalence of a disease or of a risk factor in a population. Thus, these are very helpful in assessing the disease burden and healthcare needs.

Longitudinal

Involves looking at variables and gathering of data repeatedly over an extended period of time. This research can take place over a period of weeks, months, or even years. In some cases, longitudinal studies can last several decades.

Case Study

Involves in-depth research and study of individuals or groups. Case studies lead to a hypothesis and widen a further scope of studying a phenomenon. However, case studies should not be used to determine cause and effect as they can't make accurate predictions because there could be a bias on the researcher's part.

Correlation

Used to measure the relationship between 2 or more variables, with the researcher having no control over them. It aims to find whether there is a positive correlation (both variables change in the same direction), negative correlation (the variables change in the opposite direction), or zero correlation (there is no relationship between the variables). This does not explain cause and effect but can be used in making predictions about relationships. Correlation does not equal causation.

Section 7: Explore: Qualitative Research

Qualitative research is a broad term that embraces several different methodologies. Qualitative studies use a scholarly approach to help us formulate an understanding of a phenomenon. In other words, qualitative research describes life experiences, cultures, and social processes from the perspectives of the persons involved in naturalistic settings. Qualitative research is explanatory, descriptive, and inductive in nature. It uses words, as opposed to numbers, to explain a phenomenon which allows us to gain insights without measuring concepts or analyzing statistical relationships.

Qualitative researchers assume that we can only understand things if we consider the context in which they take place, such as a client who struggles to take their antiretroviral medication, or the female client who has carefully thought through childbirth despite a debilitating illness, or social media's influence on adolescents' mental health, specifically suicidal ideation and thoughts.

The purpose of qualitative research is to bring about a description of a social experience that is so detailed and insightful that someone who has not experienced the phenomenon can understand and appreciate its distinctions.

Section 8: Explore: Characteristics of Qualitative Research

Qualitative research is a systematic approach to understanding the experiences of others. Characteristics of qualitative research include the following:

  • Seeks to answer difficult "why" questions
    • Explanatory and descriptive
    • Discovery oriented; explanatory, descriptive, and inductive in nature; uses words rather than numbers to explain a phenomenon
  • Often used when just beginning to understand a phenomenon or if a new perspective is needed
  • Phenomena studied in naturalistic settings
    • The setting that people live in daily
    • Important because qualitative researchers consider the context to be very important in understanding a phenomenon
  • Attempts to interpret phenomena in terms of the meanings people bring to them
  • Used when attempting to understand the nature of a phenomenon
  • Does NOT test interventions
  • Seeks to understand the client's experience
  • May provide information necessary to pursue quantitative studies
  • May be used for theory development or extension
  • Uses inductive reasoning

Section 9: Explore: Types of Qualitative Research

There are multiple design strategies available to the qualitative researcher and the particular type of research design is dependent upon the purpose and nature of the research. The following types are regularly used because they answer questions about human reactions and interactions.

Tab: Case Research Methods

In-depth study of a single subject/person or a small group of subjects (e.g., family group, community, or other entity) over an extended period with the goal of describing the impact of health and illness on an individual. Multiple data collection methods may be used, including observation, interviewing, and instrumentation. Mixed methods, which combine qualitative and quantitative data collection, are also widely used. Case research is an appropriate method for studying the responses of individuals and small groups related to interventions, health behaviors, and perceptions of illness. It may be the only way to study individuals with rare conditions.

Tab: Content Analysis

Technically not a specific qualitative research design, it is more accurately described as a data analysis method used for interpreting the meaning in verbal responses of respondents or in historical or written documents. This term is commonly attached to designs that rely on data collected via interviews or document analysis and that use interpretive coding to arrive at themes and patterns. Such designs are sometimes referred to as descriptive qualitative designs, but even that term is misleading because these studies may be interpretive as well. Content analysis is often used when a qualitative study does not neatly fit into one of the more formal classifications.

Tab: Phenomenology

Concerned with the lived experiences of humans in relation to a shared phenomenon, and investigates the meaning of an experience among a group whose members have lived through it. Used to describe how unique individuals respond to the circumstances of their health and illness. It is useful for understanding how patients react and respond to both everyday experiences and unique events. It is also useful for obtaining evidence of nursing practices that support and enhance the ways patients respond to the challenges in their healthcare.

Tab: Ethnography

Focuses on the culture of a group of people and studies the features and interactions of that culture. The assumption underlying this type of research is that every group of individuals evolves into a culture that guides the way members structure their experiences and view the world. The ethnographic researcher's aim is to learn from, rather than study, the members of a culture without changing it. Participant observation is very common. The researcher is more than an observer and actually becomes an active participant in the culture under study to more thoroughly understand its experiences and worldview.

Tab: Grounded Theory

Aimed at discovering and developing a theory based on systematically collected data about a phenomenon. The intent is to discover a pattern of reactions, interactions, and relationships among people and their concerns. This leads to the development of a theory to explain the phenomenon.

  • Assess the client's status or progress

Section 13: Explore: Quantitative Research

In direct contrast to qualitative research, quantitative research means that nurses can practice based on what they know, rather than what they think. Quantitative research is generally reported using numbers and statistics, which is helpful because numbers, statistics, and mathematics are universal languages. Quantitative methods and procedures allow the nurse to focus on detecting the truth and describe what is known in a standard, universally understandable way.

Quantitative research:

  • Enables the nurse researcher to determine what is known
  • Develops knowledge by a hypothesis-testing process: state them, test them, disprove them, or confirm findings by retesting
  • Systematically testing research questions and hypotheses
  • Contains a plan or blueprint
  • Provides the structure for maintaining control in the study
  • Replication of results
  • Uses deductive reasoning

The purpose of quantitative research is to use measurements to determine the effectiveness of interventions and to report these effects with an identified level of confidence. Quantitative designs are considered some of the strongest scientific evidence for nursing practice.

Section 14: Explore: Characteristics of Quantitative Research

Quantitative research involves measuring objective characteristics or responses of subjects and is reported using numbers. Quantitative studies involve comparing groups of subjects in some way (either between groups or within a single group over time). Quantitative designs are intended to be used with representative samples so that the findings can be generalized to larger groups of people with a high level of confidence that the study's outcome will be the same.

Characteristics of quantitative research include:

  • Relying on numbers to measure and quantify variables
  • Studying objective characteristics and responses that can be measured
  • Comparing groups of subjects in some way
  • Applying interventions to samples to generalize the study results to broader populations
  • Aiming to determine the effects of an intervention through a high level of control

Section 15: Explore: Types of Quantitative Research

Quantitative studies address a considerable number of research questions, though these questions are usually focused on the effects of interventions, actions, risk factors, or events. Quantitative research studies focus on establishing a statistical relationship between variables and measuring the probability that error was responsible for the outcome. There are multiple design strategies available to the quantitative researcher and the particular type of research design is dependent upon the nature of the research question and the resources available to the researcher. Fundamentally, quantitative types of research can be classified in the following ways:

Tab: Experimental Designs

Experimental designs use a highly structured study of cause and effect to answer questions about and determine the effectiveness of an intervention. These studies are considered the "gold standard" for evidence-based practice because they provide convincing support for the value of a treatment. Experimental designs are often referred to as randomized controlled trials because these designs have in common the random assignment of subjects and a high level of control.

Experimental designs provide clear evidence of the effectiveness of interventions, yet they are uncommon in nursing and healthcare in general. This rarity reflects the difficulty in achieving the high levels of control that are characteristic of experiments in an applied setting. In addition, it is not always desirable, or ethical, to manipulate an independent variable or to withhold treatment, so alternative designs must often be selected.

Tab: Quasi-experimental Designs

These studies of cause and effect are similar to experimental designs but use convenience samples or existing groups to test interventions. These studies have many of the same characteristics of experimental designs except for one key feature: they do not randomize subjects to groups, but rather work with intact groups or convenience samples. Because of this single feature, control and generalizability are limited.

Tab: Comparisons of Intact Groups
  • Ex post facto research relies on observation of the relationships between naturally occurring differences in the intervention and outcome. Data is usually collected after the fact, meaning that both the independent and dependent variables have already occurred. This design weakens the capacity of these studies to support cause-and-effect conclusions because the researcher cannot prove temporality. In other words, the researcher depends on the subject's recall to determine if the independent variable preceded the dependent one.
  • Causal-comparative study involves categorization of subjects into groups. An outcome of interest is measured, and differences are attributed to the differences in the classification of subjects. This design identifies cause-and-effect relationships by forming groups based on certain characteristics that the individuals inherently possess (e.g., gender, ethnicity, or geographic region). An outcome of interest is then measured to determine if the groups differ.
  • Case-control study involves observation of subjects who exhibit a characteristic matched with subjects who do not. Differences between the subjects allow the study of relationships between risk and disease without subjecting healthy individuals to illness. These studies are common in epidemiology and public health because they enable researchers to study the links between causative agents and disease. Case-control studies are often used to judge the relationships between risk factors and disease states or between an event and an outcome.
Tab: Time Series Designs

Time series designs answer questions about the effectiveness of interventions over time - a type of quasi-experimental design in which only one group receives the intervention. An outcome is measured repeatedly over time. The researcher is interested in both the effects of an intervention and the timing of its effects. Time series designs have only one group: the group receiving the intervention. A baseline is measured and an intervention applied, and then measures are taken periodically over a specified time period. In these studies, subsequent measures are compared to the baseline measure, and any changes that emerge are assumed to be due to the intervention. In such designs, subjects serve as their own comparison group, with subsequent responses being compared to data for the same subjects in the preintervention period.

Section 16: Explore: How Nurses Use Quantitative Evidence

Nurses can use quantitative evidence to:

  • Answer a research question
  • Identify if assessment procedures are effective in detecting client conditions
  • Determine the effects of an intervention
  • Measure the relationships between variables
  • Detect changes over time
  • Draw conclusions about the relationships between interventions and outcomes
  • Determine which outcomes can be expected from specific interventions
  • Develop standard operating procedures to standardize the way client outcomes are measured, collected, and reported
