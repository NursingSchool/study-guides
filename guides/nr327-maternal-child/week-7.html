Section 1: Introduction to Newborn Acquired Conditions (Hyperbilirubinemia)

Hyperbilirubinemia is the elevation of serum bilirubin levels that is related to the hemolysis of red blood cells (RBCs) and subsequent reabsorption of unconjugated bilirubin from the small intestines. Jaundice is considered abnormal or nonphysiologic when the total serum bilirubin (TSB) rises quickly and to a higher level and extends longer than what is considered normal.

This concept focuses on nonphysiologic or pathologic jaundice and the nursing care that supports these newborns.

Section 3: Explore: Physiologic Jaundice

During pregnancy, the placenta removes bilirubin. When the baby is born, the baby's liver must take over this function.

Physiologic jaundice occurs as a "normal" response to the baby's limited ability to excrete bilirubin in the first few days of life. Newborns with excessive bilirubin in their blood have a yellow discoloration of the skin and sclera. This occurs in over half of all term newborns and over three-quarters of all preterm infants. This type of jaundice is not present during the first 24 hours of life, but instead typically appears on the second or third day of life and peaks between days 2-4. Normal levels are usually reached within a week.

The basic process includes hemolysis of erythrocytes, which happens with the normal birth process. The newborn doesn't need as many. This breakdown of red blood cell components ends up in the blood to be used in the body. However, the bilirubin residue is not usable and is harmful to the newborn. This bilirubin is naturally released in an unconjugated form. Unconjugated bilirubin, also called indirect bilirubin, is only soluble in fat but not in water.

"Before excretion can occur, the liver must change it to a water-soluble form by a process called conjugation. The bilirubin is then known as conjugated or direct bilirubin. Conjugated bilirubin is not toxic to the body and can be excreted" (Murray et al., 2019, p. 526). If this fat-soluble, unconjugated bilirubin is absorbed into the subcutaneous fat, the skin becomes yellow, which is called jaundice.

  • Conjugated (direct) = water-soluble = able to be excreted
  • Unconjugated (indirect) = fat-soluble = unable to be excreted = absorption leads to jaundice

Reference

Murray, S. S., McKinney, E. S., Holub, K. S., & Jones, R. (2019). Foundations of maternal-newborn and women's health nursing (7th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 4: Explore: Nonphysiologic Jaundice

Jaundice is considered abnormal or nonphysiologic (or pathologic) when the transcutaneous bilirubin (TSB) rises more rapidly, rises to a higher level than is expected, or stays elevated for longer than normal.

Pathologic jaundice "can be caused by newborns who suffer hypoxemia, respiratory acidosis, infection, dehydration, or other injury that impairs the blood-brain barrier and allows unconjugated bilirubin to enter the brain" (Murray et al., 2019, p. 67).

Some examples include:

Tab: Jaundice From Hemolysis

Jaundice may occur with the breakdown of red blood cells due to hemolytic disease of the newborn (Rh disease) or from having too many red blood cells that break down naturally and release bilirubin erythroblastosis fetalis. Severe cases may lead to hydrops (a severe form of anemia).

Tab: Jaundice Related to Inadequate Liver Function

Jaundice may be related to inadequate liver function due to infection or other factors. In this condition, the direct bilirubin is increased.

Tab: Premature Birth

A baby born before 37 weeks of gestation may have difficulty processing bilirubin. Feeding issues are common, which may impact the elimination of bilirubin through the stool.

Tab: Significant Bruising During Birth

Significant bruising during birth may cause an increased risk of bruising and increased breakdown of RBCs. A common example of bruising is a cephalhematoma.

Tab: Blood Type

Mom's and baby's blood types are different.

Tab: Additional Factors

Additional factors include:

  • short red blood cell lifespan
  • lack of albumin-binding sites
  • liver immaturity
  • lack of intestinal flora
  • fatty acids from cold stress or asphyxia
  • maternal diabetes or preeclampsia
  • polycythemia

Reference

Murray, S. S., McKinney, E. S., Holub, K. S., & Jones, R. (2019). Foundations of maternal-newborn and women's health nursing (7th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 6: Explore: Symptoms of Hyperbilirubinemia

Symptoms of hyperbilirubinemia include the following:

Tab: Jaundice

The newborn's skin and eyes (usually beginning on the face and moving down to the extremities) appear yellow. Jaundice is usually visible once the bilirubin levels reach 5 mg/dL.

  • Blanching the skin over a bony prominence and looking at the color before blood returns will reveal yellowing of the skin.
  • In newborns with dark skin, assessing the color of the palate or mucus membranes of the mouth can also be used to assess for jaundice.

Assessments should take place in good lighting conditions, preferably in natural daylight, and at least every 8-12 hours, but more frequently if the newborn has risk factors or rising levels.

Tab: Lethargy

High bilirubin levels can make newborns sleepy, lethargic, and less interested in breastfeeding. They appear to have little or no energy. They may not respond to visual cues or sounds.

Tab: Poor Sucking/Feeding

Some newborns must be woken up to eat and struggle to stay awake during a feeding. This can then lead to decreased output and weight loss.

Tab: High Pitched Cry

As the amount of bilirubin increases in the body, the newborn brain can be impacted. Newborn cries should be monitored as high-pitched cries are not normal and can indicate a neurological issue.

Section 7: Explore: Bilirubin Levels

In addition to visual assessments, it is recommended that every newborn has their bilirubin level checked within 24 hours of birth.

Tab: Types of Testing

[Figure: A test of total serum bilirubin (TSB) is performed on a newborn's heel.]

Testing may include transcutaneous bilirubin (TcB) measurements using a bilirubinometer (non-invasive) device. It measures the spectrum of light reflected by the newborn's skin. Like a thermometer, a hand-held machine is placed on the forehead or sternum to obtain the bilirubin level.

Another method is a blood level measurement (invasive) called total serum bilirubin (TSB). The newborn will typically have this blood sample taken from their heel.

Tab: Bilirubin Levels

Direct and indirect bilirubin levels reflect whether the bilirubin is bound with other substances by the liver so that it can be excreted (direct) or is circulating in the blood circulation (indirect).

The average total serum bilirubin level usually peaks at 5 to 6 mg per dL (86 to 103 μmol per L) on the third to fourth day of life and then declines over the first week after birth. These newborns would fall in the green low risk zone.

For a newborn with pathological jaundice, their levels may increase rapidly within the first 24 hours and fall under the red high risk zone.

The chart shows age in hours versus level of serum bilirubin.

For example, a newborn who is 14 hours old appears jaundiced upon assessment. A bilirubinometer level is taken and is at 9 mg/dL. An additional blood draw confirms that the serum level is 9.2 mg/dL. This newborn would fall in the high risk zone and need immediate treatment.

Nurses must know the newborn's bilirubin level compared to their hours of age and track their level of risk. Testing frequency will increase based on risk. Based on their risk zone, newborns may require support, which can vary from additional feedings to more extensive therapies.

There are also digital tools that help identify risk levels. View this tool that uses the hours of birth and the bilirubin levels to calculate a newborn's risk zone: https://bilitool.org/

Section 9: Explore: Treating Hyperbilirubinemia

Treatment of hyperbilirubinemia depends on many factors, including the cause of the hyperbilirubinemia and the level of bilirubin. Current treatments include:

Tab: Treating the Underlying Cause

Treat the underlying cause, such as infection. The newborn may need antibiotics or extra IV fluids.

Tab: Phototherapy

Bilirubin absorbs light, so jaundice and increased bilirubin levels usually decrease when the baby is exposed to special blue spectrum lights. Treatment takes time, so it is often used around the clock. The newborn will be turned frequently so that the entire body is exposed to the lights. The baby's eyes must be protected and the temperature monitored during phototherapy. Blood levels of bilirubin are checked to monitor if the phototherapy is working. The skin should also be assessed at least every eight hours.

Side effects of phototherapy include frequent, loose, green stools. This is a result of additional bile and bowel motility. This causes more rapid excretion of the bilirubin but may be damaging to the skin and result in fluid loss. If there is any risk of dehydration or weight loss, newborns could be supplemented with additional breast milk or formula.

Tab: Fiberoptic Blanket

Another treatment is a fiberoptic blanket that is placed under the baby. This may be used alone or in combination with regular phototherapy. The blanket goes around the baby, right against the skin. A newborn typically only wears a diaper and the blanket and then clothes can be worn over the blanket. Eye protection is not needed with the blanket.

Tab: Adequate Hydration

Breastfeeding is recommended and some moms may also need to pump milk to maintain their supply. Frequent feedings are needed to maintain hydration and increase volume so there are more frequent bowel movements, which help excrete the bilirubin.

Supplementation with formula may sometimes be needed. Water or dextrose supplementation is not recommended. Intravenous fluids should be given if feeding is unsuccessful and the infant is dehydrated (Murray et al., 2019).

Tab: Exchange Transfusion

Exchange transfusion is used when phototherapy fails to lower dangerously high levels fast enough. An exchange transfusion is done by giving donor blood in an equal amount to the removed newborn's blood. Exchange transfusions may need to be repeated if the bilirubin levels remain high. The newborn will need to be checked for fluid and electrolyte and acid/base stability.

[Figure: A newborn is treated with phototherapy, eyes covered.]

Reference

Murray, S. S., McKinney, E. S., Holub, K. S., & Jones, R. (2019). Foundations of maternal-newborn and women's health nursing (7th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 11: Explore: Kernicterus

Dangerous levels of bilirubin may lead to bilirubin encephalopathy, which is the acute response to bilirubin toxicity. If this is not treated, the newborn may develop kernicterus, the chronic and permanent result of bilirubin toxicity. Newborns who survive could suffer subtle and significant neurologic and developmental problems which include hearing loss and cerebral palsy.

Early detection and management of jaundice can prevent disabilities caused by kernicterus. The signs of kernicterus include:

  • Yellowing of the skin that has extended to the extremities
  • Lethargy
  • A high pitched cry
  • Seizures

Frequent testing of total serum bilirubin (TSB) levels is required to ensure that the levels of bilirubin are decreasing.

Newborns should not suffer from kernicterus if they receive appropriate and timely care, including treatment of the cause, adequate hydration, phototherapy, fiberoptic blanket therapy, or exchange transfusion (if other interventions do not provide an adequate reduction in bilirubin).

Section 13: Explore: Parent Teaching and Role of the Nurse

Nurses have an important role in the assessment and treatment of a newborn with hyperbilirubinemia:

  • Temperature (low from being undressed or high from phototherapy treatment)
  • Hydration (intake and output)
  • Bilirubin levels
  • Skin assessment
  • Turning the newborn frequently
  • Eye protection

Key teaching points for parents

  • Educate and explain the basic process of the equipment.
  • Discuss newborn's skin care and eye protection as a priority.
  • Support parents emotionally. Stress and fear are very common as they see their newborn under treatment.
  • Provide verbal and written discharge instructions.
    • Teach parents about assessing for jaundice and how the color starts centrally (face and chest) and moves outward as levels rise.
    • Emphasize the importance of keeping follow-up lab work and visits.
    • Remind parents to not place the newborn in direct sunlight. Sunburn is a risk along with temperature instability. Placing the newborn near a window is the recommended process.
    • Educate on breastfeeding and to track diaper count and type of bowel movements.
    • Explain that lotions should be avoided as they can cause burns during phototherapy.

Section 1: Introduction to Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) is a result of the sudden discontinuation of fetal exposure to substances that were used by the mother during pregnancy. Withdrawal from licit or illicit substances is becoming more common among neonates in both developed and developing countries. Without proper treatment and care, the infant born with NAS is at significant risk of neonatal morbidity and mortality.

This concept will review the effects of these drugs on the neonate and how nursing care can support these children in their recovery.

Section 3: Explore: Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome Statistics

It is important to understand the incidence of neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS). The number of cases has been steadily increasing in the U.S.

Transcript — NAS Statistics

In the United States, the incidence of neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) has increased 383% during 2000-2012 (CDC, 2016).

For every 1000 infants born, seven will be diagnosed with NAS (CDC, 2021).

In 2019, 7% of pregnant women reported using prescription opioids, and one in five of those women, admitted to misuse (CDC, 2021).

Drugs that women abuse include:

  • Opioids, such as heroin and prescribed medicines such as codeine and oxycodone
  • Stimulants, such as amphetamines or cocaine
  • Antidepressant medicines, such as selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)
  • Depressants, such as barbiturates, or alcohol, or marijuana

Most medications have been shown to cross the placenta. Often, women who abuse one drug are at risk of abusing additional medications or illicit substances.

References

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2021, July 16). Data and statistics about opioid use during pregnancy. https://www.cdc.gov/pregnancy/opioids/data.html

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2016). Incidence of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome — 28 States, 1999–2013. Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report, 65(31), 799-208. http://dx.doi.org/10.15585/mmwr.mm6531a2

Section 4: Explore: Prenatal Support

The increasing use of opioid pain relievers and other addictive substances among pregnant women warrants the need for:

  • screening and education of expectant mothers
  • increased access to follow-up facilities
  • early intervention services for high-risk mothers and infants
  • drug abuse treatment and prevention programs aimed at improving the health outcomes of mother–infant couples

Women should speak to their provider about quitting before just stopping any medication. It is not recommended for women to just stop abruptly without support and monitoring.

Throughout this lesson, we'll discuss Tiffany, a client who has been using opioids since a car accident 3 years ago. She hurt her back and physical therapy alone was not enough to control the pain. She started misusing pain medication and every time she tries to stop, she relapses.

Tiffany was connected early on with a treatment program. It was determined that she would not discontinue her medication completely, but instead, her provider transitioned her to methadone.

Methadone has been the gold standard for treating pregnant women who are addicted to opiates since 1995. Pregnant women in a methadone treatment program should be acknowledged though for choosing a healthier path for themselves and their babies. Note that methadone withdrawal is more severe and prolonged than heroin withdrawal. However, the benefits of quitting outweigh the risks.

Methadone Use During Pregnancy

  • Mothers are more likely to have prenatal care.
  • They typically have more stable living conditions.
  • Women tend to participate in fewer high-risk social behaviors.
  • Mothers have improved nutrition and deliver newborns with higher birth weights.

Section 6: Explore: Finnegan Neonatal Abstinence Score

Tiffany attends a class in the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) in anticipation of her upcoming delivery. She learns about the newborn behaviors that she should be prepared for.

  • poor feeding or sucking or slow weight gain
  • breathing problems, including tachypnea
  • body shakes (tremors), seizures (convulsions), overactive reflexes (twitching), and tight muscle tone
  • fussiness, excessive crying, or having a high-pitched cry (spend more time crying than feeding)
  • fever, sweating, or blotchy skin
  • trouble sleeping and lots of yawning
  • diarrhea or throwing up
  • stuffy nose or sneezing

Newborns are not considered to have a substance-use disorder but, instead, are considered drug-exposed. A commonly used tool for assessing infants exposed in utero to psychoactive drugs, particularly opioids/opiates is the Finnegan Neonatal Abstinence Score (FNAS). It assesses the neurologic, behavioral, and stress/abstinence function of the neonate, and scores various NAS symptoms—the lower the score, the fewer withdrawal symptoms.

Signs of NAS depend on a variety of factors:

  • What drug was used during pregnancy (can urine, blood, meconium be tested)?
  • How much did they use and how long did they take it?
  • How are the drugs broken down in the body?
  • The baby's gestational age at birth.

Section 8: Explore: Additional Complications From Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

Tiffany delivers her baby, Amanda, and the infant is taken to the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) due to grunting and tachypnea. She meets with the neonatologist who shares some additional information about newborns with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS).

NAS infants are at increased risk of:

  • low birthweight (less than 5 pounds, 8 ounces)
  • jaundice and seizures
  • sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)

There are still many unknowns about long-term effects. More research needs to be conducted as each case is different and each baby responds a little differently to drug exposure.

Evidence shows that infants may experience:

  • developmental delays and motor problems—delayed or difficulty with sitting, walking
  • issues with their bones and muscles, affecting their ability to do simple movements
  • sleep problems, ear infections, and vision problems
  • speech, language, behavior, and learning problems

Section 9: Explore: Non-Pharmacologic Interventions

Non-pharmacologic interventions for an infant experiencing neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) include decreasing external stimulation, holding the infant, nonnutritive sucking, swaddling, pressure/rubbing, and rocking. These interventions meet the goals of nonpharmacologic interventions, which are to facilitate parental attachment and decrease external stimuli.

The goals of nonpharmacologic interventions are to facilitate supportive parenting behaviors and decrease the external stimuli that tend to exacerbate withdrawal signs. A multilayered and comprehensive approach is crucial to nurturing strong maternal/family-infant interactions in this population. Nonpharmacologic care is often described as modification of the environment and social interactions that support neurodevelopmental and physiologic stability.

To promote infant comfort and sleep, baby Amanda receives skin-to-skin care (often known as kangaroo care) for 1 hour after feeding. The nurses provide her mom, Tiffany, with an area where Tiffany has privacy. Tiffany has tried to be present for each feeding and when she must leave, the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) has volunteers who provide the kangaroo care for Amanda. Kangaroo care has been found to be safe and effective with decreasing length of hospital stay and improving measures of growth, breastfeeding, and maternal-infant bonding. Another supportive measure includes music therapy. Sometimes people sing or play instruments, and both have improved the neonate's outcomes.

Section 11: Explore: Nursing Interventions

Review interventions the nurse can take with neonatal abstinence syndrome.

Transcript — Nursing Interventions for Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS)

The care that the nurse provides can dramatically improve the recovery and long-term health of the newborn with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS).

  • Decreased environmental stimuli: Room should be quiet, dimly lit, and use slow infant handling.
  • Frequent small/demand feedings may help in infants with motor or tone dysregulation.
  • Breastfeeding is not contraindicated, unless the mother is taking street drugs, is involved in polydrug abuse, or is infected with HIV.
  • Holding – Infants who have poor motor control (thrashing or exaggerated rooting) respond to gentle head/limb restraint, helping them regulate.
  • Nonnutritive sucking helps organize a dysregulated infant and prevents disorganization.
  • Swaddling helps maintain regulation, promotes self-soothing, and increases tolerance to stimulation (such as diaper change).
  • Containment – Gentle containment or pressure supports motor and tone control.
  • Rubbing is often better than patting when burping during feedings to avoid triggering Moro reflex. Vertical rocking facilitates relaxation and eye contact; more soothing than "regular" rocking or side to side motion.
  • Avoid eye contact: it can cause overstimulation. Once the infant is recovering, eye contact is typically more tolerable.

Section 13: Explore: Nursing Attitude Towards Clients

Nurses may hold negative attitudes or judge pregnant women who have a substance use disorder and use substances during pregnancy. Some nurses may lack knowledge about substance use and its treatment during pregnancy.

What can be done to combat negative attitudes and judgment towards pregnant women with a substance use disorder?

  • Training programs and workshops to improve nurses' knowledge help change attitudes toward patients with substance-related problems. This can result in positive outcomes and improved client relationships.
  • Nurses need to be aware of their biases and, with training, are much more successful in helping clients to change their drug behaviors.
  • When there is a positive connection between nurses and moms, moms are often better able to recognize and interpret infant cues, thus enhancing mother-infant interactions.
  • All infants with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) should be managed with nonpharmacologic nursing interventions as first-line treatment in an atmosphere of kindness and sensitivity.

Section 1: Introduction to Newborn Acquired Conditions

A high-risk neonate can be defined as a newborn, regardless of gestational age or birth weight, who has a greater than average chance of morbidity or mortality because of conditions or circumstances associated with birth and the adjustment to extrauterine existence.

The high-risk period encompasses human growth and development from the time of viability (the gestational age at which survival outside the uterus is believed to be possible, or as early as 23 weeks of gestation) up to 28 days after birth. Therefore, it includes threats to life and health that occur during the prenatal, perinatal, and postnatal periods.

This module will review some newborn acquired conditions and the role of the nurse in caring for them.

Section 3: Explore: Nursing Role

Some mothers experience a challenging delivery that interrupts the normal course of events associated with birth and the adjustment to extrauterine existence. The nurse must be prepared to assess and identify the needs of the infant.

An infant may be considered at high risk because of:

  • birth trauma (physical injuries that a neonate sustains during labor and birth)
  • maternal substance use (alcohol, supplements)
  • infection (uterine or hospital-acquired)

After delivery, the nurse makes a rapid inspection and physical assessment to determine if any life-threatening conditions require immediate attention.

A comprehensive physical assessment of the newborn is performed after the parents have had the opportunity to interact with their new baby. Because evidence of some birth injuries may not be apparent at the initial examination, assessment continues during each contact with the neonate by each nurse and provider.

Section 4: Explore: Birth Injuries

Birth trauma (injury) is a physical injury sustained by a neonate during labor and birth. It remains an important source of neonatal morbidity.

Most birth injuries are avoidable, especially with careful assessment of risk factors and appropriate planning of the birth. The use of fetal ultrasonography (US) allows antepartum diagnosis of certain fetal conditions that may be treated in utero or shortly after birth.

Elective cesarean birth can be chosen for some pregnancies to prevent significant birth injury. A small percentage of significant birth injuries, such as especially difficult or prolonged labor or an abnormal fetal presentation, are unavoidable despite skilled and competent obstetric care. Some injuries cannot be anticipated until specific circumstances are encountered during childbirth.

Emergency cesarean birth may be performed, but in these circumstances, the injury may be unavoidable. The same injury might be caused in several ways. For example, a cephalhematoma could result from an obstetric technique such as forceps birth or vacuum extraction or pressure of the fetal skull against the maternal pelvis.

Many injuries are minor and resolve readily in the neonatal period without treatment. Other traumas require some degree of intervention; few are serious enough to be fatal.

The nurse's contributions to the newborn's welfare begin with early observation of their transition. Promptly reporting signs that indicate deviations from normal permits early initiation of appropriate therapy.

Section 5: Explore: Common Birth Injuries

Birth injuries can be skeletal, nervous system, and/or neurologically related. Click on each type of injury below to learn more.

Tab: Skeletal Injuries

The newborn's immature, flexible skull can withstand a great degree of deformation (molding) before fracture results. Considerable force is required to fracture it. Two types of skull fractures typically identified in the newborn are linear and depressed fractures. The location of the fracture and involvement of underlying structures determine its significance.

If an artery lying in a groove on the undersurface of the skull is torn as a result of the fracture, increased intracranial pressure (ICP) follows. Unless a blood vessel is involved, linear fractures, which account for most of all fractures for this age group, heal without special treatment. The soft skull may become indented without laceration of either the skin or the dura mater. These depressed fractures, or "ping-pong ball" indentations, may occur during difficult births from the pressure of the head on the bony pelvis. They can also occur as a result of the careless application of forceps.

  • Intracranial hemorrhage (ICH), as a result of birth trauma, is more likely to occur in a full-term, large infant.
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage in term infants is a result of trauma, but in preterm infants, it is a result of hypoxia.

The clavicle is the bone most often fractured during birth. Generally, the break is in the middle third of the bone. Dystocia, particularly shoulder impaction, may be the predisposing problem. Limited arm motion, crepitus over the bone, and absence of the Moro reflex on the affected side are often present. Except for the use of gentle rather than vigorous handling and containment of the limb against the chest (such as pinning the sleeve to the chest), no accepted treatment for the fractured clavicle of the newborn exists and the prognosis is good. The humerus and femur are other bones that may be fractured during a difficult birth. Fractures in newborns generally heal rapidly. Immobilization is accomplished with slings, splints, swaddling, and other devices.

Tab: Peripheral Nervous System Injuries

Plexus injury results from forces that alter the normal position and relationship of the arm, shoulder, and neck.

Erb's palsy (Erb-Duchenne paralysis) is caused by damage to the upper brachial plexus due to:

  • Excessive pulling on a child's neck or head during a difficult delivery
  • Pulling on the baby's feet in a feet-first (breech) delivery
  • The child's head, neck, or shoulders getting stuck under the pelvic bone and/or in the birth canal during delivery

The clinical manifestations of Erb's palsy are related to the paralysis of the affected extremity and muscles. The arm hangs limp alongside the body. The shoulder and arm are adducted and rotated internally. The elbow is extended, and the forearm is pronated with the wrist and fingers flexed. A grasp reflex may be present because finger and wrist movement remain normal.

In lower-plexus palsy, the muscles of the hand are paralyzed with consequent wrist drop and relaxed fingers. In a third and more severe form of brachial palsy, the entire arm is paralyzed and hangs limp and motionless at the side. The Moro reflex is absent on the affected side for all forms of brachial palsy. Total plexus is the second most common type of plexus injury.

Explore more about Newborn Brachial Plexus Injuries on Nemours' Kids Health website: https://kidshealth.org/en/parents/brachial-plexus.html

Section 7: Explore: Neurologic Injuries

Neurologic injury in newborn infants is common with the increased survival of low birth weight and very low birth weight infants. In addition, the lower the gestational age, the higher the risk for certain neurologic injuries.

These infants are particularly vulnerable to ischemic injury caused by variable (both increased and decreased) cerebral blood flow after asphyxia; and preterm infants, with a fragile cerebrovascular network, are highly prone to periventricular or intraventricular hemorrhage.

Fragility and increased permeability of capillaries and prolonged prothrombin time predispose preterm infants to trauma when delicate structures are subjected to the forces of labor, which can also be caused by a cord around the neck (CAN).

Transcript — Birth Asphyxia

Birth asphyxia is a complex condition which occurs when there is a lack of blood flow and oxygen to the baby's brain, during or just after birth. The extent of damage depends on:

  • oxygen level
  • length of oxygen-deprivation
  • prompt treatment

Signs & symptoms

Before delivery:

  • abnormal heart rate or rhythm
  • acidic blood pH

At birth:

  • abnormal skin color
  • low heart rate
  • abnormal muscle tone and reflexes
  • weak cry
  • breathing issues

Section 8: Explore: Neonatal Infections

Neonatal infections may be acquired in utero, at birth, or shortly thereafter. Select each tab below to learn more about neonatal infections.

Tab: Newborns at Risk

Newborn response to infections may be sluggish due to neonatal neutrophils being present but having decreased functional capabilities in term infants. Also, some immunoglobulins cross the placenta and some do not.

Depending on the infection, the infant may not benefit from maternal antibodies. Dysmaturity seen with intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) and preterm and postdate birth further compromise the immune system of the neonate.

Tab: Impact on Newborns

Perinatally acquired infections may cause miscarriage, stillbirth, intrauterine infection, congenital malformation, and acute neonatal disease.

Bacterial vaginal infections/sexually transmitted infections should be treated while in labor and post-delivery to prevent transmission to the newborn.

Tab: Types of Infections

Neonatal sepsis (the presence of microorganisms or their toxins in the blood or other tissues) continues to be one of the most significant causes of neonatal morbidity and mortality. The most common causative agents of neonatal sepsis include bacteria such as Group B Streptococcus (GBS), E. coli, and Staphylococcal infections.

  • With early onset sepsis, newborns typically show signs within 3 days, while late onset sepsis most commonly occurs after the first week.
  • Signs of sepsis in the newborn may be subtle and mimic other conditions. Nonspecific signs such as changes in activity, color, tone, temperature instability, or feeding may be the only signs of sepsis. Parents may also share that they feel that "something isn't right" with their newborn.
  • The newborn may show more specific signs that significantly impact multiple systems (Murray et al., 2019):
    • Respiratory: Tachypnea, respiratory distress (nasal flaring, retractions, grunting), and apnea
    • Cardiovascular: Hypotension, tachycardia, and edema
    • Gastrointestinal: Poor feeding, abdominal distention (should be tracked and document changes), and glucose instability
    • Neurologic: Change in muscle tone, irritability, jitteriness, lethargy, bulging fontanels, and a high-pitched cry

Viral infections include respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), rotavirus, herpes simplex, influenza, and varicella, and may occur in the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU). These pathogens also may cause chronic infection, with subtle manifestations that may be recognized only after a prolonged period.

Fungal infections are of the greatest concern in preterm infants. Occasionally, fungal infections, such as thrush, are found in otherwise healthy term newborns.

Tab: Nursing Role

Precautions for preventing infection and prompt recognition when it occurs are necessary for optimal newborn care. It is important to recognize the presentation of infections in the neonatal period to treat the acute infection, prevent healthcare associated infections (HAI) in other infants, and anticipate effects on the infant's subsequent growth and development.

  • Wash hands before and after caring for each newborn.
  • Teach parents the importance of hand hygiene and demonstrate proper hand washing.
  • Use personal protective equipment (PPE) – gowns and gloves.
  • Handling and trauma to newborn skin should be minimized to prevent skin breakdown.
  • Separate supplies should be available and equipment should be sanitized frequently.
  • Know the purpose and side effects of intravenous (IV) fluids and antibiotics.
  • Assist parents with understanding the illness and treatment plan.
  • Provide emotional support as time in the NICU can be stressful and emotional.

Reference

Murray, S. S., McKinney, E. S., Holub, K. S., & Jones, R. (2019). Foundations of maternal-newborn and women's health nursing (7th ed.). Saunders.

Section 9: Explore: Transient Tachypnea of the Newborn

Transient tachypnea of the newborn (TTN) is a condition involving a mild degree of respiratory distress, resulting from inadequate absorption of the fetal lung fluid after birth. There is an inability to transition from intrauterine life.

Retention of lung fluid or transient pulmonary edema usually occurs within a few hours of birth and resolves by 24–48 hours.

Tab: Newborns at Risk
  • Lower gestational age
  • Cesarean section - compared to a vaginal delivery where physical forces squeeze the newborn's chest assisting fluid to be pushed out of the lungs, in cesarean section, this does not happen
  • Males more at risk than females
  • Prolonged or precipitous labor
Tab: Signs and Symptoms
  • Tachypnea; respiratory rate over 60
  • Mild cyanosis
  • Nasal flaring
  • Retractions
  • Grunting

Tab: Treatment
  • Oxygen, if cyanotic
  • Gavage feedings, if tachypnic, to prevent aspiration and fatigue from oral feedings
  • Observe for respiratory distress syndrome and sepsis

Section 11: Explore: Respiratory Distress Syndrome (Acquired Disorder)

Respiratory distress syndrome is a result of lung immaturity and lack of surfactant.

Use the WET FROG acronym to remember the signs and symptoms of a newborn experiencing respiratory distress. Select the tabs below for additional information.

Transcript — WET FROG

  • W = wheezing
  • E = effort (head bobbing contractions)
  • T = tachypnea (greater than 60); normal 30-60 RR
  • F = flaring
  • R = retractions
  • O = oxygenation (color best indication - cyanosis)
  • G = grunting
Tab: Newborns at Risk
  • The greatest risk factor is prematurity
  • Cesarean delivery
  • Asphyxia
  • Maternal diabetes
Tab: Treatment

Pre-delivery:

  • Steroids are administered to the pregnant client to accelerate fetal lung maturity and surfactant production, such as in pre-term labor or with diabetic mothers.

Post-delivery:

  • The disease is self-limiting, but nurses may administer exogenous surfactant and place the infant on positive end expiratory pressure (PEEP) ventilation.
  • Newborn should be in the sniffing position to support oxygenation.

Section 13: Explore: Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS)

Meconium aspiration syndrome (MAS) occurs when a newborn inhales particulate meconium mixed with amniotic fluid into the lungs while still in utero or upon taking the first breath after birth. The severity depends on the amount of meconium present in the amniotic fluid. Any newborn may pass meconium while in utero, but it most frequently occurs due to fetal hypoxia, which increases peristalsis and causes the anal sphincter to relax.

Select each of the tabs below to learn more.

Tab: Risk Factors
  • Hypoxia
  • Post-term delivery
  • Dystocia - prolonged or difficult labor
Tab: Signs and Symptoms
  • Tachypnea
  • Grunting
  • Cyanosis
  • Nasal flaring
  • Crackles
  • Skin or umbilical cord may be stained with a yellowish to greenish color
Tab: Prevention and Treatment

Pre-delivery:

  • Notify the healthcare provider if meconium stained fluid is noted during labor.
  • Amnioinfusion may be ordered by the healthcare provider to thin out thick meconium.
  • Additional assistance to be present at delivery is usually arranged (such as a respiratory therapist).
  • Ensure oxygen and suction equipment are ready for delivery.

Post-delivery:

  • Assist with care at time of delivery.
  • Observe closely for infection.

Section 15: Explore: Necrotizing Enterocolitis (Acquired Disorder)

Necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC) is the most common and most serious acquired gastrointestinal (GI) disease, particularly affecting pre-term infants. Shunting of oxygen away from the bowel to more important organs, such as the heart and brain, causes damage to the bowel wall and allows bacteria to invade the gut.

Necrosis usually occurs between 3-12 days of life. The exact cause of NEC is unknown.

Select each tab below for more information.

Tab: Nursing Role
  • To prevent NEC, encourage breastfeeding because NEC is less likely to occur
  • Detect early
    • Increased abdominal girth (distention)
    • Decreased or absent bowel sounds
    • Vomiting
    • Feeding intolerance (disinterest in eating)
    • May see blood in the stool
Tab: Treatment
  • Discontinuation of oral feedings
  • IV fluids and parenteral nutrition
  • Antibiotic therapy
  • Surgery may be needed

Section 16: Explore: Toxoplasmosis

Toxoplasmosis is an infection caused by a tiny parasite called Toxoplasma gondii. This one-celled parasite is most common in cats, but humans and other animals can also get infected. This infection can impact the pregnancy.

Tab: Prevention

To help prevent toxoplasmosis, educate clients to:

  • Cook meats thoroughly to kill germs.
  • Wash hands with soap and water after handling raw meat, unwashed vegetables, or gardening outside.
  • Wash all fruits and vegetables before serving and peel the skin.
  • Wash all utensils well with soap and warm water.
  • Don't drink untreated water or unpasteurized milk.
  • Teach families the importance of washing hands well and often to prevent infection.
  • Close outdoor sandboxes, especially overnight, to prevent wandering cats from using it as a litterbox.
Tab: Symptoms

Babies with congenital toxoplasmosis often don't have any symptoms at birth but problems might show up months or years later. Symptoms can range from mild to severe.

  • Vision problems and even blindness from injury to the retina (back of the eye)
  • Brain damage leading to developmental delays, seizures, hearing loss, limp muscle tone, or an unusually large or small head
  • Fever
  • Swollen glands
  • Jaundice (yellowed skin and eyes)
  • Rash
  • Problems with blood cells, such as anemia or thrombocytopenia
  • A large liver or spleen

The most severe cases of congenital toxoplasmosis happen when the mom is infected early in the pregnancy. This can lead to miscarriage or the baby might be born early or very small.

Section 1: Introduction to High-Risk Newborn With Congenital Conditions

Congenital anomalies comprise a wide range of abnormalities of body structure or function that are present at birth and are of prenatal origin. For efficiency and practicality, the focus is on major structural anomalies. These are defined as structural changes that have significant medical, social, or cosmetic consequences for the affected individual and typically require medical intervention. Examples include cleft lip, cleft palate, and spina bifida.

This concept will review some of these conditions and the role of the nurse in caring for these clients and their families.

Section 3: Explore: Congenital Anomaly Data

An estimated 295,000 newborns die within 28 days of birth every year worldwide due to congenital anomalies (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC], 2021). Congenital anomalies can contribute to long-term disability, chronic illness, and death during infancy or childhood. Caring for children with congenital anomalies may have significant impacts on individuals, families, healthcare systems, and societies. The most common and severe congenital anomalies are heart defects, neural tube defects, and Down syndrome.

Although congenital anomalies may be the result of one or more genetic, infectious, nutritional, or environmental factors, it is often difficult to identify the exact causes. These anomalies can impact a fetus externally, internally, and through chromosomal abnormalities.

Section 5: Explore: Causes of Congenital Anomalies

Although not all congenital anomalies can be linked to a specific cause, there are some known genetic, environmental, and other causes or risk factors.

Slide 1: Genetic Factors

Many congenital anomalies are linked to genetic factors either through inherited genes or changes in genes, called mutations. The prevalence of congenital anomalies increases when parents are relatives (consanguinity). Some ethnicities have a higher prevalence of specific genetic mutations, such as cystic fibrosis in the Finnish community.

Slide 2: Socioeconomic and Demographic Factors

Income level can have an indirect impact on the development of congenital anomalies through lack of access to nutritious foods or vitamins, lack of access to healthcare, or through exposure to environmental risk factors. For example, maternal lack of folic acid increases the risk of neural tube defects. Advanced maternal age also increases the risk of chromosomal abnormalities, such as Down syndrome.

Slide 3: Environmental Factors

Chemicals (such as pesticides), substances (such as alcohol, drugs, or tobacco), and radiation during pregnancy increase the risk of congenital anomalies. Working or living in locations with environmental risk factors increases the risk of exposure.

Slide 4: Infections

Some infections cause congenital anomalies, such as rubella and the Zika virus. Pregnant women are routinely screened for immunity to rubella during prenatal care. Zika virus was found to cause microcephaly in the developing fetus and campaigns were developed for prevention during pregnancy.

Section 6: Explore: Prevention Methods - Local and International

Public health initiatives strive to reduce the incidence of anomalies through education and eliminating risk factors. Select each item below to learn more.

Tab: Interventions and Education Topics

Important interventions and education topics include:

  • Healthy diets for girls—looking at access and quality of foods.
  • Supporting healthy weights and educating about supplements needed, such as folic acid for those of childbearing age.
  • Avoid drugs and other harmful substances.
  • Verifying infection rates of places when traveling away from home.
  • While pregnant, avoid chemicals and check the safety of the environment.
  • Diabetes risks and treatment; insulin and weight management.
  • Avoiding exposure to radiation, such as x-rays.
  • Vaccinations—flu; rubella—preventing infection and congenital anomalies.
  • Screenings for infections and encouraging treatments as needed.
Tab: Healthcare System Changes

Our healthcare system needs to improve access to:

  • Prevention—proper diet access, and access to providers
  • Health research—funding and expertise
  • Quality healthcare for those with congenital anomalies
  • Access for families to resources—education, specialists, and mental health support

Section 8: Explore: Family Support

Years ago, newborns with anomalies would be whisked away from their parents in the delivery room. The parents would be told about the anomaly after the fact and sometimes encouraged not to hold their baby.

Research has shown that parents experience less stress if they are told about an anomaly as soon as it is discovered (prenatally or post-delivery) and are permitted to hold their baby, when possible. Healthcare providers and nurses have become aware of the importance of helping parents accept and bond with their newborns.

With advancements in prenatal testing, many anomalies are often discovered early in the pregnancy. Ultrasounds and bloodwork can confirm and allow for ongoing surveillance throughout the pregnancy. The discovery of an anomaly evokes a grief response for the parents and extended family. The family mourns the loss of the perfect infant they imagined. They must detach from and mourn the baby they envisioned in order to attach to the infant that is theirs.

Nurses play a crucial role in caring for clients who have a fetus or newborn with an anomaly.

  • Use therapeutic communication techniques to help clients discuss their feelings and fears.
  • Inform and educate with as much information as possible ahead of the delivery.
  • Explain vaginal and cesarean sections to prepare clients for either type of delivery, based on the infant's needs.
  • Visit the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) so clients are more prepared and understand the type of care their infant will receive.
  • Staffing should support a 1:1 ratio so a nurse can remain with the client to provide support and care.
  • Encourage parents to participate in newborn care as much as possible and assist as needed.
  • Encourage the parents to hold the infant.

Section 10: Explore: Cleft Lip and Palate

Now, let's look at some congenital anomalies a bit closer.

Cleft lip and cleft palate are openings or splits in the upper lip, the roof of the mouth (palate), or both. Cleft lip and cleft palate result when facial structures that are developing in an unborn baby don't close completely.

Most cases of cleft lip and cleft palate are caused by an interaction of genetic and environmental factors. In many babies, a definite cause isn't discovered. Parents can pass on genes that cause cleft lip and palate.

Tab: Prevention
  • Women can't prevent cleft lip and palate from occurring (other than to avoid conceiving if they know they are a carrier).
  • Smoking and anti-seizure medications do increase the incidence of congenital anomalies, one being cleft lip and palate.
  • It is most often diagnosed prenatally.
Tab: Nursing Care
  • Experiment to find methods that work best for individual infants.
  • Breastfeeding (soft breast tissue fills in a small cleft of the lip or palate).
  • Use nipples with enlarged holes, compressible bottles, or special longer nipples.
  • Feed the infant in an upright position because milk enters nasal passages through the palate, causing an increased tendency to aspirate.
  • Feed the infant slowly with frequent stops to burp because infants tend to swallow excessive air.
  • Support and help the parents deal with the disappointment over the infant with an obvious anomaly. Show them before and after pictures of plastic surgery.
  • Reinforce the physician's explanation of plans for surgery.
  • Teach parents feeding techniques. Let them observe at first, then take over gradually.
  • Emphasize the need for long-term follow-up and support groups.

Section 11: Explore: Other Congenital Anomalies

[Figure: A large umbilical hernia in a premature newborn baby.]

Other congenital anomalies include:

Tab: Choanal Atresia

Choanal Atresia

  • Occurs with blockage or narrowing of one or both nasal passages by bone or tissue.
  • Newborns are nose breathers for the first month of life.
  • If blocked, an infant can experience respiratory distress.
  • Mucous or edema on the other nostril will increase the risk further.

Nurse's Role

  • Assess by closing the infant's mouth and occluding each nostril, one at a time.
  • Observe breath sounds and patency.
  • If blocked, observe for cyanosis when not crying but pink with crying (mouth is used during crying, allowing more air in).
Tab: Tracheoesophageal Fistula/Esophageal Atresia

Tracheoesophageal Fistula/Esophageal Atresia

  • Occurs with closure of the esophagus or a connection between the trachea and esophagus.
  • Newborns may choke or gag during the first feeding or some may become cyanotic.

Nurse's Role

  • Evaluate the infant's ability to suck, swallow, and breathe in a coordinated manner.
  • Stop the feeding immediately, suction as needed, and stimulate the infant to cry by rubbing the back.
Tab: Hernia

Hernia

  • Hernia occurs when intestinal muscles fail to close around the umbilicus.
  • The intestines protrude through the weak area.
  • It is more common in low-birth-weight, male, and African American infants.

Nurse's Role

  • Palpate the abdomen when the infant is relaxed and quiet. The abdomen should feel soft.
  • Immediate surgery is not typically required. As the child grows, it may resolve.

Section 13: Explore: Spina Bifida

Neural tube defects are a set of congenital anomalies that include spina bifida, meningocele, and myelomeningocele.

Spina bifida is the failure of the vertebral arch to close. Spina bifida occulta is the failure of the vertebra to close, usually without other anomalies. It is seen by a dimple on the back, which may have a tuft of hair over it.

Meningocele is a protrusion of meninges and spinal fluid through the spina bifida, covered by skin or a thin membrane. Because the spinal cord is not involved, paralysis does not occur.

Myelomeningocele is a protrusion of a membrane-covered sac through the spina bifida. The sac contains meninges, nerve roots, the spinal cord, and spinal fluid. The degree of paralysis depends on the location of the defect. The infant also may have hydrocephalus, or it may develop after surgery.

[Transcript — Spina Bifida (Open Defect): Image of spina bifida defect on newborn's lower back with inset showing vertebra, dura mater, spinal cord, and spinal fluid.]

Tab: Prevention

Folic acid supplements in pregnancy help prevent neural tube defects, especially if they are taken before conception and in early pregnancy.

Tab: Nursing Care

Educate women on the importance of dietary folate pre-pregnancy.

Recommend that women who are or might become pregnant take 400-800 mcg of folic acid per day, and that people with spina bifida or a family history of neural tube irregularities take 4,000 mcg per day. Those who are breastfeeding should aim to take around 500 mcg per day.

Section 1: Introduction to High Risk Newborn Development

Gestational age (or the duration of pregnancy) is measured in weeks from the first day of the mother’s last menstrual period to the day of delivery.

The average gestational age is 40 weeks (280 days). However, not all women with normal pregnancies deliver at exactly 40 weeks.

New gestational age designations were developed by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG, 2022) to further define the 5-week range considered "term".

  • late preterm is infants born between 34 and 36 6/7 weeks gestation

Infants born between 37 and 41 6/7 weeks gestation:

  • early term is infants born between 37 and 38 6/7 weeks gestation
  • full term is infants born between 39 and 40 6/7 weeks gestation
  • late term is infants born between 41 and 41 6/7 weeks gestation
Tab: Postterm

Infants born after 42 weeks gestation

Most infants are born at term and have the lowest risk of complications in the newborn period. Those that are not delivered at term may face complications and need assistance from nurses and providers.

Section 3: Explore: Preterm Infant

Baby Lauren was born at 33 weeks and 3 days. She would be considered a premature infant since she was born before 37 completed weeks of gestation. Her mom smoked and worked a high-stress job.

Preterm labor may be the result of one or more risk factors that include previous preterm labor, incompetent cervix, hypertension (chronic and gestational), being pregnant with multiples (twins or more), abdominal trauma or stress, poor prenatal care, and use of alcohol or drugs during pregnancy. Other causes can include being pregnant with a fetus that has known birth defects or conception by in vitro fertilization (IVF).

Born less than term (37 weeks), Lauren could face some acute and long-term health issues for which nurses must monitor, including temperature regulation, lower resistance to infection, an immature liver, elimination problems, and sensory-perceptual function changes.

Transcript — Preterm Infant Issues

  • Temperature regulation — preterm infants can’t maintain their temperature and often need an extra layer of clothing
  • Resistance to infections — preterm infants are more susceptible to germs and increased risk of infections
  • Immature liver — predisposes the infant to jaundice
  • Elimination problems — the intestinal bacteria can infiltrate the mucosa causing infection
  • Sensory-perceptual functions — preterm infants may face long-term vision issues

    • Monitor blood pressure, heart rate, and pulse pressure.

  • Maintain a neutral thermal environment.
  • Prevent infection.
    • Wear personal protective equipment (PPE).
    • Wash hands frequently.

Section 5: Explore: Late Preterm Issues

Baby Michael was born at 35 2/7 weeks. He would be considered a late preterm infant since he falls between 34 weeks and 36 6/7 weeks (ACOG, 2021). He may be cared for in the regular nursery or remain with his mom in her postpartum room. He will be looked at as developmentally mature with a low risk of morbidity by those who care for him.

The issues encountered by late preterm infants are caused by physiologic and metabolic immaturity. Late preterm infants are at higher risk of developing medical complications that result in increased rates of mortality and morbidity during birth hospitalization when compared to term infants. Michael will also have a higher rate of hospital readmission during the neonatal period than newborns born after 39 weeks.

Section 6: Explore: Small for Gestational Age

Baby Kyle was born on his due date at 40 weeks gestation, weighing 6 pounds. At a gestational age of 40 weeks, boys who weigh less than about 6 pounds 9 ounces (3 kilograms) are considered small for gestational age (SGA). Girls who weigh less than about 6 pounds 3 ounces (2.8 kilograms) are considered small for gestational age.

An SGA infant is one whose length, weight, and head circumference are below the 10th percentile of the normal variation for gestational age as determined by neonatal examination. The SGA infant may be preterm, term, or postterm. In Kyle's case, he was term.

Causes of SGA include both genetic abnormalities and maternal factors.

Tab: Genetic Abnormalities
  • genetic problems, cardiac anomalies, kidney deformities, or renal insufficiency
  • infection, such as toxoplasmosis, cytomegalovirus, or HIV
  • congenital anomalies, such as heart defects or neural tube defects
Tab: Maternal Factors
  • height, weight, age, parity, and ethnicity
  • maternal disease, including hypertension or preeclampsia
  • chronic maternal disease, such as inflammatory bowel disease, epilepsy, or anemia
  • lifestyle factors, including smoking, alcohol or drug use, and obesity
  • social stressors
  • multiple pregnancies
  • incorrect pregnancy dating

Some notes from Kyle's assessment include:

  • soft tissue wasting and dysmaturity
  • loose, dry, and scaling skin

Kyle is also at risk of:

  • perinatal asphyxia, impaired thermoregulation, hypoglycemia, polycythemia, and poor feeding and weight gain

Section 9: Explore: Large for Gestational Age

Large for gestational age (LGA) is an indication of a high prenatal growth rate, often defined as a weight (or length, or head circumference) that lies at or above the 90th percentile for that gestational age due to overproduction of growth hormone in utero.

Audrey was born today and weighs 4213 grams. She is 39 and 3/7 weeks old. Based on her weight, she is considered large for gestational age (4000-4500 g).

Audrey may be a big baby because her mom had diabetes (poorly controlled), obesity, or was a member of a certain ethnic group. Another possibility is that Audrey's parents could just be larger individuals.

In Audrey's situation, the mom is 6'2" and the partner is 6'4". The mom weighed about 180 pounds.

Tab: Audrey's Assessment

Audrey's assessment was completed and a fractured right clavicle was discovered due to the disproportionate size of the newborn to the birth passageway. This is a common complication with LGA infants and the nurse should:

  • Monitor and manage birth injuries and resulting complications.
  • Assess the infant for crepitus, hematoma, or deformity over the clavicle.
  • Note if there is decreased movement of the arm on the affected side.
  • Check Moro reflex.
  • Limit arm motion by pinning the infant's sleeve to the shirt.
  • Manage the pain.
Tab: Common Conditions of LGA

Other common conditions of LGA:

  • Facial nerve injury
    • Nursing assessment includes assessing for symmetry of the mouth while crying.
    • Clinical manifestations include wrinkles that are deeper on the unaffected side and the paralyzed side is smooth with a swollen appearance.
  • Phrenic nerve palsy
    • Assess for respiratory distress.
    • Clinical manifestations include asymmetric movement of the abdomen during respirations.
  • Additional fractures
    • Assess for pain, guarding, or lack of movement at the affected area.
    • Clinical manifestations often include local swelling.

Section 11: Explore: Postterm Infant

Postterm pregnancy extends beyond 42 weeks gestation. The postterm infant may be large for gestational age (LGA), average for gestational age (AGA), or small for gestational age (SGA), depending on the status of the placenta. As the placenta ages, it becomes unable to provide adequate oxygen to the fetus, resulting in late decelerations of the fetal heart rate.

Justin was born yesterday at 42 weeks and 2 days. There were concerns on his fetal monitoring tracing with late decelerations. His mom initially declined to be induced as she wanted minimal interventions but agreed to avoid complications with the baby.

Tab: Postterm Infant Assessment

Justin's newborn assessment showed:

  • dry peeling skin, also known as desquamation
  • creases that cover the sole
  • meconium stained, green-tinged skin
  • minimal vernix (cheesy substance) and lanugo (hair)
  • long hair and nails
  • 6 mm breast bud
Tab: Postterm Infant Nursing Care

Nursing actions include:

  • chest physiotherapy with suctioning completed to remove excess meconium and secretions
  • supplemental oxygen and respiratory support to stabilize
  • serial blood glucose measurements
  • feedings, breast or formula, to prevent hypoglycemia
  • skin cleaned and dried to maintain integrity
    • no creams or lotions applied
    • use of tape avoided
