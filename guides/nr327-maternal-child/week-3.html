Diabetes and Pregnancy

Week 3: Diabetes and Pregnancy

NR327 Maternal-Child Nursing — Unit 3, Module 4

Section 1: Introduction to Diabetes and Pregnancy

This module will discuss diabetes during pregnancy, the complications, and subsequent nursing implications.

Diabetes mellitus type 1 (DM1) or diabetes mellitus type 2 (DM2) that are diagnosed before pregnancy are known as pregestational diabetes mellitus or pre-existing diabetes mellitus. Gestational diabetes mellitus refers to diabetes that is diagnosed during the second or third trimester of pregnancy. Diabetes diagnosed in the first trimester should be considered pregestational diabetes mellitus as women may have undiagnosed DM2 prior to pregnancy.

Section 3: Explore: Gestational Diabetes: Risk Factors

Gestational diabetes mellitus is, just as the name suggests, diabetes mellitus that occurs during pregnancy. However, to distinguish it from possible pre-existing undiagnosed diabetes, it refers to diabetes that is diagnosed during the second or third trimester of pregnancy. The development of gestational diabetes results from the developing fetus increasing the mother's need for both glucose and insulin, and placental hormones causing insulin resistance.

Many women develop gestational diabetes without any known risk factors. Some women's bodies, particularly those with the following risk factors, are unable to meet this increased demand and develop diabetes mellitus that extends beyond their pregnancy.

Risk factors:

  • Older than age 25 at the time of pregnancy
  • First degree relative with diabetes
  • History of gestational diabetes
  • High risk ethnic groups (African American, Hispanic, American Indian, Asian American, or Pacific Islander)
  • Overweight, obese, or morbidly obese
  • History of polycystic ovary syndrome

Reference: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2022, April 5). Diabetes risk factors. https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/basics/risk-factors.html

Section 4: Explore: Gestational Diabetes: Diagnostic Tests

Women are typically screened for gestational diabetes between 24-28 weeks gestation, regardless of particular risk or history, as part of the provider's preferred practice. Some practitioners may only screen based on risk factors or symptomology.

Screening tools include the glucose challenge test (GCT) and the oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT).

Section 7: Explore: Complications of Gestational Diabetes Mellitus

With diabetes, both mother and fetus are at risk for complications, particularly if blood glucose levels are not controlled during pregnancy.

Fetal Complications:

  • Macrosomia: A mother with diabetes is at risk for having a fetus with macrosomia, meaning that the fetus is large in relation to gestational age. Macrosomia results from hyperglycemia that stimulates fetal insulin production, which in turn acts as a growth hormone.
  • Neonatal hypoglycemia following birth: The most common newborn complication after birth is hypoglycemia. Maternal insulin does not cross the placental barrier and the fetus produces insulin in utero for the present maternal glucose in circulation. Neonatal hyperinsulinemia after birth can then cause hypoglycemia when the maternal glucose is no longer in the fetal circulation. Closely related to hypoglycemia is newborn thermoregulation as hypothermia can be a sign of hypoglycemia in the newborn or hypothermia can contribute to hypoglycemia.
  • Neonatal hyperbilirubinemia following birth: The excess blood glucose causes damage to red blood cells, which leads to excess bilirubin in the circulation as well. This is manifested as jaundice in the newborn.
  • Respiratory distress syndrome: Neonatal hyperinsulinemia reduces cortisol production, which can have an effect on the surfactant level, which is responsible for keeping the alveoli open after birth. This is further complicated if the newborn is also born prematurely, which affects lung maturity because surfactant has not reached adequate levels.

Maternal Complications:

  • Delivery and postpartum: A larger fetus is more difficult to deliver vaginally and can lead to complications with vaginal delivery, such as shoulder dystocia, injury to maternal tissues, or caesarian section. In addition to a large fetus, the woman with diabetes may also have excessive amounts of amniotic fluid (hydramnios). A large fetus or hydramnios can also lead to postpartum hemorrhage related to an overextended uterus that can cause uterine atony.
  • Infections: Increased risk for infection, such as urinary tract and vaginal infections, are often associated with diabetes due to the excess circulating glucose. Infections must be quickly treated as some raise a risk of ketoacidosis or preterm birth.
  • Other: Women with diabetes are also at increased risk for hypertension and preeclampsia, particularly if vascular impairment is present. Pre-existing diabetes increases these risks and ketoacidosis complications.

Section 10: Explore: Nursing Implications

Nursing assessment and care of a woman with gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is similar, although not identical, to the care of any patient with diabetes mellitus (DM). The primary focus in both GDM and DM includes monitoring blood glucose levels and maintaining them through diet, exercise, and pharmacological support. An additional focus with GDM is monitoring the fetal well-being throughout the pregnancy and after transition to extrauterine life.

Tab: Nursing Support and Education

A pregnant woman newly diagnosed with gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) will need nursing support on:

  • Understanding how GDM will impact the plan of care both during and after pregnancy, as well as the plan of care for the newborn.
  • Taking an active role in monitoring and maintaining normal glucose levels to minimize complications.
  • Need for additional fetal surveillance, such as kick counts, ultrasound, nonstress test, biophysical profile, or fetal lung maturity.
  • Understanding pharmacological management, if part of a treatment plan. Insulin needs change throughout the pregnancy and postpartum, so frequent adjustments to the regimen may be necessary.
  • Typically, insulin needs are lowest during the first trimester and after delivery, while the insulin needs increase during the second and third trimester. During labor, treatment is often with a continuous IV infusion of regular insulin in order to keep glucose levels between 80-110 mg/dL (or per agency policy), to prevent neonatal hypoglycemia following birth.
  • Time and space for freely sharing concerns and feelings.
Remember! Insulin does not cross the placental barrier and reach the fetus. Insulin needs will change during pregnancy, so dosage adjustments may be needed as the pregnancy progresses.
Tab: Monitoring

The nurse will closely monitor for the impact of gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) on the fetus and newborn.

  • During pregnancy:
    • Fetal macrosomia is the most common fetal/newborn complication of GDM. Fetal size will be monitored throughout pregnancy through fundal height measurements and ultrasound. Often, more frequent prenatal visits are included in the plan of care.
  • After birth:
    • The most common newborn complication after birth is hypoglycemia. Maternal insulin does not cross the placental barrier and the fetus produces insulin in utero for the present maternal glucose in circulation. Neonatal hyperinsulinemia after birth can then cause hypoglycemia when the maternal glucose is no longer in the fetal circulation.
    • The nurse will closely monitor the newborn's intake and output and blood glucose levels.
    • Closely monitoring body temperature and ensuring that the newborn is skin-to-skin or remains wrapped in blankets. This is especially important with newborns born to mothers with GDM. Hypothermia can be a sign of hypoglycemia and hypothermia can cause hypoglycemia.
    • Other complications to monitor are jaundice and respiratory distress syndrome. Neonatal hyperinsulinemia reduces cortisol production, which can have an effect on the surfactant level, which is responsible for keeping the alveoli open after birth. This is further complicated if the newborn is also born prematurely, which also affects lung maturity because surfactant has not reached adequate levels.

Hypertension and Pregnancy

Week 3: Hypertension and Pregnancy

Section 1: Introduction to Hypertension and Pregnancy

Women who have chronic hypertension prior to pregnancy will experience a worsening of the condition and an increased likelihood of developing preeclampsia in pregnancy. Gestational hypertension (GH) is brought on by pregnancy and develops after 20 weeks gestation without the presence of protein in the urine.

This module will discuss hypertension brought on by pregnancy and the nursing implications.

Section 3: Explore: Gestational Hypertension

Gestational hypertension develops during pregnancy after 20 weeks of gestation in a client who was previously normotensive. The mother will present with systolic blood pressure ≥ 140 mm Hg or a diastolic blood pressure ≥ 90 mm Hg. Some clients have chronic hypertension and the hormones and physical demands brought on by pregnancy will likely worsen the condition, possibly leading to preeclampsia, or even eclampsia. Gestational hypertension was previously called pregnancy-induced hypertension (PIH).

Different from chronic hypertension, gestational hypertension has these characteristics.

  • It is a hypertensive condition unique to pregnancy, whether the woman has a history of hypertension or not.
  • It is brought on by pregnancy.
  • It develops after 20 weeks gestation without the presence of protein in the urine.
  • It will resolve within 6-12 weeks postpartum.

Interactive: Hypertension in Pregnancy

Gestational Hypertension Risk Factors:

  • Primigravida
  • African American race
  • Age (< 18 and > 35 increased occurrence)
  • Personal and/or family history of hypertension
  • Obesity
  • Diabetes
  • Gestational trophoblastic disease

If gestational hypertension progresses and worsens, preeclampsia and eclampsia can develop.

Preeclampsia and Eclampsia — Progression Scenario:

  • A woman who is not pregnant has chronic hypertension.
  • She is now 10 weeks pregnant and has a blood pressure of 150/80. She does not have gestational hypertension but is at risk for developing it later in pregnancy.
  • She is now 25 weeks pregnant. Her blood pressure is 150/80 but she has no other symptoms. She has gestational hypertension.
  • She is now 28 weeks pregnant. Her blood pressure is 150/80 and she has 2+ protein in her urine. The gestational hypertension has progressed to preeclampsia.

Section 4: Explore: Preeclampsia and Eclampsia

Tab: Preeclampsia & Eclampsia

When gestational hypertension progresses into preeclampsia, which can be mild or severe, high blood pressure temporarily impairs the kidneys' ability to filter albumin (a protein found in the blood), causing protein to appear in the urine (proteinuria). Kidney impairment also leads to generalized edema. Other body systems are affected, including:

  • Intravascular volume is reduced as fluid moves out of the vessels (hemoconcentration), resulting in increased blood viscosity and tissue edema.
  • Decreased liver perfusion leads to impaired liver function and elevated liver enzymes.
    • Hepatic edema causes epigastric or right upper quadrant abdominal pain.
  • Cerebral edema leads to increased central nervous system irritability, causing headaches, hyperreflexia, ankle clonus, and seizures.
  • Decreased blood flow to the retina causes visual disturbances and blurred vision.

When preeclampsia progresses into the very serious condition of eclampsia (formerly known as toxemia), seizures occur.

Reference: A.D.A.M. (n.d.). Preeclampsia [Video]. U.S. National Library of Medicine, MedlinePlus. https://medlineplus.gov/ency/anatomyvideos/000102.htm

Tab: Case Study

Meet Mary, a 17-year-old gravida 1, para 0 (G1P0) at 29 weeks' gestation who has arrived for her antepartum visit. Mary is African American and has no personal or family history of hypertension or diabetes. She is 5'4" and currently weighs 210 pounds. Her baseline blood pressure (BP) has been around 120/75.

Today, Mary's BP is 140/82. Her urine does not contain protein. We know that Mary is at risk for gestational hypertension (GH), preeclampsia or eclampsia because of her age, her primigravida pregnancy and gestational age being over 20 weeks, her African American race, and her obesity. At this point, Mary has likely developed GH. Her BP is elevated, she has several risk factors, but she does not have protein in her urine.

Mary comes back for another visit at 33 weeks' gestation. At this point, her BP is 160/92. Her face and ankles are swollen, she has gained 4 pounds since her last visit, and she has 1+ proteinuria. Mary has likely now progressed to mild preeclampsia.

Characteristics of mild preeclampsia:

  • Blood pressure: increases in pregnancy; ≥ 140/90 mm Hg
  • Edema: face, hands, ankles, periorbital edema (more ominous finding)
  • Weight: 2nd trimester increase > 3 pounds or more per month; 3th trimester increase > 1 pound per week
  • Urine: 1+ to 2+ protein; output is at least 500 mL/24 h

Mary could also develop severe preeclampsia; symptoms may appear suddenly.

Characteristics of severe preeclampsia:

  • Blood pressure: ≥ 160/110 mm Hg x 2 (6 hours apart), even when on bedrest
  • Edema: more obvious
  • Weight: increases dramatically; 1-2 pounds gained in 3-7 days
  • Urine: 3+ to 4+ protein; output < 500 mL/24 h
  • Headache, blurred vision, and epigastric discomfort
  • Hyperreflexia and clonus (indicates a seizure is likely to occur)

The most severe form of preeclampsia is eclampsia. If Mary's condition progresses to that point, she will actually begin having tonic-clonic seizures.

This type of seizure activity is characterized by tonic (muscular tension) and clonic (spasms, muscular contractions) phases. They are often followed by a coma and may lead to death.

Section 6: Explore: Hypertension in Pregnancy — Nursing Care

The nurse assesses for the common clinical manifestations that can develop as a result of chronic hypertension and those that are unique to hypertension as a result of pregnancy. These symptoms can cue the nurse to recognize the condition may be worsening, as high blood pressure impacts the body systemically over time.

A woman with gestational hypertension (GH) will have elevated blood pressure but proteinuria will not be present. The nurse will monitor the blood pressure and assess for symptoms of progression to preeclampsia that impact many body systems.

Tab: Nursing Actions

The nurse should monitor and assess:

Blood pressure

  • Measure at least every 4 hours.
  • Blood pressure will typically be ≥ 140 mm Hg systolic or ≥ 90 mm Hg diastolic.

I&O, edema, and daily weights

  • Renal perfusion may be decreased due to high blood pressure.
  • Decreased output
  • Excessive or rapid weight gain accompanied by edema indicates fluid retention.
  • Edema amount and pitting/non-pitting

Protein in the urine

  • ≥ 1+ protein in the urine and/or ≥ 0.3 g protein in a 24-hour urine specimen

Deep tendon reflexes

  • Measured using a scale that indicates the response when the reflex is tested
  • Reflexes become increasingly hyperactive as preeclampsia progresses and are an indicator of impending seizures.
    • 4+ hyperactive, brisk with intermittent clonus (take note of how many beats of clonus occur)
    • 3+ brisk, slightly hyperactive
    • 2+ active, normal
    • 1+ sluggish, diminished
    • 0 no response

Other symptoms

  • Symptoms that may indicate arterial spasms and retinal edema: blurred vision, double vision, spots before the eyes
  • Symptoms that may indicate impending seizure activity: continuous headache, drowsiness, mental confusion, epigastric pain, or upset stomach (a particularly ominous sign)
Tab: Nursing Care

In addition to close monitoring of the client, the nurse will:

  • Administer medications as prescribed.
  • Maintain seizure prevention and precautions.
  • Provide emotional support, comfort measures, and a calm, quiet environment. Clients may be hospitalized during pregnancy at any time, and it may last through and/or after delivery. This can mean many weeks of hospitalized bed rest and is very stressful for the client, particularly if other children are at home and she is missing work.
  • Assess fetal well-being through monitoring of fetal heart rate, pattern, and daily kick counts (fetal activity).

Section 8: Explore: Diagnostic Tests and Treatment

What kind of diagnostic testing and medical management would be done for Mary? In addition to analyzing her assessment findings, her healthcare provider will also look for other diagnostic cues. Recall from previous knowledge how high blood pressure impacts the human body, restricting blood flow to key areas such as the heart, kidneys and liver. In addition, blood flow is restricted to the fetus. Diagnostic testing looks for these impacts on the body systems.

Diagnostic tests:

  • Serum testing: hematocrit, blood urea nitrogen, complete blood cell count, clotting studies, liver enzymes, platelet count
  • Urine: specific gravity and protein, 24-hour urine collection (measure creatinine and protein clearance)
  • Fetal well-being and lung maturity (if labor induction becomes necessary): amniocentesis, ultrasound scan, nonstress tests, contraction stress tests, biophysical profiles

Medical management:

Treatment of Mary's preeclampsia will be like treating hypertension in any client, except that in pregnancy, the health of the fetus is a factor too. Mary's healthcare provider will likely treat her with:

  • Activity restrictions and frequent rest; a left lateral recumbent position promotes best perfusion to the fetus
  • Hospitalization, depending on severity, with IV access available
  • Well-balanced diet with adequate protein; avoid high sodium foods
  • Severe preeclampsia or eclampsia IV medications: magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄) to prevent seizure activity, antihypertensives for BP > 160/100 mm Hg

Prognosis:

The only cure for preeclampsia and gestational hypertension is delivery of the baby, and even 6 weeks later the mother may have residual effects (bleeding problems, liver rupture, stroke, and death).

Section 10: Explore: Seizure Precautions

Preeclampsia can progress to the most severe form, eclampsia, in which seizures are very likely, often followed by a coma or even death. For women with severe preeclampsia, seizure precautions must be taken.

Tab: Seizure Precautions

Seizure precautions include:

  • Noise and stress reduction in the environment
  • Padded side rails
  • Preparation for emergency delivery; cesarean may be needed quickly
  • Keep suction and an oxygen mask nearby
  • Prepare to administer emergency seizure medication:
    • hydralazine — antihypertensive vasodilator
    • magnesium sulfate — Central Nervous System (CNS) depressants
    • magnesium sulfate antidote — calcium gluconate, in case of toxicity of magnesium sulfate
Tab: In the Event of a Seizure

In the event of a seizure:

  • Do not leave the client unattended and press the call light for additional help.
  • Turn the client to the left lateral position for perfusion and aspiration prevention.
  • Mentally note when the seizure started and the phases.
  • Monitor the client's airway, providing supplemental oxygen by mask at 8-10 L/minute.
  • Monitor oxygen saturation (SpO2).
  • Suction the mouth and nose after the seizure to prevent aspiration.
  • Observe fetal heart rate and pattern for non-reassuring signs (increased/decreased rate and/or variability, late decelerations), which may recover with the use of oxygen.
  • Notify the healthcare provider.

Section 12: Explore: HELLP Syndrome

Severe preeclampsia can develop into what is known as HELLP syndrome. It has unique diagnostic characteristics showing severe liver impairment and occurs in up to 12% of preeclampsia cases (Cleveland Clinic, 2021).

Nursing assessment for early detection and treatment is crucial to prevent severe complications and health risks. Indications of HELLP syndrome include:

  • Peripheral blood smear will reveal hemolysis of red blood cells
  • Development of thrombocytopenia (platelets < 100,000/mm³)
  • Development of abnormal elevations of the liver enzymes alanine aminotransferase (ALT) or aspartate aminotransferase (AST)
  • Severe continuous pain in the right upper quadrant or the epigastric area described as a "stabbing" pain over the region of the liver
  • Malaise and flu-like symptoms that worsen at night

Treatment is the same as with preeclampsia, along with the possible need for blood transfusion if bleeding occurs and persists. Preterm birth may be needed.

For more information regarding preeclampsia for healthcare providers: Preeclampsia Foundation — https://www.preeclampsia.org/healthcare-providers

Remember HELLP mnemonic:

  • H: hemolysis
  • EL: elevated liver enzymes
  • LP: low platelet count (less than 100,000/mm³)

Reference: Cleveland Clinic. (2021). Preeclampsia. https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/17952-preeclampsia

Week 3: Infections and Pregnancy

NR327 Maternal-Child Nursing — Unit 3, Module 3

Section 1: Introduction to Infections and Pregnancy

In this concept, we will discuss the nursing implications related to infections that develop in pregnancy and how different kinds of infections put the mother and fetus at risk.

Section 3: Explore: Infection and Pregnancy

When the mother has an infection in pregnancy, it often impacts the fetus as well. However, not all infections in the mother reach the fetus but may still impact fetal well-being even if the mother is only mildly ill.

Pregnant women are more susceptible to vaginal infections due to hormone related anatomical changes, and some infections in the female can cause lifelong infertility.

The acronym TORCH is used to group certain organisms that can cross the placenta and reach the fetus.

Slide 1: T = Toxoplasmosis

Toxoplasmosis is contracted from eating raw, contaminated meats or coming into contact with the feces of infected cats. Pregnant women do not need to give up their cats but should not change the cat litter prior to or during pregnancy.

Toxoplasmosis symptoms may not appear but can be passed on to the fetus and cause serious symptoms later in life, including blindness and mental disability. To prevent toxoplasmosis, pregnant women should avoid uncooked meats and contact with cat feces.

Slide 2: O = Other Diseases
  • Hepatitis A is caused by poor hand washing after defecation. Exposure in early pregnancy is most serious for the fetus. A preventive hepatitis A vaccine may be ordered by the pregnant woman's healthcare provider.
  • Hepatitis B is caused by bodily fluid exchange. The hepatitis B vaccine is routinely given to all mothers and newborns. If the mother has hepatitis B, the newborn is at highest risk of becoming infected with hepatitis B during vaginal delivery. Hepatitis B can cause lifelong complications such as liver problems and cancer.
  • Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) is transmitted from the mother to the fetus through the placenta. Prevention of transmission can be accomplished by administering antiretroviral medication during pregnancy.
  • Vaginal infections caused by sexually transmitted diseases, such as syphilis, gonorrhea, and chlamydia, seriously impact the fetus when contracted during vaginal birth. Active vaginal infections may require a cesarean delivery to avoid passing the infection on to the newborn.
Slide 3: R = Rubella

Rubella virus can be contracted by coming into contact with an unvaccinated person. Transmission to the fetus in the first trimester, when development is underway, causes serious congenital defects and fetal death. Rubella immunity is checked routinely during antepartal care. If the client is found to be nonimmune, she must be cautious to avoid exposure, and the vaccine will be given to her postpartum and prior to discharge.

Slide 4: C = Cytomegalovirus

Cytomegalovirus (CMV) is a very common virus, and the infected person may not even exhibit symptoms. It is spread by means such as close contact, kissing, and breastfeeding. CMV infection during pregnancy can cause serious birth defects. The best means for preventing contraction is to reduce contact with saliva and urine from babies and young children.

Slide 5: H = Herpes Genitalis

Herpes genitalis causes painful genital lesions that can infect the fetus in the uterus if the amniotic membranes rupture when there are active lesions. Cesarean delivery is done when there are active lesions present.

Section 6: Explore: HIV and Pregnancy

For a pregnant woman living with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), it is no longer an inevitability that the infection will be passed on to the fetus. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2021), "advances in HIV research, prevention, and treatment have made it possible for many women with HIV to give birth to babies who are free of HIV" (para. 3). Since the early 1990s, the annual number of perinatal HIV transmissions in the U.S. has declined more than 95% (CDC, 2021). Perinatal HIV infections vary by race in the United States.

HIV testing is vital to reduce the rate of children born with HIV. Being tested prior to pregnancy can prevent transmission, and HIV testing is part of routine perinatal care in the United States (CDC, 2021). The CDC (2021) also states that a pregnant woman living with HIV can take medication to reduce the viral load of HIV to an undetectable level, minimizing the risk of perinatal transmission, as long as the medication is taken strictly as directed throughout the pregnancy and childbirth. Also, a cesarean delivery can help prevent perinatal HIV transmission (CDC, 2021). The CDC recommends that mothers do not breastfeed newborns to prevent HIV transmission after delivery (2021).

This is all good news, of course, but think about those who are struggling economically. Can they afford infant formula? Pregnant women with HIV who do not have access to healthcare will not have access to HIV medications. As we have learned, access to healthcare is a social determinant of health, particularly for certain racial groups in the United States.

Section 7: Explore: TORCH Infections - Nursing Implications

The nursing implications of caring for women and newborns impacted by TORCH infections (organisms that can cross the placenta and reach the fetus) include:

T: Toxoplasmosis

Assess the woman for fatigue, muscle pains, and swollen glands. If the fetus survives pregnancy, assess the newborn for jaundice and increased bleeding.

O: Other Infections

  • Hepatitis A: Practice proper hand hygiene, particularly to prevent transfer of fecal matter.
  • Hepatitis B: The vaccination of newborns of non-positive mothers is routine. For newborns of mothers who are HBsAg-positive, the nurse will administer the hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG) vaccine prior to the routine Hepatitis B vaccine.
  • Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV): The nurse will assess fetal well-being in HIV positive women and may administer antiviral medications. Newborns of HIV positive mothers will be asymptomatic at birth and may not test positive for the virus. These newborns should not breastfeed because the virus can enter through the breastmilk.
  • Vaginal infections such as bacterial vaginosis or those caused by sexually transmitted diseases such as syphilis, gonorrhea, and chlamydia:
    • Bacterial vaginosis - Assess for vaginal discharge, itching and burning, and treat with medication as prescribed.
    • Syphilis - Administer antibiotic during pregnancy (typically penicillin); may cross the placenta in pregnancy so monitor for signs of congenital syphilis in the newborn (skin lesions, enlarged liver, pneumonia, hepatitis).
    • Gonorrhea (administer antibiotic during pregnancy, typically a cephalosporin) and chlamydia, which often appear together - Monitor for signs of ophthalmic infection in the newborn which will likely be treated with antibiotic eye ointment.

R: Rubella

If the fetus of a mother with active rubella survives to term or the newborn's mother is not immune to rubella, assess for infection in the newborn (fever, malaise, and a maculopapular rash on the face and body).

C: Cytomegalovirus (CMV)

Symptoms in pregnancy are vague and may be missed. Assess for infection in newborn present at birth (growth restrictions, jaundice, and microcephaly) and assist with diagnosis by obtaining urine specimen for culturing.

H: Herpes Genitalis (herpes simplex virus [HSV])

Assess for signs of preterm labor, intrauterine growth retardation (IUGR), and active vaginal lesions which appear as papules, vesicles, or shallow ulcers, and eventually crust over. Assess the neonate born vaginally to a woman with active lesions for signs of infection, lethargy, jaundice, and seizures.

Section 9: Explore: Chorioamnionitis

Chorioamnionitis is an infection of the chorion and amnion (the amniotic membrane or sac) and the amniotic fluid usually caused by bacterial and viral organisms.

Most often, the infection results from the amniotic fluid being ruptured for a prolonged period of time. The longer the barrier surrounding and protecting the fetus from the outside world (and the organisms in the mother's vagina, anus or urinary tract) is ruptured, the longer these organisms can proliferate and cause infection and inflammation. Once the amniotic membranes have been ruptured for more than 24 hours, the risks to both mother and fetus increase more and more.

The nurse's clock begins ticking once the amniotic fluid has ruptured. It is important to note when it occurs in order to monitor the increasing risk for chorioamnionitis. The woman's temperature and heart rate will be monitored regularly for increases that may be signs of infection. Her abdomen may become tender to touch and foul-smelling vaginal discharge or leaking of amniotic fluid may be present.

It is best to deliver the fetus before infection sets in and increases the risk for additional issues for both mother and fetus. Amniocentesis can diagnose chorioamnionitis if the membranes are not ruptured; otherwise, the leaking amniotic fluid can be tested to diagnose. Antibiotics will likely be administered in addition to a speedy delivery.

The nurse must use excellent standard precautions and use personal protective equipment (PPE) to avoid further spreading of the infection. After delivery, the newborn may require additional testing and perhaps antibiotics to prevent or treat the infection.

Section 10: Explore: Infection and Pregnancy - Nursing Implications

There are a lot of opportunities for exposure to blood and bodily fluids when providing antepartal and intrapartal care. Many organisms can be transmitted to the fetus in pregnancy and to the newborn during delivery. Many of these are spread by means of bodily fluids. Universal precautions are key to nursing care in all areas. The nurse plays a key role in preventing the spread of infection.

To prevent infection spread and exposure to these fluids as part of antepartal, labor, and delivery care, the nurse uses personal protective equipment (PPE) in situations such as:

  • During amniotomy and delivery, the nurse wears clean gloves along with a mask, gown, and glasses.
  • When assisting with breastfeeding (or touching the breasts for assessment, for example), the nurse wears clean gloves.
  • When touching the perineal area to assist with perineal care or assessing a vaginal laceration or episiotomy, the nurse wears clean gloves.
  • Before the newborn's first bath, the nurse wears clean gloves when handling the newborn and with all diaper changes.
  • When suctioning or resuscitating the newborn, the nurse wears a facial barrier.

The use of internal monitoring electrodes, particularly the spiral fetal scalp electrode that actually penetrates the scalp, also introduces bacteria from the outside into the warm, moist, dark, uterine environment. To help prevent intrauterine infection, healthcare providers will put in effort in allowing the amniotic membranes to remain intact for as long as possible. This membrane is an infection barrier for the fetus against many organisms. Once ruptured, delivery should occur within 24 hours regardless of the presence of a known infection. A woman who has an episiotomy and/or laceration, or has had a cesarean birth, is obviously more at risk for infection. However, the infection is relatively uncommon.

Newborns are also at risk for infection, particularly from having come into contact with maternal uterine or vaginal bacteria or viruses. One method to prevent eye infections in newborns is the use of prophylactic erythromycin (antibiotic) ophthalmic ointment or drops. They are administered in each of the newborn's eyes to prevent infection from exposure to bodily fluids during vaginal delivery.

Section 12: Explore: Pregnancy Infection Prevention

Nurses can help prevent pregnancy infection by providing client education.

Tab: Vaccinations
  • Rubella immunization should be obtained before a woman's childbearing years as part of the standard MMR vaccine in childhood. If this vaccination was not obtained and the woman is found to be rubella non-immune as part of routine antepartum testing, she should receive the vaccination postpartum.
  • Hepatitis B immunization and vaccination will also be part of antepartum and postpartum care.
  • The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2021) also recommends that pregnant women receive the flu vaccine.
Tab: Safe Sex

Women should be encouraged to use safe sex practices to avoid contracting sexually transmitted diseases and hepatitis B.

Tab: Toxoplasmosis

Pregnant women should avoid contact with raw meat and cat litter to prevent toxoplasmosis.

Tab: Cytomegalovirus

Pregnant women should avoid or use caution when in close contact with others to avoid infections such as cytomegalovirus (e.g., kissing, breasts are being touched while breastfeeding).

Tab: Vaginal Infections

Women with vaginal infections and Herpes genitalis lesions should use measures such as sitz baths, cotton underwear, avoidance of sexual contact during outbreaks, and keeping the genital area dry.

Additional CDC recommendations:

  • To prevent bacteria and viral transmission, particularly hepatitis A, use proper hand hygiene with soap and water after using the bathroom, handling pets, being around people who are sick, touching soil or dirt, and changing diapers.
  • Listeria is another infectious disease of concern during pregnancy which can be prevented by avoiding unpasteurized (raw) milk and foods (feta, brie, etc.).
  • Group B strep status will be evaluated towards the end of pregnancy. This bacteria is commonly carried in the vagina without a woman's knowledge. A woman who is Group B strep positive can pass this infection to the newborn during vaginal delivery. Prophylactic antibiotics will be administered to these women and the newborns will be monitored closely.

Week 3: Nursing Care: High-Risk Mother

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Care of the High-Risk Mother

Numerous maternal and fetal adaptations must occur in an orderly sequence for the childbearing process to be considered normal. If physiologic or emotional problems develop, complications can arise, threatening the well-being of the mother and/or the developing fetus.

Infant mortality rate in the United States is surprisingly high, mostly associated with prematurity, birth defects, maternal complications, sudden infant death syndrome, and birth-related injuries.

Women experiencing high-risk pregnancy require additional medical interventions and close observation to limit complications. Nurses play a key role through:

  • identifying risk factors
  • providing education
  • implementing quality care

In this lesson, we will explore some of the risk factors and causes of high-risk pregnancy including maternal pregestational and gestational conditions.

Section 3: Danger Signs During Pregnancy

The woman's body will naturally respond and adapt to a normal, healthy pregnancy. However, complications interfere with normal adaptation.

Let's discuss some signs in pregnancy that may indicate the presence of a problem requiring medical attention. The nurse will assess for these signs throughout the antepartum period and, when documented in the electronic medical record, many of these will trigger a maternal early warning (MEWS) alert requiring specific follow-up interventions. The healthcare provider may order various laboratory and diagnostic testing to analyze the symptoms. It is important for the nurse to educate the pregnant woman regarding these warning signs and to emphasize the need to report them. For more information regarding maternal early warning (MEWS) criteria and interventions, visit the Council on Patient Safety in Women's Health Care (n.d.).

Section 5: High-Risk Pregnancy: Common Causes

Most pregnancies are considered low-risk; however, sometimes preexisting factors or gestational complications develop that increase pregnancy risk.

Many psychosocial, medical, and obstetric factors directly impact the health of a pregnancy. Listed below are the most common risk factors impacting pregnancy. For more information regarding providing support for women after a severe maternal event, visit the Council on Patient Safety in Women's Health Care (2020).

Tab: Genetics and Congenital Anomalies

Genetic abnormalities may interfere with normal fetal development and impact the health of the pregnancy, such as Down syndrome, heart defects, and neural tube defects.

Explore: For more information regarding common birth defects, visit the March of Dimes to learn more.

Tab: Financial and Socioeconomic Burdens

Financial and socioeconomic burdens may prevent some clients from receiving adequate prenatal care, contributing to the risk of complications.

Tab: Impaired Nutritional Status

Impaired nutritional status of the mother will impact fetal growth and development. Adolescents and clients with socioeconomic burdens are most at risk for nutritional deficiencies. Obesity in pregnancy may cause complications as well. Adequate weight gain, determined by prepregnancy BMI, and consumption of sufficient nutrients are essential to support the health of both the mother and the fetus.

Tab: Maternal Behaviors

Maternal behaviors such as smoking, drinking alcohol, and drug use increase pregnancy risks. When a pregnant woman uses a substance, the fetus experiences the same systemic effects but often more severely and for a longer time.

  • Tobacco is the most common harmful substance used by pregnant women. Nicotine causes vasoconstriction and reduces blood flow and oxygen through the placenta.
  • Alcohol consumption can cause both mental delays and birth defects in the developing fetus. Intake during the first trimester has the largest negative impact. However, no amount of alcohol consumption at any time is considered safe during pregnancy.
  • Pregnant women abusing opioids and amphetamines has increased dramatically over the last decade. Increased education and close monitoring is necessary.
Tab: Age

Adolescents and women of advanced maternal age have increased pregnancy risks.

  • Teenage pregnancy increases the risk for pregnancy-related hypertension, anemia, poor nutrition, preterm labor and birth, depression, substance abuse, and intimate partner violence.
  • Delayed pregnancy increases the risk for miscarriage, multiple gestations, and chromosomal abnormalities, such as Down syndrome.
Tab: Multifetal Pregnancies

Multifetal pregnancy exerts an increased burden on the mother's body systems and increases the risk for various complications, including pregnancy-related hypertension, anemia, preterm labor and birth, and postpartum hemorrhage.

Tab: Environmental Pathogens

Environmental factors contribute to pregnancy risks through chemical exposures via air, water, soil, food, and other consumer products. The physiologic changes that occur in pregnancy increase sensitivity to chemicals that may impact the pregnancy and the woman's long-term health (Boyles et al., 2021).

Section 6: Explore: Hyperemesis Gravidarum

Hyperemesis gravidarum refers to severe nausea and vomiting lasting beyond the first trimester, causing dehydration, nutritional deficits, weight loss, and fluid and electrolyte imbalances. Some women require hospitalization because the severe, chronic vomiting can lead to acid-base imbalance, and kidney and cardiac dysfunction. Left untreated, hyperemesis gravidarum may lead to fetal death.

The human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) hormone causes mild nausea and vomiting in the first trimester of pregnancy. In hyperemesis gravidarum, the HCG levels are very high or rise rapidly.

Medical treatment of hyperemesis gravidarum focuses on preventing severe vomiting and restoring physiologic balance using intravenous (IV) hydration and possibly enteral nutrition.

Pharmacologic treatment may include:

  • pyridoxine (vitamin B6)
  • doxylamine
  • metoclopramide
  • ondansetron
  • proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole)
  • antacids (calcium carbonate)
  • H2 histamine blockers (famotidine)

Nurses will:

  • Administer the medications as ordered.
  • Encourage small, frequent meals consisting of bland, non-greasy foods.
  • Teach other ways to counteract nausea.
  • Provide empathy and compassion.

Section 7: Ectopic Pregnancy

Ectopic pregnancy (sometimes called a tubal pregnancy) develops in the early stages of conception when the zygote implants in the fallopian tube (or other areas) rather than the uterus. The embryo continues to develop and grow until the narrow fallopian tube can no longer accommodate it.

Tab: Pathophysiology

Pathophysiology of Ectopic Pregnancy

  • Fertilization of the ova occurs in the fallopian tube. The zygote (first cell of a new person) travels from the fallopian tube to the uterus, which takes 3 - 4 days.
  • During this time, the zygote is rapidly undergoing mitosis (cell division) and becomes a blastocyst consisting of a circular mass of cells with a fluid-filled cavity in the middle that is surrounded by 2 cell layers.
  • If the zygote is prevented from leaving the fallopian tube and cell division continues, the blastocyst/embryo may implant itself in the fallopian tube, resulting in an ectopic pregnancy.

The woman's body will continue as if the pregnancy is normal and implantation has occurred in the uterus, resulting in missed menses and a positive pregnancy test. However, as the displaced embryo grows, she will begin to feel persistent or severe abdominal pain and experience vaginal spotting or bleeding that signal an ectopic pregnancy. If medical intervention does not occur, the fallopian tube will eventually rupture, causing severe bleeding. Ectopic pregnancies are a major cause of maternal death from hemorrhage when the condition is not quickly recognized.

(Video: "What is Ectopic Pregnancy?" — Cleveland Clinic, 2020 [1:56])

Tab: Treatment

Treatment of Ectopic Pregnancy

Once the embryo has implanted itself in the wrong location, there is no longer any chance of viability. An ectopic pregnancy must be removed to avoid rupture of the fallopian tube and subsequent hemorrhaging. Surgical removal of an ectopic pregnancy before tubal rupture may damage the tube, resulting in an increased risk for future ectopic pregnancies. If the tube ruptures, future fertility is impacted. Depending on the size of the embryo, the ectopic pregnancy may be treated with medications such as methotrexate. The goal is to preserve the fallopian tube and the woman's future fertility.

Ectopic pregnancy is a very emotional experience for the woman, who is not only losing the pregnancy but also future fertility may be impacted. Nursing care focuses on early detection, pain control, and emotional support. Nurses who are administering methotrexate, a chemotherapeutic agent, to treat ectopic pregnancy must handle the medication carefully and follow all safety protocols associated with chemotherapeutic agents.

Section 8: High-Risk Pregnancy: Maternal Substance Use

About 7% of women reported using prescription opioid pain relievers during pregnancy. . . [and] of those, 1 in 5 reported misuse of prescription opioids, defined as getting them from a non-healthcare source or using them for a reason other than to relieve pain. — Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2024, Opioid Use section)

The use of alcohol, tobacco, or illicit drugs (cocaine, marijuana) by the mother increases her risk for medical complications and subsequent outcomes for the newborn. Substance abuse has become a more common occurrence in modern society.

Tab: Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) is a disorder in which infants exposed to opiates before birth demonstrate signs of drug withdrawal after birth. SSRIs and other antidepressants taken during pregnancy can result in NAS behaviors as well. Many newborn nurseries and intensive care units have dedicated neonatal abstinence syndrome areas just to manage the number of newborns with this condition. When it is determined that a newborn has neonatal abstinence syndrome, treatment begins to safely and comfortably detox the newborn.

According to 2020 data, about 6 newborns were diagnosed with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) for every 1,000 newborn hospital stays. That is about 1 baby diagnosed with NAS every 24 minutes in the United States, or more than 59 newborns diagnosed every day (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2024, Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome section). The number of babies born with NAS in the United States increased by 82% from 2010 to 2017.

Tab: NAS Symptoms

Symptoms of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

Signs of drug exposure in the neonate usually occur within 24 to 72 hours after birth, but symptoms may be delayed up to 2 weeks. Appearance of manifestations is determined by the specific drug, dose, and timing of the mother's last use. Drug use in the late third trimester results in a later onset of symptoms in the neonate, but the symptoms tend to be more severe.

Symptoms of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS) include:

  • behavioral: jitteriness, tremors, increased muscle tone, exaggerated Moro reflex, high-pitched cry, difficulty to console, poor sleeping patterns, and yawning
  • feeding: exaggerated rooting reflex, excessive sucking, uncoordinated sucking and swallowing, regurgitation, and weight loss
  • cardiovascular and respiratory: tachypnea, apnea, retractions, and tachycardia
  • others: fever, mottling, diaphoresis, and hypertension
Tab: Common Substances' Impacts

Common Substances' Impacts

The long-term effects of neonatal abstinence syndrome are still being researched, but this condition is believed to possibly cause developmental delays (walking, talking, social, and thinking skills), motor problems (bones, muscles, and movement), behavior and learning problems, speech and language problems, sleep problems, ear infections, and vision problems (March of Dimes, 2021).

Section 9: High-Risk Pregnancy: Fetal-Maternal Blood Type Incompatibility

Certain hemolytic diseases can put the pregnancy and the newborn at risk for complications.

Tab: Rh Incompatability

Rh Incompatibility

Rh incompatibility occurs when the mother is Rh- and the fetus is Rh+

  • Fetal red blood cells contain the Rh antigen (Rh+). When the blood cells of the fetus enter maternal circulation, the mother's body reacts as it would to a foreign substance by developing antibodies to destroy the invading antigen. This antigen then enters fetal circulation and begins destroying red blood cells.
  • This process often occurs at delivery but may occur from small leaks of fetal blood across the placenta during the pregnancy.
  • Issues resulting from this incompatibility can typically be prevented with the use of antepartum RhoGAM.

Prenatal Management of Rh Incompatibility

  • Rh immune globulin (RhoGAM) is administered to unsensitized Rh-negative women at 28 weeks' gestation to prevent sensitization.
    • RhoGAM is a commercial preparation of passive antibodies against Rh factor. It prevents the formation of active antibodies in maternal circulation.
  • If the neonate is Rh+, RhoGAM administration is repeated within 72 hours after birth.
  • RhoGAM should also be administered to unsensitized Rh negative women following an abortion, ectopic pregnancy, chorionic villus sampling, and amniocentesis, regardless if previously administered.
Tab: ABO Incompatability

ABO Incompatibility

ABO incompatibility occurs when the mother is blood type O and the fetus is blood type A, B, or AB.

  • A mother who has type O blood will naturally have anti-A and anti-B antibodies. These antibodies may cross the placenta and cause hemolysis of fetal red blood cells.
  • If the mother is A and the infant is B, or if the mother is B and the infant is A, incompatibility can also occur.
  • No specific prenatal care is needed. However, the nurse should be aware of the possibility of ABO incompatibility.
Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Clinical Manifestations

As fetal red blood cells are destroyed, fetal bilirubin levels increase. Clinical manifestations of both Rh and ABO incompatibility include:

  • Fetal
    • anemia caused by RBC destruction
    • erythroblastosis fetalis (rapid production of erythroblasts that cannot carry oxygen)
    • fetal hydrops (generalized edema) results from severe anemia and leads to congestive heart failure
  • Neonatal
    • pathologic jaundice (hyperbilirubinemia) occurs within the first 24 hours after birth
    • kernicterus (a toxic accumulation of bilirubin in central nervous system tissues) resulting in brain damage or death
Tab: Laboratory Testing

Laboratory Testing

  • Indirect Coomb's test (maternal blood) is done in early pregnancy to determine if antibodies are present in maternal circulation.
    • If positive, the mother is termed "sensitized."
    • If negative, repeat testing is done at 28 weeks gestation.
  • Direct Coomb's test (neonatal blood) is done after delivery to determine the presence of antibody-coated red blood cells in neonatal circulation.
  • Direct bilirubin levels will be drawn after delivery to detect hyperbilirubinemia in the neonate.

U.S. National Library of Medicine. (2021, February 2). Rh incompatibility. MedlinePlus. https://medlineplus.gov/ency/presentations/100217_1.htm

Section 10: High-Risk Pregnancy: Multifetal Pregnancy

Multifetal pregnancy (twins, triplets, etc.) contributes to complications for a variety of reasons. Pregnancy challenges each body system to adapt to the increasing demands of the fetus. When multiple fetuses are present, physiologic adaptation poses more potential problems.

The type of multifetal pregnancy may impact the degree of risk.

  • Identical (monozygotic [MZ]) twins result from a fertilized ovum that splits in two. Fetuses usually have two amnions, a single chorion, and share a placenta; however, other configurations are possible.
  • Fraternal twins (dizygotic [DZ]) start as two separate fertilized ovums that both implant in the uterus and are two separate pregnancies occurring at the same time.
  • Triplets, quadruplets, and beyond result in even greater risks to the pregnancy.
Tab: Medical Management and Nursing Care

Medical management and nursing care of multifetal pregnancies are focused on keeping the pregnancy healthy for as long as possible to prevent preterm delivery complications. This often involves reduced activity, frequent prenatal visits, close monitoring, constant nutritional assessment, and management of physical discomforts.

The woman and her support system may be concerned about increased financial, emotional, and health risks that accompany multifetal pregnancy. The nurse can provide emotional support, anticipatory guidance, education, and resources.

Tab: Developing High-Risk Pregnancy

In many situations, the woman's body accommodates a multifetal pregnancy as it would a single pregnancy. However, women with preexisting conditions that are already complicated by pregnancy will likely have more problems.

Multifetal pregnancies are more likely to result in spontaneous abortion (of one or more fetuses), gestational hypertension, polyhydramnios (more than normal amniotic fluid volume), placenta previa, preterm deliveries, and tangled or nuchal (wrapped around the fetus' neck) cords.

Tab: Amniotic Fluid Embolism

Multifetal pregnancies are also at higher risk for amniotic fluid embolism (also known as anaphylactic syndrome of pregnancy).

  • Although rare, this medical emergency occurs during labor or within 30 minutes of delivery. It happens quickly and can be fatal. Amniotic fluid enters the mother's circulatory system and the body's allergic-like reaction causes rapid respiratory failure, hypotension, and cardiac arrest followed by blood clotting or hemorrhaging with no apparent explanation (Cleveland Clinic, 2019).
  • Risk factors for amniotic fluid embolism include multifetal pregnancy, advanced maternal age, fetal distress, and operative deliveries (Cleveland Clinic, 2019).
  • The nurse must act fast to save the lives of the mother and the fetus. While amniotic fluid embolism cannot be prevented, the nurse can help identify it as quickly as possible. Rapid emergency treatment by the healthcare team may include blood transfusions, supplemental oxygen, urgent delivery, and CPR.

Reference

Cleveland Clinic (2019, November 12). Amniotic fluid embolism (anaphylactic syndrome of pregnancy). https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/15463-amniotic-fluid-embolism-anaphylactic-syndrome-of-pregnancy

Section 12: High-Risk Pregnancy: Placenta Previa

Sometimes the placenta is not implanted in the correct location in the uterus, contributing to pregnancy risk. This complication is called placenta previa.

Ideally, the placenta should implant near the fundus, but instead it implants near (marginal) or completely over the cervical opening.

  • The classic sign of placenta previa is the sudden onset of painless, bright red uterine bleeding occurring in the last half of pregnancy. Bleeding (scant or perfuse) results from tearing of the placental villi from the uterine wall as the lower uterine segment thins and the internal os dilates near term.
  • Placenta previa is diagnosed through an ultrasound during the antepartum period. This condition is more common with delayed pregnancy, multiparity, fetal malposition, previous cesarean birth, and prior uterine surgery. Other risk factors include use of tobacco and the male fetus.
  • Once the pregnancy reaches term, a cesarean section will be scheduled to safely deliver the fetus. If the amount of bleeding is heavy, close fetal and maternal monitoring is required. Delivery may need to occur before the pregnancy reaches term.
Reminder: You will not perform a vaginal exam on a patient with any vaginal bleeding or a known placenta previa!

Section 13: High-Risk Pregnancy: Abruptio Placentae

Another type of placental dysfunction is abruptio placentae (typically referred to as an abruption, or a placental abruption).

Tab: Abruptio Placentae

Abruptio Placentae

Abruptio placentae occurs when the placenta prematurely detaches from the uterus prior to delivery, either partially or completely.

  • Placental abruption is often caused by physical trauma (such as a car accident or a fall down the stairs) or pregnancy-related hypertension, but sometimes the cause is unknown.
  • Classic signs of placental abruption include bleeding (which may be concealed in the uterus or apparent) and a sudden onset of severe abdominal pain with uterine rigidity. When evaluating with an external fetal monitor, the uterine resting tone is elevated and fetal heart tones are non-reassuring. If the amniotic sac ruptures, amniotic fluid will appear bloody.
    • Other signs include increased fundal height (as the uterus fills with blood).
  • When the placenta detaches before the baby is delivered, the blood vessels from the mother feeding into the placenta are left exposed and hemorrhage occurs. The fetus no longer receives adequate blood flow and oxygen from the placenta. The degree of detachment determines the severity of maternal hemorrhaging and fetal hypoxia.
    • Marginal abruption occurs with partial detachment and external bleeding.
    • Partial abruption occurs with detachment of the center of the placenta only and concealed bleeding.
    • Complete abruption occurs with complete detachment and either concealed or apparent bleeding.
Tab: Nursing Actions

Nursing Actions

  • A placental abruption is a life-threatening emergency for both the mother and the fetus. Regardless of gestational age, abruptio placentae most likely results in fetal delivery. The healthcare team must recognize and respond quickly to early warning signs and act quickly.
  • The nurse's role is to monitor fetal well-being and prepare for medical or surgical management.
  • Medical management involves managing maternal hemorrhage and maintaining fetal perfusion until emergency delivery can occur. If the degree of placental separation is minor and fetal response is stable, delivery may be delayed.

Section 16: Nutritional and Age-Related High-Risk Pregnancies

Adolescents and mothers of advanced maternal age both have an increased incidence of high-risk pregnancy. Delayed pregnancy, beyond the age of 35 years, is becoming more common in American society. Select each item below to learn more about these risks.

Tab: Adolescents
  • Adolescents who become pregnant often endure additional psychological and emotional stress during pregnancy.
    • For those with a support system, the risks are minimized. For those with psychosocial risk factors, pregnancy can be a difficult experience.
    • The nurse can greatly impact the health of an adolescent pregnancy with education and recognition of danger signs.
  • Adolescent pregnancy presents physiologic complications. The nurse provides anticipatory guidance, emotional support, and physiologic nursing care.
    • Antepartum: increased risk for gestational hypertension, cephalopelvic disproportion (the pelvis too small or the wrong shape to accommodate vaginal delivery), abruptio placentae, low birth weight, intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), anemia, infections, and preterm delivery
    • Intrapartum: increased anxiety and fear, and decreased preparation for labor
    • Postpartum: impaired bonding and deficits of knowledge about infant care
Tab: Delayed Pregnancy
  • Women who become pregnant after the age of 35 have an increased risk for certain maternal and fetal complications, particularly when a chronic preexisting condition exists, such as diabetes mellitus or hypertension.
    • Maternal complications include spontaneous abortion and multiple gestations.
    • Fetal complications include an increased risk for chromosome disorders, such as Down syndrome.
    • Prenatal testing is done to diagnose complications.
  • Advantages of delayed childbirth include higher maternal education level and socioeconomic status. This results in increased emotional and financial stability.
Tab: Nutritional Factors

Nutritional factors can increase pregnancy risk.

  • Impaired Nutrition
    • Socioeconomic status can contribute to a diet deficient in necessary nutrients.
    • Low prepregnancy weight and lack of adequate weight gain can contribute to pregnancy complications.
    • Eating disorders are associated with miscarriage, preterm birth, and congenital anomalies.
    • Skipping meals and convenience foods may result in diet deficiency.
  • Obesity
    • Obesity in pregnancy can worsen related comorbidities like hypertension, diabetes mellitus and asthma.
    • Obese pregnant women are at increased risk for stillbirth, spontaneous abortion, gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, blood clots, preterm birth, and operative deliveries.
    • Nurses focus on helping the woman with education, support, and treatment.
  • Multiparity
    • The number and spacing of pregnancies influence nutritional requirements.
    • Previous pregnancies can result in ongoing nutritional deficits.
  • Substance Abuse
    • Cigarette smoking, excessive caffeine use, and alcohol and drug abuse can further complicate a pregnancy.
