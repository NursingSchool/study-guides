Section 1 — Introduction to Breastfeeding

The choice to breastfeed or not is one that every parent makes based on a personal set of beliefs, values, and unique circumstances. The nurse plays a major role in ensuring the success of their choice.

In this module, you will review the benefits of breastfeeding, the basic techniques of breastfeeding, and the resources available to breastfeeding mothers.

Section 2: Prepare — Breastfeeding

Q1: Breastfeeding Duration (Fill in the Blank)

Question: How long should infants be exclusively breastfed, and for how long ideally?

Correct Answer: 6 months exclusively; ideally at least 12 months total.

Feedback: It is recommended that for the first 6 months of life infants are exclusively breastfed, but ideally should be breastfed for at least the first 12 months of life (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2012).

Q2: Infant Feeding Cues (Multiple Choice)

Question: When caring for a newborn, which action indicates the newborn is ready to breastfeed?

Correct Answer: Places hands in mouth

Feedback: Infant hunger cues include rooting, sucking on hands/fingers, and rapid eye movement. Crying is a late sign of hunger. An infant that is sleeping quietly is not showing signs of hunger. Spitting up clear mucous is common in the first days of life and is not a sign of hunger. Turning the head to the side while opening the mouth (rooting reflex) is a sign of hunger. However, turning the head toward sound is not.

Q3: First Newborn Stool (Select All That Apply)

Question: The nurse is assessing the newborn's initial meconium stool. What characteristics should the nurse expect the stool to have? Select all that apply.

Correct Answers:

  • Thick
  • Dark green
  • Odorless

Feedback: At birth, the newborn bowel is sterile, and so are the thick, dark green, tarry, odorless plugs of stool (meconium). Meconium forms as a result of swallowing and digesting sterile amniotic fluid in utero. As the newborn digests the initial colostrum first and then breastmilk, the intestinal flora are established and the stools gradually change from meconium to more frequent, thinner, and yellow-colored stools with an odor.

Section 3: Explore — Breastfeeding Overview

Melissa and her partner attended a breastfeeding class at their local hospital when she was about 6 months pregnant. The class was taught by one of the nurses, who is also a lactation consultant.

Breastfeeding is a natural extension of pregnancy and childbirth; it is much more than simply a means of supplying nutrition for infants. Women most often breastfeed their babies because they are aware of the benefits to the infant.

Melissa is excited about the bonding experience she will have with her daughter, Taylor. She dreams of having three children and plans to breastfeed all of them. Melissa's partner, John, is very supportive of the decision to breastfeed. From his partner's prenatal class, he learned that his opinion about breastfeeding could influence Melissa. Women who perceive that their partners prefer breastfeeding are more likely to choose this method of infant feeding. Women are more likely to breastfeed successfully when partners and family members are positive about breastfeeding and have the skills to support breastfeeding.

Tab 1: Breastfeeding Benefits

Human breast milk meets the balanced macro- and micro-nutrient needs of the newborn. It is the most easily digested newborn nutrition source and supplies the newborn with some of the mother's immunity to various pathogens. Other benefits to the newborn include decreased risk for:

  • Gastrointestinal infections
  • Respiratory complications, such as otitis media and asthma
  • Celiac disease
  • Sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)
  • Developing diabetes mellitus (type 1 and type 2)
  • Leukemia

Breastfeeding provides hormone regulation benefits for the mother, which promotes uterine involution, thereby reducing blood loss. Breastfeeding also reduces the incidence of some cancers (breast and ovarian), is less expensive than formula, and the associated infant bonding provides psychological benefits.

Tab 2: Recommendations for Breastfeeding

The full-term newborn's caloric requirements when breastfed are 39 to 45 kcal per pound of body weight, but higher if formula fed (45 to 50 kcal/lb) because of the differences in nutritional makeup.

NOTE: It is recommended that for the first 6 months of life, infants are exclusively breastfed. Breastfeeding should continue for at least 12 months while introducing complementary foods. Breastfeeding may continue beyond the first year of life as long as the parent and infant desire (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2012).

There are national and international efforts underway to increase the percentage of breastfed infants until at least 6 months of age. Studies indicate that the incidence of infant deaths as well as maternal deaths is less when mothers breastfeed exclusively for 6 months. Some birth facilities are seeking designation as "baby friendly" for their efforts in providing education to staff and parents regarding breastfeeding and ensuring their facility is ideally suited to breastfeeding on-demand, without pacifiers or the use of formula unless medically necessary.

Tab 3: Breast Milk Composition

Breast milk is specifically made for human infants and proportioned appropriately for the neonate. It will vary to meet the newborn's changing needs. Breastmilk provides protection against infection and is easily digested. It contains maternal immunoglobulins, leukocytes, antioxidants, enzymes, and hormones that are important for growth. These components are not available in formula.

Protein, carbohydrates, and fats are present in the amounts and proportions needed by the infant. Vitamins A, E, and C are high in breast milk.

Tab 4: Decisions Not to Breastfeed

It is recognized that formula does not match breastmilk in its benefits for optimal maternal and newborn health. However, not every newborn is breastfed for a variety of reasons. Occasionally, there are maternal and/or newborn medical reasons that require the use of formula instead. For example:

  • The mother may be receiving medications that would cross into the breastmilk or has a condition restricting her from breastfeeding, such as HIV.
  • Sometimes the newborn is physically unable to breastfeed, such as with a cleft lip or palate. The mother may be able to manually pump breastmilk and feed the baby using a bottle.
  • Some mothers choose not to breastfeed for personal reasons.

Remember! Whatever the situation or decision, the nurse supports the mother's choice and provides assistance and guidance.

Section 4: Explore — Prepare for Breastfeeding During Pregnancy

For the postpartum client who wants to breastfeed, the preparation for success begins in pregnancy.

  • The client must be physically and psychologically prepared for the challenges that may come with breastfeeding.
  • Maternal nutrition and a strong support system play a big role in breastfeeding success.
  • Education about breastfeeding is essential to success.
  • Nurses can provide education and guidance on preparing to breastfeed before delivery.

Hormone changes occur after delivery in preparation for breastfeeding.

  • Decrease in estrogen and progesterone once placenta is delivered allows prolactin levels to rise.
  • Prolactin levels rise as physical stimulation (infant sucking) triggers milk production.
  • Oxytocin is released by nipple and areola stimulation during nursing to encourage milk let down from alveoli and milk ducts.

Before delivery, milk production has already begun and a supply of the first milk, called colostrum, is already present. Colostrum contains immunoglobulins (IgA) and protein.

Section 5: Explore — Breastfeeding at Delivery

Breastfeeding should be initiated within the first hour of life, if mother and baby are stable. This action stimulates milk production and promotes bonding.

Tab 1: Newborn Assessment

At delivery, the newborn, if stable, should be placed on the mother's bare chest for assessment and intervention rather than taking the newborn to the radiant warmer. This may include activities such as vital signs, suctioning, warming and drying the newborn, and assessing the Apgar scores.

Tab 2: Oxytocin & Let-Down Reflex

Breastfeeding within the first hour is an important step in which oxytocin is released from the posterior pituitary gland. From the first moment the placenta is removed, the newborn attempts to latch to the breast (called suckling) and the mother's body is signaled to release hormones that continue milk production.

Oxytocin is responsible for the milk let-down from the breast ducts. This let-down reflex is also stimulated by the thought, sight, or sound of the infant and is felt by the mother when it is time to feed. The oxytocin also stimulates uterine contractions, the same way it does in labor, and causes the uterus to constrict. Therefore, breastfeeding can help prevent postpartum hemorrhage due to the release of oxytocin.

Tab 3: Skin-to-Skin Contact

The newborn will likely be alert and express interest in breastfeeding soon after delivery. Signs that the infant is ready to feed include sucking on its hands/fingers, rooting around searching for the nipple, and rapid eye movements. The newborn is soothed when brought to the mother's bare skin and breast. For this reason, the first bath is often delayed for 8-12 hours after delivery to allow for constant skin-to-skin contact and intermittent breastfeeding on demand.

Section 7: Explore — Breastfeeding Anatomy and Techniques

Assisting mothers with breastfeeding newborns in the hospital setting involves teaching correct techniques and providing encouragement. Nurses must understand breast anatomy and the phases of lactogenesis.

Tab 1: Breast & Nipple Anatomy

The characteristics of the nipples impact how the woman will breastfeed. The newborn struggles to latch if the nipples are flat or inverted rather than protruding. Breast size may also impact the shape of the nipples, which impacts latch.

There are 15-20 lobes in each breast. Each lobe contains lobules that provide a system of ducts that end at the opening of the nipple. Milk is produced in the alveoli, contained in each lobule.

Tab 2: Breastfeeding

Breastfeeding Techniques: It is best to attempt breastfeeding on-demand and when the newborn is awake and alert but not crying. Clients should be taught to recognize infant feeding cues. Breastfeeding should occur 8-12 times in 24 hours with varying amounts of time in between each session. Each feeding needs to be long enough for the newborn to receive enough milk, particularly in the early postpartum days when the let-down reflex can take up to 5 minutes. The newborn at this stage should typically be allowed to feed for as long as a consistent sucking pattern exists, typically around 15 minutes or more.

Establish a Sucking Pattern: By the second or third day, the newborn will become more alert and fussier, showing more signs of readiness to eat. The infant often starts cluster feeding (several times close together) and then waits a longer period in between as the newborn's appetite fluctuates with growth spurts. When the sucking pattern becomes non-nutritive (choppy without swallowing), the newborn can be unlatched, burped, and moved to the other breast if desired. Switching sides also helps minimize sore nipples and provides equal stimulation of milk production in each breast.

Position of the Mother's Hands: Hand position on the breast is important. The client can use either a "C" or a "U" position so that her fingers and thumb are parallel to the baby's mouth. Fingers should remain behind the areola so the infant has room to latch. As the infant becomes more adept at feeding, the mother will no longer need to support the breast.

Remember! The nurse works with the mother to recognize the right time to attempt breastfeeding and to ensure enough spacing to allow the mother to rest without impeding newborn nutrition, breastfeeding progress, or bonding.

Tab 3: Lactogenesis

Lactogenesis is the process of breastmilk production. It comes in three stages.

  • Lactogenesis I: Begins during the second half of pregnancy. By late pregnancy, some clients can express some colostrum. Colostrum production continues for the first several days after birth.
  • Lactogenesis II: Begins on day 2-3 after birth, transitional milk production begins. Transitional milk contains lactose, fat, and calories.
  • Lactogenesis III: Begins around day 10 after birth, mature milk is produced. Mature milk contains 20 calories/ounce.

The length of feeding is important because it impacts the type of breastmilk the newborn receives. Clients should nurse for up to 15 minutes on each breast once breastmilk is established.

  • Foremilk comes first and is thinner.
  • Hindmilk follows at the end of feeding, is fattier, and leads to infant weight gain.

Section 8 — Explore: Breastfeeding Positions

Assisting mothers with breastfeeding newborns in the hospital setting involves assisting with correct techniques and encouragement. It can be difficult at first, particularly for first-time mothers, but it is a wonderful bonding experience.

Melissa noted that the baby had the rooting reflex. She was amazed when her nipple was brushed across Taylor's lips; Taylor searched for the nipple and her instinct was there to latch. A proper latch is crucial to preventing nipple soreness and injury, as well as milk letdown. Review the different breastfeeding positions that can help ensure proper latch.

Breastfeeding Positions (Transcript)
  • Cradle
  • Cross-cradle
  • Football hold
  • Laid-back
  • Side-lying
Breastfeeding Tips
  • To break the suction of the latch, a finger is placed between the baby's lips and the breast rather than pulling the breast out of the newborn's mouth. This can prevent nipple soreness.
  • Many mothers worry that their newborn cannot catch their breath properly when at the breast because their nostrils are pressed into the breast tightly. Educate mothers that newborn nostrils are wide set to allow for breathing while at the breast but positioning also assists with this. Melissa did well with the cradle hold.
  • Breastfeeding time will vary for each baby. The newborn will establish its own pattern.
  • Burping in between sides is encouraged. This will help prevent trapped air from causing regurgitation of milk.
  • Reinforce with mothers that the presence of the initial milk (colostrum) is thin but very beneficial to the newborn. It can take several days after delivery for the mother's breast milk to come in. Some mothers worry that this means the newborn is not getting enough to eat and may wish to supplement with formula or stop breastfeeding altogether.
  • Avoid nipple confusion by not offering supplemental formula, pacifiers, or soothers until breastfeeding has been established.
  • Newborns should breastfeed at least 8 to 12 times within a 24-hour period, but each baby will have its own schedule and mothers should always look for feeding cues and feed as often as the baby needs.
  • Melissa made sure to unwrap Taylor and talk to her when it was time to eat so the baby would latch easier.

Section 9 — Explore: Breastfeeding Latching

Many hospitals and birthing facilities employ registered nurses (RNs) who are certified lactation consultants. They have specialized training to provide inpatient and outpatient services for postpartum breastfeeding assistance and assessment of infant nutrition status. Lactation consultants can troubleshoot breastfeeding complications and evaluate the newborn for appropriate weight gain.

Tab 1: Eliciting Latch

Instruct the mother to brush the nipple against the infant's lips. When the mouth is wide open, aim the nipple toward the roof of the infant's mouth. Then instruct the mother to quickly bring the infant close so that the infant can latch on to the areola with the chin touching the breast first.

Tab 2: Latching Position

There are various holds that can be used based on the mother's comfort level and needs. The newborn's head and body should face the breast directly so that the ear, shoulder and hip are in a straight line. The newborn's neck should be slightly extended to aid in swallowing.

The infant's lips should be positioned on the areola to allow the nipple to be drawn toward the back of the mouth. Most of the areola on the underside is covered by the infant's mouth; some of the areola on the top side may be visible. The tongue is between the lower gum and the breast, and the infant's lips are flared outward.

Many mothers worry that their newborn cannot breathe properly when at the breast because their nostrils are pressed into the breast tightly. Educate mothers that newborn nostrils are wide set to allow for breathing while at breast but positioning also assists with this.

Tab 3: Assessing Latching

The nurse assists the mother and newborn with proper latch as needed. This is crucial to ensure the nipples are not damaged and the newborn gets a good feeding without becoming frustrated or losing interest.

The LATCH Scoring Tool — The LATCH scoring system is used by nurses to assess and document the degree to which breastfeeding assistance is needed.

  • L — Latching of infant onto the breast
  • A — Amount of audible swallowing
  • T — Type of nipple (inverted, flat or protruding)
  • C — Comfort of mother
  • H — Help needed to hold baby to breast

A score of 0, 1, or 2 is given to the five letters of the LATCH acronym. The higher the score, the better the latch. A score of less than 7 indicates the need for assistance with breastfeeding.

Tab 4: Unlatching

In addition to a proper latch, it is also important to remove the infant from the breast carefully to avoid nipple injury. To break the suction of the latch, a finger is placed between the baby's lips and the mother's breast rather than pulling the breast out of the newborn's mouth.

Section 11 — Explore: Common Breastfeeding Challenges

Tab 1: Is my baby getting enough milk?

Both the initial disinterest and sleepiness of newborns along with their subsequent fussiness and eagerness to eat often lead many women to believe they are not producing enough milk and their newborn is not getting enough to eat. Some mothers decide to supplement breastfeeding with formula or completely switch to bottle-feeding because of this concern.

As milk is demanded by the infant, milk supply is increased. Therefore, it is important to teach mothers that breastfeeding at least 8-12 times daily is essential to ensuring adequate breastmilk supply.

Additionally, it is important to teach mothers how to know that the infant is getting enough breastmilk. These signs include:

  • Listen for frequent swallowing
  • Change at least 6-8 wet diapers every day
  • Notice that breasts feel softer after nursing the infant
  • Observe that the infant seems content after feeding
  • Change at least one diaper with stool daily
Tab 2: Can I give my baby a pacifier?

It is recommended that formula supplementation or the use of pacifiers be avoided when the newborn's demand increases during the first four weeks of life. This can be a very difficult concept for some families to understand.

The newborn may become confused by the nipple of the bottle or pacifier and have more difficulty latching to the breast, which is softer and shaped differently. Also, the interest in feeding should be used to latch the baby to the breast to stimulate milk production so that a breastfeeding pattern can be established.

Tab 3: Do I have enough breastmilk?

Colostrum production begins in late pregnancy and continues into the first few days after birth. Though not much in quantity, colostrum is a highly nutritious first milk for the newborn to receive. Within 2-3 days after birth, transitional milk production begins with increased quantity. Breastmilk is the best food for a newborn during the adjustment to extrauterine life.

The first breastmilk available at birth is colostrum. It is gentle, thick, and yellow, containing just the right mix of protein and other macro- and micro-nutrients that are perfectly suited to this point in the newborn's life. It also provides the necessary infection-preventing immunoglobulins, has a laxative effect that expels meconium, and establishes the infant's normal intestinal flora in preparation for the mature milk to come. Adequate nutrition and hydration are essential for milk production.

As the newborn becomes more alert and interested in the comfort of breastfeeding, the increased demand stimulates milk production and transition from colostrum to the more nutrient-dense transitional and mature milk. This gradual progression is gentler on the newborn's maturing digestive system. So, the mother can be reassured that the increased demand is expected rather than a sign that the newborn is uncomfortable or hungry.

The more the infant nurses, the more milk the breasts will produce.

Tab 4: How do I treat sore nipples?

Nipple soreness can occur with or without skin breakdown and is common in the first few days of breastfeeding. However, it can be the result of poor latch on the breast, faulty sucking habits, or continuous sucking after the breast is empty. If nipple soreness lasts longer than one week, evaluation is necessary.

After evaluating for breastfeeding problems, the nurse should instruct the mother on comfort measures, including:

  • Ice to nipples and areola for a few minutes before feeding
  • Allow breasts to air dry after feeding
  • Express a few drops of milk onto dry nipples to promote healing
  • Change breast pads frequently
  • Begin nursing on the least sore breast first
  • Avoid the use of petroleum based products or soap on the nipples

NOTE: It is important to teach mothers not to delay feedings or switch to bottle feeding due to sore nipples.

Tab 5: How do I wake my baby?

To maintain breastmilk production and ensure adequate infant weight gain, newborns and young infants need to feed every 2-3 hours. Sometimes waking a drowsy baby to feed can be challenging.

Nurses should teach mothers strategies to wake a sleepy baby for feeding, including:

  • Touch and talk to the baby
  • Touch the infant's face with a warm, wet washcloth
  • Change the infant's diaper
  • Unwrap and undress the infant

Additionally, mothers must be taught infant feeding cues.

Tab 6: Is my baby gaining weight?

It is common for newborns to lose up to 10% of birth weight in the first week of life. However, this weight should be regained by two weeks of age. Birth weight should double by 6 months of age and triple by one year. Infants should gain approximately 5-7 ounces/week during the first six months of life.

The best way for a mother to ensure appropriate weight gain is with frequent feedings and observing for signs of dehydration, such as sunken fontanelles, lack of wet diapers, and fewer tears when crying.

Section 12 — Explore: Feeding Difficulties and Weaning

Melissa worried whether Taylor was getting enough milk. Her milk came in about day three postpartum, right on time. Melissa knew that she should count the diapers and there were eight wet ones that day (6 is the minimum). The baby had at least 3-6 bowel movements that day and the stool was yellow and soft. Taylor was getting enough breastmilk.

After about two weeks, Melissa started noticing that Taylor was spitting up a lot after each feeding. Newborns and infants have immature digestive systems. Occasionally, some antigens in foods the mother has eaten may pass into her milk. If the infant reacts to the mother's diet, the offending food should be identified and eliminated.

Tab 1: 6 Months Later

Melissa continued to breastfeed but also started the baby on some solid foods.

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months after birth and breastfeeding in combination with solid foods until at least age 1. Breastfeeding is recommended if the client and the baby wish to continue.

It is often easiest to begin weaning when the baby starts the process. Sometimes it happens when solid foods are introduced around 6 months of age, but each baby is different.

Tab 2: Taylor's 1st Birthday

Taylor started to decline her breastfeeding sessions. She was more interested in solid foods and pushed away from the breast. Melissa knew it was time to start the weaning process. She was careful to do it slowly.

Tab 3: Weaning

The best way to begin weaning is slowly tapering off how long and how often a woman breastfeeds each day over the course of weeks or months. This will cause the milk supply to gradually diminish and prevent engorgement. Some suggest dropping a midday breastfeeding session first. Once one feeding is dropped, moms can work on dropping another.

Section 15: Explore: Breastfeeding Complications: Engorgement

Engorgement occurs when the breasts fill, the milk ducts become swollen, and then cannot sufficiently empty.

Causes of Engorgement

Transitional fullness due to venous congestion is most common at 72-96 hours postpartum, when milk production begins and the body is still balancing supply and demand. This uncomfortable fullness should only last 24 hours.

After the let-down reflex occurs, it is normal for the breasts to become firm, congested, and uncomfortably tight. Milk often spontaneously leaks. This happens less often as feedings settle into a pattern and the milk production is able to balance itself.

Engorgement can become an issue when feedings are spaced too far apart, skipped, or too brief to empty the breast sufficiently. Wearing a bra that is too tight can also prevent sufficient emptying. Engorged breasts are hard, painful, warm, and appear taut and shiny.

NOTE: The most important aspect of ongoing milk production and successful breastfeeding is regular emptying of the breast.

(Image: Left image: Normal breast. Right image: Engorged breast with full milk glands and ducts.)

Prevention of Engorgement

To prevent engorgement of the breast, the mother should nurse the infant consistently. Infants should suckle for at least 15 minutes per feeding per breast and feed 8-12 times in 24 hours. If the baby is unable to feed this frequently, the mother should pump her breasts to remove breastmilk and avoid engorgement.

Interventions

The nurse should teach the mother comfort measures if engorgement occurs, including:

  • Apply warm compresses to the breasts before feeding to stimulate let-down and soften the breasts for latching
  • Apply ice packs after nursing to slow refilling and provide comfort
  • Choose a well-fitting bra to support the breasts and wear it daily
  • Feed the infant consistently

Section 16: Explore: Breastfeeding Complications-Mastitis

Mastitis is an inflammation of breast tissue in lactating women usually caused by an infection. When the breasts fill and cannot empty, milk stasis can lead to infection of the breast. A breast infection is typically caused by Staphylococcus aureus, methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), Escherichia coli, and streptococci entering through an injury such as a cracked or blistered nipple. Mastitis is usually unilateral and seldom occurs before the fourth week postpartum.

The postpartum nurse may not encounter mastitis but will teach the client how to prevent it using good hygiene practices before and after breastfeeding and keeping the breasts clean and dry.

The client should follow the guidelines for preventing engorgement and nipple injury and report flulike symptoms such as fever, chills, malaise, and headache. Often the area is painful, red, warm, and a lump (wedge-shaped) may be present which can progress to an abscess if left untreated.

Mastitis is treated medically with antibiotics. Comfort measures may include the use of moist heat, ice, breast support, and mild analgesics. If mastitis develops, it is important to evaluate the client's nipples and the infant's latch.

The client must continue to empty the breast through pumping or breastfeeding. The baby may still breastfeed during mastitis. However, if an abscess develops, the milk must not be consumed by the infant.

Section 17: Breastfeeding: Nursing Care

At the time of delivery, the breasts have enlarged and the nipples have areolas that have darkened and become more pronounced in response to hormonal changes in preparation for breastfeeding.

The breasts should be smooth with even pigmentation. For the first few postpartum days, they should feel soft. As transitional milk arrives, the breasts become warm and fuller. Nipples should be supple, intact, and erect when stimulated.

Assessment of the breasts occurs when assisting with breastfeeding. It is also an opportunity to reinforce teaching regarding breastfeeding techniques and breast self-care.

The nurse provides the following guidance to the woman who is breastfeeding:

Hygiene
  • Use only warm water and allow your breasts to air dry.
  • Wash your hands before and after breastfeeding.
Breastmilk Storage
  • If you pump extra milk, the expressed milk can be:
    • Used at room temperature within 4 hours
    • Refrigerated at under 4°C (39°F) for 72 hours (optimal); 5 days is acceptable
    • Frozen under −18°C (0°F) for up to 6 months
  • Do not defrost or warm the milk in the microwave because it will heat unevenly. Instead, defrost it in the refrigerator and heat it up under warm water. It will need to be inverted a few times in the bottle to gently mix the separated milk components. Any that is not used within two hours should be discarded.
  • Once frozen breastmilk has been thawed, do not refreeze it.

(Medela, n.d.)

Formula Supplementation

Avoid the use of formula supplementation in the hospital unless medically indicated. Supplements may impede successful breastfeeding because they decrease feeding frequency and milk production.

Drug Transfer to Breastmilk

Most medication taken by the client is compatible with breastfeeding; however, some drugs interfere with milk production. All prescription and over-the-counter medications should be approved by a healthcare provider.

If the client must take a medication that is harmful to the infant, she should pump her breasts and discard the milk while taking the medication. Some drugs may be taken right after feeding and may not reach the infant in harmful amounts.

The National Library of Medicine (2006) provides the LactMed database with information on the effects of drugs and other chemicals on breastfeeding. It also includes information on therapeutic alternatives, where appropriate.

Other Guidelines
  • Continue taking prenatal vitamins while breastfeeding.
  • Continue to increase caloric intake by 400 calories/day.
  • Consume adequate protein (71 grams/day).
  • Continue to hydrate well, consuming 2-3 L of water/day.
  • Avoid drinking alcohol, smoking tobacco, or using illicit substances while breastfeeding.
  • Avoid excessive caffeine consumption (<200 mg/day).
  • Be sure to use a contraceptive to avoid pregnancy, because breastfeeding does not prevent pregnancy.
  • Remember to use proper breastfeeding and latching techniques.

Family Planning

Week 5: Family Planning

Module 4 of 5 — NR327 Maternal-Child Nursing, Unit 5

Section 1: Introduction to Family Planning

The major functions of a nurse in family planning are to provide care, reassure the client, and provide information. During the consultation, the client may feel a greater rapport with the nurse than with the healthcare provider and may show greater willingness to speak about medical history or significant symptoms. The nurse carefully explains prescriptions after the provider consultation and instructs the client if any additional tests or procedures are required.

Nurses who participate in client education for family planning must understand birth control methods. Client education is the focus of this module. The method of family planning selected by a client is personal and may involve cultural, religious, and lifestyle influences. The nurse must reinforce information received by the healthcare provider and help support the client in their decision making.

This module will review the role of the nurse in caring for clients during the family planning process.

Section 2: Prepare: Family Planning

Q1: Birth Control Implant

Question: A woman asks the nurse, "What should I know about the birth control implant in the arm and how it prevents pregnancy?" What should the nurse explain about the impact of birth control implants? Select all that apply.

Correct answers:

  • There may be some temporary tenderness or swelling around the implant after insertion.
  • It provides continuous contraception for 3 years.
  • A possible side effect is irregular bleeding (spotting), especially in the first 6-12 months.

Feedback: The implant is placed in the client's upper inner arm with the use of a local anesthetic. There may be some temporary tenderness or swelling around the implant. A possible side effect is irregular bleeding (spotting), especially in the first 6-12 months. The failure rate of this device during the first year of use is 0.05% (99.5% effective). The device releases progestin continuously to provide 3 years of contraception.

Q2: Pregnancy Prevention

Question: Fill in the blanks.

  • Abstinence means avoiding any activity that would allow sperm to enter the vagina.
  • The only completely effective method of preventing pregnancy is: abstinence.

Feedback: Abstinence as a means to avoid pregnancy is considered avoiding any activity that would allow sperm to enter the vagina. It is the only completely effective method of preventing pregnancy.

It is important to note that sexually transmitted infections (STI) can still be communicated through other means of intimacy. Celibacy is the only completely effective method to prevent STI and pregnancy.

Q3: Oral Birth Control Pills

Question: Which client history would prohibit the use of combined oral contraceptives? Select all that apply.

Correct answers:

  • Breast cancer
  • Blood clots
  • Chronic uncontrolled hypertension
  • Migraines

Feedback: Combined oral contraceptives should not be used by clients with a history of blood clots, stroke, breast cancer, uncontrolled hypertension, migraines, or diabetes mellitus with vascular involvement.

Multigravida and history of fetal demise do not prohibit the future use of combined oral contraceptives.

Section 3: Explore: Purpose of Family Planning

Family planning is a way of thinking and living that is adopted to delay, prevent, or actualize a pregnancy. It is also called a reproductive life plan. Each plan is unique to the client's personal goals and dreams.

The nurse will counsel and educate clients on contraceptive methods to prevent unintended pregnancies. Education must also be directed to the individual needs of the client. Culture, personal concerns, and preferences should all be taken into consideration.

Scenario: Mary is a 19-year-old client going to her gynecologist appointment to discuss family planning. She is dating a man named Jake and desires to be sexually active. She states that she does not want to get pregnant because she is still in college and eager to have a career as a nurse after graduation.

Mary wants to know her options and has never really thought about family planning. She and her previous boyfriend used condoms when they had sex. Right now, Mary does not want to get pregnant and wants to delay having a baby for at least 5 years. Once she has a child, she will probably wait at least another 2 years to get pregnant again.

Note: Although using condoms is part of family planning, learning more and being proactive are critical for those who are sexually active.

Section 4: Explore: Client Education and Contraception Methods

Family planning methods include natural family planning, barrier methods, oral contraceptives, transdermal contraceptive patch, contraceptive vaginal ring, long-acting reversible contraceptives, and sterilization. The nurse must assess the client's goals and support their decision making. Safety, protection from sexually transmitted infections, effectiveness, acceptability, convenience, benefits, side-effects, effects on spontaneity, availability, cost, personal preference, and religious and cultural beliefs all must be taken into consideration.

Contraception methods include: condom, female condom, sponge, cervical cap, oral contraceptives, vaginal ring, intrauterine device, hormone injections, contraceptive implant, diaphragm, calendar method, contraceptive patch, spermicide, and surgical sterilization.

Section 5: Explore: Natural Family Planning Methods

Some women prefer not to have any chemicals or foreign materials introduced into their bodies. This could be related to religious beliefs or just personal preferences. Natural family planning methods are also called fertility awareness methods and use physiologic cues to predict ovulation. These methods can also assist a client who is trying to become pregnant. None of these methods provide any protection against sexually transmitted infections.

Natural family planning methods require the client to be highly motivated because they must abstain from intercourse during certain times of the month. Errors in predicting ovulation may lead to pregnancy. These methods (all types) have a 24% failure rate during the first year of use.

Calendar Method

Method: based on the assumption that ovulation occurs approximately 14 days before the onset of menses

Considerations:

  • The client must abstain or use a barrier method during the days calculated to be fertile.
  • This method is unreliable because not all women have regular menstrual cycles.
Cervical Mucus Method

Method: Cervical mucus is checked daily to estimate when ovulation occurs.

  • scant, thick, and sticky = before and after ovulation
  • thin, slippery, and clear (spinnbarkeit) = during ovulation

Considerations:

  • Vaginal infections, sexual arousal, some medications, and semen can affect mucus consistency.
  • Avoid intercourse from the first thin, slippery mucous until 4 days after the last thin, slippery mucous.
Lactational Amenorrhea Method

Method: Lactation inhibits ovulation because prolactin interferes with the secretion of gonadotropin-releasing hormone and luteinizing hormone.

Considerations:

  • The frequency, intensity, and duration of nursing are important for inhibiting ovulation.
  • The use of formula or solid foods decreases the frequency and duration of breastfeeding.
  • The menstrual cycle generally resumes by 6 months after birth, even in women who breastfeed exclusively.
Basal Body Temperature (BBT)

Method: Slight elevation in temperature occurs just before ovulation.

Considerations:

  • Basal body temperature (BBT) rises near ovulation and remains higher during the second half of the menstrual cycle.
  • BBT remains higher if conception occurs and falls about 2–4 days before menstruation if there is no conception.
  • Oral temperature should be checked daily upon awakening in the morning but before rising from bed.
  • BBT may be altered by illness, inadequate sleep, disrupted sleep, alcohol intake, and sleeping under an electric blanket.
Symptothermal Method

Method: Combines assessment of basal body temperature and cervical mucous daily.

Considerations:

  • Consider other symptoms that signal ovulation, such as weight gain, abdominal bloating, and pain.
  • Using all three indicators of ovulation increases effectiveness.
Coitus Interruptus

Method: the removal of the penis from the vagina before ejaculation

Considerations:

  • Requires great control by the male partner.
  • Preejaculate fluid may contain sperm.
  • The least reliable of all methods, with a 54% failure rate in the first year of use.

Section 6: Explore: Barrier Methods of Birth Control

These barrier methods of contraception provide a barrier that prevents the male sperm from reaching the female egg. Some of these methods can be used to decrease exposure to sexually transmitted infections.

Failure rates during the first year of use vary by method:

  • Male condom = 18%
  • Female condom = 21%
  • Diaphragm/Cervical Cap = 12%
  • Sponge = 12%
  • Spermicide (used alone) = 28%
Spermicide

Method: chemical that kills sperm

Considerations:

  • Available in many forms (creams, gels, tablets, suppositories, film).
  • Effective for less than 1 hour.
  • Most effective when used in combination with another barrier method.
  • Available without a prescription, inexpensive, and easy to use.
  • It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.
Male Condom

Method: sheath that covers the penis to prevent sperm from entering the vagina

Considerations:

  • Latex condoms provide the best protection against sexually transmitted infections.
  • Avoid if allergic to latex.
  • Other types of condoms are available but are more likely to break and do not provide adequate protection against infections.
  • Available without a prescription and inexpensive, but does interfere with spontaneity.
Female Condom

Method: sheath inserted into the vagina with a flexible ring inside the closed end that fits over the cervix; another ring extends outside the vagina to partially cover the perineum

Considerations:

  • Female and male condoms should not be used together.
  • Provides protection against sexually transmitted infections.
  • Available without a prescription but more expensive than male condoms.
  • Avoid if allergic to latex.
  • It may be inserted up to 8 hours before intercourse.
Sponge

Method: soft polyurethane that traps and absorbs semen and contains spermicide when activated with water

Considerations:

  • Provides contraception for 24 hours without the need to add spermicide for repeated intercourse.
  • Available without a prescription and easy to use.
  • It can be inserted just before or hours ahead of intercourse.
  • It should remain in place for 6 hours after intercourse.
  • It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.
Diaphragm

Method: latex dome surrounded by a spring or coil that covers the cervix and prevents passage of sperm

Considerations:

  • Place spermicide into the dome and around the rim before insertion.
  • Do not use for the first 6 weeks after birth.
  • Avoid if allergic to latex or has history of toxic shock syndrome.
  • It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.
  • It may be inserted up to 6 hours before intercourse and must be left in place for at least 6 hours after intercourse.
Cervical Cap

Method: cuplike device placed over the cervix to prevent sperm from entering

Considerations:

  • Similar to a diaphragm but must be fitted by a healthcare provider.
  • Place spermicide on both sides and in the vagina after placing the cap.
  • It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.
  • It may be inserted up to 6 hours before intercourse and must be left in place for at least 6 hours after intercourse.
  • Avoid if allergic to latex or have history of toxic shock syndrome.

Section 7: Explore: Nursing Education for Barrier Methods

Now that you have learned about how barrier methods work, let's look at the nursing education needed for your clients who are interested in using a barrier method of birth control.

Diaphragm Instruction:

  • How to insert and remove the device — allow for practice
  • Insert prior to intercourse, but must remain in for 6–8 hours after intercourse
  • Empty bowel and bladder prior to insertion
  • Cleaning, storing, and inspect for damage
  • Must be refitted after pregnancy or weight change

Cervical Cap Instruction:

  • How to insert and remove the device — allow for practice
  • Cleaning, storing, and inspect for damage
  • Cannot be used with cervical inconsistencies or pelvic inflammatory disease

Male Condom Instruction:

  • Application: roll sheath along erect penis, leave room at the end for semen
  • Removal: hold condom after ejaculation and when withdrawing from the vagina to prevent emptying sperm in the vagina
  • Fingernails may tear the condom
  • Petroleum jelly deteriorates latex condoms, only use water-based lubricants
  • Should be applied only one time

Female Condom Instruction:

  • How to lubricate, insert and remove device — allow for practice
  • More expensive than male condoms

Section 10: Explore: Hormonal Birth Control

Hormonal birth control is considered safe for most women today as it contains a relatively small amount of hormones. All hormonal methods of birth control require a prescription for use.

Combination Oral Contraceptives

Description:

  • contains progestin and estrogen or progestin only
  • prevents ovulation, but does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
  • should be avoided in the first month postpartum and if breastfeeding (may use progestin only)
  • must be taken daily

Side effects: weight gain, nausea and vomiting, breakthrough bleeding, breast tenderness, headache, and irritability; risks for thromboembolism, stroke (especially over age 35 and a smoker), gallstones, benign liver tumors, myocardial infarction

Benefits:

  • does not interfere with spontaneity
  • highly effective (91%)
  • regulates menstrual cycle
  • ovulation returns within 3 months of discontinuation
  • reduces risk for benign breast disease and ovarian, endometrial, and colorectal cancers
  • improves acne, endometriosis, bone mass, and dysmenorrhea

Teaching:

  • If client misses one pill, take as soon as remember and take the next dose at the usual time.
  • If client misses two pills in the first two weeks, take both as soon as remember, then take the next dose at the usual time.
  • If client misses two pills in the third week, take one active pill each day until finished, and discard the inactive pills. Start a new pack the same day. Use backup contraception for 7 days if unable to start a new pack of pills.
  • Use backup contraceptives if taking certain medications, including anticonvulsants, antiretrovirals, and rifampin.
Emergency Contraception

Description:

  • contains levonorgestrel
  • taken due to contraceptive failure or unprotected intercourse
  • not used as primary birth control
  • delays or inhibits ovulation
  • involves taking one or two pills as soon as possible (up to 120 hours) after unprotected intercourse
Hormone Injections

Description:

  • medroxyprogesterone acetate injected intramuscularly or subcutaneously to prevent ovulation for 15 weeks
  • injections scheduled every 13 weeks
  • does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
  • may be used with breastfeeding
  • failure rate during the first year of use is 6%

Side effects: amenorrhea, breakthrough bleeding, headaches, decreased libido, weight gain, depression, decreased bone density; return to fertility may take 6–12 months or more after discontinuing

Benefits:

  • does not interfere with spontaneity
  • avoids the need for daily use of medication
Contraceptive Implant

Description:

  • single rod implant inserted subcutaneously into the upper inner arm
  • releases progestin continuously and provides 3 years of contraception
  • does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
  • may be used with breastfeeding
  • highly effective (99.5%)

Side effects: irregular bleeding

Benefits:

  • does not interfere with spontaneity
  • safe during lactation
  • fertility returns immediately after removal
Intrauterine Device (IUD)

Description:

  • inserted into the uterus to provide continuous pregnancy prevention
  • two types: copper and levonorgestrel containing
  • copper effective for 10 years; levonorgestrel effective for 3–5 years
  • does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
  • do not use in women with history of pelvic infections, ectopic pregnancy, or bleeding disorders
  • may be used with breastfeeding
  • highly effective (99.2–99.8%)

Side effects: increased menstrual bleeding, ectopic pregnancy, pelvic infection, perforation of the uterus, infertility

Benefits:

  • does not interfere with spontaneity
  • low long-term cost
  • fertility returns once device removed
  • safe for use in women who cannot use estrogen-containing contraception
Transdermal Contraceptive Patch

Description:

  • patch that releases small amounts of estrogen and progestin which are absorbed through the skin to suppress ovulation
  • new patch applied weekly to a different site for 3 weeks, then removed for 1 week to allow for menstruation
  • as effective as combination oral contraceptive pills
  • no protection against sexually transmitted infections
  • may not be used with breastfeeding
  • highly effective (91%)

Side effects: breakthrough bleeding, breast tenderness, headaches, skin reactions; less effective in women who weigh more than 198 lbs; risk for blood clots is higher than with pills

Benefits:

  • used weekly instead of daily
  • fertility returns within one month of discontinuation
  • adherence is not disrupted by water or sweat
  • does not interfere with spontaneity
  • regulates menstrual cycle
Contraceptive Vaginal Ring

Description:

  • soft, flexible vinyl ring that releases small amounts of progestin and estrogen continuously to prevent ovulation
  • inserted into the vagina and left in place for 3 weeks
  • after 3 weeks the ring is removed for 1 week to allow for menstruation then reinserted
  • no protection against sexually transmitted infections
  • may not be used with breastfeeding
  • highly effective (91%)

Side effects: similar to combination oral contraceptives; vaginitis; vaginal discharge

Benefits:

  • does not interfere with spontaneity
  • no fitting required
ACHES — Warning Signs for Hormonal Birth Control

Hormonal birth control that includes both progestin and estrogen (combination oral contraceptives, vaginal ring, or transdermal patch) increases the risk for thrombosis and high blood pressure, which can cause a heart attack or stroke. These are rare but serious side effects of hormonal birth control.

The risks are increased in women over 35 years old and in women who smoke. A black box warning label exists for smoking and oral contraceptives.

Contraindications for use: pregnancy, thromboembolic disorders, uncontrolled hypertension, migraines, breast or endometrial cancer

Remember ACHES — Seek medical care while on hormonal birth control if you experience:

  • Abdominal pain
  • Chest pain
  • Headaches
  • Eye problems
  • Speech disturbances, severe leg pain, or swelling

Permanent methods of sterilization are performed through surgical procedures. These procedures eliminate the chance of pregnancy but do not protect against sexually transmitted infections. Though possibly reversible, these methods should be considered permanent.

Tubal Sterilization

Method: Fallopian tubes are sterilized by crushing, litigating, clipping, or plugging.

  • Methods include: tube with plastic band; tube cut and cauterized; tube cut and tied

Side effects: Include bowel injury, hemorrhage, or infection.

Teaching:

  • May be performed at time of cesarean birth. The optimal time is within 48 hours of vaginal birth.
  • Report fever, severe pain, bleeding, or incisional discharge.
Vasectomy

Method: Bilateral litigation and resection of vas deferens, which carries sperm from the testes to the penis.

Side effects: hematoma, edema, infection, pain

Teaching:

  • It may take up to 3 months for complete sterilization.
  • Semen specimen should be analyzed at 8–16 weeks after the procedure.

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Care: Postpartum Mother

The amount of time the mother and newborn stay in the hospital during the postpartum period has decreased over time, leaving less time for the nurse to assess and intervene. It is crucial for the postpartum nurse to facilitate bonding and recognize physical and psychosocial warning signs to provide both the mother and newborn with the best possible chance of positive outcomes.

Stage four of labor and delivery, known as the recovery period, begins immediately after delivery of the placenta. Many changes are happening in both the mother and the newborn during this time, and the nurse acts fast to complete a lot of important tasks.

In this module, we will discuss the basics of maternal postpartum nursing care, including nursing assessment of the mother and maternal newborn bonding.

In some maternity units, there are separate units for the various phases of labor and delivery and there may be a separate newborn nursery.

The woman's body is back to a pre-pregnancy state in 6 weeks, undoing the last 9 months of anatomical and physiological changes.

Section 3: Explore: Postpartum Assessment of the Mother — BUBBLE-HEER

Stage four of labor and delivery, known as the recovery period, begins immediately after delivery of the placenta. Many changes happen in both the mother and the newborn during this time, and the nurse acts fast to complete many important tasks.

In a maternity unit set up with labor, delivery, recovery, and postpartum (LDRP) rooms, the mother and newborn continue their care with the same nurse in the same room following labor and delivery. In some maternity units, there are separate units for the various phases of labor and delivery and there may be a separate newborn nursery.

A typical delivery recovery lasts 1-2 hours. For the first 6 hours after delivery, the mother is monitored closely. She needs emotional support and close physical assessment as her mind and body adjust to significant changes. Her body immediately begins returning to nonpregnant status, undoing in just 6 weeks the last 9 months' worth of anatomical and physiological changes.

The main areas of nursing assessment are represented by the acronym BUBBLE-HEER.

B — Breasts

At the time of delivery, they have enlarged, the nipples have areolas that have darkened and become more pronounced in response to hormonal changes in preparation for breastfeeding. In supine position, palpate both breasts for engorgement, inspect nipples for soreness or skin breakdown. Assessment of the breasts also occurs when assisting with breastfeeding, which includes determining if the woman is producing early milk which is thin, watery and slightly yellow (colostrum) or if she has begun producing true milk.

U — Uterus

After delivery, the uterus gets smaller and smaller, receding to the level of the umbilicus and eventually back under the symphysis pubis. This is called involution. Natural oxytocin hormone and oxytocin administered intravenously cause contractions that compress blood vessels at the open venous site of placental attachment on the uterine wall. The uterine lining is also shed out through the vagina, and this material is called lochia. In supine position, with the bladder empty, use deep palpation to determine the location of the firm uterine fundus. It should be midline and somewhere between 2 fingerbreadths above, at, or below the umbilicus. Fundal massage should firm up a boggy uterus and may elicit a flow of lochia. Breastfeeding should also stimulate contractions. A firm uterus feels like a grapefruit. Upon palpating the abdomen to assess the uterus, it may be observed that the rectus muscles are not present anteriorly; the large uterus separates these muscles laterally as pregnancy progresses.

B — Bladder

Postpartum assessment of bladder status is considered the "new vital sign" to prevent complications associated with retained urine and a full bladder. A full bladder will cause the uterus to deviate, contributing to subinvolution, increasing the risk of hemorrhage, or contributing to the development of a urinary tract infection. Monitor for pain with urination that may indicate structural damage, infection, or an unnoticed laceration. Monitor for difficulty urinating, particularly after an epidural, so that the bladder does not fill up. Whenever possible, have her empty her own bladder, but empty the bladder by catheterization if necessary. Women who have had a C/S will have an indwelling catheter in place until they can ambulate, and after removal they may initially have difficulty emptying their bladder.

B — Bowel

The bowel becomes an important assessment of the postpartum woman in both vaginal and C/S deliveries. Hormonal influence (progesterone), pain medications, dehydration (blood loss), decreased maternal activity, and surgical interventions can lead to constipation during the postpartum period. It is not uncommon for the woman to be discharged with a stool softener medication and without having had a bowel movement if bowel sounds are active and flatulence is present. Provide education regarding constipation prevention with ambulation, adequate fluid intake and a fiber-rich diet.

L — Lochia

The fluid shed from the uterus through the vagina consisting of uterine blood and tissue is called lochia. It has a distinct odor, similar to menstrual flow, but should not be foul-smelling. A steady flow is normal, and a stream of lochia with small clots with ambulation or uterine palpation is normal.

Assess how many peri pads she is filling in an hour. One pad saturated every 30 minutes is cause for concern and further assessment of the uterus and perineum. Document using the acronym COAT (color, odor, amount, and time):

  • Days 1–3 — Lochia rubra: bright red, dark red, or reddish brown
  • Days 3–10 — Lochia serosa: pink or brown-tinged, thins over time
  • Days 10–14 — Lochia alba: white, cream, or light yellow

Excessive bright red (lochia rubra) may indicate a retained placental fragment or other issue. With excessive blood loss, sanitary pads should be weighed (dry weight subtracted from used weight) to determine accurate blood loss.

E — Episiotomy (and Lacerations: Perineum Assessment)

Episiotomy and lacerations are common following labor and delivery. Lacerations are characterized as first-degree (superficial) through fourth-degree (laceration goes into the rectal wall). Third- and fourth-degree lacerations may impact the woman's ability to have a bowel movement. Hematomas can occur in this area as well and are silent causes of postpartum hemorrhage.

In supine position with adequate lighting, ask the mother to relax her knees then inspect the perineum. Episiotomy and laceration repairs should be well approximated and remain intact, free of hematomas and drainage. Assess for hemorrhoids, common after a long 3rd stage (pushing).

Document perineal assessment using the acronym REEDA:

  • R = Redness
  • E = Edema
  • E = Ecchymosis
  • D = Drainage
  • A = Approximation

Initially, ice packs are recommended for swelling, vasoconstriction, and comfort. Other assistive measures: peri-donut, witch hazel pads, anesthetic sprays, sitz bath, peri-bottle. Teach the mother to pat dry after voiding (not wipe) and to always change peri pads front to back.

H — Hematoma & Hemorrhoids

Hematoma vulvar is caused by rapid bleeding into soft tissue causing severe pain often described as "pressure," associated with precipitous delivery or vacuum/forceps delivery. May require surgical intervention to ligate the bleeding vessel.

Hemorrhoids are varicosities of the rectum from straining or pressure from the enlarged pregnant uterus. Usually resolve after delivery but can be treated for pain/pressure relief.

E — Extremities

The woman's blood during the postpartum period is hypercoagulable and, in combination with inactivity following delivery, she is at risk for deep vein thrombosis (DVT). Lower extremities are assessed for localized redness, warmth, edema, and tenderness. Weak pedal pulses in the affected extremity may indicate obstruction due to thrombus formation. Pedal pulses should be assessed bilaterally. Encouraging early ambulation can prevent DVT.

A Homan's sign (discomfort in the calf upon dorsiflexion of the foot) may be used to gauge DVT presence, although it is nonspecific and not sensitive. A DVT may not always cause pain — all assessment findings should be reported to the healthcare provider.

E — Emotional Status

Women go through a rush of emotions during the postpartum period. Assess for signs of bonding with the newborn, engagement in parenting skills, and the relationship with her partner. Postpartum depression is not uncommon.

Assess ability to rest and amount of sleep. A breastfeeding baby will want to nurse every 2-3 hours at least; encourage her to sleep when the newborn sleeps. If further assessment is needed, collaborate interdisciplinarily with the provider and social worker.

Puerperal phases of maternal adaptation:

  • Taking-In Phase: Mother is focused on her own needs, may be content to allow others to make decisions, may have difficulty assimilating information. Allow her to discuss her labor and delivery experience as often as needed.
  • Taking-Hold Phase: Mother may have anxiety about being a mother. Encourage her to provide as much care for the baby as possible.
  • Letting-Go Phase: Parents give up previous roles as a carefree couple and relinquish part of infant care to a caretaker. Provide opportunity to discuss unexpected feelings.
R — RhoGAM, Rubella, and Reflexes
  • RhoGAM: Required for all mothers who are Rh(−) who carried an Rh(+) fetus. Administered via IM injection within 72 hours of delivery. At birth, the baby receives a direct Coombs test from the umbilical cord to perform the antibody titer.
  • Rubella vaccine: Subcutaneous (SQ) injection for nonimmune or equivocal rubella status. Rubella is a live virus; administered after childbirth to prevent acquiring rubella during subsequent pregnancies. Avoid pregnancy for 1 month after vaccination.
  • Reflexes: Check brachial, radial, and patellar deep tendon reflexes (DTRs). 1+ and 2+ are normal; 3+ and 4+ are hyperactive and indicate cerebral irritability — may suggest preeclampsia. Check clonus: dorsiflexion of foot sharply while knee is flexed. Positive clonus = rapid muscle contraction/relaxation ("beats of clonus") against examiner's hand.

Section 6: Explore: Parent-Newborn Bonding After Delivery

Let's look closer at the emotional status aspect of the BUBBLE-HEER postpartum assessment in relation to bonding.

Currently, under normal circumstances, the mother and newborn stay in the hospital for 48 hours after vaginal delivery, and 72 hours after cesarean delivery. The mother may choose to be discharged earlier at the healthcare provider's discretion.

The assessment of the parent-newborn relationship begins early in the pregnancy. The nurse considers how the woman and her family are reacting to the news of an impending addition to the family. At delivery, the nurse observes the mother's initial reaction to seeing her child — this varies and there isn't necessarily one "right" reaction.

Facilitating the Initial Parent-Newborn Bond

The nurse plays an important role in helping to facilitate the initial parent-newborn bond after delivery.

  • Postpartum bonding is encouraged from the moment of birth through early physical contact between the newborn and the mother.
  • A common practice is to lay the newborn on the mother's chest immediately after delivery before the cord is even cut. Even in cesarean deliveries, the newborn is brought to the mother's chest while still in the operating room.
  • The mother makes eye contact with her newborn and begins talking to it. The newborn can best see objects held 8-10 inches away. The newborn sees the mother's face already at birth and responds to her differently than other sights and sounds. For this reason, the oily prophylactic antibiotic eye drops used routinely at delivery are withheld for about an hour after delivery to allow the infant to clearly see the mother and father.
  • When the newborn is soothed by being in the mother's arms, this attachment is strengthened further.
  • The postpartum nurse will conduct most, if not all, necessary assessments and interventions while the newborn remains on the mother's chest, as long as the infant remains stable. This includes auscultating heart and lung sounds, warming and drying the newborn, and assessing Apgar scores.
  • The newborn will likely express interest in breastfeeding at delivery — sucking on its hand, rooting around searching for the nipple. The first bath is often withheld for up to 24 hours after delivery to allow for constant skin-to-skin contact and intermittent breastfeeding on demand to further strengthen this initial bond.
  • The partner will often exhibit engrossment right away: eye-to-eye contact with the infant and intense fascination.

Section 7: Explore: Promoting and Understanding the Parent-Newborn Bond

What can postpartum nurses do to promote bonding and parenting skills?

  • Compliment parents on their newborn.
  • Reassure parents when their newborn's assessment is normal and when their appearance will change. Often, the sight of a facial rash, swelling, etc. causes the parents concern and may impact bonding.
  • Encourage sibling participation and involvement.
  • Be welcoming to the woman's family and significant others.
  • Reassure parents as they learn newborn care skills and praise them when they accomplish a new task.
  • Act as a role model and demonstrate attentive newborn care.

Consider that women in various circumstances may react differently to seeing their newborn for the first time:

  • a woman who is alone and is giving her child up for adoption
  • a woman who has a supportive spouse and family
  • a young teenager with self-image problems
  • a woman whose values/beliefs do not include the outward showing of emotion

Each of these women has a unique story that plays into how they bond with their newborn. The nurse's role is to provide:

  • physical support
  • emotional comforting
  • parenting guidance
  • acceptance without judgment

Section 9 — Explore: Postpartum Assessment of the Mother - Vital Signs

Tab: Normal Postpartum Vital Signs

Normal postpartum vital signs for the mother are:

  • Temperature: may be elevated for 24 hours after delivery due to dehydration
  • Pulse: may be low for 6–8 days after delivery due to increased cardiac output and stroke volume
  • Respirations: should be normal following delivery
  • Blood Pressure: may be low when standing (orthostatic hypotension) due to anatomical changes as the body returns to normal
Tab: Monitoring

After a normal vaginal delivery, vital signs are monitored every 15 minutes during recovery, and then every 4 hours for 24 hours, and then every 8 hours until discharge, if the mother remains stable.

Causes for alterations in vital signs include:

  • Analgesic or anesthetic medications
  • Hypovolemia due to excessive delivery blood loss
  • Presence of pain
  • Presence of infection after 24 hours
  • Complications from epidural or spinal placement may alter respirations

Monitor for signs and symptoms of hypovolemic shock:

  • Filling a peri pad every 15–30 minutes or more
  • Dizziness with standing
  • Hypotension and tachycardia
  • Cold, clammy skin
  • Anxiety
  • Uterine atony (boggy, loss of tone)
  • Notify the healthcare provider immediately. Provide supplemental oxygen and prepare to provide medications, intravenous (IV) fluid, and perhaps prep for surgery.

Section 11 — Explore: Maternal Postpartum Nursing Actions - Pain Control

The primary areas of maternal postpartum nursing actions in addition to assessment are pain control, self-care, promoting the return to pre-pregnancy physical status, education, and emotional support.

Vaginal Delivery Comfort Measures

  • After a vaginal delivery, most women experience uterine cramping and perineal pain.
  • Ice packs for the perineal area are a common remedy used by postpartum nurses.
  • Intense vaginal or perineal pain may be an indication that a hematoma is present and must be investigated immediately.

Cesarean Delivery Comfort Measures

  • After a Cesarean delivery, if the woman did not go through labor first, the presence of normal post-operative pain is in addition to uterine cramping pain.

General Comfort Measures

  • There are women who go through hours of difficult labor, and possibly even an episiotomy, only to require a Cesarean delivery anyway. These women in particular experience many discomforts.
  • Keeping pain under control helps encourage the woman to resume normal activity, which aids her body in returning to pre-pregnancy status.
  • Pharmaceutical pain management may be needed.

Section 12 — Explore: Maternal Postpartum Nursing Actions - Ambulation and Self-care

The first time the woman ambulates after delivery involves ensuring her physical readiness.

Slide 1 — The nurse should consider the following:

Are her vital signs stable? Is her blood pressure low? Is she losing an excessive amount of blood? Is her uterus firm? Is her bladder distended? If she had an epidural, has the feeling returned to her lower extremities?

Slide 2 — Following a cesarean delivery:

The woman will have an indwelling catheter in place for a period of time. The catheter is keeping her bladder empty. Following a vaginal delivery, the nurse must make sure her bladder does not fill and compete with the uterus for space. This will cause excessive blood loss because the uterus is unable to adequately contract and occlude the blood vessels where the placenta was attached. Therefore, the initial void should take place within 4–6 hours of delivery if not sooner.

Slide 3 — Slow and in steps:

The nurse will take things slow and in steps. First, the patient will sit up on the side of the bed and allow her blood pressure to normalize. Does she feel dizzy? Next, she can touch her toes to the floor and see if they feel tingly. Then, she can stand up in place at the bedside. If she is not dizzy and she can bear weight easily, two nurses will typically assist her to the bathroom monitoring continuously.

Slide 4 — Once in the bathroom:

The bed sheets from delivery are changed. It may take several minutes for her to be able to urinate due to edema in the area from delivery as well as anesthesia. Sometimes, turning on the faucet and letting her listen to the sound of the water is enough to initiate a urine stream. The nurse then uses a peri bottle filled with lukewarm water to spray the perineal area. The woman pats dry with toilet paper rather than wiping which will irritate the perineum and disrupt the sutures. A new peri pad is put in place and on top she will typically also use witch hazel pads and possibly an ice pack. She may at this time also spray her perineum with lidocaine spray.

Slide 5 — Keep the perineum clean:

She washes her hands and returns to the bed or chair. This process is repeated every time she uses the bathroom. She may need assistance with this several times before she is safe to do so on her own. After she is able to ambulate several times without difficulty and her vital signs remain stable, the woman can shower and ambulate as tolerated. Ambulation is important for many reasons, including return of normal bowel function. It is very important to keep the perineum clean to prevent infection. Baths, other than sitz baths, are not permitted for at least 6 weeks because of the risk of infection.

Slide 6 — Fundal and lochia assessment:

The nurse may lay her back down and assess her fundus and bladder after urination to ensure she was able to empty it and that her uterus is firm and has normal placement. Uterine fundal and lochia assessments continue every 15 minutes for up to 2 hours after delivery or until stable. As discussed, the uterus should be firm, midline and be going lower in the abdomen in relation to the umbilicus. A boggy uterus should firm up with fundal massage. Abnormalities in this area may necessitate postpartum hemorrhage medications.

Section 1: Explore — Introduction to Postpartum Complications

Introduction to Nursing Care: Postpartum Complications

Although many women experience the joy of birth without any complications, the potential for maternal complications continues postpartum, particularly hemorrhage, blood clots, and infections.

The postpartum nurse anticipates these and other complications, using the nursing process to recognize cues and provide care that greatly impacts maternal outcomes.

Section 2: Prepare — Postpartum Complications

Section 3: Explore — The Fourth Stage of Labor: Closer Look

Select each tab below to learn more about the potential for postpartum hemorrhage during the fourth stage of labor.

Tab 1: Postpartum Hemorrhage

Postpartum hemorrhage refers to excessive bleeding following delivery of the placenta. Early postpartum hemorrhage is defined as a cumulative blood loss of greater than or equal to 1000 mL or blood loss accompanied by clinical manifestations of hypovolemia within 24 hours following birth. Late postpartum hemorrhage is significant blood loss occurring after 24 hours and up to 6-12 weeks after birth.

Postpartum hemorrhage is one of the leading causes of death related to pregnancy, accounting for 10.7% of pregnancy related deaths (CDC, n.d.), and has been on the rise in the U.S. (CDC, 2019). According to a recent study, 70% of deaths related to hemorrhage were preventable with provider factors and systems of care factors among the highest contributing factors (Building U.S. Capacity to Review and Prevent Maternal Deaths, 2018). Healthcare providers and healthcare systems have a significant role in reducing maternal deaths related to hemorrhage.

  • Assessment was the most common factor among the provider factors.
  • "Inadequate training and inadequate or unavailable personnel, absence of policies and procedures, and a lack of coordination between providers in patient management" were the common themes for the systems of care factors.
  • Patient factors were related to knowledge of warning signs and the need to seek care.

NOTE: Nurses play a key role in preventing maternal deaths related to hemorrhage by taking action to ensure thorough nursing assessment, communication and coordination between providers, and patient education are addressed throughout the patient's stay.

Tab 2: Placenta

To understand why postpartum hemorrhage occurs and why it is a dangerous situation, let's first review some key aspects of the placenta. The placenta is attached to the inner wall of the uterus by blood vessels, allowing the maternal circulation to be filtered by the placenta and enter the fetal circulation.

Transcript:

  • The placenta is attached to the wall of the uterus and grows in size during pregnancy.
  • [The placenta] Produces progesterone hormone.
  • The placenta delivers oxygen and nutrients to the fetus, while filtering waste products.
  • The fetus has its own blood supply and does not share blood with the mother.
  • Deoxygenated blood from the developing baby travels through the umbilical cord to the placenta.
  • Blood from the mother, rich in oxygen and nutrients, passes through the placenta as well.
  • Gas exchange occurs in the intervillous space of the placenta, allowing oxygenated blood to be returned to the fetus and deoxygenated blood to be transported away.
Tab 3: Placental Delivery

The third stage of labor begins with the infant delivery and ends with the placental delivery. When the placenta pulls away from the uterine wall, the uterine blood vessels are open, and the maternal circulatory system continues to pump maternal blood into these now-exposed, open blood vessels with each heartbeat.

Tab 4: Uterine Involution

After placenta delivery, the uterus begins the postpartum process of returning to a pre-pregnancy state, going from the full-term size of a watermelon back down to its former pear size. This process is known as uterine involution (regression).

Transcript:

Uterus Contractions: The uterus continues contracting through the delivery of the placenta and beyond (although not usually as severe), remaining rigid and firm, and drops down further and further in the abdomen and behind the symphysis pubis. These contractions are necessary to constrict the open blood vessels in the uterine wall where the placenta was once attached.

Administering Intravenous Oxytocin: To ensure the uterus contractions persist and do not weaken, routine delivery orders typically include the nurse administering intravenous oxytocin (an oxytocic medication) after the placenta delivers.

Assessing the Placenta: The placenta is also assessed to ensure that it is detached from the uterus in one piece as it should. The placenta is always inspected by the provider after it delivers to ensure it is intact. If some of the placental fragments remain in the uterus, it can cause excessive bleeding. The fragments will need to be removed by the healthcare provider.

Assessing the Birth Canal: The visible structures of the birth canal (cervix, vagina, and perineum) are also assessed by the provider following vaginal delivery, and lacerations that continue bleeding are repaired using sutures.

Section 4: Explore — Postpartum Hemorrhage: Overview

We have looked at what normally occurs during the 4th stage of labor in order to prevent and assess excessive postpartum bleeding. Now, let's look at what happens when things don't occur normally.

Tab 1: Causes of Postpartum Hemorrhage

The uterine fundus (the muscular area that produces contractions) must continue to contract. If uterine contractions stop after delivery and the uterus becomes flaccid (known as uterine atony), the maternal circulation will continue pumping blood into the open blood vessels where the placenta was once attached.

Complications that inhibit postpartum uterine contractions and involution are the main reasons excessive, uncontrolled bleeding occurs, but may also result from unnoticed structural damage sustained following traumatic vaginal deliveries. The amount of lost blood (that doesn't return to the maternal circulation) can become excessive, leading to postpartum hemorrhage, hypovolemia, hypovolemic shock, and death.

Tab 2: Types of Postpartum Hemorrhage

Postpartum hemorrhage is classified as early or late, depending on when the hemorrhaging occurs in relation to childbirth.

Early Postpartum Hemorrhage:

  • It usually occurs within the first hour after delivery.
  • The total estimated blood loss is ≥1000 mL.
  • It is most often caused by uterine atony, in which the uterine muscle relaxes rather than contracts.
  • It can also be related to structural damage caused by a traumatic vaginal delivery rather than ineffective uterine tone.
    • Hematomas (occur when blood collects into a localized tissue) are a hidden source of postpartum hemorrhage that may be visible on the vulva (opening of the vagina) or hidden in the vagina or retroperitoneal area.
    • Unrepaired vaginal, perineal or cervical lacerations or other trauma from delivery that go unnoticed and unrepaired may continue to bleed excessively.

Late (or Secondary) Postpartum Hemorrhage:

  • It occurs after 24 hours or up to 6-12 weeks after delivery.
  • It is most often caused by complications that inhibit the normal process of uterine involution, including:
    • Subinvolution—The uterus is delayed in returning to its pre-pregnancy characteristics.
    • Retained placental fragments—The placenta detaches from the uterine wall in pieces rather than whole, so uterine tone and contractions are inhibited.
    • Uterine infection can weaken the uterus, making it ineffective at contracting.

Section 6: Explore — Postpartum Hemorrhage and Uterine Atony

Postpartum Hemorrhage and Uterine Atony: Causes and Contributing Factors

As you have learned, the most common cause of postpartum hemorrhage is uterine atony. What is uterine atony? And what causes it to occur?

Tab 1: Uterine Atony

Uterine atony is a condition in which the muscles of a woman's uterus lose their tone and their ability to contract after childbirth. Hemorrhage can result at any time when uterine atony occurs.

A uterus that functions properly will contract and control postpartum blood loss as the uterus heals from the inside out. These contractions (along with the physiologic changes the uterus undergoes after delivery) begin the process of postpartum uterine involution (regression) from the full-term watermelon size back down to its former pear size. The maternal fundus should be firm and round by palpation and located midline on the abdomen at the umbilicus or just below following delivery.

In terms of the uterus, think of a balloon that has been filled full of air, and then either gradually or suddenly has the air let out. What does the balloon look like? It is stretched out, weakened, and even a bit wrinkled.

Tab 2: Causes & Contributing Factors

The uterus normally retains enough tone and strength after delivery to constrict the open blood vessels in the uterus where the placenta was located. However, sometimes it does not.

Excessive Distention of the Uterus: The uterus is excessively stretched out, like an overfilled balloon, and loses its tone and shape. One of the following situations can cause the uterus to excessively stretch out:

  • a multiple pregnancy (twins, triplets, etc.)
  • hydramnios (excessive amniotic fluid)
  • macrosomia (excessively large fetus)
  • obesity
  • large placenta

A Full Bladder: A full bladder will compete with the uterus for space, pushing it up and possibly off to one side. A uterus that is offset from the midline frequently indicates a full bladder is impeding the involution and postpartum hemorrhage may result.

Dysfunctional Labor: Prolonged labor or the ineffective second stage (pushing) of labor overworks and weakens the uterus, causing it to lose tone. When the labor is particularly traumatic to the uterus, such as with instrument-assisted deliveries, the uterine muscle can be damaged and weakened.

Placenta Accrete: Placenta accrete (which causes retained placental fragments) is a condition in which the placenta implants too deeply into the uterine wall, making it difficult or impossible to detach during the 4th stage of labor.

Uterine Infection: Uterine infection can also cause the uterus to become weakened and ineffective at constricting.

Medications: Medications used to induce, augment, or stop labor act on the uterine muscle.

  • Medications used to induce or augment labor: Uterus is artificially forced to contract, overworks, and weakens the uterus.
  • Magnesium sulfate: The uterus is artificially forced to stop contracting (preterm labor) or to prevent seizures (preeclampsia). If delivery occurs near the time that magnesium sulfate has been administered, this uterine atony can lead to postpartum hemorrhage.
  • Nifedipine: Nifedipine is an antihypertensive that limits muscular contractions. If the delivery occurs near the time nifedipine has been administered, the unintended effect can be uterine atony and postpartum hemorrhage.

Section 8: Explore — Postpartum Nursing Assessment: Preventing Uterine Atony

Remember! The best way to prevent uterine atony is to keep the uterus firm and the bladder empty.

Uterine atony and postpartum hemorrhage can progress rapidly to hypovolemic shock. The nurse plays a key role in preventing and detecting postpartum hemorrhage, allowing early treatment to minimize blood loss.

Tab 1: Palpate the Uterine Fundus

The postpartum nurse palpates the uterine fundus routinely and frequently to ensure it is firm and in the anticipated location relative to the umbilicus.

  • The protocol for many hospitals is every 15 minutes for the first hour postpartum, with a decreasing frequency over the next 24 hours, provided the mother remains stable. Those with predisposing risk factors may require more frequent assessment.
  • For the first 24 hours postpartum, the uterus should feel like a firm grapefruit when palpated and be midline with the umbilicus. If the uterus is found to be boggy (soft and not contracted) and/or displaced, the nurse will massage the fundus until firm.

Section 9: Explore: Postpartum Nursing Assessment: Detecting Uterine Atony

Throughout postpartum care, the nurse assesses for early signs of uterine atony and postpartum hemorrhage, which can be detected through the amount and characteristics of lochia, the uterine location and firmness, as well as bladder status.

Tab 1: Signs of Uterine Atony
Boggy Uterine Fundus

What does uterine atony "look" like? Because, like a deflated balloon, it has lost its tone, the uterine fundus will show some of these signs:

  • It is difficult to palpate or boggy.
  • It firms up with massage, but then becomes boggy again.
  • It is located above the expected level of uterine involution.

Lochia

Uterine atony may also be indicated by excessive lochia flow that is bright, red with large clots present. It is important to accurately assess and track the amount of postpartum lochia or blood loss.

Remember, lochia should be dark red and moderate in amount (saturating no more than 1 peri-pad per hour) and may be a trickle or a gush with or without small clots.

Tab 2: Assessment of Uterine Atony

Let's look at some common situations that a postpartum nurse experiences when palpating the fundus.

Scenario: The fundus is boggy and there is heavy lochia rubra.

If the uterus isn't firm, it will not be effective at clamping down on the exposed blood vessels in the uterus, which means there will be heavy bleeding.

Scenario: The fundus is boggy, above the umbilicus and slightly lateral, and there is heavy lochia rubra.

What would cause the uterus to be above the umbilicus and displaced laterally?

A full bladder. This displacement impacts the uterus's ability to contract and will lead to excessive bleeding.

Scenario: The fundus is boggy initially, but the lochia rubra is light. With fundal massage, the fundus contracts and is in the expected location, but there is a gush of lochia rubra with small clots.

Fundal massage made the uterus contract, but it also caused lochia to gush out of the vagina. This is not concerning because when the woman lays down for a while, the lochia will pool in the uterus and clot. Then, when she stands up or the uterus is massaged, the lochia and clots are expelled from the uterus.

Scenario: The fundus is firm and in the expected location, but there is moderate to heavy bleeding.

Where is the blood coming from? Some bleeding may be caused by open vaginal/cervical lacerations or tears. When the fundus is firm but bleeding is heavy, a laceration should be anticipated. The healthcare provider should be notified so a repair can occur.

Section 10: Explore: Postpartum Nursing Assessment: Other Cues of Postpartum Hemorrhage

In addition to prevention and detection of uterine atony, the postpartum nurse will frequently and closely monitor for other cues related to postpartum hemorrhage.

Tab: Perineum

Hematomas are a hidden source of postpartum hemorrhage that may be visible as a bulging mass or discoloration on the vulva (opening of the vagina), but hematomas can also be hidden in the vagina or retroperitoneal area. Assess for signs that a hematoma has formed or lacerations are bleeding excessively. The signs include:

  • deep, severe pelvic or rectal pain
  • changes in vital signs and pallor without outward excessive bleeding

If suspected, a healthcare provider should be notified immediately.

Tab: Uterine Involution

Frequently monitor for signs of subinvolution (fundal height and placement relative to the umbilicus).

Tab: Uterine Infection
  • Pain in the lower abdomen or pelvis (uterine tenderness) is an indication of uterine infection, especially when accompanied by elevated temperature, chills, tachycardia, headaches, fatigue, and nausea.
  • Foul-smelling, profuse lochia is another primary indication of uterine infection.
  • Blood or intrauterine cultures are needed to determine the pathogen to treat.
Tab: Hypovolemia and Hypovolemic Shock

Monitor for signs that the blood loss has progressed to the point of hypovolemia or hypovolemic shock. As the body works to restore blood volume to vital areas, these signs may be present:

  • Tachycardia is an early sign that the body is working to compensate.
  • Blood pressure may remain stable initially, but the systolic and diastolic pressures will show signs of trouble (e.g., decreased pulse pressure).
  • As compensation begins to fail, hypotension will be noted.
  • Inadequate organ profusion will cause oliguria, restlessness, air hunger, and ultimately organ failure.

Section 11: Explore: Nursing Actions

Treatment of Postpartum Hemorrhage & Hypovolemia
  • Notify the healthcare provider immediately.
  • Ensure the client maintains bed rest to increase venous return and maintain cardiac output.
  • Prepare for medical interventions:
    • Interventions to address uterine atony include fundal massage, keeping the bladder empty, and administration of prescribed oxytocic drugs such as methylergonovine maleate, misoprostol, and oxytocin.
    • Specimen collection for laboratory studies includes a complete blood count (hemoglobin, hematocrit, and platelets).
    • Ensure that intravenous access points are patent and possibly add a second site using a large-bore catheter for infusion of replacement fluids, medications, and blood transfusion.
    • Identify and repair possible lacerations.
    • Prepare for a possible dilation and evacuation (D&E) procedure to remove retained placenta fragments.
    • If bleeding cannot be controlled, a hysterectomy (surgical removal of the uterus) may be necessary.

Section 13: Explore: Puerperal (Postpartum) Infection

Puerperal (postpartum) infection is a term used to describe the various bacterial infections that can occur in the postpartum period. Physiological conditions are favorable for the spread of infection in pregnancy and after childbirth. Consider what you already know about infections, pregnancy, and childbirth so far. There are many opportunities for bacteria to proliferate and spread during childbirth and beyond.

Tab 1: Puerperal Infection

A puerperal infection is defined as a temperature of ≥38 ºC (100.4 ºF) after the first 24 hours postpartum and occurring on 2+ days of the first 10 days postpartum.

Elevated body temperature in the first 24 hours postpartum is not unusual because of the physical exertion and dehydration of childbirth, so additional cues that a true infection is present are assessed as well.

Transcript:

  • Occur: After the first 24 hours of postpartum
  • Sign: Fever, ≥38°C (100.4 °F), remaining more than 2 days within the first 10 days of postpartum
Tab 2: Types of Puerperal Infections

The most common puerperal infections are:

Endometritis (Infection of the Uterine Lining)

Risk factors include:

  • prolonged rupture of membranes (allows vaginal organisms to ascend into the uterine cavity)
  • prolonged labor (more interventions and more opportunities for contamination)
  • cesarean birth
  • retained placental fragments

Nursing assessment includes:

  • abdominal pain and cramping
  • decreased or absent bowel sounds
  • nausea and vomiting
  • uterine tenderness
  • purulent foul-smelling lochia

Nursing interventions include:

  • promote drainage of lochia with high-Fowler's position
  • provide additional support if the woman may need to remain hospitalized after the newborn is discharged

Medical treatment will include antibiotics, antipyretics, and oxytocics.

Wound Infections

Wound infections (including surgically repaired lacerations, episiotomies, and cesarean incisions) are further complicated by medical conditions that increase infection risk and poor healing (such as malnutrition and diabetes mellitus).

Nursing assessment includes:

  • edema, warmth, redness, tenderness, and pain
  • purulent drainage

Nursing interventions include:

  • warm compresses
  • sitz bath

Medical treatment may include antibiotics, analgesics, and surgical debridement, if needed.

Urinary Tract Infections

During childbirth, the bladder may be traumatized by pressure from the descending fetus. Frequent catheterization also increases the risk for infection. After childbirth, the bladder is hypotonic. Urinary retention and stasis contribute to the risk for infection.

Nursing assessment includes:

  • dysuria, urgency, frequency
  • suprapubic pain
  • hematuria
  • may progress to pyelonephritis

Nursing interventions include:

  • encourage oral hydration
  • teach about drinks that acidify the urine (cranberry, plum, prune juice)
  • avoid grapefruit juice and carbonated drinks as they increase urine alkalinity
  • teach measures to prevent UTI

Medical treatment will include antibiotics, hydration, and urinary analgesics.

Section 15: Explore: Postpartum Thrombosis

Pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state, and the risk for thrombus formation is increased. During labor, delivery, and the initial postpartum period, the woman does not ambulate as much as usual, which results in venous stasis. In addition, blood vessels are likely to be damaged during delivery. These circumstances combine to increase the risk of thromboembolism. The most common thromboembolisms in pregnancy and postpartum include:

  • Superficial Venous Thrombosis (SVT) — most often associated with varicose veins and limited to the calf area.
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) — most concerning because of the risk that the embolus will travel to and occlude the pulmonary artery and result in a pulmonary embolism (PE), which is potentially fatal.
  • Pulmonary embolism (PE) — "a blood clot that develops in a blood vessel in the body (often in the leg). It then travels to a lung artery where it suddenly blocks blood flow." (Johns Hopkins Medicine, n.d., para. 1)
Tab: Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

Signs and symptoms of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE) are often absent, but the woman may report pain in the leg, groin, lower back or right lower quadrant. The most common signs are swelling of the leg, erythema, heat, and tenderness over the affected area.

Tab: Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Clinical signs and symptoms of PE depend on how large the mobile clot is. When assessing the client, subjective reports of chest pain or abdominal pain may be received. Tachycardia, dyspnea, and tachypnea are objective findings reported during a PE. Upon auscultation, pulmonary rales may be assessed; coughing, hemoptysis (expectoration of blood or the presence of bloody sputum), or a low-grade fever may be observed with a PE.

[3D interactive graphic: pulmonary embolism]

Tab: Prevention

The best treatment is prevention. The nurse recognizes those most at risk and monitors signs and symptoms closely. Anticoagulant medications may be ordered for those with a history of DVT or PE. Take extra care to prevent injury during labor, delivery and the postpartum period. Limit venous stasis by encouraging women to wear compression stockings and ambulate early and often after delivery to limit venous stasis.

Section 17: Explore: Postpartum Seizures and Postpartum Depression

Additional maternal postpartum complications include postpartum seizures and postpartum depression.

Tab: Postpartum Seizures

Preeclampsia and/or eclampsia that develops in pregnancy are not instantly cured after delivery. Seizure precautions remain a safety priority, possibly for 48-72 hours or more. The nurse will continue monitoring deep tendon reflexes, urinary output, and blood pressure.

Tab: Postpartum Depression (PPD)

All women are at risk for postpartum depression. Postpartum depression is a childbirth complication unique to the postpartum period that persists for longer than 2 weeks (up to a year) and usually presents within the first 3 months postpartum. Clients most at risk for postpartum depression may have:

  • pre-existing depression or a history of sexual abuse
  • unplanned pregnancy or strained relationships, young maternal age, financial difficulties, or lack of family/partner support
  • complicated pregnancy, traumatic delivery

Symptoms of postpartum depression include:

  • expressing guilt, excessive crying, or loss of pleasure in normal activities
  • agitation, irritability, confusion or difficulty concentrating and making decisions
  • feeling sick, changes in appetite, or lack of energy
  • less responsive to newborn and disengagement

Let's review the main nursing considerations related to maternal postpartum complications.

Always:

  • Be aware of those who are at increased risk for complications and be vigilant with assessment.
  • Notify the healthcare provider immediately if signs exist so that treatment can begin as soon as possible.
  • Provide ongoing teaching regarding the prevention and detection of postpartum complications.
Tab: Postpartum Hemorrhage

The best way to prevent uterine atony is to keep the uterus firm and the bladder empty. Prevent and detect postpartum hemorrhage with frequent monitoring of uterine tone (firmness), involution progress (the level of fundus relative to the umbilicus), and lochia characteristics.

Tab: Puerperal Infection

Use infection prevention techniques. Women during and after childbirth are very susceptible to bacterial spread from one body area to another and the physiologic conditions for proliferation are ideal. Be aware of the early signs of infection which can be mistaken for normal post-delivery physiologic responses.

Tab: Thrombosis

Pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state, so every woman is at risk to some degree. Prevent thrombosis by limiting venous stasis and detecting signs and symptoms.

Tab: Postpartum Seizures

Preeclampsia symptoms and seizure precautions remain a safety priority for up to 48-72 hours or more after delivery. The nurse needs to continue monitoring deep tendon reflexes and blood pressure (among other signs).

Tab: Postpartum Depression

All women are at risk for postpartum depression, though some more than others. The nurse provides education and resources for the woman and her loved ones to monitor the early warning signs and intervene as soon as possible.

Section 1 — Introduction to Postpartum Teaching

Throughout pregnancy and the postpartum period, the nurse provides self-care education the woman will need to remember and perform after she is discharged. Typically, the mother will return for a postpartum visit with her healthcare provider between 4–6 weeks following a vaginal delivery and 2 weeks after a cesarean delivery. In the meantime, she will need to know what is normal and what should be reported to her healthcare provider. The education the nurse provides prior to discharge is key to health maintenance and promotion for the mother and infant, particularly in between well-care visits.

In this module, we will discuss maternal postpartum teaching. We will discuss components of newborn postpartum teaching in another module.

Postpartum Support International is a great resource for healthcare providers to share with the postpartum woman.

Section 2 — Prepare: Postpartum Teaching

Q1 — Postpartum Discharge (Multiple Choice)

Correct Answer: The mother may be discharged quickly, possibly as soon as 24 hours postpartum.

Feedback: Standard discharge is 48 hours following a vaginal birth and 72–96 hours following a cesarean birth. However, early discharge at 24 hours is possible. Regardless of birth experience level, all mothers are fatigued from managing pain, visitors, and infant care.

Q2 — Postpartum Follow-Up Care (Fill in the Blank)

Correct Answer: 4–6 weeks after vaginal birth; 2 weeks after cesarean birth.

Feedback: The mother is typically seen by her healthcare provider 4 to 6 weeks after vaginal birth and 2 weeks after cesarean birth.

Q3 — Postpartum Infection (Select All That Apply)

Correct Answers:

  • Temperature greater than 100.4°F (38°C)
  • Redness, drainage, separation of cesarean incision
  • Chills
  • Foul-smelling vaginal discharge

Feedback: The warning signs of infection for the postpartum mother include a temperature greater than 100.4°F (38°C), chills, redness, drainage, separation of cesarean incision, and/or foul-smelling vaginal discharge. Lochia that becomes lighter and decreases in amount is a normal finding. Hemorrhoids and constipation are not warning signs of infection.

Section 3 — Explore: Postpartum Teaching — Maternal Self-Care

Let's look at some of the key statements the nurse will make to a client throughout her postpartum stay and reinforce prior to discharge.

Handwashing

Handwashing is one of the most important tools you have to keep yourself and your newborn healthy. Always wash your hands thoroughly before you touch your breasts, after diaper changes, after urinating or having a bowel movement, after changing your peripads, and always before you touch the baby.

Uterus

Your uterus is working hard to return to where it was pre-pregnancy, under your pubic bone. For that to happen, it will continue to cramp and contract, sometimes called "afterpains," particularly when you are breastfeeding. It should feel firm like a softball and stay in line with your belly button. It should go down the width of a finger every day. Remember to check your uterus and massage it through your abdomen. If it goes off to one side, doesn't feel firm, or isn't as low as it should be, empty your bladder and massage it again. That should make it firm up.

Around postpartum day 14, you won't be able to feel your uterus through your abdomen anymore. You should begin menstruating again between 10 weeks to 6 months postpartum but use a contraceptive when you are cleared to resume sexual activity because you may still become pregnant before your menses return. Breastfeeding does not protect you from pregnancy.

Lochia

The vaginal bleeding you have is called lochia. It is normal and will dwindle and change over time, but you might continue to feel a gush of it when you're breastfeeding for a little while longer. It is important to keep an eye on the amount of lochia and what it looks like, because that tells us that your uterus is healthy and healing properly. You noticed that today you had about a 4-inch stain of lochia on your peripad every hour, but with breastfeeding, it is about 6 inches. You also noticed that it has changed to more of a pink-brown color. This amount and color are normal at this stage.

Around postpartum day 10, it should lighten even further until it stops sometime between day 14 and up to 3–6 weeks. Keep using the peripads until it stops and use a fresh one every time you go to the bathroom to keep that area clean. If you begin passing large clots, the lochia becomes foul-smelling, or you go back to saturating the peripad in 1 hour, massage the top of your uterus, rest, and call your healthcare provider. Also, if you have a temperature greater than 100.4°F (38°C) and/or chills, let your healthcare provider know because this may be a sign of infection.

Breasts

Your nipples may be sore from breastfeeding, so use only warm water and keep them dry between feedings. You can use lanolin cream between feedings to soothe sore nipples and it does not need to be wiped off before breastfeeding. If your nipples become cracked or blistered, let your healthcare provider know. It is important to prevent an infection in your breasts, called mastitis, due to germs getting in through open areas of injured nipples. Use excellent hand hygiene.

Also, your breasts have enlarged quite a bit, but in particular, because you are breastfeeding. Make sure you continue to wear a supportive bra at all times to help regulate how much your breasts fill. If an area of the breast has redness, is warm to the touch, you feel a hard lump, or there is a sore area, be sure to call your healthcare provider. This may be normal engorgement as your breasts fill for a feeding, but over time this should not be uncomfortable. Make sure your breasts empty with each feeding and do not skip feedings. If the milk sits in the breasts, you may get an infection. You can use the breast pump to empty if you need to. If there is a sore area, you can apply moist heat and massage the area. If you decide not to breastfeed, and you want your milk to dry up, keep wearing a supportive bra but avoid things that stimulate milk production (warm showers and expressing milk). Instead, use cold compresses or cold cabbage leaves that have been washed and dried inside the bra.

Section 6 — Explore: Postpartum Teaching — Maternal Self-Care

Use the buttons below the slides to view maternal self-care information.

Slide 1 — Perineum

You had an episiotomy (or laceration) in the area between your vagina and your anus. Your healthcare provider stitched it up and it has remained intact. These sutures will dissolve on their own and should begin to heal in about 2–3 weeks, but it could take 4–6 months to completely heal. You must continue the routine taught in the hospital each time you go to the bathroom. Continue to use the peri bottle after each time you urinate or have a bowel movement. Spray the water from front to back, pat dry, and use a fresh peripad. You can continue to use the spray and witch hazel pads. You can also use a sitz bath 2–4 times a day but do not submerge in a bathtub until your healthcare provider clears you to do so after your first postpartum visit in 6 weeks. The body needs time to rest and heal, so new moms are advised not to use any tampons, douche, or have sexual intercourse until their healthcare provider clears them to do so after their first postpartum visit in 6 weeks.

Slide 2 — Bowel Function

You may get constipated because of everything your body is going through, and prenatal vitamins can have that effect too. Do not strain to have a bowel movement because it will put stress on your perineum and your healing episiotomy. Your healthcare provider will prescribe a stool softener for you to help prevent constipation. Eating a healthy diet, drinking a lot of water, and staying active in a safe way will help prevent constipation.

Slide 3 — Bladder Function

You may notice that you leak some urine when you sneeze, laugh, or cough. This is because your bladder has been bumped around and has lost some tone because of pregnancy and delivering vaginally. This may improve in a few months, but it may continue to some degree as well. It is important to keep your bladder empty because it will get in the way of your uterus if it is full, and that will make the vaginal bleeding worse. If you feel pain or burning when you urinate or don't feel like you can empty, let your healthcare provider know.

Slide 4 — Nutrition

Be sure to continue eating a healthy diet and drink about 2–3 liters of fluid a day. Keep taking your prenatal vitamins and following your pregnancy nutrition guidelines, particularly while you are breastfeeding. Five hundred additional calories are needed for breastfeeding mothers compared to pre-pregnancy requirements. You should continue to lose most of the pregnancy-related weight within the first 3 months postpartum, and breastfeeding will help with that as well.

Slide 5 — Rest and Exercise

It is very important to get enough sleep, which is difficult with a newborn. Try to sleep between feedings and allow family and friends to help as much as possible. Staying active is important for your overall health, helping your body recover, and preventing constipation. Kegel exercises will help strengthen your pelvic floor muscles. Your healthcare provider will let you know if there are any restrictions.

Section 8 — Explore: Maternal Postpartum Teaching

Click each tab below to review maternal postpartum teachings.

Emotions

There are a lot of hormonal changes happening right now which, along with lack of sleep and stress, cause many women to experience the baby blues. You may experience fluctuations in mood, sometimes feeling happy and other times tearful.

  • It is normal for emotions to fluctuate, but if you experience prolonged, increased, or unmanageable tearfulness, sleeping difficulties, or loss of appetite, let your healthcare provider know as these can be signs of postpartum depression. Sometimes, you don't even know it is happening but if you have any doubts, be sure to call your healthcare provider right away.
  • Many hormonal changes are happening in your body and you will feel very tired at times. This is normal and can be treated. Ask for help from others so that you can rest and then when you are with your baby you are better able to bond and enjoy the experience.
  • Postpartum blues pass after 2 weeks postpartum, whereas postpartum depression is more serious and makes it difficult to deal with daily life, lasts beyond the first 2 weeks, and requires therapeutic management to recover.

For more information regarding postpartum depression, visit the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists website for answers to frequently asked questions.

Legs

Your blood was more prone to clots while you were pregnant and that is why you were advised to keep active and tell us if you felt pain, tenderness, or swelling in your calves, or warmth and tenderness behind your knees. Keep following these instructions until around week 6 postpartum, when the risk of clots from pregnancy is over.

Medications

Be sure to take the medications prescribed by your healthcare provider as directed for cramping and pain in your perineum. The medication will not harm the baby. The pain should lessen over time as your body heals.

Incision Care

Because you've had a cesarean birth, it is very important to keep an eye on your incision and keep it from becoming infected. If you notice redness, drainage, separation of the incision, or have a temperature over 100.4°F (38°C), let your healthcare provider know right away. The staples will be removed before you go home and the sutures that remain will dissolve away over time. To help ensure that the incision doesn't separate, do not lift anything heavier than your baby and increase your activity gradually. You will check your uterus and bleeding as we talked about, but don't press down on the incision. Keep the area clean by letting shower water wash over it and then pat it dry. Your healthcare provider is sending you home with a prescription for pain medications that we will discuss.

Section 10 — Explore: Postpartum Maternal Danger Signs

As part of maternal postpartum teaching, the nurse should provide education regarding postpartum maternal warning signs to monitor and report to the healthcare provider. These include fever, breast issues (pain, swelling, redness), abdominal tenderness (persistent), pelvic fullness/pressure, perineal pain (persistent), urination issues, abnormal lochia, leg issues (edema, tenderness, warmth), and cesarean incision issues.

The Association of Women's Health, Obstetric and Neonatal Nurses (AWHONN, 2021) suggests the acronym "POST BIRTH" to help women remember the postpartum warning signs requiring them to call 911 and those to report to their healthcare provider.

"These post-birth warning signs can become life-threatening if you don't receive medical care right away because:"

  • Pain in the chest.
  • Obstructed breathing or shortness of breath (trouble catching your breath) may mean you have a blood clot in your lung or a heart problem.
  • Seizures may mean you have a condition called eclampsia.
  • Thoughts or feelings of wanting to hurt yourself or someone else may mean you have postpartum depression.
  • Bleeding (heavy), soaking more than one pad in an hour, or passing an egg-sized clot or bigger may mean you have an obstetric hemorrhage.
  • Incision that is not healing, increased redness, or any pus from episiotomy or cesarean section site may mean you have an infection.
  • Redness, swelling, warmth, or pain in the calf area of your leg may mean you have a blood clot.
  • Temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher, bad smelling vaginal blood, or discharge may mean you have an infection.
  • Headache (very painful), vision changes, or pain in the upper right area of your belly may mean you have high blood pressure or post-birth preeclampsia.
