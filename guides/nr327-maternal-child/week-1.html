Culture Religion and Pregnancy

Week 1: Culture, Religion, and Pregnancy

Section 1: Introduction to Culture and Religion in Pregnancy

There are cultural considerations that impact pregnant clients, from religious and family dynamics to guidelines around personal space and privacy. While it is not feasible for nurses to be fully immersed in every culture represented by their clients, it is a nurse's duty to have a basic understanding of client needs and communicate with them effectively and in a way that makes them feel comfortable.

This concept will review how nurses can support their clients' cultural and religious needs during pregnancy.

Section 3: Explore: Nursing Role with Culture in Pregnancy

Diversity and Inclusion in Pregnancy

Lack of understanding of cultures (between the nurse and the childbearing family) may create difficulties related to communication style, decision making, touch, spirituality and religion, and time orientation.

Pregnancy providers should be open-minded and get to know their clients' situations and preferences without making assumptions. For example, we see clients with a variety of circumstances and unique situations, such as women with more than one partner, same-sex couples, and clients who are surrogates for other couples.

Certain medical procedures associated with pregnancy, such as circumcision and blood transfusions in emergency situations, are not acceptable in all religions. Some cultures also have guidelines or traditions around co-sleeping, baptism, and baby naming. Clients might also seek traditional remedies before or along with treatments prescribed by their healthcare provider.

Nurses must conduct a thorough assessment and try to learn more about their clients. Family structure, roles, cultural beliefs related to health and illness, and an understanding of who serves as decision maker are all central to an effective assessment of the cultural factors that influence the care provided for pregnant women and newborns.

Section 4: Explore: Religion and Pregnancy

Sofia

Sofia is 34 weeks pregnant with her first baby. Her partner, Mike, serves in the military. She often feels alone and is very scared that she will have to deliver the baby without him by her side.

Sofia is visiting her local synagogue a few times a week to try and find a source of support. She shares that she feels like there is an "extra set of arms" around her now that she is practicing her faith more.

Like Sofia, some women use faith or spirituality during pregnancy as they feel it provides them with many benefits, which include:

  • guidance and support
  • protection, blessing, or reward
  • communication with a higher entity
  • strength and confidence
  • help with difficult moral choices
  • a generalized positive mood

Nurses must attend to spirituality as a resource in pregnancy for those who value it and further explore the meaning of spirituality in pregnancy. During pregnancy, some women intensify their prayers to a higher being for protection, a safe delivery, and blessing. For example, some women panic at the mention of Cesarean section delivery out of fear of death during surgery, while others who undergo Cesarean section feel stigmatized. Some of these women will commune with a higher being, either individually or in a group. The prayers offered by/for these pregnant women increase their faith and hope in a higher being and afford them the confidence to progress through delivery.

Section 6: Explore: Cultural, Religious and Ethnic Customs

Rashmi, a 27-year-old client who delivered a newborn 5 days ago, returned to the emergency department with her newborn who was limp and having seizures. The elders in her family direct the care of the newborn after delivery and following their recommendations is a sign of respect. The newborn was not eating well and was becoming jaundiced. The baby needed additional care, but Rashmi's father instructed her to stay at home so the family could pray over the child.

Nurses are expected to provide competent care, which requires an awareness of, sensitivity to, and respect for the diversity of the clients they serve. Part of this awareness includes recognizing how some groups face healthcare issues and disparities. For example, some groups have difficulty accessing care and some choose not to access modern medicine. More government programs are needed to increase healthcare for indigent women and children. Ongoing education is also needed to find a balance between a client's religion and cultural practices while still obtaining safe care.

Here are some examples of how religion, culture, and ethnic customs can influence the ways your clients interact with you.

Health Beliefs

  • In some cultures, people believe that talking about a possible poor health outcome will cause that outcome to occur.
  • Maria wants to follow instructions but she is afraid to talk about gestational diabetes as she doesn't want it to happen to her and her baby. She decides to cancel her glucose challenge test appointment.

Health Customs

  • In some cultures, family members play a large role in health care decision making.
  • Julia arrives at her prenatal appointment with her extended family each week.

Ethnic Customs

  • Differing roles of women and men in society may determine who makes decisions about accepting and following through with medical treatments.
  • Michelle defers to her father when making medical decisions. Michael is the leader of the family and must be consulted.

Religious Beliefs

  • Religious faith and spiritual beliefs may affect health care-seeking behavior and people's willingness to accept specific treatments or behavior changes.
  • Laura is a Jehovah's Witness and will not accept a blood transfusion if needed. Her plan of care needs to include this as pregnancy has a risk of significant blood loss.

Interpersonal Customs

  • Eye contact or physical touch will be expected in some cultures and inappropriate or offensive in others.
  • Sophia asks if the evil eye charm could be placed in the baby bassinet as she wants to protect her newborn from any negative curses.

Dietary Customs

  • Disease-related dietary advice will be difficult to follow if it does not conform to the foods or cooking methods used by the patient.
  • Priya needs to drink warm water and soups after delivery, so it's important to discuss dietary needs upon admission and throughout a patient's treatment.

Section 8: Explore: Culture and Religion in Pregnancy

Diversity, Culture, and Religion in Pregnancy

Childbirth occurs in every culture, and within each culture there are unique meanings and customs associated with pregnancy, childbirth, and postpartum. Nurses should have the knowledge of cultural beliefs, attitudes, and traditions of the clients and families they serve, so they can communicate effectively with the client, and plan and provide appropriate, individualized client care. Nurses need to embrace and understand the differences among various client populations. In caring for clients with different sociocultural perspectives, nurses will better understand and be able to honor cultural differences, traditions, and religious preferences during birth and in the postpartum period.

Language and cultural needs should never be assumed. The nurse should ask the client what their preferred language is and access an interpreter as needed. The nurse should also ask how they can best support each client and show respect for cultural preferences. Health practices can vary widely within each culture.

Tab: Nursing Practice

Nurses should use the information collected from assessments and create a plan of care that is client-centered. For example, for a client whose culture always involves a religious elder in health care decision making, nurses can provide education to the extended family so they can hear the rationale for each intervention. This may help the client feel comfortable with accepting modern health care while still respecting their cultural practices.

Section 10: Explore: Family Structures

John and Caleb are awaiting the arrival of a baby they are adopting through a national adoption agency.

There are multiple family structures. Differing family patterns of functioning are defined by how members of the family relate to each other. Nurses should support each client and their support system. The goal is to provide client-centered care and ensure the family adapts to their new parenting role.

Adults in a healthy family communicate and support each other through the changes and stress that come along with raising a child. Daily chores and responsibilities may need to be adjusted based on the day and how members are feeling, but having a tentative plan and being flexible is key. Members of a healthy family accept support from family and friends.

Family Structures:

Single Parent — An adult who is divorced, separated or widowed living with a child or children is a single-parent family.

Same Gender — Same-gender families include two adults of the same sex living together, with or without children.

Nuclear — Nuclear families include a husband and wife who are married and raising their biological children.

Adoptive — Foster or adoptive families are those who take responsibility for children who were not born to them. This may include foster or adoptive children from another country or of another cultural, ethnic, or racial group.

Extended — Extended family includes the nuclear family with grandparents, aunts, uncles, or cousins living together.

Blended — A blended family includes a married husband and wife who are raising children from previous relationships.

Cohabitative — Couples living without the legal bonds of marriage with or without children are in a cohabitative relationship.

Communal — A communal family is a group of unrelated people who choose to live together, with the children the responsibility of the group.

Ethics Legal and Pregnancy

Ethics, Legal, and Pregnancy

Course: NR327 Maternal-Child Nursing Module: Week 1 — Module 5

Section 1: Introduction to Ethical and Legal Issues During Pregnancy

Some pregnancies are accompanied by additional ethical and legal issues for the mom, baby, or even both. Women may have other health issues, be involved in an abusive relationship, or be victims of human trafficking. Nurses play a key role in the assessment and treatment of these clients. To find solutions, nurses and other healthcare personnel should apply ethical theories and principles to determine the burdens and benefits of any course of action.

This concept will review some of the ethical and legal issues that occur in the maternal-child population and the role of the nurse.

Core Values:

  • Ethics
  • Integrity
  • Respect
  • Honesty

Section 3: Explore: Ethical Models

Vignette — Kate: Kate is a 28-year-old client, unmarried, and 6 weeks pregnant. She was the victim of a rape that occurred when she was leaving work late one evening. She is a marketing executive with a college degree who lives alone not far from her job. Kate comes from a close-knit group of family and friends, with whom she spends time each week.

Kate has an appointment at her obstetrician's office later today and plans on requesting an abortion as she cannot imagine carrying the baby for nine months, even if it means placing the baby for adoption. She does not want to keep the baby, knowing that the baby was conceived through sexual assault.

For many, this is an ethical situation that may cause some nurses to question their beliefs and the rights of the client.

Tab: Deontological Model

The deontologic model determines what is right by applying ethical principles and moral rules. The solution does not vary according to individual situations.

For some religions, killing is considered a sin, so abortion would fall under those rules. The fact that the child was conceived by rape does not matter in this model.

Tab: Utilitarian Model

The utilitarian model approaches ethical dilemmas by analyzing the benefits and burdens of any course of action to find a solution that will result in the greatest amount of good. Appropriate actions may vary with the situation when using the utilitarian model.

In this model, the fact that Kate was raped is taken into consideration. Some questions may include:

  • How will Kate handle this pregnancy emotionally and physically?
  • Could Kate care for a baby that was a result of rape?
  • Who is the father? Will there be any genetic concerns?
Tab: Human Rights Model

The human rights model is the belief that each person has human rights and this is the basis for making ethical decisions.

In this model, the fetus has a right to live even if the mother does not feel that she could or should continue with the pregnancy.

Tab: American Nurses Association (ANA) Code of Ethics (2015)

The Code of Ethics for Nurses With Interpretive Statements (American Nurses Association, 2015) was developed as a guide for carrying out nursing responsibilities in a manner consistent with quality of nursing care and the ethical obligations of the profession.

  • With the support of their work organizations, colleagues, and policies, nurses strive to achieve the best patient outcomes.
  • Some provisions include compassion, respect, scholarly inquiry, collaboration, and social justice, which nurses strive to uphold.

Remember! When a nurse speaks with a rape victim, the most helpful thing the nurse can do is listen and let the survivor talk. A victim who feels understood may no longer feel alone and more in control of the situation. It is critical to avoid placing blame on the victim. Once the nurse has established a rapport with the victim and established trust by listening non-judgmentally, the nurse will be better poised to explain procedures and follow-up care.

Reference: American Nurses Association. (2015). Code of ethics for nurses with interpretive statements. https://www.nursingworld.org/coe-view-only

Section 5: Explore: Malpractice

Vignette — Lauren: Lauren, 37 weeks pregnant, presents to the labor and delivery unit with uterine contractions every 2 minutes. This is the second baby for Lauren and her partner, Pat. Lauren had a quick labor with her first child three years ago. Lauren does not have any risk factors and is 4 cm dilated on admission. The nurse contacts the healthcare provider to make them aware of Lauren's arrival and her history of short labor.

After less than an hour of labor, Lauren cries out, stating that the uterine contractions won't stop and that there is blood between her legs. The nurse feels a rigid belly and urgently contacts the provider again. The healthcare provider shares that he cannot get there quickly as he is about 90 minutes away and asks the nurse to keep the client comfortable. He did not set up a colleague to be his back-up, as he did not anticipate any women going into labor. Lauren needs to be delivered as quickly as possible as both she and her baby are in danger of serious harm.

Negligence is the failure to perform as a reasonable or prudent person of similar background would act in a similar situation. Negligence may consist of doing something that should not be done or failing to do something that should be done. Malpractice is negligence by professionals, such as nurses and healthcare providers, in the performance of their duties. (Murray et al., 2019, p. 39)

Many reasons exist for perinatal malpractice claims. Complications are usually unexpected because parents view pregnancy and birth as normal. However, emergencies can happen in a matter of seconds. In this case, the healthcare provider should have been within 30 minutes of the hospital or have a colleague who can cover in their absence.

Healthcare agencies and individual nurses should work together to prevent malpractice claims. Nurses are responsible and accountable for their own actions. Therefore, they should be aware of the limits of their knowledge and scope of practice, and they should practice within those limits. Documentation of nursing care can defend and support a nurse and demonstrate that prudent care was given to the client. Nurses should also complete ongoing education and skills practice to maintain their level of expertise. Staff education records may be reviewed as part of a lawsuit.

Reference: Murray, S., McKinney, E., Holub, K, & Jones, R. (2019). Maternal-newborn and women's health nursing. Elsevier

Elements of Negligence:

Tab: Duty

The nurse has a duty to act or give care to the client. It must be part of the nurse's responsibility. Nurses who provide care to pregnant mothers have a duty to keep their clients safe. It is their role.

Tab: Breach of Duty

A violation of that duty must occur. The nurse fails to conform to established standards in performing that duty. The healthcare provider asks the nurse to keep the client comfortable and states that he will be there in 90 minutes. The nurse cannot wait for the doctor and must take action (initiate chain of command) or it will be a breach of duty.

Tab: Damage

Actual injury or harm to the client as a result of the nurse's breach of duty must occur. If the baby is not delivered urgently, the baby and mother could be harmed by the blood loss and potential lack of oxygen.

Tab: Proximate Cause

The nurse's breach of duty must be proved to be the cause of harm to the client. If this case results in a lawsuit, the nurse's actions will be reviewed and the legal system will decide if there is proximate cause.

Section 6: Ethical Principles

View the scenario below and learn how each ethical principle is applied to the context.

Vignette — Kelly: Kelly is a 34-year-old pregnant client who is seen at the office for a 20-week ultrasound after an abnormal alpha-fetoprotein test. Kelly is married to Jay and they have four boys who range from 2 to 12 years old. They were trying again to see if they could have a girl, and a few weeks earlier they found out that they were indeed pregnant with one. They have already picked out a name and the other kids talk to "Baby Gabby" each day.

The doctor confirms that Gabby has significant anomalies. The baby could be compatible with life but would probably not walk or talk. There is also an issue with the kidneys, which could require dialysis or a transplant at some point. Delivery would require a cesarean section. They also discover that Kelly has a placenta accreta that causes a high risk for hemorrhage after delivery and potential risks to the uterus in general.

Ethical principles are important for solving ethical dilemmas. These principles guide decision making but, in some situations, the application of one principle conflicts with another. In such cases, one principle may outweigh another in importance.

Autonomy — Clients have the right to self-determination. This includes the right to respect, privacy, and information necessary to make decisions based on personal values and beliefs.

Example: Kelly knows that she will need to share her wishes with her healthcare providers.

Beneficence — Make a decision that produces the greatest good or the least harm.

Example: Kelly has to think about the long-term quality of life for Gabby. Does living a life with pain and suffering outweigh the decision to end the pregnancy?

Nonmaleficence — Avoid risking or causing harm to others.

Example: The health of Kelly has to be taken into consideration as well. If the pregnancy is maintained, Kelly's life could be in danger. With four boys at home, can she risk her own life?

Justice — All people should be treated equally and fairly regardless of disease or social or economic status.

Example: The healthcare team must provide the same care for Kelly as they would for any other client. Kelly's married status or financial situation should not impact decision making.

Fidelity — Keep promises, and do not make promises that cannot be kept.

Example: When explaining another high risk to Kelly and Jay, the healthcare provider shows the statistics and tells the couple that they won't be able to promise any type of outcome. It's difficult to predict exactly how the delivery will go or the health of Gabby and Kelly until that time. They can anticipate and prepare, but there are many unknowns.

Veracity — Tell the truth.

Example: Although the information was devastating to Kelly and Jay, they appreciated hearing all of the facts and that their healthcare providers told them the reality of what could potentially happen with the pregnancy and delivery.

Confidentiality — Keep information private.

Example: The next day, a team of healthcare providers are meeting about the case and sharing it with the ethics committee. One provider is in a crowded elevator when a call comes in regarding the case. He asks to call them back and not discuss the client's case in front of strangers.

Accountability — Accept responsibility for actions as a healthcare professional.

Example: The nurse and the healthcare provider consistently reflect on their actions and if they should do anything different in the future, as they are responsible for each interaction and the care of their clients.

Section 8: Explore: Human Trafficking

Vignette — Lisa: Lisa, a 17-year-old pregnant woman, presents to the labor and delivery unit. She avoids eye contact and does not answer any questions. She keeps looking over at the male who accompanied her, and he controls the entire conversation. Because she did not receive prenatal care, all labs were drawn on admission. Lisa is positive for chlamydia and has a hemoglobin of 8.2 g/dL.

More than one in four women become pregnant while trafficked, indicating that maternity services offer an important contact point for identification and care. Given the prevalence of sexual exploitation and abuse among trafficking survivors, clinicians should ensure antenatal care and screening for sexually transmitted infections can be readily accessed by women.

Clinicians require specialized training alongside designated pathways and protocols with clear referral options to ensure confidential maternity care is tailored to each woman's needs. Always look for signs of human trafficking when providing care for women.

The National Human Trafficking Hotline is 1-888-373-7888.

Signs of human trafficking that nurses should look for:

  • false identification
  • someone else speaking for the client
  • client avoids eye contact
  • late prenatal care
  • bruises in various stages
  • partner who won't leave the client's bedside
  • sexually transmitted infections

Section 10: Explore: Intimate Partner Violence

Vignette — Melanie: Melanie, 28 weeks pregnant, presents to the obstetrical unit complaining of vague pain, increased urinary frequency, and pain with urination. The nurse asks Melanie to collect a clean-catch urine sample so they can check for infection. Melanie's partner insists on being in the bathroom and steps inside the door with her.

The nurse waits for Melanie to come out and observes both partners' body language as Melanie returns to the triage bed. Melanie is on edge and trembling. While placing the fetal monitors, the nurse notices various stages of bruising on Melanie's lower abdomen and groin area. The partner starts answering all of the questions and will not leave the bedside. Melanie states that it is probably better if they go home as she is already feeling better.

Intimate partner violence remains a serious and frequent aspect of women's intimate relationships, and women and children suffer health consequences as a result. Intimate partner violence rates increase during pregnancy. It happens with all socioeconomic situations. Nurses have a significant role to play in working toward the prevention and early intervention of intimate partner violence.

Tab: Physical Signs

When assessing women, nurses should be aware that some of the following physical signs of injuries might be related to intimate partner violence:

  • bruising in the chest, abdomen, or private areas
  • multiple injuries
  • minor lacerations
  • ruptured eardrums
  • delay in seeking medical attention
  • patterns of repeated injury
Tab: Other Symptoms

However, it is unlikely women will present with a physical injury. They will more likely present with issues such as:

  • stress-related illness
  • anxiety, panic attacks, stress, and/or depression
  • substance use including tranquilizers and alcohol
  • chronic headaches, asthma, vague aches and pains
  • abdominal pain, chronic diarrhea
  • sexual dysfunction, vaginal discharge
  • joint pain, muscle pain
  • sleeping and eating disorders
  • suicide attempts, psychiatric illness
  • gynecological problems, miscarriages, and chronic pelvic pain
Tab: Psychological Signs

The woman may also:

  • appear nervous, ashamed, or evasive
  • describe her partner as controlling or prone to anger
  • seem uncomfortable or anxious in the presence of her partner
  • be accompanied by her partner, who does most of the talking
  • give an unconvincing explanation of the injuries
  • be recently separated or divorced
  • be reluctant to follow advice

Section 12: Explore: Nurse Assessment and Support

Nurses are encouraged to ask questions that may help identify intimate partner violence or human trafficking, along with providing support. The response of nurses to women in these circumstances can have a profound effect on their willingness to open up or seek help.

Tab: Questions Asked Indirectly
  • How are things at home?
  • How are you and your partner relating?
  • Is there anything else happening that may be affecting your health?
Tab: Questions Based on Clinical Observations
  • You seem very anxious and nervous. Is everything okay at home?
  • When I see injuries like this, I wonder if someone may have hurt you?
  • Is there anything else that we haven't talked about that might be contributing to this condition?
Tab: Questions Asked Directly (When a Partner is Absent)
  • Are there ever times when you are frightened of your partner?
  • Are you concerned about your safety or the safety of your children?
  • Does the way your partner treats you ever make you feel unhappy or depressed?
  • I think there may be a link between your illness and the way your partner treats you. What do you think?

Healthcare providers have an obligation to report suspected or confirmed abuse to the proper authorities. Each state has different laws and nurses should become familiar with laws in the states where they practice. Reports typically include client history, full physical assessment, and any lab work completed. Failure to report could result in criminal convictions and be considered neglect.

Health Promotion and Pregnancy

Week 1: Health Promotion and Pregnancy

Section 1: Introduction to Health Promotion

The nurse plays a key role in the prevention of pregnancy and childbirth-related complications through the delivery of quality health care preconception (before pregnancy), prenatally (during pregnancy), and interconception (between pregnancies). For many women, family planning and sexual health services are the only medical care they receive. This presents many preconception-related health promotion opportunities, risk identification opportunities, and risk reduction opportunities in order to improve pregnancy outcomes.

This concept will review the importance of health promotion during pregnancy.

Section 3: Explore: Healthy People 2030

Once pregnancy is determined, antepartum (prenatal) care should begin. Women are often very motivated to improve health practices during pregnancy for the benefit of their unborn child, providing many general health benefits and pregnancy-related health promotion opportunities. The cognitive and physical development of infants and children may be influenced by the health, nutrition, and behaviors of their mothers during pregnancy and early childhood. Increasing the number of pregnant women receiving early and adequate prenatal care is a major focus of many organizations, such as Healthy People 2030.

Healthy People 2030 focuses on preventing pregnancy complications and maternal deaths and helping women stay healthy before, during, and after pregnancy, as women's health can have a major impact on the infant's health and well-being.

  • Women who get recommended health care services before they become pregnant are more likely to be healthy during pregnancy and to have healthy babies.
  • Strategies to help women adopt healthy habits and get health care before and during pregnancy can help prevent pregnancy complications.
  • Interventions to prevent unintended pregnancies can help reduce negative outcomes for women and infants.
  • Strategies to help pregnant women get medical care and avoid risky behaviors—like smoking or drinking alcohol—can also improve health outcomes for infants.

The Healthy People 2030 website offers many examples of the initiatives in place, such as preventing drug and alcohol abuse and family planning.

Section 4: Explore: Preconception Care and Health Promotion

Preconception care and health promotion are important to women's health. The following issues are of significance:

Tab: Prevention of Unintended and Adolescent Pregnancies

Often, women do not or cannot seek prenatal healthcare services when the pregnancy is unintended. Preventable birth defects, low birth weight, and postpartum depression occur.

Tab: Early Detection and Prevention

Early detection and prevention of gestational diabetes, pre-eclampsia, sexually transmitted infections (STIs), and intimate partner violence is important before they negatively impact the pregnancy and/or cause unwanted infertility.

Tab: Pre-Pregnancy Nutritional Status

Pre-pregnancy nutritional status also plays an important role in pregnancy outcomes. Early nutrition support includes the reversal and prevention of obesity. Obesity places women at higher risk of pregnancy and childbirth complications, including high blood pressure and diabetes.

Tab: Early Education

The use of some prescription and over-the-counter medications may cause birth defects. Approximately 3% of women take these medications in early pregnancy.

Tab: Early, Daily Folic Acid Supplementation

Folic acid is believed to greatly decrease the risk for newborn neural tube defects, such as spina bifida, which form in the early weeks of fetal development. Many women are not even aware they are pregnant at this point. For this reason, a folic acid supplement is recommended for all women of childbearing age. Increasing the proportion of women of childbearing age who have optimal red blood cell folate concentrations is a Healthy People 2030 goal. The most recent data provided indicates that only 82.6% of non-pregnant women aged 15-44 years had optimal red blood cell folate concentrations.

Section 5: Explore: Nutrition During Pregnancy

Nutrition is important since maternal nutritional status is a significant health factor that can prevent a variety of health problems for both the mother and baby. When a pregnant woman has inadequate nutrition, the baby can be born with a low birth weight or preterm. There is continuing evidence to indicate that maternal nutrition and lifestyle have long-term effects on children's health. Nutritional assessment starts in the preconception stage and continues throughout pregnancy.

A widely used government program called Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC) provides federal grants to states for supplemental foods, healthcare referrals, and nutrition education for low-income pregnant women and breastfeeding postpartum women and to infants and children up to age 5 who are found to be at nutritional risk.

Slide 1: Key Points

The goal of preconception nutrition is for all women to achieve a desirable weight before conception because fetal risks increase when the pregnant woman is significantly underweight or overweight when pregnancy begins.

Slide 2: Key Points

Adequate nutrition is important during pregnancy. However, there are many social determinants that may affect a woman's ability to consume adequate nutrition. As a nurse, it is important to assess the pregnant woman's access to nutritional foods and, if needed, find ways to obtain an adequate supply of food.

Slide 3: Key Points

The body needs vitamins and other nutrients to stay healthy and strong. During pregnancy, the growing baby depends on these nutrients for growth and development. Recommended prenatal vitamins, along with eating healthy foods, can help clients get the nutrients needed to support a healthy pregnancy.

Slide 4: Key Points

Vegan diets do not contain sufficient quantities of vitamins B12 and B6, calcium, and zinc; therefore, pregnant women following this dietary pattern must consume fortified foods and take prenatal vitamins.

Slide 5: Reference

USDA Food and Nutrition Services — Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC)

Section 8: Explore: Weight Gain Recommendations During Pregnancy

During pregnancy, appropriate weight gain is important. Less than or greater than expected weight gain can be due to preexisting conditions and can increase the likelihood of complications. However, more important than weight gain is the nature of the nutrients contained in the food consumed.

Recommended weight gain during pregnancy is based on the client's pre-pregnancy body mass index (BMI).

Women are not "eating for two." The amount of calories that should be consumed during pregnancy is based on the trimester:

  • First trimester: same as pre-pregnancy
  • Second trimester: an additional 340 kcal/day
  • Third trimester: an additional 452 kcal/day

Practice Questions — Recommended weight gain during pregnancy:

Q: 18-year-old pregnant woman, pre-pregnancy BMI 17 (underweight) A: 12.5-18 kg / 28-40 lb

Q: 41-year-old pregnant woman, pre-pregnancy BMI 32 (obese) A: 5-9 kg / 11-20 lb

Q: 35-year-old pregnant woman, pre-pregnancy BMI 29 (overweight) A: 6.8-9.1 kg / 15-25 lb

Section 10: Explore: Physical Activity

Click through each slide below to explore more.

Slide 1: Physical Activity

Changes in activity are not necessary during a healthy pregnancy; however, high risk activities should be avoided. Exercise in pregnancy is typically recommended for many benefits including childbirth preparation.

Slide 2: Varicose Veins

Varicose veins and dependent edema are common. They are believed to be caused by increased intra-abdominal pressure. Support hose and elevating the legs are common remedies.

Slide 3: Growing Uterus

The growing uterus puts pressure on and stretches the ligaments in the lower abdomen, commonly causing round ligament pain and tenderness in the area. Certain exercises and supportive garments can help reduce the pain and tenderness.

Slide 4: Kegel Exercises

Kegel exercises strengthen the pubococcygeal muscles that stop the flow of urine. These muscles support the pelvic floor, bladder, and urethra. These muscles can become weakened during pregnancy and childbirth due to increased pressure and trauma. Kegel exercises (tightening and relaxing these muscles) should be done daily and for the rest of the woman's life.

Slide 5: Rest and Sleep

Fatigue begins early in pregnancy as the body adjusts to pregnancy-related changes and energy needs. Napping and lying on the left side to promote oxygenation through the placenta are recommended. As pregnancy progresses, it becomes increasingly difficult for women to find a comfortable position, which makes resting and sleeping more challenging. Later in pregnancy, the uterus may compress the vena cava, then the supine position needs to be avoided as it can cause hypotension. The gravid uterus pressing on the vena cava can result in syncope, vertigo, and decreased perfusion to the fetus. A side-lying position is recommended.

Slide 6: Pillows

Use of pillows under the legs and abdomen, elevation of the feet, and a side-lying position will improve rest and sleep during pregnancy.

Section 11: Explore: Dental Care

Clients should tell their dentists if they think they could be pregnant. Keeping up regularly scheduled checkups is important. Increased progesterone and estrogen in a woman's body during pregnancy can increase the risk for certain oral health problems. Gums are more likely to become inflamed or infected because of hormonal changes and increased blood flow during pregnancy.

Some women may fear having dental work during pregnancy. However, there is no proven increase in preterm births or other adverse outcomes for pregnant women who received dental care.

Food choices could change during pregnancy and these choices could affect dental health—a desire to eat more sweets or salty foods, for example.

Some women brush and floss their teeth less than before they got pregnant due to fatigue, nausea, or gum tenderness.

Section 13: Explore: Drug and Alcohol Use During Pregnancy

During pregnancy, women should not use tobacco, alcohol, marijuana, illegal drugs, or prescription medications for nonmedical reasons. Avoiding these substances and getting regular prenatal care are important to having a healthy pregnancy and a healthy baby. Counseling and treatment are available if a client is having a hard time quitting unhealthy substances.

Early education and interventions regarding smoking, alcohol, and substance abuse are critical to preventing fetal, infant, and childhood disorders and poor outcomes. The following highlights some of the harm that could result from women using drugs and alcohol during pregnancy.

Developmental and Behavioral Disorders

  • legal and illegal substances

Drug Withdrawal Disorders

  • prescription drugs

  • no minimum amount of alcohol is deemed safe during pregnancy

Introduction to Maternal Child Nursing

Section 1: Introduction to Maternal Child Care

Maternity care has changed over the years to increase the emphasis on safety and quality of care.

Many expectant mothers have other health concerns such as obesity, high blood pressure, and even substance abuse. Their socioeconomic status can have an impact on their pregnancy, along with their cultural preferences. Abuse and human trafficking may be other risks that these women face during this period of their lives.

This concept will explore some of the trends in maternal child nursing and how nurses can positively influence healthcare outcomes. Critical thinking and evidence-based practice must be used, and nurses should also make sure that the client is involved in their own care.

Section 3: Explore: Changes in Maternity Care

Changes in Maternity Care Over Time

Prior to the twentieth century (before 1901):

  • Deliveries happened at home; "granny midwives" were in attendance
  • High rates of maternal and infant death, both at home and in hospitals
  • Postpartum hemorrhage, postpartum infection, toxemia, prematurity, dehydration r/t diarrhea, contagious diseases

20th century developments:

  • Increase in physician assisted births in hospitals
  • By 1960, 90% of all births in the U.S. were in hospitals
  • Lay midwifery became increasingly illegal
  • Women's role in delivery became passive; doctors delivered
  • Twilight sleep; narcotic and scopolamine
  • Continued problems with poor maternal nutrition, infection, inadequate prenatal care & cost issues

Section 5: Explore: Mortality and Funding

Despite technological advances and the move from home to hospital, maternal and infant mortality rates in the United States have declined slowly. In addition, statistics of U.S. infant mortality rates still show a wide disparity in outcomes among races.

Due to these high mortality rates, the federal government became involved. Programs to support the health of mothers, infants, and young children were developed and supported. However, rural areas are still at risk due to a lack of obstetrics (OB) care and a need for nurses.

Tab: Care Impact

How funding impacted care:

  • Insurance companies paid hospitals a fixed amount (of insurance coverage) for a specific condition.
    • The facilities absorbed the extra costs if needed, or kept the extra money if they could provide care cheaper. This resulted in:
    • the decreased lengths of hospital stay
    • shorter stay = less money spent on care = greater profit
  • Capitated care, which is an employer or government who is providing insurance, paid a set amount to a specific physician group to care for individuals.
    • Example: public aid
  • Additional resources were developed to reduce mortality and control costs, including:
    • Case management
    • Outcomes management
    • Clinical pathways
Tab: Current Practices

All of these impacts on care in history have resulted in current practices, such as:

  • Length of stay
    • vaginal discharged at 48 hours
    • cesarean section discharged at 96 hours
  • Home visits
    • If leaving at or before 24 hours, home visits may be provided.
  • Discharge teaching issues
    • Lack of time with clients and the quality of information can affect the value of discharge teaching.
  • Decreased access to care
    • Some women still have difficulty seeing specialists or picking providers if they are out of network.

Section 6: Explore: Evidence-Based Practice

Nurses strive to provide care that is based on the latest evidence.

  • Women in the United States are more likely to die from childbirth than women living in other developed countries.
  • The maternal mortality rate in the U.S. for 2018 was 17.4 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births.
  • Wide racial/ethnic gaps exist between non-Hispanic black (37.1 per 100,000 live births), non-Hispanic white (14.7), and Hispanic (11.8) women, which is consistent with earlier data.
  • The maternal mortality rate for women aged 40 and over (81.9 per 100,000 live births) is nearly 8 times that for women under age 25 (10.6).

This research and evidence has led to the following changes over the years:

  • More family involvement in birth
  • Keeping infants with mothers after delivery
  • Fostering family unity while maintaining safety
  • Nurses play a major role in pregnancy care
  • Mandating a minimum length of stay post-delivery and providing follow up care

Some organizations create standards for developing practice guidelines based on evidence to promote quality and the best patient outcomes:

  • Association of Women's Health, Obstetric and Neonatal Nurses (AWHONN)
  • March of Dimes
  • Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ)
  • National Academy of Medicine

Section 8: Explore: Maternal Child Health Care Providers

In addition to physicians, there are other members of the healthcare team who provide excellent care for mothers and children. Nurses can pursue additional education to obtain their Advanced Practice Nursing (APN) licenses. Each provider must practice within their scope of practice or there may be increased risks to moms and babies.

Slide 1: Staff Nurse

RN license and may hold additional certifications

  • Care for clients in obstetrics (OB) offices
  • Care for clients in labor and delivery and mother-baby units, including medication administration, assessments, circulate cesarean (C)-sections
  • Teach prenatal classes
  • Complete home visits/outpatient services
Slide 2: Doula

Certified professional

  • Provide continuous emotional, physical, and informational support to mothers before, during, and after childbirth
  • Trained in labor support
  • Cannot administer medications, monitor baby, monitor labor, deliver a baby, or have any input in medical decisions
  • May work in a birthing center, hospital, and/or with home births
Slide 3: Lactation Consultant

RN license and certification

  • Specialized support for breastfeeding
  • Inpatient and outpatient services
Slide 4: Certified Nurse Midwife

Registered nurses (RNs) who have completed a program of study and clinical experience

  • Provide complete care during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postpartum period
  • Counsel and support the childbearing family
  • Provide annual well-woman exams
  • Approach to childbirth is noninterventionist and supportive
  • May work in a birthing center, hospital, or client home
Slide 5: Nurse Practitioner

RN with advanced preparation

  • Women's health nurse practitioners (WHNPs) provide wellness-focused, primary, reproductive, and gynecologic care throughout the lifespan, starting at adolescence.
  • Neonatal nurse practitioners (NNPs) assist in the care of high-risk newborns in the immediate post-birth care or in a neonatal intensive care unit (NICU).
Slide 6: Midwife

Direct–Entry Midwife, Certified Midwife (CM), or Certified Professional Midwife (CPM)

  • Recognized as legal practitioners in some U.S. states
  • May deliver babies in hospitals and homes depending on training and state regulations
  • Not as common as Certified Nurse Midwife (CNM)
  • In the U.S., national certification is available for the Certified Professional Midwife, but national midwifery licensing does not exist as it does in many other countries

Section 9: Explore: Choices of Childbirth Facilities and Support

Clients will work with their providers to decide the best delivery location based on their desired experience, such as a hospital, birthing center, and even at home.

Some clients may choose a tub birth or may require a cesarean section based on the health needs of the mom and baby.

  • Level III
    • highest level of care
    • able to care for high-risk moms and infants
  • Level II
    • midlevel
    • Typically, 32 weeks and above can receive care
  • Level I
    • low risk clients
Tab: Environments
  • Labor-Delivery-Recovery Room (LDR)
    • The client labors, delivers and recovers in the same room but is transferred to a postpartum (PP) unit.
  • Labor-Deliver-Recovery-Postpartum Room (LDRP)
    • The client labors, delivers, and recovers in the same room.
    • The client stays the duration of their 2-4 day stay.
  • Birth center
    • It may offer tub births and not require as much medical monitoring.
  • Home births
    • Physicians and midwives who specialize in home births prepare the home birth setting and follow certain protocols of their own.
Tab: Phases of Labor Terminology
  • antepartum: before labor
  • intrapartum: during labor
  • postpartum: after delivery
Tab: Birth Support
  • Mothers benefit from having support during the delivery and at home. The support person may vary based on the relationship with the mom. The person can be:
    • the father of the baby
    • a relative or friend
    • a doula (trained labor support)
    • a sibling
    • The presence of children at birth is controversial.
    • They may attend all or part of labor and birth with provider approval.
  • Client education
    • Many classes provide instructions on infant care and Lamaze.
    • Client teaching can help with emotional and physical pain support.

Section 11: Explore: Perinatal Trends: Core Measures

The United States Department of Health and Human Services has objectives for improving the health of the people of the United States.

There are five core measures specific to perinatal care:

  • Decrease the rate of elective deliveries.
  • Decrease the rate of Cesarean births.
  • Increase the rate of antenatal administration of steroids in preterm labor.
  • Decrease the rate of newborns with septicemia or bacteremia.
  • Increase the rate of exclusive breastfeeding.

Nurses play an active role in supporting these core measures:

  • Reducing the rate of elective deliveries
    • Encouraging prenatal classes and educating parents can reduce elective deliveries.
    • Women will see the importance of carrying their baby to term.
  • Cesarean (C)-section
    • Nurses can provide labor support (position changes/pain relief), teach about drug use, and protect patients who may be abused, which can cause risk for injury and the need for surgery.
  • Preterm labor
    • Nurses can collaborate with high-risk providers to provide care that helps sustain the pregnancy.
    • Clients should be notified about the risk of delivering newborns.
    • Complications could include respiratory and infection.
  • Decrease rate of infection
    • Nurses can administer maternal and newborn antibiotics as needed and use personal protective equipment (PPE) appropriately.
  • Increase the rate of breastfeeding
    • Nurses can teach breastfeeding classes and general breast care, along with connecting clients with lactation consultants.

Social Determinants of Health

Week 1: Social Determinants of Health and Maternal-Child Nursing

Section 1: Introduction: Social Determinants of Health

Pre-pregnancy health behaviors and health status are influenced by a variety of environmental and social factors, such as access to medical care and chronic stress. Some of these factors can affect and compound others, creating a vicious cycle. For instance, factors ranging from age to medical insurance coverage affect a woman's general health status. A woman's health status directly influences her risk of pregnancy complications and her child's cognitive and physical development. For some, the cycle will continue with future pregnancies.

Understanding the many factors that affect women and infants—both negatively and positively—is key to improving the health of all Americans, particularly the next generation.

This concept will explore some of the determinants of health and how a nurse can positively influence maternal outcomes.

Section 3: Explore: What Are Social Determinants?

Social determinants of health are the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age. These circumstances are shaped by the distribution of money, power, and resources at global, national, and local levels. They are mostly responsible for health inequities—the unfair and avoidable differences in health status within and between countries.

Some examples of social determinants include:

  • availability of resources to meet daily needs (safe housing, local food markets)
  • access to educational, economic, and job opportunities
  • access to health care services (insurance, providers)
  • public transportation options (bus, train, car)
  • social support (friends, family)
  • substance abuse (alcohol, drugs)
  • access to technology (lacking phone, internet access)
  • social norms and attitudes (discrimination, racism, distrust of government)
  • exposure to crime, violence, and social disorder (presence of trash, lack of cooperation in a community)
  • socioeconomic conditions (concentrated poverty and the stressful conditions that accompany it)
  • culture (e.g., food, religion, and healthcare practices)

Social Determinants of Health (Healthy People, 2030):

  • Health Care Access and Quality
  • Neighborhood and Built Environment
  • Social and Community Context
  • Economic Stability
  • Education Access and Quality

Section 5: Explore: Teenage Sex and Pregnancy

Molly is a 16-year-old who attends a local public high school. She spends a lot of time alone, as her mom works two jobs trying to make ends meet. Molly's mother got pregnant when she was 16 and her father left home when she was still an infant, so she has never met him. Molly has always looked for attention from boys and started having sex six months ago due to pressure from her boyfriend. Molly's period is now a week late. The only sex education her mother provided was abstinence, other birth control options were not discussed.

Teenage sexual activity and pregnancy are influenced by many factors.

Slide 1: Peer Pressure

Peer pressure may occur from boys or girls within the peer group.

Slide 2: Lack of Education

No or minimal sex education.

Slide 3: History

Kids having kids is generational. Daughters of teen mothers are three times more likely to be teen parents.

Slide 4: Income

Girls and women in lower socioeconomic situations may not have adequate education and don't always have money for birth control.

Slide 5: Lack of Role Models

Teenagers need encouragement from positive role models and to hear reasons for avoiding pregnancy.

Slide 6: Health Care Professionals

Nurses can play a key role in providing education in schools, clinics, and hospital settings. Teenagers need a non-judgmental provider that can encourage them to practice safe sex, answer questions, and avoid risky behaviors.

Slide 7: Culture

In some cultures, high value is placed on early pregnancy and becoming pregnant validates one's feminine role. The family helps raise the child. Teenagers without this support need to consider the risks of raising a baby alone.

Section 7: Explore: Race and Access to Care Impact on Health Outcomes

Race impacts a woman's preconception, prenatal, and interconception health care.

  • African American, Native American/American Indian, and Alaska Native women are more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes than Caucasian women (CDC, 2024).
  • Causes of pregnancy-related deaths in the United States include infection or sepsis, hemorrhage, cardiovascular conditions, cardiomyopathy, and thrombotic pulmonary embolism (CDC, 2024).

Think About It:

Lack of access to healthcare is also linked to negative maternal, infant, and child health outcomes.

For example, women who do not have access to healthcare services prior to pregnancy are more likely to engage in risky behaviors, such as smoking and drinking alcohol, which affect the woman's pregnancy and infant outcomes.

Maria is a nurse who works for the health department tracking data regarding disparities for pregnant women and pregnancy mortality. She is preparing training for local hospitals and clinics.

    • World Health Organization Maternal and Reproductive Health
