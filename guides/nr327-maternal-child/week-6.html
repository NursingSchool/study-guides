Module 1: Initial Care of the Newborn

Course: Maternal-Child Nursing-Kennedy | Unit: 6

Section 1: Introduction to Initial Care of the Newborn

Drastic physiological changes occur at the moment of birth as the newborn transitions from intrauterine to extrauterine life.

This module will discuss the nurse's priority areas of focus with the immediate assessment, stabilization, and care of the newborn in the first critical minutes of life.

Section 2: Prepare: Initial Care of the Newborn

Q1. Which nursing actions promote initial bonding immediately following birth? Select all that apply.

  • [ ] Complete initial assessment in radiant warmer in client's room

Q2. The nurse is caring for a newborn one minute after delivery. Which is the best indication that the newborn is adjusting well to extrauterine life?

  • [ ] Minimal secretions
  • [ ] Temperature 98.6ºF (37ºC)
  • [ ] Acrocyanosis
  • [x] Strong, vigorous cry

Feedback: A vigorous cry is a good indication of adequate cardiopulmonary function in newborns adjusting to extrauterine life. While minimal secretions and normal temperature are good signs, the best indication of adjustment is the strong cry. Acrocyanosis is normal at one minute of life.

Section 3: Explore: Initial Care of the Newborn

There will be at least one nurse present in the delivery room who is assigned to the newborn from the moment of birth. That nurse's focus is on the assessment and stabilization of the newborn's airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCs). Immediate physiological changes occur in the newborn's body when the head emerges and the nurse is trained to prevent, detect, and manage the impact these changes may have on the newborn as it begins its adjustment to extrauterine life.

Tab: First Breaths

Remember, the fetus gets its supply of oxygen in utero from the placenta and doesn't need its lungs to pull in air from the outside. Instead, the lungs are filled with protective fetal lung fluid while they develop and mature. As the fetus nears term, the amount of fluid produced decreases and, then during labor, some of the remaining fluid is compressed out while passing through the birth canal.

When the newborn's head emerges, its body starts signaling it to breathe. The newborn takes its first breath and the chain reaction begins. The newborn's body must quickly switch from fetal to independent neonatal cardiopulmonary function. The placenta detaches, the umbilical cord is cut, and the newborn must now supply oxygen-filled blood to its body on its own.

To do that, the newborn needs to take air into its lungs and alveoli, however, they are still filled with some remaining fluid. The fluid is a barrier to pulling in air. The first breaths taken need to be sufficient to force the rest of this fluid out of the alveoli and into the interstitial spaces around the alveoli, creating the negative pressure needed to allow air to enter and for respirations to continue. In addition, the action of the first breaths and the removal of the maternal blood supply triggers the new cardiopulmonary pattern to begin functioning properly. The fluid contained in the airway (mouth, nose) is compressed out manually during labor.

Section 4: Explore: Initial Care of the Newborn — Airway, Breathing, Circulation

Click the arrows at the side of each slide to view steps the healthcare team takes in a synchronous process to aid the newborn.

Slide 1: Airway

The provider uses a bulb syringe to remove some of the fluid first from the newborn's mouth and then the nose before delivering the rest of the body. Removing the fluid from the posterior pharynx diminishes the amount of fluid that is inhaled into the lungs from the nasal passages when the newborn starts taking breaths and it unblocks the passages so that air can be taken in. Under normal circumstances, the newborn is then placed on the mother's chest where the nurse jumps into action, simultaneously assessing and performing actions that help stabilize the newborn's airway. The nurse may gently pat or vigorously (but still gently) rub the newborn's back to elicit more crying and loosen pockets of fluid left in the lungs. Additional suctioning may be needed to remove the excessive fluid.

Slide 2: Breathing

The nurse assesses the newborn's respiratory efforts and will continue to elicit crying if needed. The breathing efforts and crying help force remaining fluid out of the lungs and airway to make breathing easier. The nurse will visually inspect for retractions or flaring of the nostrils, signs that the newborn is struggling to take in air. The nurse will auscultate breath sounds for signs of excessive fluid or obstruction and assess the respiratory rate. A respiratory rate in these moments will average 40-49 breaths per minute, the normal range is 30-60 breaths per minute.

Slide 3: Circulation

The nurse auscultates the newborn's apical heart rate, which should range from 120-160 beats per minute. Simultaneously, the nurse observes outward signs of circulatory perfusion evident in the newborn's coloring. Supplemental oxygen may be used to help the newborn "pink up." Cyanosis appears as a centralized purple/blue color indicating decreased oxygen supply. Acrocyanosis, however, only appears on the fingers and toes and is common in newborns for up to 7-10 days, until circulation extends to distal areas. Pallor is unusual paleness indicating hypoxia or anemia. Ruddiness is a reddish color indicating polycythemia.

Section 6: Explore: APGAR Scoring

In these first critical moments following delivery, the nurse is also gathering cues for the newborn's APGAR score, a tool used to rapidly evaluate the newborn's cardiopulmonary adaptation to extrauterine life and need for immediate intervention.

At 1 and 5 minutes after birth, each of the following areas related to cardiovascular, neurologic, and respiratory status are scored between 0-2 points.

APGAR Scoring Table (Heart Rate, Respiratory Effort, Muscle Tone, Reflex Response, Color):

Category0 Points1 Point2 Points
Heart RateAbsentSlow <100 bpm>100 bpm
Respiratory EffortAbsentSlow, irregularGood crying
Muscle ToneFlaccid, limpSome flexion of extremitiesActive motion
Reflex IrritabilityNo responseGrimaceVigorous cry, cough, or sneeze
Color*Pale blueBody pink; extremities blueCompletely pink

*Skin color or its absence may not be a reliable guide in nonwhite infants. Melanin (the pigment that gives color to skin) is less apparent at birth than later.

Tab: Scoring 8 or Higher

An APGAR score of 8 or higher is a good indication that at 1 and 5 minutes of age, the newborn only needs standard, ongoing thermoregulatory and respiratory assessment and support.

Tab: Scoring 7 or Less

An APGAR score of 7 or less indicates to the healthcare team that additional interventions and cardiopulmonary support are needed by gently rubbing the newborn's back and providing supplemental oxygen.

The APGAR will be repeated every 5 minutes until the score is greater than 7. Causes for these issues will be considered and treated accordingly, such as whether the mother received a narcotic analgesic prior to delivery that may be causing respiratory distress.

If the infant is obviously in distress (no or low heart rate and respiration, limp muscle tone, lack of response to stimulation), interventions to correct the problem are instituted immediately rather than waiting for the 1-minute APGAR score.

Tab: Newborn's Transition

Factors that impact the newborn's transition to life and are reflected in the APGAR Score include:

  • Fetal congenital and/or structural abnormalities that impact the newborn's ability to adjust to extrauterine life (such as heart defects) or clear respiratory fluids from their airway, or complications related to lung immaturity, such as prematurity.
  • Maternal antepartum complications that cause harm to the fetus.
  • Maternal intrapartum complications that result in decreased blood flow to the fetus and fetal distress, such as traumatic delivery (shoulder dystocia), cord prolapse, cord compression, nuchal cord, or abruptio placentae.
  • Additional complications may impact the newborn's ability to clear fluids from the airway after birth. For example, during labor, some of the remaining fluid in the lungs and airway is compressed out while passing through the birth canal during vaginal delivery. A newborn that is delivered via cesarean section, particularly without the mother having labored at all, will tend to have more of this fluid still present in the lungs than the newborn of a woman who has labored for a while.

Section 8: Explore: Thermoregulation

The newborn's ability to self-regulate body temperature at birth is immature. Thermoregulation is a key area of initial and ongoing assessment to ensure that heat loss is minimized.

  • At birth, newborns are wet and their body temperatures begin falling immediately. They are vulnerable to hypothermia and even slight heat loss will result in hypoglycemia. Their bodies burn stored energy to raise their temperatures, which will then increase oxygen consumption and metabolic dysfunction.
  • The newborn is typically placed on the mother's bare chest after a vaginal birth for the benefit of heat from skin-to-skin contact. Warmed blankets are used to cover the newborn after wiping off fluid, blood, and vernix. Blankets are often replaced with more dry, warm blankets as they cool down to minimize heat loss. Under normal circumstances, much of the initial care of the newborn can be done while remaining on the mother's chest.
  • Once the newborn is removed from the mother's chest, the nurse places it in a pre-heated radiant warmer at the bedside to finish the delivery assessments and interventions requiring the newborn to be uncovered. It is often recommended that the newborn's first bath be delayed for up to 12 hours to promote skin-to-skin contact, bonding, early breastfeeding, and thermoregulation, but it will also take place under the radiant warmer at the bedside.
  • If the newborn needs to remain in the warmer, it is kept at the bedside in full view of the mother. Sometimes the newborn needs to be taken to a nursery for advanced care.
  • The temperature of the neonate should be verified with an axillary thermometer and measured frequently. Axillary temperature is the standard route in newborns. The normal axillary temperature is 97.7°F to 99.5°F (36.5°C to 37.5°C). When not skin-to-skin, newborns are kept wrapped in blankets with their heads covered.

Section 10: Explore: Newborn Measurements

While under the warmer, the nurse will obtain a birth weight and measure the newborn's head circumference, chest circumference, and length using a disposable paper tape measure. This data will be used to further evaluate the newborn's overall health and compared to the normal ranges for the newborn's gestational age.

  • The newborn's weight will be remeasured daily until discharge.
  • The head circumference measurement will be affected by factors that cause the head to be misshapen, such as molding of the skull that occurs during vaginal birth, hydrocephalous, or microcephaly.
Measurements and Normal Ranges

Length: Measured in supine position from the top of the head to the bottom of the heel with the leg fully extended along the ruler.

  • Normal range: 19–21 inches (48–53 cm)

Head Circumference: Measured around the fullest part of the head (the occiput) and just above the eyebrows.

  • Normal range: 13–15 inches (32–38 cm)

Chest Circumference: Measured at the level of the nipples, keeping the tape measure even and taut.

  • Normal range: 12–14 inches (30–36 cm)

Birth Weight (term infant): 5 pounds 8 ounces to 8 pounds 13 ounces (2500–4000 grams)

  • Weight remeasured daily until discharge.

Section 13: Explore: Nutrition and Bonding

The nurse needs to ensure that the newborn is adjusting to extrauterine life and promote bonding between the newborn and the parents. Nurses should be sensitive to parents' wishes while also ensuring newborn safety. Most of the initial care of the newborn can be completed while the parents bond with the newborn. Encouraging early breastfeeding is important as newborns are often most awake and alert in the first hour following birth, thereby providing more successful breastfeeding opportunities.

Sometimes the mother is tired or experiencing pain and may not be able to hold the newborn. The nurse can encourage the partner to provide skin-to-skin care and participate in the initial care of the newborn while the mother rests. As part of the taking in phase of adjusting to parenthood, it is not unusual for the mother to be preoccupied with the recent labor and birth experience. This does not indicate a lack of bonding, but rather is part of the normal transition to parenthood.

Module 4: Newborn Nutrition

Course: Maternal-Child Nursing-Kennedy | Unit: 6

Section 1: Introduction to Newborn Nutrition

Newborn nutrition is a critical aspect of caring for newborns and infants. Appropriate feeding practices stimulate bonding with the caregiver and psychosocial development. This leads to improved nutrition and physical growth and reduced susceptibility to common childhood illnesses. Improved health outcomes in young children have health effects throughout the lifespan.

This concept will review newborn nutrition, with the exception of breastfeeding, and how nurses can support clients with feeding their newborns.

Section 2: Prepare: Newborn Nutrition

Q1. The nurse is teaching a client about proper techniques for bottle feeding. Which action by the client indicates an understanding of the instructions?

  • [ ] Holds the infant in a supine position
  • [x] Keeps the nipple full of formula while feeding
  • [ ] Avoids burping the infant until the end of the feeding
  • [ ] Refrigerates the remaining formula in the bottle

Feedback: When bottle feeding an infant, the nipple should remain full of formula throughout the feeding to avoid the infant swallowing air while sucking. The client should burp the infant after 1/2 to 1 ounce of formula has been consumed throughout the feeding. The infant should be held with the head elevated 45 degrees to avoid aspiration. Remaining formula left in the bottle after feeding must be discarded.

Q2. When preparing formula from powder, the client should mix with _____ water and warm the formula _____. Prepared but unused formula may be stored in the refrigerator for _____ hours.

  • [x] tap | by running under warm tap water | 48
  • [ ] sterile | in the microwave | 72

Feedback: Formula comes in several preparations, including powder, ready-to-feed liquid, and liquid concentrate. When preparing formula from powder, the client should mix the powder with tap water and warm the formula by running under warm tap water. Heating formula in the microwave results in uneven heating and "hot spots" that may scald the baby's mouth and throat. However, formula can be served chilled, warmed, or at room temperature. Prepared but unused formula may be stored in the refrigerator for up to 48 hours. Any remaining formula left in the bottle after feeding must be discarded.

Q3. Which statement best represents recommendations from the American Academy of Pediatrics about infant nutrition?

  • [ ] Begin complementary foods no sooner than 1 year of age
  • [ ] Avoid baby formula unless medical contraindications to breastfeeding
  • [x] Exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life
  • [ ] Do not promote breastfeeding in all hospital settings

Feedback: The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life, followed by continued breastfeeding as complementary foods are introduced. The mother should continue to breastfeed for 1 year or longer as desired by the mother and infant (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2012). The client's decision to breastfeed or formula feed should be supported in all healthcare settings.

Section 3: Explore: Nutritional Needs of the Newborn

The full-term newborn needs sufficient calories per kilogram of body weight daily to meet energy needs, prevent use of body stores, and provide growth.

  • 85–100 calories per kilogram if breastfeeding
  • 100–110 calories per kilogram if formula feeding

Both breast milk and formula provide 20 calories per ounce.

Newborns need larger amounts of fluid in relationship to size than an adult.

  • 60–100 mL per kilogram on days 1 to 5 of life
  • 150–175 mL/kg after day 5 of life

Remember, it is not unusual for newborns to lose up to 10% of birth weight in the first week of life. This is a result of normal excretion of extracellular water and meconium, as well as consuming fewer calories than needed.

  • The newborn has a small stomach capacity.
  • They may fall asleep before feeding adequately.
  • They may sleep through feeding times.

Nurses should closely monitor the urine output and weigh the newborn daily. If weight loss exceeds 7% of birth weight in the first 3 days of life or if birth weight is not regained by 2 weeks of age, intervention may be needed.

Calculating newborn weight loss:

  • Subtract the current weight from the birth weight. This number represents the amount of weight lost.
  • Take the weight lost and divide it by the birth weight. Then multiply by 100 to get the percentage of weight loss.

Practice question: The newborn's birth weight is 3350 grams. Today's weight is 2950 grams. What is the newborn's percentage of weight loss? Answer: 12% (400 ÷ 3350 × 100 = 11.94%)

Section 4: Explore: Formula Feeding

Madison, a new mom, was undecided about breastfeeding. She was formula-fed as a baby and was unsure if she could breastfeed and work full time. During her breastfeeding class, the nurse shared information about formula feeding. The nurse felt it was important for moms and their support persons to also hear about alternative feeding options.

A woman may choose to formula feed and should be empowered to make the choice that is best for them. Even women who are educated about the advantages of breastfeeding may still decide to solely formula feed. Cultural beliefs, as well as myths and misconceptions about breastfeeding, influence women's decision making.

Many women see formula feeding as more convenient or less embarrassing than breastfeeding. Some view formula feeding as a way to ensure that the partner, other family members, and daycare providers can feed the baby.

Some women lack confidence in their ability to produce breast milk of adequate quantity or quality. Women who have had previous unsuccessful breastfeeding experiences may choose to formula feed subsequent infants. Some women see breastfeeding as incompatible with an active social life or think that it will prevent them from going back to work. Modesty issues and societal barriers exist against breastfeeding in public. A major barrier for many women is the influence of family and friends.

A nurse's personal feelings about breastfeeding and formula feeding should not influence the information provided to the client.

In this case, Madison needed to do what was best for her and her baby.

Tab: Formula Composition

Manufacturers must adapt formula to match the components of breast milk as closely as possible. An exact match is not possible and formula will lack the immunologic components of breast milk.

An iron-fortified, commercially prepared formula is recommended for all clients who choose not to breastfeed. Unmodified cow's milk is not recommended for infants under 1 year of age.

Tab: Formulas For Infants With Special Needs

Many formulas exist for infants with special needs, including:

  • soy formula for infants with lactase deficiency or vegetarian families
  • protein hydrolysate formula for infants with allergies and malabsorption disorders
  • concentrated formula with more calories in less liquid for preterm infants
Tab: Choosing to Formula-Feed

It is important for the mother to understand newborn implications when choosing to formula-feed:

  • more likely to develop allergies
  • nutritional properties do not change according to the infant's needs
  • not as easily digested and nutrients are not as well absorbed
  • can be improperly diluted
  • can be contaminated by water supply
  • more easily overfed and constipation is more common

Section 7: Explore: Formula Bottle Feeding

The hospital typically provides a commercial newborn formula. There are a variety of types available. The choice of which formula to use is a joint effort between the healthcare provider and the client. There are milk-based, soy-based, and other specialty formulas for special needs. Unmodified cow's milk should not be given to newborns as it provides inadequate calories. There are also several sizes of disposable nipples available.

Commercially prepared formula comes in several preparations, including powder, ready-to-feed liquid, and liquid concentrate.

The nurse teaches proper bottle-feeding techniques, including:

  • Infant held in a semi-upright position when feeding and never lying down, which can cause aspiration.
  • Avoid overfeeding.
  • Burp the newborn every ½ to 1 oz of formula.
  • Feed every 3–4 hours.
  • Do not prop the bottle, which increases the chance of aspiration.
  • Begin with 15–30 mL during the first 24 hours, then increase by 15 mL.
  • Do not force the infant to finish the bottle.

The nurse documents the amount of formula taken in by the newborn and the newborn's tolerance of the feeding. Watch for any reactions or signs of allergies.

Madison decided to bottle feed her baby, and needed to prepare her first formula bottle.

Tab: Preparing Formula Bottles (Ready-to-Feed)
  • She washed her hands.
  • She washed the lid of the can, checked the expiration date, and shook the container before opening it.
  • She poured the proper amount of formula into a clean bottle and placed a clean nipple on top.
  • She checked the nipple flow to ensure that it was not too slow or too fast.
  • She understands she must use the bottle within 48 hours (if untouched by the baby).
    • Once the infant has used the bottle, any remaining formula must be discarded.
Tab: Preparing Formula Bottles (Powder or Concentrate)
  • She washed her hands and gathered clean supplies.
  • She washed the lid of the can and checked the expiration date.
    • Powdered formula is not recommended for use with infants who are premature or immunocompromised as the powder is not sterile.
  • She read the formula label and diluted the formula exactly as recommended.
  • She used cold tap water to prepare the formula.
  • She understands never to heat the formula in a microwave, but instead to run warm water over the bottle.
  • She tested the bottle temperature and nipple flow before feeding.
  • She understands she must use the bottle within 48 hours (if untouched by the baby).
    • Once the infant has used the bottle, any remaining formula must be discarded.
Tab: Combination Feeding

Some clients wish to use a combination of breast and bottle feeding. It is best to delay giving the newborn a bottle or formula until lactation has been well established around 4 weeks after birth. Giving formula to a breastfeeding infant often decreases feeding frequency and leads to decreased milk production.

If the client wishes to combination feed, the nurse should be supportive of this decision so that both the mother and infant receive the benefits of breastfeeding at least part of the time.

Madison now has bottles to clean, so she bought a bottle and nipple brush to ensure she washed them well. Hot, soapy water is needed or bottles and nipples can be placed in the dishwasher or sterilizer. Madison still needs to check for wet and stool diapers to ensure that her baby, Taylor, is getting enough formula. The stool will be darker and more firm compared to the breastfed infant's stool.

Section 10: Explore: Role of the Nurse

Though mothers are capable of feeding their newborns in the hospital, the nurse plays a key role in assessing and supporting the mother during their stay.

Tab: Nursing Care Tips
  • Assess the mother's knowledge of newborn feeding to provide a starting point for teaching.
  • Observe a feeding session to provide a baseline assessment for positive reinforcement and problem identification.
  • Describe and demonstrate ways to stimulate the sucking reflex, proper positions for feeding, and the use of pillows during a session to promote maternal and neonatal comfort.
  • Teach the mother ways to stimulate the neonate to maintain an awake state by diapering, unwrapping, massaging, or burping to complete a feeding session thoroughly and satisfactorily.
  • The nurse can remind the client to support the baby's head during feeding and to burp frequently.
  • Make sure the mother has written information on all aspects of feeding to reinforce oral instructions and demonstrations.
  • Refer to support groups, lactation consultants, or both, if needed, to provide further information and group support.
Tab: Diagnosis, Outcomes & Interventions

Nursing Diagnosis: Ineffective infant feeding pattern related to inability to coordinate sucking and swallowing.

Expected Outcomes: The neonate will coordinate sucking and swallowing to accomplish an effective feeding pattern.

Nursing Interventions/Rationales:

  • Assess for factors that may contribute to ineffective sucking and swallowing to provide a basis for a plan of care.
  • Teach the mother to observe feeding readiness cues to enhance effective feeding.
  • Modify feeding methods as needed to maintain hydration status and nutritional requirements.
  • Promote a calm, relaxed atmosphere to provide a pleasant feeding experience for the mother and neonate.

Module 3: Nursing Care: Newborn

Course: Maternal-Child Nursing-Kennedy | Unit: 6

Section 1: Introduction to the Nursing Care of a Newborn

Knowledge of essential newborn care and proper practice is vital for a newborn's survival, growth, and development. The nursing care of the newborn is directed toward promoting the physical well-being of the baby and supporting the family unit. The nurse will provide care and teach clients during the postpartum period, and then provide educational reinforcement before discharge. This information is key to health maintenance and promotion for the mother and infant, particularly between well-care visits.

This concept will discuss the nurse's role in newborn care.

Section 2: Prepare: the Nursing Care of a Newborn

Q1. Which facts about newborn safety and activity should the nurse know and understand? Select all that apply.

  • [x] A high-pitched cry should be evaluated by the healthcare provider.
  • [x] The identification bracelets should be matched to the mother's every time the newborn is brought back to the mother.
  • [ ] A newborn who sleeps more than 12 hours a day is likely not receiving adequate nutrition.
  • [ ] Newborns who are positioned on their backs with several blankets loosely covering them are sleeping safely.

Feedback: Most newborns sleep 16-20 hours a day. This depends on many factors, including breast versus bottle feeding. Crying is the newborn's means of communicating hunger, pain, need for attention, or not feeling well. A high-pitched cry may signal a neurologic problem and should be evaluated by the healthcare provider. The newborn is positioned in a supine position and never prone to prevent sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS). Safe sleep practices include ensuring that there are no extra linens or stuffed toys in the newborn's crib, which can unintentionally end up over the face. At birth, several security devices are put in place. Identification bracelets that match between the mother, the newborn, and another adult are checked every time the newborn is brought back to the mother. Some units have tracking bracelets for newborns. When a member of the healthcare team comes to take the newborn to the nursery for a circumcision, for example, the mother is taught to ensure they are who they say they are.

Q2. A woman delivers a newborn vaginally. What can the postpartum nurse do to encourage mother-newborn bonding? Select all that apply.

  • [x] Encourage the mother to hold the newborn from birth.
  • [x] Postpone nursing actions that require the newborn to be removed from the mother's arms.
  • [x] Encourage breastfeeding within the first hours of birth.
  • [ ] Encourage the mother to let the newborn be taken to the nursery every 2 hours to allow her to rest.
  • [x] Assess for signs that indicate normal attachment is taking place.

Feedback: Postpartum bonding is encouraged from the moment of birth through early physical contact between the newborn and the mother. The postpartum nurse will conduct most, if not all, necessary assessments and interventions while the newborn remains on the mother's chest as long as the newborn remains stable. This includes auscultating heart and lung sounds, warming and drying the newborn, and assessing APGAR scores. Encouraging breastfeeding in the first few hours further strengthens this initial bond. The oily prophylactic antibiotic eye drops used routinely at delivery are withheld for about an hour after delivery to allow the infant to more clearly see the mother and father. The nurse can also assess for signs that indicate that normal attachment is taking place. It may be recommended that the mother allows the nurse to care for the newborn at times to allow the mother to sleep. She should be encouraged to sleep when the newborn sleeps.

Q3. Why is vitamin K administered to newborns? Select all that apply.

  • [ ] As a vaccination for a variety of bacteria and viruses
  • [x] The newborn intestinal tract is sterile and does not contain bacteria that produces vitamin K
  • [ ] To aid the newborn in digesting infant formula
  • [x] To prevent vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB)

Feedback: Newborns cannot adequately absorb and synthesize vitamin K on their own because the intestinal bacteria that produce it are not yet present in the sterile newborn's intestine. Vitamin K is needed for preventing a complication called vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB), a potentially fatal illness easily prevented.

Section 3: Explore: Routine Newborn Prophylactic Medications

After delivery, the nurse administers several routine prophylactic medications to the newborn to prevent potential complications. Parental consent is usually obtained prior to administration.

Tab: Erythromycin Ophthalmic Ointment or Drops
  • Prophylactic antibiotic ophthalmic ointment is placed in each of the newborn's eyes to prevent infection from exposure to bodily fluids as well as active bacterial infections (such as chlamydia and gonorrhea).
  • It is clear but cloudy and will impact the newborn's vision until it is absorbed. Therefore, administration may be delayed during the initial period of bonding and close physical contact with the mother after delivery, or some mothers may elect not to allow the newborn to receive it.
  • It comes in a prefilled tube and the ointment is applied to the conjunctival sac of each eye after the area has been cleansed. The infant's blinking will distribute the medication in the eye. The excess medication is left in the eye and not wiped away.
Tab: Vitamin K (AquaMEPHYTON)
  • Newborns cannot adequately absorb and synthesize vitamin K on their own because the intestinal bacteria that produce it are not yet present in the sterile newborn's intestine. Vitamin K is needed to prevent excessive bleeding. Without adequate vitamin K, newborns are at risk for preventable hemorrhagic complications such as vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB), a potentially fatal illness.
  • 0.5 to 1 mg is administered intramuscularly at the vastus lateralis site using a 1 mL syringe and a 5/8 inch 25-gauge needle (in an adult, this would be a subcutaneous needle). The leg is secured and held in place with one hand and the needle is inserted into the thigh muscle at a 90-degree angle.
  • It is administered soon after delivery or delayed until after the first breastfeeding.

Section 5: Explore: Culture and Infant Bonding

A woman's culture, values, and/or beliefs impact parenting style and bonding, and may also impact their interactions with the healthcare team.

As with all nursing care, the nurse should validate which cultural practices are important to individual parents as these vary greatly and are unique to each parent. This is done through observation of both verbal and nonverbal behaviors. Also, the nurse should be knowledgeable about childbearing practices among various cultural and ethnic groups to be sensitive to this diversity. Age and education level can also play a role in maternal-newborn bonding. Additional support may be needed. Each client's needs should be addressed and assumptions should never be made.

Section 7: Explore: Newborn Nursing Care - Skin Care

For a healthy, stable newborn, a nursing assessment including vital signs is completed at least every 8 hours. Axillary temperature, apical heart rate, and respiratory rate are monitored. Blood pressure is not typically part of the normal newborn assessment.

The newborn's skin is very sensitive. During routine newborn care, the nurse takes several measures to prevent skin breakdown, while preventing hypothermia as well.

Additional areas of nursing care of the newborn include:

Slide 1: Diapering

With diaper changes, the diaper area is kept as clean as possible to prevent skin infection and rash. The nurse wears clean gloves to wash the area with mild soap and warm water from front to back to avoid urinary tract infections. Powder is no longer used due to its respiratory impacts. The nurse wears clean gloves and practices hand hygiene.

Slide 2: Bathing

The initial newborn bath removes the amniotic fluid, vernix, and blood after delivery. Sponge baths are performed under the radiant warmer to prevent hypothermia and warm water with mild soap is used. The bath should be demonstrated for the parents. Often a hat is placed on the newborn to support temperature maintenance.

Slide 3: Cord Care

It is important to keep the umbilical cord stump area clean and dry. If necessary, the cord can be cleaned with water. The diaper is folded down below the area to avoid moisture retention and rubbing. The nurse removes the clamp after 24 hours. Once the stump falls off in 10-14 days, sponge baths can advance to the use of soaking bath water. Redness, purulent drainage, and odor are signs that the cord stump is becoming infected.

Section 9: Explore: Newborn Testing

Newborn screening is completed to assess for serious but rare and mostly treatable health conditions at birth. It may include blood, hearing, and heart screening.

A child can be born with a health condition but may not show any signs of the problem at first. If a health condition is found early with newborn screening, it can often be treated. Early treatment is important, because it may help prevent more serious health problems.

Tab: Blood Test

The nurse performs a heel stick, collects the blood on special paper, and sends it to a lab for testing.

One example of a condition that is tested for within the newborn screening is phenylketonuria (PKU). PKU is a metabolic disorder that some babies are born with. It is caused by a defect in the enzyme that breaks down the amino acid phenylalanine. Testing is completed before discharge. Blood is collected after the newborn is 24 hours of age and has consumed formula or breastmilk. This lets healthcare providers start treatment, usually a special diet, right away to help prevent problems such as developmental delays.

Tab: Hearing Screening

This test checks for hearing loss. The tester places tiny earphones in the newborn's ears and uses special computers to check how the newborn responds to sound. Genetic hearing loss can be present at birth or develop later in life. The genes that cause hearing loss can come from one or both parents. Early assessment and treatment for hearing loss are important for a newborn's health and development.

Tab: Heart Screening

This test is used to screen babies for a group of heart conditions called critical congenital heart defects (also called critical CHDs or CCHDs).

Section 11: Explore: Newborn Nursing Care - Circumcision

A circumcision is the elective surgical removal of the male foreskin (prepuce) that covers the glans penis.

This procedure is performed by an obstetrician or family practice physician during the postpartum period, typically in the newborn nursery. Anesthetic pain medication is typically provided before the procedure to numb the area. A dorsal penile nerve block may be administered by the provider or an eutectic mixture of local anesthetics (EMLA) cream may be prescribed and applied by the nurse. The provider will use a Gomco (Yellen) clamp or a Plastibell ring to isolate the foreskin from around the glans penis so that it can be removed. The Plastibell ring is left in place until it falls off in 10-14 days.

A registered nurse assists the healthcare provider during the procedure by preparing the supplies and instruments, preparing the newborn for the procedure, and comforting the newborn during the procedure with a pacifier and a soft soothing voice.

Tab: Nursing Care

Following the procedure, nursing care focuses on preventing bleeding and infection, pain assessment, and ensuring the mother knows how to care for the circumcision as it heals.

  • If a Gomco clamp was used, the nurse applies petroleum jelly after the procedure to keep the area moist so that it does not stick to the diaper (which will cause it to bleed). More petroleum jelly is applied to the area with each diaper change. If a Plastibell ring was used, petroleum jelly is not used because it will interfere with the function of the Plastibell.
  • The newborn is taken back to the mother, identification bands are verified, and the mother is encouraged to provide comfort to the newborn.
  • For the first few hours, the site will be checked frequently for bleeding. The nurse will take note of the first urination after the circumcision to ensure that edema in the area does not obstruct urine flow.
  • The nurse educates the mother regarding care of the circumcision as it heals. Some blood noted on the diaper as it heals is normal, but the healthcare provider should be notified if there is excessive bleeding or if purulent drainage occurs. Pressure should be used to stop the bleeding. Petroleum jelly is applied to the circumcision area with each diaper change for 4-7 days (unless a Plastibell was used). The area is cleaned by squeezing warm water from a clean washcloth over the penis and gently patting it dry. A yellow scab will form over the site and should be left alone. It will fall off in 7-10 days and the Plastibell will fall off in 10-14 days. The provider should be alerted if excessive bleeding or signs of infection are present.

Section 13: Explore: Newborn Nursing Care - Activity, Sleep, Security and Safety

There are several things to consider regarding sleep, activity, security, and safety requirements for newborns.

Tab: Activity and Safe Sleep

Most newborns sleep 16-20 hours a day. This depends on many factors, including breastfeeding versus bottle feeding.

Crying is the newborn's means of communicating hunger, pain, need for attention, or not feeling well. A high-pitched cry may signal neurologic problems and should be evaluated by the healthcare provider.

The newborn is positioned in a supine—not prone—position to sleep to prevent sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS). Safe sleep practices also include ensuring that there are no extra linens, blankets, or stuffed toys in the newborn's crib which can unintentionally end up covering the face and cause suffocation. The nurse reinforces safe sleep practices with the mother and family to help prevent SIDS, including the use of clothing and blanket wraps to keep the newborn warm, with a cap on, and placing the newborn in a warm room.

Tab: Security

At birth, several security devices are put in place. Identification bracelets that match between the mother, the newborn, and another adult are checked every time the newborn is brought back to the mother. Some units have tracking bracelets for newborns. When a member of the healthcare team comes to take the newborn out of the room, the mother is taught to ensure the provider is who they say they are. The bracelets should be securely attached to the ankles and/or wrists and should be checked each shift.

Tab: Safety

The nurse includes car seat safety in the discharge teaching and provides information and resources regarding state car seat laws. It is important to use the correct car seat in the correct configuration for the newborn's age and needs. Infants remain in the back seat in a rear-facing car seat and should be reclined at a 45-degree angle. Smaller newborns and those with additional special needs will need additional accommodations to ensure the car seat fits properly and does not cause them harm.

Some facilities have the parents bring the car seat to the hospital to test that it fits the newborn properly before actually using the car seat. This is particularly necessary for newborns who are less than 37 weeks gestation or low birth weight. While the newborn is sitting in the car seat, the nurse will measure the newborn's vital signs and oxygen saturation. The nurse, however, is typically not permitted by facility policy to install a car seat. This responsibility is typically with the parents. Community agencies or organizations may provide car seat check-up and inspection stations or events.

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Care: Newborn Assessment

This module will review the key areas of nursing focus in the initial and postpartum assessment of the normal newborn as it adapts to extrauterine life, from the delivery room through the postpartum period. Nursing interventions and screenings are discussed. With proper nursing observation, complications can be detected and treated early for optimal newborn outcomes.

Section 2: Prepare: Nursing Care and Newborn Assessment

Q1. Which statement reflects the newborn's visual abilities at birth?

  • [ ] Abnormal accumulation of cerebral spinal fluid
  • [ ] Use of forceps during vaginal delivery

Q3. The nurse is assessing a newborn's vital signs. Which findings require follow-up? Select all that apply.

  • [x] Respiratory rate 20 breaths per minute

Section 3: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Head

The head-to-toe physical assessment of the newborn takes place after delivery and throughout the postpartum stay.

Following a vaginal delivery and depending on the degree to which the newborn's skull had to accommodate the birth canal, the newborn's head is typically misshapen to some degree. The newborn's skull is not fused. During labor and delivery, as the head fits through the vaginal canal, the unfused cranial bones will override (overlap). This often results in:

  • overriding sutures – ridges that can be palpated
  • molding - the head is somewhat elongated and misshapen
  • caput - a soft pad of edema on the skull often seen with molding
  • head circumference measurement – may be affected and need to be remeasured later

Head circumference is measured around the fullest part of the head (the occiput) and just above the eyebrows. The normal range is between 13-15 inches (32-38 cm). The head is disproportionately large for the body. The measurement will also be affected by conditions that impact the brain and skull, such as hydrocephalous or microcephaly.

Tab: Fontanelles

Fontanelles are soft spaces between the unfused skull bones. A sunken fontanelle may indicate poor hydration. A bulging fontanelle may indicate increased intracranial pressure (ICP).

Tab: Milia

Milia are small white spots found on the nose and chin of the newborn, similar in appearance to acne.

Tab: Eyes

The eyelids of the newborn are edematous and no tears are present. From birth, newborns can best see objects held 8-10 inches away.

Tab: Ears

The ears should be even and set appropriately.

  • Low-set ears may indicate chromosomal abnormalities.
  • The presence of skin tags or dimples may indicate chromosomal abnormalities, hearing problems, or kidney defects.

Section 4: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Chest and Abdomen

Review chest and abdomen newborn assessment below.

Tab: Chest
  • At birth, the newborn must quickly begin breathing and the heart must quickly reverse blood flow within its chambers. Up until this point, the lungs were not responsible for filtering oxygen from the air through inhaling and exhaling. The mother's blood supplied the needed oxygen.
  • The lungs are filled with protective fluids. When the nurse is auscultating the newborn's lungs after birth, it is not uncommon to hear adventitious lung sounds. The first breaths begin to clear out this fluid. The mouth and nares are suctioned with a bulb syringe. The newborn's lungs will sound wet but will quickly begin improving the more it cries, forcing the fluids out. After the initial assessment, it is best to auscultate the lungs when the newborn is asleep.
  • Respirations should not be labored and there should be no retractions, barrel-shaped chest, or use of accessory muscles. The normal range for the respiratory rate is 30-60 breaths per minute with brief periods of apnea being normal.
  • The normal range for the apical heart rate is 120-160 beats per minute. Murmurs are common in the newborn due to the heart's ongoing transition to close off previously open pathways within the structure of the heart. Though common, all newborn murmurs must be reported to the healthcare provider for further evaluation. Always note the newborn's activity level when assessing the heart rate, as crying can increase the heart rate and sleeping can reduce the heart rate.
  • Chest circumference is measured at the level of the nipples, keeping the tape measure even and taut. The normal range is between 12-14 inches (30-36 cm).
Tab: Abdomen
  • The abdomen should be soft, round, and not distended. It should protrude slightly. Bowel sounds should be able to be heard 15 minutes after birth.
  • The nurse will visually inspect the stump of the umbilical cord at delivery, after it has been clamped, to ensure it contains Wharton's jelly (gelatinous protective tissue), one vein, and two arteries. A two-vessel cord is associated with congenital anomalies. The cord will dry and begin turning black after 2-3 days. The area surrounding the umbilical cord should be free of redness, drainage, odor, or other signs of infection. This area must be kept dry and should not be covered by a diaper. The nurse removes the clamp before discharge after the cord has dried sufficiently.

Section 6: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Skin

Review some components of a newborn assessment of the skin.

Slide 1: Skin

The newborn has been surrounded by sterile, warm, amniotic fluid. Vernix, a cheesy substance, covers the skin as a protectant but dissolves away as the fetus nears term. The body is also covered in fine hair (lanugo) until the fetus reaches term. Once exposed to the external environment, non-contagious newborn rashes are common.

Slide 2: Temperature

The temperature of the neonate should be verified with an axillary thermometer and measured frequently. Newborns are very vulnerable to hypothermia. Axillary temperature is the standard route in newborns. The normal axillary temperature is 97.7° to 99.5°F (36.5° to 37.5°C). When not skin-to-skin, newborns are kept wrapped in blankets with their heads covered.

Slide 3: Dry Skin

Newborns may have dry skin, particularly those whose gestational age is over 40 weeks. It may crack and peel in some areas.

Slide 4: Jaundice

Many newborns experience jaundice (yellowing skin) resulting from hyperbilirubinemia. Jaundice can be either physiologic (normal) or non-physiologic (pathologic). Timing of jaundice is the most important differentiator with non-physiologic occurring in the first 24 hours. The nurse will assess for jaundice at least every 8-12 hours. It begins at the head and moves down the body as the bilirubin levels increase. Jaundice is visible when the bilirubin level reaches 5 mg/dL. The nurse can obtain a transcutaneous bilirubin measurement using a noninvasive device, called a bilirubinometer. A serum bilirubin level may also be ordered by the healthcare provider.

Slide 5: Mongolian Spots

Mongolian spots occur most commonly in newborns of African American, Asian, and Hispanic ethnic backgrounds. They look similar to a bruise and appear in various locations, such as buttocks, sacrum, and shoulders.

Slide 6: Birthmarks

Strawberry birthmarks and stork bites (reddened areas that become more prominent with crying) are not uncommon.

Section 7: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Arms, Legs, Spine, and Genitalia

Review the components of a newborn assessment of arms, legs, spine, and genitalia.

Slide 1: Arms and Legs

The arms and legs usually remain flexed against the body. Traumatic delivery may result in a fractured clavicle or brachial palsy. There should be 10 fingers and 10 toes, free of malformations, extra digits, or webbing. The hands and feet commonly have a bluish-purple color, called acrocyanosis, as the distal circulation improves over the first 7-10 days. The areas of central circulation, however, should not be cyanotic.

Slide 2: Spine

The spine should be straight without indentations, curves, or tufts of hair that may indicate a hidden spina bifida (neural tube defect).

Slide 3: Female Genitalia

Labia may be swollen, there may be white, mucousy vaginal discharge, and even a small amount of vaginal bleeding, which are normal findings. The remaining maternal hormones circulating in the newborn's body can cause pseudo-menstruation.

Slide 4: Male Genitalia

The testicles are normally descended at birth. The nurse will palpate the scrotum to determine if the testes have descended. A hydrocele, a collection of fluid, may be present and palpated. The placement of the urethral opening should also be assessed. An opening below the midline is called hypospadias.

Section 8: Explore: Newborn Reflexes

Various reflexes are assessed at birth to assess the nervous system.

Slide 1: Palmar Grasp Reflex

Elicited by placing a finger on the palm of the newborn's hand. The newborn grasps the finger.

Slide 2: Plantar Grasp Reflex

Elicited by placing a finger or stroking on the bottom of the foot. The toes curl downward.

Slide 3: Babinski Reflex

Elicited by stroking the heel upward to the outer toes. The big toe dorsiflexes and the other toes fan out.

Slide 4: Tonic Neck Reflex

Elicited by turning the head to one side. The arm and leg on the same side extend while the opposite arm and leg flex.

Slide 5: Moro or Startle Reflex

Elicited by a sudden noise or movement. The arms extend outward and then return to the body.

Slide 6: Crawling Reflex

Elicited by placing the newborn prone and applying pressure to the foot. The newborn makes a crawling motion.

Slide 7: Stepping Reflex

Elicited by holding the newborn upright with feet touching a flat surface. The legs move in a stepping motion.

Slide 8: Sucking Reflex

Elicited by an object touching the roof of the mouth. Begins to suck.

Slide 9: Rooting Reflex

Elicited by stroking the cheek or side of the mouth. Turns head towards the stroking and opens mouth.

Section 9: Explore: Assessing for Hypothermia and Hypoglycemia

Hypoglycemia and hypothermia are interrelated complications for the newborn that the nurse must assess.

Newborns are vulnerable to hypothermia because of their inability to thermoregulate. Even slight heat loss will cause hypoglycemia along with increased oxygen consumption in a newborn as their bodies work to burn stored fat (called brown fat), glucose, and oxygen for the first time.

Tab: Hypoglycemia

Using a standard bedside monitor, the nurse may check the newborn's blood glucose by collecting a sample from the thickest area of the heel. The normal glucose level in the term newborn ranges between 40-60 mg/dL on the day of birth and 50-90 mg/dL from then on.

The nurse will help prevent and detect hypoglycemia to prevent additional complications. Newborns whose temperatures are low may also show signs of hypoglycemia such as jitteriness, lethargy, and poor feeding. The presence of maternal diabetes, those who are premature or post-term, and those who are large or small for gestational age are most at risk. These newborns may be put on a hypoglycemia protocol which includes regular blood glucose monitoring and strict feeding schedules and amounts.

Tab: Hypothermia

The newborn will produce heat by metabolizing stored brown fat, a type of fat unique in the neonate, in a process called non-shivering thermogenesis. Newborns rarely shiver when they are cold. Nurses can help minimize heat loss using the following.

  • Radiant warmers may be used to bring up body temperatures to prevent complications.
  • Skin-to-skin contact is encouraged throughout the postpartum period unless contraindicated. Nursing assessment and actions are completed while in the mother's arms as much as possible. The newborn's first bath is delayed to allow for body temperature stabilization (as well as breastfeeding and bonding).
  • Warmed blankets are used to dry and swaddle the newborn at birth and the nurse ensures the newborn is adequately dressed and wrapped throughout the postpartum period. The nurse also ensures there are no extra blankets in the crib with the newborn as this can cause suffocation. The nurse reinforces safe sleep practices with the mother and family to help prevent sudden infant death syndrome, including the use of clothing and blanket wraps to keep the newborn warm, along with a cap and a warm room.

Section 11: Explore: Gestational Age Assessment

Calculation of the newborn's gestational age is another critical area of assessment typically completed by the nurse as part of the initial newborn care. Neonates born before or after term are at higher risk for complications, so it is important to verify the amount of time the newborn gestated (from conception to birth), a more accurate assessment than assuming the estimated date of delivery (EDD or due date). Even with an ultrasound during pregnancy, the EDD is still an estimate of gestation time.

This assessment helps answer the question, "Does this newborn who was born after gestating for xx weeks (based on the estimated date of delivery) look and act like most other newborns born at xx weeks gestation?" If not, why? What is the newborn at risk for that we can assess and treat early?

Tab: New Ballard Score

The New Ballard Score is a rating and classification system commonly used to compare certain neuromuscular and physical characteristics of maturity displayed by the newborn to the expected characteristics exhibited by most newborns of the same gestational age. While under the warmer, the newborn's responses to 6 tests of neuromuscular maturity and 7 tests of physical maturity are scored. The total score of all assessed characteristics determines the newborn's gestational age, accurate within 2 weeks.

[Figure: New Ballard Score — from Ballard et al., 1991]

New Ballard Scale Example

Let's consider Alex, who was born on January 8th. Alex's EDD was January 1st. We estimate that Alex was born after 41 weeks of gestation, and we anticipate that Alex will look and act like most other 41-week newborns.

The 13 areas of physical characteristics are assessed using the New Ballard Score. Alex's score for those 13 areas totals 35. According to the maturity rating table, Alex's score of 35 equates to a gestational age of 38 weeks. We expected him to have the physical characteristics of a newborn who gestated for 41 weeks. There is a discrepancy there.

The nurse will plot the newborn's weight, length, and head circumference on a graph of intrauterine development relative to this estimated gestational age. Where the measurements fall off the graph illustrates how they compare to other newborns of the same gestational age. How well did the newborn grow for the amount of time it spent gestating compared to other newborns who spent the same amount of time?

  • Small for Gestational Age (SGA): the newborn's size is below the 10th percentile on the graph.
  • Appropriate for Gestational Age (AGA): the newborn's size falls between the 10th and 90th percentiles on the graph.
  • Large for Gestational Age (LGA): the newborn's size is above the 90th percentile on the graph.

Alex's measurements are plotted on a graph that shows where most other newborns' measurements are at 41 weeks gestation. Alex's measurements are in the 10th percentile, which means they are lower than 90% of other newborns who gestated for 41 weeks.

Alex is SGA, small for gestational age, and is likely at risk of some complications and will require additional observation and intervention.

Section 12: Explore: Routine Newborn Screenings

Routine newborn screenings completed during inpatient care include:

Tab: Hearing Screening
  • The nurse assesses the newborn's hearing by observing reactions to sudden loud, high-pitched, or rhythmic noises as well as response to the mother's voice.
  • Prior to discharge, a hearing screening involving otoacoustic emissions or acoustic brainstem response tests is completed by an audiologist technician while the newborn is asleep. If the newborn does not pass the initial or follow-up test, the mother will be referred to a specialist for further testing after discharge.
Tab: Phenylketonuria (PKU) Screening
  • Phenylalanine is an amino acid (and a common protein in foods) that needs to be converted to tyrosine by the liver enzyme phenylalanine hydrolase to be used. Phenylketonuria (PKU) is a genetic disorder in which phenylalanine hydrolase is deficient. Phenylalanine can reach toxic levels in the blood, causing central nervous system damage and severe cognitive impairment.
  • Most state laws require all newborns to be screened for PKU after 24 hours of age and prior to discharge in order to identify the presence of this genetic condition. This delay allows enough time for proteins to be consumed.
  • The nurse typically collects the blood sample for this test, which requires a heel stick and enough blood to fill several circles on the testing form.
  • If found, a low-phenylalanine diet will begin immediately to prevent the toxic build-up of phenylalanine and central nervous system damage.

Section 14: Explore: Assessment of Newborn Nutrition - Intake

During the postpartum hospital period, the nurse ensures that the newborn is receiving adequate nutrition in a safe manner by assessing both the amount and quality of intake, as well as physical cues that reflect adequate nutrition. Newborns' bodies do not adequately balance energy storage and consumption, thermoregulation, and oxygen demand without proper nutrition. Without adequate nutrition, they are prone to hypoglycemia and hypothermia.

The newborn will be breastfed, bottle-fed, or a combination of both.

Tab: Breastfeeding

The first milk (colostrum) contains protein, calories, and immunity boosters, and is thin by design to pass through the newborn's digestive tract easily. The breast milk components are perfectly balanced for the newborn and change as the newborn's body adapts to extrauterine life.

The intake of milk after birth is the first introduction of bacteria through the digestive system, which remained sterile in utero. Intestinal flora populate the intestinal tract, making the newborn able to digest the thicker, more dense milk that will come.

Tab: Bottle Feeding

The mother may choose or need to use formula rather than breastfeed the newborn. These newborns may eat less often than breastfed newborns because the formula is dense and satisfying right away, and it takes longer to digest. Some may prefer bottle feeding for this reason and the reassurance of adequate nutrition that comes with the ability to measure the exact volume of intake.

  • Manufacturers produce formulas that come as close as possible to creating a breast milk substitute. Commercially available formulas made from powdered, modified cow's milk are most common. Other variations are available for special needs, such as milk allergy or sensitivity (soy or lactose-free formula) or the need for a different balance of macronutrients (preterm infants requiring more calorically dense milk).
  • The nurse ensures that the newborn is bottle-fed every 3-4 hours, depending on the newborn's needs and hunger signs. For the first 24 hours, about 15-30 mL at a time is best and then gradually increases by 15 mL intervals without forcing the newborn to take more.
Tab: Burping

At first, newborns may regurgitate mucous left in their stomachs from swallowing amniotic fluid in utero and may not keep milk down. Whether breastfeeding or bottle feeding, burping the newborn at intervals during feeding helps prevent some regurgitation due to swallowed air.

Section 15: Explore: Assessment of Newborn Nutrition - Output

Nursing assessment of urinary output and bowel movements is accomplished by visualization of the diaper. Typically, the newborn has more than 3 wet diapers + 3 stools per day by the 3rd day postpartum (333).

Slide 1: Urinary Elimination

The first urination should take place within 24 hours of delivery, but most newborns void within 12 to 24 hours of birth. The nurse carefully monitors for the first urine to take place and documents this. The absence of urine output may indicate anomalies requiring further testing. It can be difficult to tell when the first void occurs because it is usually a small amount and can easily be missed during the chaos of delivery and the transition interventions taking place.

  • Some facilities weigh diapers to determine the exact urinary output, but normally a general visual assessment of the amount is adequate. The newborn will only void small amounts and as little as once or twice during the first 2 days of life. By day 4, the newborn will have > 6 wet diapers, which indicates adequate fluid intake.
  • Occasionally, reddish or pink stains will appear with urine in the diaper and may concern parents. The nurse can assure them that this is caused by the presence of uric acid crystals and not a sign of blood in the urine.
Slide 2: Bowel Elimination

Newborn bowel movements are called meconium. The first stool may be passed while still in utero, sometimes the result of post-term or traumatic deliveries. This can be a dangerous situation if the fetus inhales some of the meconium-stained amniotic fluid into the lungs (MSAF).

  • Meconium is a sterile, odorless black tarry stool that is not the result of intake postpartum but digestion of amniotic fluid in utero. As the newborn digests breast milk or formula, the intestinal flora is established and the stools gradually change from meconium to a more frequent, thinner, mustard color with an odor. The characteristic of the stool is also an indication that breast milk or formula is being ingested and digested.
  • Typically, the first stool is passed within the first 12-48 hours of birth. By day 4, the newborn will pass 4+ stools per day.

Section 17: Explore: Assessment of Newborn Nutrition - Weight Loss and Gain

The birth weight of a term infant ranges between 5 pounds, 8 ounces and 8 pounds, 13 ounces (2500-4000 grams). The birth weight, along with the other birth measurements and assessment criteria, is used to evaluate the newborn's overall health and is compared to the normal ranges for the newborn's gestational age.

  • Newborns typically lose up to 7% of their birth weight in the first 24-48 hours. At birth, the bladder and colon contain urine and stool from swallowed amniotic fluid in utero. The urine and stool not yet excreted are included in the birth weight measurement.
  • This weight loss is anticipated by the nurse, who assures the mother that this is normal and a sign that the newborn is receiving adequate nutrition.
  • The birth weight provides a baseline by which the normal amount of initial weight loss is determined. The newborn's weight will be measured daily until discharge.
  • The newborn will typically return to or exceed their birth weight by day 14 and gain around 0.7-1 oz/day for the first 3 months.
