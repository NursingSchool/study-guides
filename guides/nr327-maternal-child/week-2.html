Section 4: Explore: Physiology of Pregnancy - Assessment of Fetal Well-Being

Fetal Well-Being Data

Fetal and maternal health are assessed frequently by healthcare providers as part of routine antepartal care using a variety of methods. Fetal viability, the ability to survive outside of the mother's body, is currently as young as 22 weeks gestation. With advancing technology, fetal viability is becoming earlier and earlier.

Participation in fetal assessment through antepartal care is a key nursing role. Preterm birth is one of the factors that can be mitigated by regular assessment of fetal and maternal well-being throughout pregnancy.

According to Healthy People 2030, the reduction of the rate of preterm births is among the nation's most important challenges.

U.S. Preterm Births Data (from Transcript):

  • The rate of preterm births rose by more than 20% from 1990 to 2006.
  • The percent of live preterm births in 2007 was 10.4%.
  • Healthy People 2030 goal: reduce preterm births from 10% (2018) to 9.4%; reduce rate of fetal deaths at 20 weeks or more of gestation from 5.9 (2017) to 5.7 per 1,000 live births and fetal deaths.
  • The rate of preterm births in the U.S. varies by race/ethnicity, suggesting health care disparities exist. The March of Dimes (2020) reports the average percentage of preterm births from 2016–2018 was highest among black women at 13.8%.

Section 5: Explore: Fetal Assessment - Heart Tones and Kick Counts

Fetal well-being begins pre-conception when the nurse assesses and guides the woman of childbearing age in preparing her body for a successful pregnancy, and that support continues throughout and following pregnancy to ensure the best outcomes.

Fetal well-being assessment begins early in the pregnancy by auscultating fetal heart tones and teaching the woman to measure and record kick counts. These are assessed throughout pregnancy to monitor for signs of fetal distress.

Fetal Heart Tones

The fetal heart rate can be auscultated just above the symphysis pubis with a Doppler as early as 10 weeks' gestation. Auscultation is often difficult in obese women. Later in pregnancy, the fetal heart rate can be heard in a variety of locations on the abdomen depending on fetal and placental positioning.

The nurse should auscultate fetal heart tones for one full minute to determine rate and regularity. A normal fetal heart rate ranges between 110–160 beats per minute (bpm).

Kick Counts

Measurement of fetal movement by the woman is called "kick counts" and is used to assess fetal well-being. Absent or decreased fetal movement is very concerning and should be reported immediately to the healthcare provider. Pregnant women should be taught to lay or sit quietly and count each fetal movement felt for one to two hours or until ten movements are felt. Typically, at least ten fetal movements are felt in a one to two-hour time period. Further evaluation of fetal health is necessary if the mother perceives that movements are decreased or absent compared to a baseline evaluation. Women, especially those with pregnancy complications, are encouraged to evaluate kick counts several times daily.

Section 7: Explore: Fetal Assessment - Fundal Height

Tab: Fundal Height Measurement

Fundal height measurement is routinely used by the nurse to assess fetal growth and confirm gestational age. The top of the uterus is called the fundus. Beginning in the second trimester, when the uterus rises from the pelvis into the abdomen, a tape measure is placed from the top of the fundus to the symphysis. From 20–32 weeks gestation, the fundal height measurement in centimeters should approximately correspond to the gestational weeks as determined by the estimated due date (EDD).

McDonald's Rule is used to measure the centimeter (cm) length from the top of the fundus to the symphysis pubis. After emptying her bladder, the woman should be positioned supine with her knees slightly flexed. A full bladder will displace the uterus and may alter the measurement.

A fundal height that is higher than anticipated may indicate excessive amniotic fluid (polyhydramnios) or the presence of multiple fetuses. A fundal height that is lower than anticipated may indicate intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR). Fundal height measurement is impacted by maternal obesity, fetal and maternal position, and the presence of uterine fibroids.

Tab: Exercises

Practice scenario: Arial is 20 weeks gestation. Top of uterus measured halfway between symphysis pubis and umbilicus; client reports first fetal movement (quickening) yesterday.

Question: Using McDonald's Rule, how many cm should Arial's abdomen measure at 20 weeks? Answer: 20 cm (fundal height in cm ≈ weeks gestation from 20–32 weeks).

Fundal height by gestational age (matching exercise answers):

  • 12 weeks → Just above symphysis pubis
  • 16 weeks → Halfway between symphysis pubis and umbilicus
  • 20 weeks → At the umbilicus
  • 20–32 weeks → Fundal height in cm matching week of gestation
  • After 36 weeks → "Lightening" — fetus drops into pelvis; decrease in fundal height seen

Section 9: Explore: Fetal Assessment - Non-Stress Test, Contraction Stress Test

A nonstress test or contraction stress test may be ordered by the healthcare provider as a means of further assessing fetal well-being. The nurse performs these tests and reports the results to the healthcare provider.

Tab: Non-Stress Test (NST)
Non-Stress Test (NST)

An NST is used to evaluate how periods of fetal movement impact the fetal heart rate, and may be done once or repeated regularly. An external fetal monitor (EFM) records fetal movement with two transducers:

  • tocodynamometer—monitoring contractions, and being placed at the top of the fundus
  • fetal ultrasound—monitoring the fetal heart rate, and being placed over the fetal back

In addition to applying external monitors, the mother is provided with a button to push when fetal movement is felt.

Pregnancy Conditions

Pregnancy conditions such as pregnancy-induced hypertension, diabetes mellitus, post-maturity, maternal smoking, or inadequate maternal nutrition may cause placental insufficiency. The NST determines fetal well-being in the presence of these complications.

Normal Fetal Heart Rate Ranges

Normally, the fetal heart rate ranges between 110-160 beats per minute (bpm). During fetal movement, the heart rate typically accelerates. Fetal movement may occur spontaneously or with external stimulation. An NST is interpreted as either reactive or non-reactive based on fetal heart rate accelerations during fetal movement.

A Reactive NST

In a fetus that is 32 weeks gestation or greater, a reactive NST occurs when the fetal heart rate accelerates at least 15 beats above baseline and the acceleration lasts for at least 15 seconds. Two fetal heart rate accelerations must occur in a 20-minute period of monitoring.

Before the 32 week gestation, accelerations must only exceed 10 beats above baseline and last for at least 10 seconds to be considered reactive. If a reactive NST is not obtained within a 20-minute period, a second 20-minute monitoring period can commence after external fetal stimulation.

A Non-Reactive NST

A non-reactive NST is indicative of fetal distress.

In a fetus 32 weeks gestation or higher, if accelerations do not exceed 15 beats above baseline, last at least 15 seconds, or do not occur at least twice in 40 minutes of monitoring, further evaluation using a biophysical profile (BPP) must occur.

In a fetus less than 32 weeks gestation, if accelerations do not exceed 10 beats above baseline, last at least 10 seconds, or do not occur at least twice in 40 minutes of monitoring, further evaluation is needed.

Additional Nursing Roles

Before performing the NST procedure, the nurse should instruct the client to empty her bladder, place her in a side-lying position, and explain the procedure. After interpreting the NST, the nurse should notify the healthcare provider and document the results.

Tab: Contraction Stress Test (CST)
Contraction Stress Test (CST)

Similar to an NST, a CST is used to evaluate fetal well-being in the presence of maternal complications, but in response to stimulated contractions instead of fetal movement. Contractions temporarily decrease oxygen supply through the placenta. Similar to an NST, an external fetal monitor should be applied to the abdomen before beginning the procedure.

Oxytocin

Contractions are stimulated using intravenous oxytocin or by nipple stimulation, which releases endogenous oxytocin.

A Negative CST

The desired result for a CST is a negative response, indicating that the fetal heart rate did not decrease after any contractions.

A Positive CST

A positive result indicates that the fetal heart rate decreased after at least 50% of the contractions. This repeated decline in fetal heart rate in response to contractions is concerning, indicating that fetal oxygen reserve is not sufficient to sustain decreased oxygen supply during uterine compression (contractions).

Additional Nursing Roles

Before performing the CST procedure, the nurse should instruct the client to empty her bladder, place her in a side-lying position, explain the procedure, and initiate the intravenous access, if indicated. After the procedure, the nurse should notify the healthcare provider and document the results.

Section 10: Explore: Fetal Assessment - Other Tests

Additional testing may be ordered by the healthcare provider as a means of assessing fetal well-being. The nurse's role is to provide education, prepare the client, and provide support throughout the procedure.

Tab: Ultrasound Scan
  • High-frequency sound waves visualize the fetus to monitor fetal growth, placental position, amniotic fluid volume, and detect fetal anomalies.
  • In early pregnancy, transvaginal ultrasound can determine the presence and viability of the pregnancy and accurately determine gestational age by obtaining fetal measurements.
  • Beginning in the second trimester, abdominal ultrasound monitors fetal growth, anatomy, and presentation, as well as the placenta location and amniotic fluid volume.
  • During abdominal ultrasound, the nurse ensures that the woman's bladder is full in order to elevate the uterus for better visualization. The nurse also provides education, prepares the woman, and monitors both mother and fetus after the procedure.
Tab: Amniocentesis
  • Guided by ultrasound, a needle is passed through the abdominal wall into a small pocket of amniotic fluid, which is removed for testing.
  • Amniocentesis is performed beginning between 15-20 weeks gestation and can be used throughout pregnancy.
  • This test is used to evaluate for various conditions including chromosomal and metabolic abnormalities and fetal lung maturity.
  • The nurse provides education, prepares the woman prior to the procedure, assists the provider during the procedure, and monitors both mother and fetus after the procedure.
  • There is an approximately 1% risk of pregnancy loss following an amniocentesis. Following the procedure, the woman should report vaginal bleeding, leaking amniotic fluid, abdominal pain, or fever to the healthcare provider.
  • The client will be discharged approximately 2-4 hours after the procedure if both mother and fetus are stable. Overnight hospital stay is only required if complications arise. The client should rest and reduce activity for 24–48 hours.
Tab: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • MRI provides more detailed images of fetal soft tissue than ultrasound and without the need to inject contrast medium used in computed tomography scans (CTs).
  • This test is used to visualize the placenta and fetal anatomy.
Tab: Maternal Serum Alpha-Fetoprotein (AFP) Screening
  • Maternal biomarker screening is used to predict the presence of neural tube defects and certain chromosomal abnormalities (trisomies 18 and 21).
  • Maternal blood is drawn between 15 and 22 weeks gestation.
  • The Triple Screen evaluates three maternal markers (AFP, estriol, and hCG) compared to maternal age.
  • The Quadruplet Screen evaluates four maternal markers (AFP, estriol, hCG, and inhibin-A) compared to maternal age.
  • Abnormal findings will prompt additional testing such as ultrasound (to confirm birth defects) and amniocentesis (to confirm chromosomal abnormalities).
Tab: Nuchal Translucency (NT) Testing
  • NT testing is performed between 10-13 weeks gestation using ultrasound to measure nuchal (the nape of the neck) thickness.
  • A measurement greater than 2.5 mm may indicate the presence of Down Syndrome or other genetic abnormalities and requires follow up with an amniocentesis.
Tab: Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS)
  • Chorionic villi are microscopic projections that burrow from the placenta into the endometrial tissue early in pregnancy. These projections come into contact with maternal circulation for nutrient and gas exchange. They also contain the genetic material of the fetus and therefore, help to detect fetal chromosomal, metabolic, and DNA abnormalities.
  • Sampling can occur between 10-13 weeks gestation and is guided by ultrasound. A small amount of chorionic villi are aspirated from the placenta through the cervix or the abdomen.
  • The nurse provides education, prepares the woman prior to the procedure, assists the provider during the procedure, and monitors both mother and fetus after the procedure.
Tab: Biophysical Profile (BPP)
  • BPP combines electronic fetal monitoring (a nonstress test) with ultrasound to assess fetal oxygenation in utero. Each of the following areas is assessed and scored: fetal breathing movements, fetal muscle tone, fetal body movements, volume of amniotic fluid, and non-stress test (NST) results (reactive or non-reactive).
  • BPP is typically performed after 32 weeks gestation to ensure that each component can be accurately measured, but the test can be done as early as 24 weeks' gestation, if warranted. Some indications for BPP include maternal decreased fetal movement, gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, and intrauterine growth restriction.
  • Each of these areas is given a score of 0 or 2 for a maximum of 10 points:
    • Scores of 8–10 indicate adequate fetal oxygenation.
    • A score of 6 requires retesting within 24 hours and possibly a contraction stress test (CST).
    • Scores of 0–4 may justify the delivery of the fetus.

Section 13: Explore: Perinatal Loss

At any time during pregnancy, perinatal loss (also referred to as fetal demise) can occur. This may happen early in pregnancy before the woman even realizes she is pregnant, but sometimes fetal demise occurs later in pregnancy or as a result of childbirth (stillbirth), even if the pregnancy has been otherwise healthy. The grief and loss affects the entire family. The nurse plays a key role in assessing needs, as well as providing comfort, education, and resources.

Spontaneous abortion (also known as a miscarriage) occurs when a pregnancy is terminated naturally by the body prior to 20 weeks' gestation. This may occur before the woman ever knew she was pregnant and is mistaken for a normal menstrual period. When the miscarriage occurs later in the pregnancy, the woman is more likely to have begun bonding with the fetus and announcing the pregnancy to loved ones.

Common causes of spontaneous abortion include abnormal embryonic development, chromosomal defects, maternal age, multiparity, chronic or acute illnesses, and/or maternal nutrition.

If all products of conception are not expelled naturally, they must be removed manually or surgically to prevent bleeding and infection.

The nurse provides emotional support and education to women experiencing perinatal loss. The woman should be encouraged to wait 2-3 months before becoming pregnant after a loss. Excessive bright red bleeding or foul-smelling discharge should be reported to the healthcare provider. If the woman has experienced multiple perinatal losses, the healthcare provider may recommend testing to determine the causes.

Classifications of a spontaneous abortion:

  • threatened (vaginal bleeding)
  • inevitable (membranes rupture and cervix dilates)
  • incomplete (some products of conception remain in the uterus and must be removed)
  • complete (all products of conception are expelled from the uterus)
  • missed (the deceased fetus remains in the uterus)

Section 4: Explore: Physiologic Changes in Pregnancy: Hormones

Pregnancy changes a woman's anatomy and physiology. There are many hormones involved in creating and sustaining a pregnancy. Several of these hormones are the cause of the signs and symptoms and other physical changes occurring during the antepartum period.

Tab: Main Pregnancy Hormones

The placenta functions as an endocrine gland to support the pregnancy and produces four hormones: human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), human placental lactogen (hPL), progesterone, and estrogen.

Estrogen is produced by the placenta from early in pregnancy and then throughout.

  • It enables the uterus to grow.
  • It increases blood supply to the uterus.
  • It contributes to lactation preparation.
  • It causes vascular changes in the breasts, skin, upper respiratory tract, and bladder.

Progesterone is considered the maintenance hormone of pregnancy.

  • It causes menstruation to stop.
  • It helps prevent spontaneous abortion.
  • It contributes to lactation preparation.
  • It stimulates respiratory ventilation.
  • It relaxes smooth muscles of the uterus, bowels, and bladder.

Human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) causes a positive pregnancy test.

  • It stimulates estrogen and progesterone production.
  • It contributes to morning sickness.

Human placental lactogen (HPL) acts as a growth hormone.

  • It is an insulin antagonist.
  • It allows adequate glucose for fetal demand.
  • It decreases maternal metabolism of glucose.
Tab: Other Pregnancy-Related Hormones

Several other hormones within the body support pregnancy: prolactin, relaxin, and oxytocin.

Prolactin

  • Maturation of breast ducts and alveoli, stimulates milk production (lactation)

Oxytocin

  • Stimulates uterine contractions and milk letdown

Relaxin

  • Causes cervical and pelvic softening to facilitate childbirth.
  • It inhibits uterine activity.
  • It causes increased mobility of the pelvic joints to accommodate the growing uterus.
  • It eases childbirth.

Section 8: Explore: Positioning for Optimal Pregnancy Cardiorespiratory Function

Pregnancy puts a great deal of demand on the mother's cardiovascular and respiratory systems, both anatomically and physiologically.

Tab: Sitting Position

The anatomical changes in pregnancy mean that the different gravitational impacts of the uterus on circulation are minimized in various positions. For the most accurate measurement of blood pressure, the most ideal position for the mother is sitting or standing.

The client's arm should be at the level of the heart and the blood pressure cuff should be an accurate size. Prenatal blood pressure should be assessed at every visit using the same arm and position.

Tab: Lateral Position

To decrease systemic vascular resistance (the vascular pressure that results from the uterus pressing on the inferior vena cava) and provide optimal circulation to the mother and fetus, a lateral recumbent position is recommended. Such a position allows blood to return to the heart and optimal cardiac output, particularly in later pregnancy when the uterus becomes heavier.

A lateral recumbent position results in the prevention of fetal hypoxia, supine hypotension, pedal edema, and varicose veins of the legs and rectum.

A lateral recumbent position allows:

  • Optimal blood to flow to the placenta, improving fetal oxygenation
  • Decreased pressure on the inferior vena cava
  • Decreased pressure from pooling blood on vessels

Nursing care of a pregnant client always includes correct positioning to ensure optimal circulation. Nurses educate pregnant clients to lay in a lateral recumbent position as the uterus becomes heavier.

Remember! If a supine position is necessary, the weight of the uterus is shifted by using a pillow or rolled blanket under one hip.

Supine Position & Lateral Position (Transcript):

Supine position increases risks of:

  • Fetal hypoxia
  • Supine hypotension
  • Pedal edema
  • Varicose veins of the legs and rectum (hemorrhoids)

The pressure from the weight of the uterus in the supine position impacts the aorta and inferior vena cava along the spine.

Lateral position relieves pressure on the inferior vena cava, decreasing systemic vascular resistance. The weight of the uterus pulls away from the spine.

Section 9: Explore: Cardiovascular and Respiratory System Changes in Pregnancy

Physiologically, pregnancy causes increased blood and oxygen demands on the maternal cardiopulmonary system in order to supply the growing uterus and the rapidly growing fetal tissue, and to accommodate the accelerated maternal metabolic rate.

Maternal Cardiovascular and Respiratory Systems Changes During Late-Stage Pregnancy (Transcript):

  • Pressure on viscera: the heart and diaphragm are pushed upwards.
  • Pressure on inferior vena cava: result in fetal hypoxia and systemic vascular resistance.
Tab: Respiratory System

Respiratory System Changes:

  • Increased oxygen demand; increased oxygen consumption 20%
  • Tidal volume increases 30-40%
  • Basal metabolic rate increases 10-20% by end of pregnancy
  • Enlarging uterus presses upward and elevates diaphragm
    • Shortness of breath
  • The hormone relaxin causes relaxation of ligaments around the ribs to allow for expansion
  • Progesterone decreases airway resistance

Vital Signs, Laboratory Results, Clinical Manifestations:

  • Slight hyperventilation (deep breathing) but respiratory rate is unchanged
  • Mild respiratory alkalosis (PaCO2 decreases)
    • Facilitates transfer of carbon dioxide from fetus to the mother
  • Nasal stuffiness and epistaxis due to estrogen-induced edema & vascular congestion
Tab: Cardiovascular System

Cardiovascular System Changes:

  • Enlarged myocardium by 10-15%
  • Increased circulatory volume by 40-45%
    • Plasma increases 40-60% (1200-1600 mL)
    • Red blood cell volume increases 20-30% (500 mL)
  • Increased cardiac output by 30-50%
    • Highest in a side-lying position
  • Hypercoagulable state; increased risk of venous thrombosis
  • Progesterone and prostaglandins cause vasodilation
  • Venous stasis due to pressure from an enlarging uterus

Vital Signs, Laboratory Results, Clinical Manifestations:

  • Pulse increases 15-20 bpm
  • Split S1 heart sound and systolic murmur (95% of clients)
  • S3 heart sound (90% of clients)
  • Decreased hemoglobin (Hgb) and hematocrit (Hct) due to hemodilution
    • Fatigue, weakness, headache
    • Iron supplementation may be needed if Hgb falls below 10.5-11 g/dL or if Hct falls below 32-33%
  • Increased red blood cell count to transport additional oxygen
  • Increased white blood cell count up to 15,000 cells/mm3
  • Increased platelet count
  • Fibrinogen and clotting factors increase by 50% (fibrinolysis is suppressed)
  • Systolic BP decreases slightly as pregnancy progresses
  • Diastolic BP decreases in the first half of pregnancy then gradually returns to pre-pregnancy levels
  • Dependent peripheral edema, varicose veins, hemorrhoids
  • Postural hypotension

Section 12: Explore: Sexual Desire, Breasts, and Skin Changes in Pregnancy

Slide 1: Sexual Desire
  • Sexual desire may be impacted by the increasing discomfort and size of the abdomen.
  • Sexual activity may be contraindicated with the presence of pregnancy complications, including:
    • Vaginal bleeding
    • Preterm labor
    • Rupture of membranes
Slide 2: Changes of the Breasts
  • Nipples become more erectile and the areolas darken.
  • The breasts become larger throughout pregnancy resulting in increased sensitivity, tingling, and heaviness.
  • The mammary glands grow in size in preparation for milk production.
  • Superficial veins become more prominent due to venous congestion.
  • Lactation is inhibited until estrogen decreases after birth.
Slide 3: Changes of the Skin (vary depending on the woman's size, skin tone, and racial background)
  • A linea nigra may appear — a dark line from the umbilicus to the abdomen.
  • Angiomas may be present on the neck, thorax, face, and arms.
  • Erythema of the hands may be present due to increased estrogen.
  • Other skin changes include mild puritis of the abdomen, gum hypertrophy, and accelerated nail growth.
Slide 4: Changes of the Skin (continued)
  • Striae gravidarum, or "stretch marks", may appear on the thighs, abdomen, and breasts.
    • They are caused by the stretching of the skin.
    • They typically fade after delivery.
Slide 5: Changes of the Skin (continued)
  • Pigmentation increases as a result of increased melatonin primarily in the areolae, nipples, vulva, and perineum.
  • Facial areas may appear darkened.
Slide 6: Changes of the Skin (continued)
  • The growth of hair and nail is accelerated.
  • Hirsutism may occur (masculine pattern of excessive hair growth).

Section 14: Explore: Musculoskeletal, Gastrointestinal, and Genitourinary Changes in Pregnancy

Pregnancy requires every body system to adapt.

Tab: Musculoskeletal Changes
  • Postural changes due to the increasing weight of pregnancy include:
    • shift of the center of gravity
    • gait changes
    • forward pelvic tilt
    • decreased abdominal muscle tone
    • lumbar spinal curvature
  • Other physiologic changes include:
    • blood vessel compression causing venous stasis and edema
    • nerve compression causing numbness and tingling in the legs
    • pressure on abdominal ligaments and muscles causing diastasis recti
    • stoop-shouldered stance causing numbness and tingling in the hands
    • lordosis causing lower back ache and pain
    • electrolyte imbalances causing muscle cramps
  • Hormonal changes in preparation for childbirth include:
    • Pelvic joints relax and move more flexibly while connective tissue softens and becomes more elastic.
    • Kegel exercises help to strengthen the perineal area before and after childbirth.
Tab: Gastrointestinal Changes
  • Nausea
    • Cause: increased human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) levels and changes in carbohydrate metabolism
    • Treatments: eat small amounts of bland foods (soda crackers and dry toast) slowly and before rising in the morning
  • Pyrosis (heartburn)
    • Cause: gastric reflux caused by the enlarging uterus and decreased peristalsis
    • Treatment: small, frequent low-fat meals and remaining upright after meals
  • Ptyalism (increased salivation)
    • Cause: increased nausea and vomiting associated with pregnancy
    • Treatments: frequent oral care and increased water intake
  • Abdominal distention, flatulence, constipation, and hemorrhoids
    • Cause: slowed intestinal peristalsis, straining to defecate, and increased pressure on pelvic blood vessels
    • Treatments: increased fiber and fluid intake and exercise
Tab: Genitourinary Changes
  • Frequent urination, particularly in the last trimester, due to pressure on the bladder and the increased circulating blood volume. Bladder irritability and urgency may occur as well.
    • During the second trimester, the uterus pushes out of the pelvis and into the abdomen, decreasing urinary symptoms.
    • Symptoms reappear in the third trimester when the fetal presenting part descends into the pelvis and exerts pressure on the bladder.
  • Slight proteinuria and glucosuria may result from the increased demand for urination on the renal system.
  • Urine capacity increases but urine flow slows.

Section 15: Explore: Hyperemesis Gravidarum

Hyperemesis gravidarum refers to severe nausea and vomiting lasting beyond the first trimester, causing dehydration, nutritional deficits, weight loss, and fluid and electrolyte imbalances. Some women require hospitalization because the severe, chronic vomiting can lead to acid-base imbalance, and kidney and cardiac dysfunction. Left untreated, hyperemesis gravidarum may lead to fetal death.

The human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) hormone causes mild nausea and vomiting in the first trimester of pregnancy. In hyperemesis gravidarum, the HCG levels are very high or rise rapidly.

Medical treatment of hyperemesis gravidarum focuses on preventing severe vomiting and restoring physiologic balance using intravenous (IV) hydration and possibly enteral nutrition.

Pharmacologic treatment may include:

  • pyridoxine (vitamin B6)
  • doxylamine
  • metoclopramide
  • ondansetron
  • proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole)
  • antacids (calcium carbonate)
  • H2 histamine blockers (famotidine)

Nurses will:

  • Administer the medications as ordered.
  • Encourage small, frequent meals consisting of bland, non-greasy foods.
  • Teach other ways to counteract nausea.
  • Provide empathy and compassion.

Section 17: Explore: Maternal Psychological Responses

The physical demands placed on the woman's body during, throughout, and after pregnancy cannot be overstated. The nurse should not only be aware of the woman's physical changes and needs in pregnancy, but must also be sensitive to their psychological responses to these changes. Pregnancy results in changes to every system of the body, and these changes will result in various individualized psychological responses and adaptations.

For some clients, pregnancy is a happy time; for others it is a sad time due to an infinite combination of both medical and personal reasons. However, psychological responses can change over time. Feelings are impacted by the individual's environment, culture, life experiences, pre-existing psychological pattern of adapting to life's changes, and many other factors unique to the person.

Tab: Planned or Unplanned Pregnancy

Examples of situations that shape psychological response:

  • first child in the family
  • pregnancy after a long infertility battle
  • pregnant adolescent who lacks family support and has to quit school to begin working
  • pregnancy resulting from a relationship that has ended
  • strong family support
Tab: Healthy or Unhealthy and Physically Demanding Pregnancy

Examples of situations that shape psychological response:

  • needs to quit work for bedrest while has several other younger children that need to be cared for
  • body image issues like stretch marks and weight gain
  • can continue to exercise throughout her pregnancy to support fitness goals
  • hyperemesis gravidarum who is sick for weeks or months
  • health complications for a typically healthy woman who now needs medical care
Tab: Healthy or Unhealthy Fetus

Examples of situations that shape psychological response:

  • facing emotional and financial fears associated with raising a disabled child
  • all girls and is finally expecting a son
  • clinically depressed and is expecting a healthy child and begins to find hope in the future
  • unexpectedly ambivalent about becoming a mother despite expecting a healthy child

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Care During Pregnancy

The cycle of childbearing is a major life change for women, whether this is their first pregnancy or one of many. The nurse will assist with and assess more than just physiological status when caring for a woman and her family throughout pregnancy and childbirth. The nurse will meet with the pregnant woman and her family throughout the pregnancy in the medical office, clinic, home care, or hospital settings and should be assessing factors that may influence childbirth preparation such as coping, roles, past sexual abuse, and cultural considerations.

We will discuss nursing care during the antepartum period, including how pregnancy is determined, the normal physiologic changes, and the basics of antepartum care.

Section 3: Explore: The Physiology of Pregnancy

It is important for the nurse to have a basic understanding of how conception occurs and how pregnancy develops in order to recognize and analyze cues that indicate a healthy or at-risk pregnancy. Many physiologic processes and factors work together to achieve conception in the human body. Click on each item below to learn more.

Tab: Fertilization

For fertilization to occur, sperm ejaculated during sexual intercourse enters the female's vagina and must travel through cervical mucous into the outer third of the fallopian tube (called the ampulla). An ovum must be produced prior to this moment and be waiting in that spot in the fallopian tube. Fertilization (conception) can now take place when the sperm fuses with the ovum. This newly fertilized ovum, called the zygote, contains 46 chromosomes (2 of which are sex chromosomes), which means that the sex and all the genetic characteristics are determined and will not change.

Transcript: Fertilization occurs when a sperm nucleus of the paternal origin units with an egg nucleus of the maternal origin. This results in forming a zygote. The zygote undergoes mitosis (cell division) and becomes a blastocyst. The blastocyst possesses an inner cell mass that subsequently forms the embryo.

Tab: Zygote to Blastocyst

Following fertilization, another process begins. For 3-4 days, the zygote travels from the fallopian tube into the uterine cavity. During this time, the zygote is rapidly undergoing mitosis (cell division) and becomes a blastocyst consisting of a circular mass of cells with a fluid-filled cavity in the middle that is surrounded by two cell layers. If the zygote is prevented from leaving the fallopian tube and cell division continues, the blastocyst/embryo may implant in the fallopian tube, resulting in an ectopic pregnancy.

Transcript — The first week of pregnancy:

  • Secondary oocyte: 10 to 19 days of your menstrual cycle
  • Fertilization: 12 to 24 hours
  • Zygote formation: 24 hours
  • First segmentation: 24 hours
  • 2-cell stage: 36 hours
  • 4-cell stage: 48 hours
  • Morula: 4 to 5 days
  • Blastocyst: 6 days
Tab: Implantation

The blastocyst has been floating in the uterine cavity for 1-2 days and is now ready to implant into the uterine lining. The lining must be in the right condition for implantation to occur in the uterine fundus. If the vascular or hormonal conditions are not right to sustain an implanted embryo, a spontaneous abortion (pregnancy loss prior to 20 weeks' gestation and commonly referred to as a miscarriage) occurs. This is a common cause of spontaneous abortion prior to 8 weeks of pregnancy, often before the woman is even aware of the pregnancy.

Some bleeding can occur during implantation, which women sometimes mistake for their menstrual cycle.

Also, during these first early weeks of pregnancy, the chorionic villi begin forming and secreting human chorionic gonadotropic (HCG) hormone which signals the body to stop ovulating and menstruating. Tests verify pregnancy by detecting the presence of HCG.

3D scene — early embryo milestones:

  • Leg buds appear
  • Heart begins to form
  • Arm buds appear
  • Spinal cord begins to form
  • Brain begins to form
  • The embryo is 0.04 inches long

Section 4: Explore: Embryonic and Fetal Development

  • Zygote: fertilized ovum (cell mass) prior to implantation
  • Blastocyst: stage in which the zygote forms into an inner and outer group (layer) of cells. The inner cell mass develops into a fetus, while the external cell mass develops into fetal membranes and the placenta. Implantation of the blastocyst occurs 6-10 days after conception.
  • Embryonic: stage in which the embryo further differentiates into 3 distinct layers and tissue: the placenta, the embryonic membranes, and the embryo itself. Cell growth is very rapid, the heart begins beating, and all the major organs and systems develop simultaneously. This is a very critical time when birth defects develop, and the developing embryo is vulnerable to teratogenic agents.
    • When an infant is born with a birth defect or abnormality, it is often accompanied by other defects.
    • Teratogenic agents include any drug, virus, or substance that can result in fetal malformation and cause serious harm. However, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC, 2024) still recommends that pregnant women receive certain vaccinations, while others are contraindicated.
  • At the end of the embryonic stage, the embryo looks human. The fetal stage begins at week 9.

3D scene — fetal development milestones (end of embryonic stage):

  • Fingers begin to form
  • Heart beats with regular rhythm
  • Toes begin to form
  • Eyelids are formed and eyes have moved forward
  • All major organs have begun to form
  • The fetus is 1 inch long, weighing less than 1/8 of an ounce
  • Umbilical cord is now visible

Section 6: Explore: Embryonic and Fetal Physiology

Let's look more closely at each of the structures that form during pregnancy: the placenta, fetal membranes, umbilical cord, and amniotic fluid.

Structures That Form During Pregnancy:

  • Placenta
  • Amniotic membranes (sac)
  • Umbilical cord
  • Amniotic fluid (fills sac)
Slide 4: Placenta

The placenta, an endocrine organ, is a truly amazing and unique structure that forms at the beginning of pregnancy and exists only to support the pregnancy. Functions of the placenta include:

  • secretes the hormones that maintain the pregnancy
  • exchange of nutrients, oxygen, and waste products occurs between the fetus and the maternal circulation
  • blocks the transfer of certain substances (some drugs, some viruses, most bacteria) to the fetus, called the placental barrier

The placenta is made of many lobes called cotyledons and by the end of pregnancy grows to 8 inches in diameter and weighs about one pound. The side that attaches to the uterine wall through a web of vasculature is called the "Dirty Duncan." The other side faces the fetus and is called the "Shiny Schultze." The condition of each side of the placenta is assessed at delivery.

Slide 6: Fetal Membranes
  • This is the amniotic sac that contains the fetus.
  • Chorion (outer layer) attaches to the Shiny Schultze side of the placenta.
  • Amnion (inner layer) blends with the umbilical cord.
Slide 7: Umbilical Cord

The umbilical cord begins at the center of the Shiny Schultze side of the placenta and attaches to the embryo/fetus. Characteristics of the cord include:

  • around 20 inches long and about 1 inch wide at the time of delivery
  • a pale white, gelatinous-mucoid substance called Wharton's jelly cushions the blood vessels in the cord from compression
  • contains 2 arteries (carry deoxygenated blood back to the placenta) and 1 vein (carries oxygenated blood to the fetus); if the cord contains 1 artery and 1 vein there may be associated with fetal anomalies

At delivery, the cord is cut and the newborn no longer receives oxygen from the placenta but through its own respiratory efforts.

Slide 8: Amniotic Fluid
  • Amniotic fluid surrounds the fetus in the uterus and is a clear yellow fluid that increases in volume throughout pregnancy, peaking at 34 weeks.
  • The fluid cushions the fetus from compression, regulates fetal temperature, allows room for growth, and provides for fetal lung development.

Section 8: Explore: Conception and Prenatal Milestones

During pregnancy, there are many development changes occurring in both the mother and the fetus. There are several periods of cell division, organ development, and other physical and genetic changes. As a result of these complex alterations, fetal growth is divided into three stages, known as trimesters. Knowing key milestones in each trimester can help the nurse provide anticipatory guidance and identify deviations from the expected pattern of development.

Tab: First Trimester

Weeks 1–13

  • Embryo Stage: This stage lasts from day 15 to the 8th week; all organs form in this period.
  • Fetal Stage: This stage lasts from the beginning of the 9th week till the pregnancy completion.
  • 8–12 Weeks: The fetal heart tones (FHTs) are audible using a doppler.
  • 12 Weeks: The sex of fetus can be determined; kidneys secrete urine.
  • 13 Weeks: The head is 1/3 of the total length.
Tab: Second Trimester

Weeks 14–26

  • 16 Weeks: The "quickening" fetal movement can be perceived by the mother.
  • 18–20 Weeks: Primitive respiratory movement begins; the heartbeat can be heard via fetoscope.
  • 24 Weeks: The fetus can hear.
  • 26 Weeks: The lungs have developed the alveolar sacs and REM sleep can be identified in the fetus.
Tab: Third Trimester

Weeks 27–40

  • 28 Weeks: Brown fat is present; eyes open; suck reflex begins; and surfactant is present.
  • 38 Weeks: The fetus receives antibodies from the mother.

Section 9: Explore: Determination of Pregnancy

During the initial stages of pregnancy, information about a woman's gynecological, menstrual, and obstetric history is obtained. Some of the information obtained includes signs of pregnancy. Click on each item below to learn more.

Tab: Determination of Pregnancy
  • The woman's body begins changing after the embryo has implanted itself in the woman's uterus.
  • Some of these changes can be felt and seen early on and the woman will take steps to determine if she is pregnant.
  • A pregnant woman will often schedule her first obstetric appointment after experiencing a variety of signs, or indications, of pregnancy.
  • There are presumptive, probable, and positive signs of pregnancy.
Tab: Presumptive Signs of Pregnancy

These are subjective indicators that the woman may be pregnant but may also be caused by other non-pregnancy-related conditions. Subjective indicators include:

  • amenorrhea (absence of menstruation)
  • nausea
  • vomiting
  • breast changes (swelling, tenderness, darkening areolae)
Tab: Probable Signs of Pregnancy

These are objective indicators confirmed upon examination but may also be caused by other non-pregnancy-related conditions. Objective indicators include:

  • uterine enlargement
  • Hegar's sign (softening of the space between the uterus and the cervix)
  • Goodell's sign (cervical softening)
  • ballottement (upon abdominal palpation a rebound is felt)
  • positive pregnancy test (detects presence of HCG in blood or urine)
  • Palpation of fetal outline

*Depending on the source, Chadwick's sign (increased vascularity noted upon pelvic exam) may be classified as a presumptive sign or a probable sign of pregnancy.

Tab: Positive Signs of Pregnancy

These signs only occur in pregnancy and confirm the presence of the fetus. Positive signs include:

  • Fetal movements felt by the examiner
  • visualization of the pregnancy on ultrasound
  • presence of the fetal heartbeat

  • Probable Signs of Pregnancy: Hegar's and Goodell's Signs; positive pregnancy test

Section 10: Explore: Nursing Care - Infertility

A nurse's understanding of the concepts of conception and prenatal development will also inform the nurse's ability to assist in the care of those struggling to become pregnant or maintain a pregnancy. Genetic counseling and treatment may be recommended. The nurse can play an important role in the infertility, genetic counseling, and treatment processes.

The nurse understands that many factors can cause infertility (the prevention of normal conception) or prevent a pregnancy from developing. While spontaneous abortions (miscarriages) occur relatively frequently during the first trimester, those that occur repeatedly could be the result of genetic issues. This could be caused by disruptions in normal conception or other maternal factors that contribute to an increased risk for infertility. The nurse also knows that women who have received infertility treatments have an increased likelihood of twins, for example, as a result of hormonal medications that induce the release of multiple ovum.

In general practice, nurses will encounter these couples in many settings and will address the couple's emotional needs, assist in genetic testing and counseling, and assist in the evaluation and treatment of infertility. Nurses will care for women currently receiving infertility treatments, including hormone therapy, and will know to expect certain side effects, such as alterations in mental alertness and mood. Couples who have experienced prenatal loss followed by successful pregnancy and delivery following infertility treatments have unique needs which nurses can address. These couples are often focused on maintaining control over the situation, following many disappointments and feelings of helplessness. The nurse can be sensitive by providing ways to ensure they feel involved and informed.

The nurse also plays a key role in assessing nutritional status and the need for follow-up guidance or resources during pregnancy.

Section 12: Explore: Antepartum Nursing Care

Once pregnancy is determined, antepartal (prenatal) care should begin. Women are often very motivated to improve health practices during pregnancy for the benefit of their unborn child. This provides many general and pregnancy-related health promotion opportunities. The cognitive and physical development of infants and children may be influenced by the health, nutrition, and behaviors of their mothers during pregnancy and early childhood. It is crucial that women are provided with routine antepartum and postpartum health care to ensure the best possible maternal, infant, and child outcomes.

The initial antepartum visit will include obtaining medical, gynecological, obstetrical and genetic histories, as well as a socioeconomic assessment to determine any needs that may hinder access to health care.

Assessment of obstetric history includes the woman's pregnancy and delivery history, complications, and outcomes, denoted by the acronym GTPAL.

Transcript: GTPAL

Remember...GTPAL

  • G = Gravida, the number of pregnancies the woman has had

Para – the number of births the woman has had:

  • T = the number of term births (37 weeks to 42 weeks gestation)
  • P = the number of preterm births (between 20 and 37 weeks gestation)
  • A = the number of spontaneous and/or elective abortions
  • L = the number of living children the woman has birthed

This first visit will also include the determination of the "due date." There are fetal milestones and measurements that are monitored throughout antepartal care, and these ranges are normal or abnormal based on the due date.

Certain routine exams and tests are conducted at various gestational ages. For example, a woman at 26 weeks gestation should have gained a certain amount of weight and fasting blood sugar testing begins to assess for gestational diabetes. If the due date is incorrect, the results of these exams will be inaccurate and, therefore, diagnosis and treatment will be inaccurate.

Following the initial visit, routine antepartum care visits for a normal pregnancy typically occur monthly through the 7th month, every 2 weeks in the 8th month, and then weekly until delivery.

A normal pregnancy lasts 40 weeks from the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP). When the woman does not know this date, other methods of gauging the due date are used, such as physical measurements of the uterus and fetus. These 40 weeks of pregnancy, around 9 months, are divided into 3-month periods called trimesters.

Transcript: Pregnancy Trimesters

  • 1st trimester: weeks 1-13
  • 2nd trimester: weeks 14-26
  • 3rd trimester: weeks 27-40

Section 14: Explore: Antepartum Nursing Care - Due Dates

If the woman does not keep a menstrual record, the primary care provider must then rely on observations, such as quickening, estimation of fetal size by palpation, or ultrasonic tests, all of which can be unreliable. However, for women who do keep an accurate menstrual record, the healthcare provider will be able to calculate the due date based off a woman's last menstrual period (LMP).

Many textbooks and healthcare organizations refer to a woman's due date in a variety of ways, such as:

  • EDC: estimated date of confinement
  • EDD: estimated due date
  • EDB: estimated date of birth

For the purposes of this lesson, we will use EDC.

In today's healthcare systems, electronic medical records are commonplace and may be of assistance when calculating an EDC. What if the software system was corrupted and the nurse was required to calculate the due date of a client without the use of a computer software program? In order to make this calculation, Nagele's Rule may be used. To begin, the nurse assesses the client's LMP date. To utilize Nagele's Rule, you simply subtract 3 months and add 7 days to the stated LMP date. However, be mindful of the year; in some cases, you may need to add a year as well.

A pregnancy wheel was commonly used prior to electronic medical record software implementation. You may still see a maternity nurse or nurse midwife utilize this handheld wheel as a quick reference tool at the bedside to determine the EDC.

Practicing Nagele's Rule

  • LMP = 11/21 (November is the 11th month) so 11 - 3 months = 8 (the 8th month). She is due in August.
  • Now, take the day of the client's LMP (first day of her last menstrual period). Your client states her LMP was 11/21, so the 21st day will be used.
  • Finally, add 7 days to it (21 + 7) = the 28th day.
  • August 28th is her EDC for the delivery of her unborn child.

Practice Questions (Nagele's Rule):

  • Q1: LMP March 19th, 2020 → March - 3 months = December; 19 + 7 = 26 → EDC: December 26th, 2020
  • Q2: LMP July 12th, 2020 → July - 3 months = April; 12 + 7 = 19; add 1 year → EDC: April 19th, 2021
  • Q3: LMP November 21st, 2020 → November - 3 months = August; 21 + 7 = 28; add 1 year → EDC: August 28th, 2021
