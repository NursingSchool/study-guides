1. Introduction to Fetal Assessment and Monitoring

The primary purpose of fetal assessment during labor is to assess the oxygenation status of the fetus.

Fetal assessment during the labor process is largely the responsibility of the registered nurse, who must be competent in interpreting data, implementing corrective actions to improve fetal oxygenation, accurately communicating findings with healthcare providers, and assisting in making clinical decisions to ensure the safety and quality of care.

Although both low and high technology methods of fetal monitoring are used during labor, the high technology method of electronic fetal monitoring is more commonly used in the U.S. The low technology methods of intermittent fetal heart rate auscultation with doppler or fetoscope may be used depending on the stage of labor and the risk status of the patient during labor.

In this module, we will discuss basic fetal monitoring during the labor process.

3. Explore: Leopold's Maneuvers

Accurate fetal heart monitoring begins with the correct placement of the monitoring device. Leopold's maneuvers are a systematic assessment using hands to palpate the gravid abdomen and suprapubic area to determine fetal presentation and position.

The fetal heart tones are best heard over the upper fetal back. Placement of the ultrasound transducer over the location of the fetal back will assist in accurate fetal heart tracing.

The nurse ensures that the patient's bladder is empty and assists them to a comfortable supine position with knees slightly flexed and a pillow under one hip. This allows for ease of palpation while also preventing the weight of the uterus from compressing the vena cava, which can result in maternal hypotension.

There are four maneuvers that involve using both hands in different positions to palpate the abdomen and suprapubic area for the recognizable anatomical features of the fetus.

(Figure: "An external fetal heart rate monitoring device is placed above the back of the fetus.")

The Four Maneuvers (slideshow transcript)

First Maneuver

  • Purpose: Determine the fetal presenting part.
  • Method: Use your fingers and palms to determine if the fetus is in breech (feet down) or cephalic (head down) position (presenting part). The buttocks feel soft and irregular while the head is firm and round.

Second Maneuver

  • Purpose: Determine fetal lie.
  • Method: Use your fingers and palms to determine which way the fetus is facing. The fetal back is smooth and convex while the fetal arms and legs ("small parts") are nodular and irregular.

Third Maneuver

  • Purpose: Determine fetal engagement.
  • Method: Use your thumb and middle finger to palpate the mother's suprapubic area, attempting to grasp the fetal presenting part to determine whether the fetus is engaged in the mother's pelvis. If it is engaged (firmly nestled in the pelvis), it will remain fixed and difficult to move. If the fetus is not engaged ("floating"), you will be able to move it up into the uterus.

Fourth Maneuver (only used when the fetus is cephalic)

  • Purpose: Determine cephalic flexion or extension.
  • Method: Switch your position so that your fingers are now pointing down toward the mother's pelvis and use one hand to slide into the upper edge of the symphysis while the other hand finds the fetal head. This determines if the head is flexed with the chin to the chest as it should be (vertex presentation) or extended back with the chin pointing out (face presentation).

4. Explore: Auscultation of Fetal Heart Tones — Fetoscope and Doppler

There are several devices used to auscultate fetal heart tones and determine the fetal heart rate (FHR) for the assessment of fetal well-being. Two manual, low fidelity devices used for periodic fetal heart rate auscultation: a fetoscope and a doppler.

Tab 1: Fetoscope & Doppler

Both fetoscope and doppler are used more frequently in the case of home births and clinics to periodically assess the FHR and its response to contractions. Fetal heart tones are auscultated for at least one full minute to assess the range and regularity as well as the relationship to various stimuli, such as uterine contractions.

A fetoscope can be used to auscultate fetal heart sounds by 18-20 weeks gestation. The electronic doppler can pick up fetal heart tones as early as 6 weeks gestation.

(Figures: Fetoscope; Doppler.)

Tab 2: Fetal Heart Sounds

It can be difficult to distinguish maternal heart sounds from fetal heart sounds. Palpate the mother's pulse while auscultating fetal heart tones to differentiate the two. Also, remember that the normal range for fetal heart rate is 110-160 bpm, much faster than that of the average adult at rest.

6. Explore: Internal Electronic Fetal Monitoring

After the rupture of membranes and cervical dilation of at least 2-3 cm, internal electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) probes can be used for greater accuracy and additional data when it is not possible with external means. This method is more invasive and limits the mother's activity. The same EFM machine and paper strips are used.

Fetal Heart Rate (FHR)

A spiral fetal scalp electrode is attached to the fetal scalp to directly measure FHR. Its pattern on the monitor appears and is interpreted the same as with an external monitor; no additional data is provided. However, accuracy is improved by using an internal monitor because it is directly attached to the fetus and is not impacted by movement.

Uterine Activity

An intrauterine pressure catheter (IUPC) electrode is inserted between the fetus and the uterine wall to directly measure contraction frequency and duration (as with an external EFM) with the additional ability to measure contraction intensity and uterine resting tone at mmHg.

In the example image, each vertical line represents 5 mmHg. For example, these contractions measure 55 mmHg with a 20 mmHg resting tone between contractions.

9. Explore: Fetal Monitoring Basics

For continuous rather than periodic monitoring of fetal heart rate (FHR) over time, along with FHR patterns and their relationship to uterine contractions, electronic fetal monitoring is used. These factors form an overall picture that indicates fetal well-being. Nurses who care for women during childbirth are legally responsible for correctly interpreting the FHR pattern, appropriate nursing actions based on that pattern, and documenting the outcome of those actions.

Tab 1: Electronic Fetal Monitoring
Important! The basic elements of EFM that indicate fetal well-being are: - the baseline fetal heart rate - the presence and pattern of accelerations and/or decelerations from the baseline fetal heart rate - the presence or absence of variability (variation) in the fetal heart rate from the baseline - the relationship of all these elements to the force of uterine contractions and descent into and through the woman's pelvis

These patterns determine how well the fetus reacts and recovers, which are reflections of central nervous and cardiac system status.

Electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) generates a reading strip like that of an EKG. There are two rows of tracings. The top row represents the FHR pattern and the bottom row represents the uterine activity pattern. This configuration allows practitioners to interpret the FHR pattern in relation to simultaneous uterine activity.

Each box on the paper from left to right represents 10 seconds and 6 boxes represent a minute. This allows the interpreter to determine how far apart uterine contractions are and determine the FHR baseline.

Strip image transcript:

  • Each box on the paper from left to right represents 10 seconds and 6 boxes represent a minute.
  • The top row of tracing represents the fetal heart rate (FHR) pattern and the bottom row of tracing represents the uterine activity pattern.
  • The variability of the fetal heart rate (FHR) is a good sign; it indicates the fetus is reacting and recovering. An adult heart rate doesn't stay at 80 bpm when there is stress, and neither should the FHR.

Tab 2: Scenario (deep-sea diving analogy)

Let's say you are deep-sea diving and the pressure of the depth cuts off your air circulation. Let's say this occurs for 60-90 seconds, every 2-3 minutes.

What would your heart rate do before, during, and after that time? If your central nervous system (CNS) is intact, it will react with an accelerated heart rate during that time and then your heart rate returns to normal afterward. The heart and nervous system are reacting normally and your air circulation is not cut off to the point of hypoxia.

What if the heart decelerates during that time but then recovers afterward? That might indicate that it is time to surface and call it a day before a lack of oxygen causes problems, but at least the heart is recovering.

But what if your central nervous system doesn't tell your heart to beat faster and it maintains a steady rate? What if it does not vary at all? What if your heart rate goes down and stays there? What if you are sitting on the boat and your heart rate decelerates for no reason at all? These would all be indications that something is not right and it is time to get out of there. These are examples of concerning FHR patterns that indicate fetal distress from hypoxia.

Accordion 1: Uterine Contractions

Strength: The strength of uterine contractions is determined by palpation. Results depend on the size of the client's adipose tissue.

  • Mild: Palpation feels like the tip of the nose.
  • Moderate: Palpation feels like the tightness of the chin.
  • Strong: Palpation feels like the forehead.

Resting Tone: The resting tone is determined by palpation.

  • It can be felt in between contractions.
  • Remember to adjust the monitor to zero during resting tone.

Frequency: Frequency is calculated from the beginning of a contraction to the beginning of the next contraction.

  • It is recorded in minutes and as a range (for example: q 2-4 min).

Duration: Duration is calculated from the beginning of the contraction to the end of the contraction.

  • It is measured in seconds and as a range (for example: 50-80 sec).
  • Usually, a minimum of 1 minute rest period is needed in between contractions to ensure adequate blood flow and oxygenation of the uterus and the fetus.

Accordion 2: Baseline Fetal Heart Rate (FHR)

Baseline fetal heart rate is the approximate mean fetal heart rate (FHR) rounded to increments of 5 bpm during a 10 minute segment.

  • Normal range: 110-160 bpm
  • Bradycardia: < 110 bpm, for < 10 minutes
  • Tachycardia: > 160 bpm, for < 10 minutes

12. Explore: Fetal Heart Rate Patterns

Each of the different fetal heart rate patterns has a different etiology that determines the interventions that are needed.

Tab 1: Early Decelerations

Early decelerations are caused by head compression. The fetus's head is pressed into the pelvis and compressed, causing the vagal nerve to be stimulated and resulting in a gradual decrease in the fetal heart rate (FHR), mirroring each contraction and then returning to baseline. Usually, this means fetal descent in the birth canal. Early decelerations are usually not a cause for concern. They may indicate that delivery is close.

Strip transcript: Early deceleration — Head compression (HC); onset at start of contraction; early onset.

Tab 2: Late Decelerations

Late decelerations are caused by uteroplacental insufficiency. This is a problem with uterine perfusion (such as with maternal hypo or hypertension) or uterine activity (such as caused by too much oxytocin infusion), or there may be a problem with the placenta, or both. Uteroplacental insufficiency indicates fetal distress requiring immediate intervention.

The nurse will change the mother's position, typically to a left lateral position, to increase fetal perfusion. Increased intravenous (IV) fluids and supplemental oxygen may be ordered as well. Any underlying cause will need to be corrected, such as too much uterine activity or maternal hypertension or hypotension. The healthcare provider will need to be alerted about recurrent late decelerations.

Strip transcript: Late deceleration — Uteroplacental insufficiency (UPI); late onset; late recovery; FHR uniform.

Tab 3: Variable Decelerations

Variable decelerations are caused by umbilical cord compression. The cord may be compressed due to maternal position, low amniotic fluid to cushion the cord, or it may be wrapped around the neck of the fetus (nuchal cord). The nurse will change the client's position to release pressure on the cord and increase fetal perfusion. The client may also have IV fluids ordered and have supplemental oxygen used. The healthcare provider will be notified about recurrent variable decelerations and additional interventions may be performed, such as an amnioinfusion by the healthcare provider to add fluid to cushion the cord.

Strip transcript: Variable deceleration — Umbilical cord compression (CC); variable time relationship to contractions; sudden drop; rapid return.

Tab 4: Accelerations

Accelerations of the fetal heart rate usually occur due to fetal stimulation or fetal movement. Just like our heart rate increases with activity, such as walking, so does the fetus's.

Accelerations are an indication of fetal well-being.

15. Explore: Nursing Implications for Fetal Heart Rate Patterns

Being able to interpret, document, and communicate fetal heart rate patterns is an important part of the nurse's role in intrapartum care. The fetal heart rate pattern provides data on fetal well-being, helps identify concerns, and determines courses of action when problems are identified. Failure to properly assess and communicate fetal heart rate patterns will result in poor outcomes for the fetus, which may have been prevented if promptly identified and resuscitative actions were taken.

Three categories of fetal heart rate interpretation have been established for nurses and healthcare providers in interpreting, communicating, and taking action.

Category I — Normal

Normal and strongly predictive of normal fetal acid-base status at the time of observation:

  • normal baseline
  • accelerations present or absent
  • absence of variable or late decelerations
  • early decelerations present or absent
Category II — Indeterminate

Indeterminate and fetal acid-base status is uncertain (fetal heart rate [FHR] patterns not in Category I or III).

Category III — Abnormal

Abnormal and predictive of abnormal fetal acid-base status at the time of observation:

  • bradycardia
  • absent variability
  • recurrent late or variable deceleration
  • sinusoidal pattern

1. Introduction to Nursing Care: Intrapartum Complications

This module will discuss some of the complications that can arise during the intrapartum period of labor and childbirth. These complications may be related to pre-existing conditions or abnormal situations that occur during labor, which put the woman and the fetus at risk for poor outcomes. The nurse plays a key role in preventing, detecting, and managing these complications.

2. Prepare: Nursing Care of Intrapartum Complications

Each of these interventions carries its own risks and benefits.

3. Explore: Dysfunctional Labor - Labor Dystocia and the Five Ps

During the intrapartum period, several complications can develop. Antepartum complications can also lead to intrapartum complications. Labor is a diverse and individualized process with complex, interacting factors related to passageway, passenger, powers, position, and psyche (Five Ps).

In labor and delivery, these five factors intermingle and influence each other to determine the outcome. To produce positive outcomes, these factors must act in harmony. When one or more of these factors have complications or abnormalities, labor can become difficult or dysfunctional.

Throughout labor, the healthcare team, including the nurse, will monitor for maternal and fetal signs of dysfunctional labor. Fetal signs of dysfunctional labor are, for example, the amniotic fluid that contains meconium (called meconium-stained amniotic fluid [MSAF]) or non-reassuring fetal heart rate patterns such as late decelerations and decreased variability.

4. Explore: Labor Dystocia - Passageway (Pelvis)

Complications related to the woman's pelvic shape or size impede fetal descent and safe delivery. Labor will likely involve ineffective uterine contractions, slow cervical dilation, and slow fetal descent. All these will prolong labor.

Also, if the woman's bladder does not remain adequately empty, it will get in the way and impede labor progress. The nurse will ensure the bladder remains relatively empty every 1-2 hours, non-palpable, and also ensure the woman has adequate urinary output throughout labor. When receiving an epidural in labor, the woman cannot feel the urge to urinate, and may not be able to empty her bladder, so an indwelling urinary catheter could be used.

5. Explore: Labor Dystocia - Passenger

Complications related to passenger (such as fetal presentation, fetal anomalies, and multifetal pregnancy) and/or position cause mechanical barriers to normal labor.

Abnormal Positioning

For optimal labor progression and delivery of the fetal head, the fetal position begins in the occiput posterior position and then spontaneously rotates to the occiput anterior (OA) position. If this doesn't happen and the fetus remains in an occiput transverse (OT) or occiput posterior (OP) position (possibly due to the fetal size, fetal anomalies, and/or maternal pelvis size), the labor will take longer, be more painful, require more interventions, and the mother and fetus may also become fatigued. The assessment of a sterile vaginal exam will indicate the fetal position based on the fontanel landmarks on the fetal head (anterior, posterior, transverse, etc.).

Breech Presentation or Multifetal Pregnancy

If the fetus is in breech presentation rather than cephalic presentation or there is more than one fetus, cervical dilation and effacement will be slower because the fetal head does a better job of providing the firm, even pressure needed to dilate the cervix. The danger with breech presentation is that once the body is delivered, the cervix will close around the fetal neck and make it difficult for the provider to deliver the head, causing fetal hypoxia.

Usually, a breech presentation is confirmed before labor and reconfirmed throughout labor. An attempt to turn the fetus into a cephalic position (a procedure called a "version") may be attempted by the provider if it is deemed safe to do so, but otherwise a cesarean delivery will be scheduled.

Labor and delivery for a multi-fetal pregnancy will involve careful monitoring of each fetus separately and preparation for the complications that can result from the presence of more than one fetus.

Shoulder Dystocia

Shoulder dystocia occurs when the shoulders get caught behind the maternal symphysis pubis and delivery stops (after the fetal head is delivered).

  • Shoulder dystocia happens most often when the fetus is too large for the maternal pelvis (e.g., due to macrosomia related to gestational diabetes), but it can also happen very unexpectedly. One sign that shoulder dystocia is about to happen is — the "turtle sign" — the fetal head retracts against the perineum after it is delivered like a turtle drawing its head back into its shell.
  • When the head is out, and the rest of the body isn't, fetal hypoxia can occur rapidly. The umbilical cord can become compressed, but also with the fetal chest being compressed, respirations are prevented.
  • Shoulder dystocia is a critical situation that requires prompt action by the entire healthcare team present in the room and the woman. The nurse will assist the woman with a McRobert's maneuver, pulling the client's knees up as far as possible toward the shoulders to assist the healthcare provider in delivering the shoulders safely. The healthcare provider may also apply suprapubic pressure by pressing above the pubic bone to release the shoulder. After delivery, the newborn will be assessed for a fractured clavicle and any signs of Erb's palsy, such as a unilateral flaccid and extended arm.

6. Explore: Labor Dystocia - Powers

Powers are complications related to ineffective uterine contractions or ineffective maternal pushing.

Ineffective Uterine Contractions

Ineffective uterine contractions can either be too weak, causing failure to progress (labor dystocia) or too frequent contractions (tachysystole).

  • A secondary arrest occurs in the active phase of labor. The contractions are weak or too irregular to effectively dilate the cervix, and labor progression slows or stops. Labor will likely need to be augmented with intravenous oxytocin that strengthens and regulates uterine contractions enough to dilate the cervix. The nurse will administer and titrate the oxytocin per protocol based on the assessment of its effect on the uterus, cervix, and fetal heart rate pattern.
  • Tachysystole can happen on its own or as a result of oxytocin infusion. Tachysystole is excessive uterine activity (more than 5 contractions in 10 minutes averaged over 30 minutes) and can cause maternal fatigue and fetal distress. The uterine perfusion is reduced with frequent contractions without adequate rest in between, so oxygen flow to the placenta and the fetus is reduced. To allow adequate placental (and therefore fetal) perfusion and oxygenation, contractions should not last longer than 2 minutes each and the uterus needs to rest for at least 1 minute between contractions. This rest is called resting tone and is determined by either palpating the uterine tone or by a measurement of the uterine intensity of under 25 mmHg via intrauterine pressure catheter (IUPC). A risk from excessive force of contractions is uterine abruption.

Ineffective Maternal Pushing

Ineffective maternal pushing can result for a variety of reasons, such as fatigue or excessive analgesia or anesthesia.

  • Maternal fatigue: If the woman does not have the physical or mental strength to carry on due to no sleep or inefficient sleep, the labor can be particularly stressful or physically demanding. If she's very anxious, the pushing can be ineffective as well. The nurse needs to monitor for signs that show the woman is becoming fatigued, both verbal (e.g., "I can't do this anymore!") and non-verbal (e.g., ineffective coping, irregular breathing pattern), and objective findings (e.g., changes in vital signs or contraction pattern). The nurse can also take steps to preserve the woman's energy and help her relax, breathe effectively, and provide a calming environment.
  • If pain is the cause of fatigue, an epidural or other medication may help with rest and relaxation. The nurse will assist in this decision making through collaboration with the healthcare team. A warm bath or shower, comforting music or aromatherapy, or various labor positions can also provide comfort and relief for the woman.

7. Explore: Labor Dystocia - Position

Complications related to anatomical positioning of the woman in labor impact the risk for intrapartum complications in several ways by causing mechanical barriers to fetal descent, maternal discomfort and fatigue, and restricting circulation.

Positioning Best Practices

It is best for a pregnant woman to change positions frequently and remain physically active during labor when it's safe to do so. Encourage using positions in which the woman feels more comfortable.

  • When the fetal position is an issue and labor is not progressing, evidence of fetal distress is present in the fetal heart rate pattern, and the mother is particularly uncomfortable. As a result, the nurse will employ different labor positions for comfort and to promote fetal rotation and descent through the maternal pelvis.
  • The nurse monitors for signs of improvement following the position change. The fetal heart rate pattern may show signs of improved perfusion after a position change and may show signs that the position allowed it to descend further into the pelvis and is now compressed against the cervix (early decelerations).

The use of various positions in labor and delivery is most often done by the labor and delivery nurse. It can be difficult to maintain electronic fetal monitoring when the woman changes to different positions, so the nurse will need to make sure the monitoring remains continuous and accurate.

Types of Positions

Positions that may be used with rationales:

  • When an epidural is in use and the woman is unable to ambulate, regular rotation of various positions (typically side-lying) in labor is critical to ensure optimal maternal and fetal perfusion.
  • Remaining upright improves circulation and minimizes venous compression while improving cardiac output, providing the mother and fetus with more needed blood flow and oxygen. A side-lying position is ideal for placental (and therefore fetal) perfusion.
  • Changing positions is also used when fetal distress is present. A side-lying position often relieves possible head and cord compression.
  • Certain positions use the force of gravity more effectively to allow the fetus to descend into the pelvis and put additional pressure on the pelvic floor, dilating the cervix. When the fetal occiput is posterior (OP), the hard round skull presses on the mother's tailbone with contractions and it is very uncomfortable for the woman. Sometimes the pain even breaks through the epidural anesthetic. The nurse can assist the woman to assume a hands and knees (getting on all fours) position to help relieve that pressure and may actually rotate the fetus into the right occiput anterior (ROA).
  • Maternal positioning will be used to attempt to release the impacted fetal shoulders and deliver the shoulders safely. Three things will happen immediately upon identifying a risk for or actual shoulder dystocia:
    • The healthcare provider will immediately attempt to quickly release the impacted fetal shoulders.
    • The healthcare provider will apply suprapubic pressure to push and disengage the fetal shoulders/clavicle from the symphysis pubis.
    • The nurses will perform McRobert's maneuver (the woman's knees are pulled up toward her shoulders as tightly as possible, with a nurse pushing or pulling on her legs to accomplish this).
  • Squatting, sitting, kneeling, standing, and walking are also positions the nurse may assist with.
  • During delivery, the lithotomy position may be used with the legs in stirrups.

9. Explore: Labor Dystocia - Psyche

Complications related to psyche are based on the woman's perception of and response to stress, pain, fear, becoming a mother, or what that role means in her life. These emotions can hinder a woman's labor progress depending on many factors.

To achieve the best labor outcomes, the nurse can help the woman relax physically and mentally by providing a calming environment, assisting her in maintaining control of labor pain in a way that is best for her (using both pharmacological and non-pharmacological pain management methods), and keeping her informed as appropriate.

The woman's emotional status can be impacted by the length of labor.

Prolonged Labor

The longer the labor continues, the more of a toll it has on both the mother and her baby. Epidurals may sometimes slow labor down. Women attempting a vaginal birth after a cesarean (VBAC) will also have slower labors. For primigravidas (the first time being in labor), labor almost always takes longer.

During prolonged labor, the woman may become concerned that something is wrong and she may feel like it's never going to end and question her ability to make it through. The nurse can use the Friedman curve to help illustrate labor progress. This assessment tool factors in the rate of cervical dilation relative to the rate of fetal descent. Typically, the cervix dilates at a minimum rate of 1.2 cm/hr for the primigravida and 1.5 cm/hr for the multigravida.

Precipitous Labor

A precipitous labor is defined as a birth that occurs within 3 hours after the labor onset. When the labor starts and ends quickly, a precipitous birth may result. A precipitous birth is defined as a childbirth that occurs so fast that a trained attendant was not present to assist, either within a medical facility or otherwise. Normal labor is typically a progression, allowing time for the mother's body to dilate and stretch and the fetus to descend and recover from the pressure of descent and uterine contractions.

  • A precipitous labor and/or birth can result in complications for the mother and the fetus, including uterine rupture, birth canal lacerations, amniotic fluid embolism, postpartum hemorrhage, fetal hypoxia, fetal intracranial hemorrhage, and meconium staining and/or aspiration. The result of a fast descent may be fetal head compression against the cervix, which is seen in the fetal heart rate pattern as early decelerations (occur just before or with contractions).
  • A precipitous labor may be treated with a tocolytic to stop uterine contractions and prevent complications.
  • If the nurse is present for a precipitous labor or precipitous birth (not attended by a physician or nurse-midwife), the focus is on promoting fetal oxygenation, maternal comfort, and safety. A side-lying position is best for placental blood flow.

10. Explore: Preterm Labor

Preterm labor is defined as labor that occurs between 20–36 6/7 weeks gestation. Early term refers to 37 0/7–38 6/7 weeks gestation.

Risk Factors

The exact cause for the onset of preterm or term labor is unknown, but there are some common predisposing factors for preterm labor.

Social, Demographic, and Environmental Components

  • inadequate/absent prenatal care
  • history of domestic violence
  • maternal tobacco or substance abuse
  • financial burden or homelessness
  • time intervals between births
  • parental race
  • age

Fetal Factors

  • growth restriction
  • low amniotic fluid volume
  • chromosome abnormalities
  • birth defects

Maternal Factors

  • Antepartum complications such as multifetal gestation, preterm membrane rupture (which can cause the uterus to begin contracting and/or lead to infection that can lead to uterine contractions), abnormal placental implantation, uteroplacental ischemia or hemorrhage (abruption or previa), and uterine overdistention
  • Antepartum conditions such as preexisting or gestational diabetes, chronic hypertension and preeclampsia, infection or inflammation, stress, various immunologically mediated processes, bleeding disorders, and conceptions enhanced by assisted reproductive technology, particularly those which result in a single rather than multi-fetal gestation
Preterm Newborn

Under normal circumstances, the mother is no more at risk for physical harm in labor than she would be at term, but the risks to the preterm newborn are significant. The infant is born before the body has fully developed and matured. It is not yet fully equipped to survive extrauterine life on its own, without the mother's body to provide it with oxygen, nutrients, blood volume, warmth, and so on. The newborn's body, with the help of medical technology, must work on its own. If the newborn survives, long-term effects of prematurity include cerebral palsy, developmental delay, and vision and hearing impairment.

The earlier in pregnancy preterm labor and birth occur, the greater the risks to the newborn. In terms of the likelihood of survival with the fewest short-term and long-term complications, medical science has progressed to where an infant born after 32-34 weeks' gestation will fare better than those born earlier than 32 weeks. The cost for neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) care is a great burden on the child's parents.

Prenatal Care

Prenatal care is crucial in preventing and detecting preterm labor. The healthcare team is trained to recognize risks and signs, and to help the woman and her family prevent preterm labor when possible. The signs that preterm labor is occurring can be subtle and easily missed until the cervix begins dilating and more obvious signs of labor become apparent. By then, it may be difficult or impossible to stop labor.

The woman may say she does not feel right, have a low backache, cramps that resemble menstrual cramps feeling like the baby is "balling up", vaginal discharge or spotting, and a sensation of pelvic or vaginal pressure.

Treatment

Treatment is focused on prevention for those identified as most at risk. Medical treatment includes attempting to quiet the uterus long enough to ensure the best chances for neonatal stability in case preterm delivery does occur.

Treatment Options

  • Transfer the woman to a neonatal intensive care unit (NICU)-equipped facility, and provide hydration, prophylactic antibiotics and prescribed inactivity or bed rest.
  • Maternal steroids betamethasone or dexamethasone are used to accelerate fetal lung maturity, thereby reducing the incidence and severity of neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) and intraventricular hemorrhage in the preterm infant.
  • Use indirectly tocolytic medications to slow uterine activity such as beta-adrenergic drugs like the bronchodilator terbutaline and magnesium sulfate.
    • Magnesium sulfate has many side effects requiring vigilant nurse monitoring and intervention, so the decision to use this medication is not taken lightly.
    • Magnesium sulfate is also used to treat pregnancy-associated gestational hypertension and prevent subsequent seizures. These smooth muscle actions also make it an effective treatment choice to quiet uterine activity, but also mean that deep tendon reflexes will be diminished, which leads to the risk of cardiac and respiratory depression, and makes the woman feel lethargic, nauseated, and can cause headaches.

12. Explore: Postterm Pregnancy

According to the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (2014), pregnancy gestation is defined as follows:

Pregnancy Gestation

  • Term pregnancy: 39 0/7–40 6/7 weeks' gestation
  • Late-term pregnancy: 41 0/7–41 6/7 weeks' gestation
  • Postterm Pregnancy: exceeds 42 0/7 weeks' gestation

The gestational weeks are based on the determined estimated date of delivery (EDD). The EDD is initially determined using a calculation based on the woman's recollection of the first day of her last menstrual period (LMP). Later in pregnancy, as part of routine prenatal care, the EDD may be adjusted based on more objective findings such as fetal measurements.

Accuracy of the EDD is crucial because it is then used to determine when the pregnancy is preterm, term, late-term, and postterm. This date will impact medical intervention and care. If the term dates of a pregnancy are accurate, it is unlikely that a pregnancy will go past 42 weeks' gestation before the attempts to induce labor have been made.

Postterm pregnancy is a complication primarily because of the risks to the fetus caused by the aging placenta (placental insufficiency). The nurse will monitor for and address these possible intrapartum findings:

Intrapartum Findings

  • Amniotic fluid volume decreases (oligohydramnios), making cord compression more likely. The newborn will likely have no lanugo or vernix, and instead the skin will be dry and cracked.
  • Oxygen and nutrient transmission to the fetus and waste removal from the fetus are reduced, making late growth restriction visually apparent at birth (large head and thin body) and will impact the fetus' overall health.
  • Tolerance to uterine contractions is thereby reduced and intrapartum fetal distress is much more likely.
  • The fetal heart rate pattern may reflect these effects with late decelerations and decreased variability.
  • Higher risk for meconium aspiration syndrome, in which meconium in the amniotic fluid is aspirated before or during birth, causing respiratory distress in the newborn.
  • Macrosomia: The longer the pregnancy continues, the larger the fetus may grow. The intrapartum risks associated with macrosomia are related to:
    • dysfunctional labor — due to cephalopelvic disproportion
    • traumatic deliveries — physical trauma and/or need for forceps, vacuum extraction, episiotomy and/or cesarean delivery
    • postpartum bleeding — due to an overly distended and weakened uterus

Women with a prolonged pregnancy tend to be very physically and mentally fatigued. The nurse caring for these clients monitors maternal and fetal well-being and provides labor support for difficult labor and delivery. Labor induction is a likely medical treatment.

Section 13: Explore: Cord Prolapse

Tab: Cord Prolapse

Typically, the amniotic membranes rupture spontaneously, or are artificially ruptured, during the first stage of labor. Once the amniotic membranes are ruptured, a cord prolapse can occur if the fetal presenting part is not engaged and the umbilical cord propels out ahead of or under the fetal presenting part. If the cord slips alongside the fetal head or shoulders, this is called an occult prolapse.

Cord prolapse may or may not be visually apparent, but the fetal heart rate pattern will show decelerations and/or bradycardia. When the cord prolapses and is compressed by the presenting part, the umbilical blood vessels are also compressed, causing fetal hypoxia. It is crucial to recognize a prolapsed cord and relieve the pressure as soon as possible. Failure to do so within 5 minutes will likely result in central nervous system damage or the death of the fetus.

Tab: Risk

A particular risk for cord prolapse is when the fetal presenting part is not engaged in the pelvis. It can be because there is too much amniotic fluid (hydramnios), the fetus is in breech or transverse presentation, or the fetus is small. Also, when the presenting part is a body part rather than the head, there is more room for the cord to slip past these structures than when the fetal head is presenting and engaged in the pelvis.

Cord prolapse is an emergency requiring quick action by the healthcare team to reduce the amount of time that the cord is compressed and fetal hypoxia lasts before pressure can be relieved. Pressure is relieved by the nurse or healthcare provider by lifting the presenting part of the cord in the vagina using a sterile gloved hand, then assisting the mother into a knee-chest (all fours) position until delivery can be achieved. If the cervix is fully dilated, vacuum or forceps may be used if delivery can be achieved quickly. An emergency cesarean section may be needed with a neonatal resuscitation team present. The woman and her family will be anxious during this emergency, and the nurse should help them remain calm while working quickly to prepare for the surgery.

Section 15: Explore: Uterine Rupture and Uterine Inversion

Uterine Rupture

Uterine rupture occurs when the uterine wall tears partially or completely due to pressure that exceeds its ability to withstand the pressure.

Tab: Risk Factors

The common risk factors include:

  • Previous cesarean birth with a classic incision (vertical into the upper uterine segment) weakens the uterus across the scar, and the uterus can partially or completely separate under extreme pressure. Vaginal birth after cesarean (VBAC) will likely not be attempted for these women. If the scar only partially separates (called dehiscence), it may present few or no obvious cues that can be directly attributed to it and go unnoticed until seen visually.
  • Women who have had several pregnancies (high parity) have a thin uterine wall.
  • Blunt abdominal pressure (such as a car accident) may cause uterine rupture.
  • Excessively strong contractions, particularly from uterine stimulant medications (oxytocin, misoprostol) for induction or augmentation of labor can cause uterine rupture. These drugs must be administered with caution. Close monitoring for fetal distress and tachysystole, for which a tocolytic may be needed.
Tab: Signs

Signs of uterine rupture depend on how severe the rupture is and may not be detected before birth. Symptoms may mimic other complications or be masked by epidural analgesia. Pain is an obvious symptom to expect with a ruptured uterus, but in labor it can be hard to know that the uterine pain the woman is feeling is not typical uterine contraction pain.

  • Abdominal pain and tenderness make the woman feel like something "gave way" or "ripped."
  • Chest pain, pain between the shoulder blades, or pain when breathing in, may occur when blood causes irritation in these areas.
  • Hemorrhage and hypovolemic shock: These symptoms may or may not be present, depending on the severity of the rupture. An incomplete rupture may not show the typical signs of hypovolemic shock (tachycardia, tachypnea, falling blood pressure, pallor, cool and clammy skin, and anxiety). Hypotension is a late sign of hemorrhage, so normal blood pressure does not necessarily mean hemorrhaging is absent. Blood and blood products may be needed.
  • Fetal distress may be noted if the rupture disrupts placental perfusion enough, and the fetal heart rate pattern may show late decelerations, reduced variability, tachycardia, and bradycardia. Following a complete uterine rupture, the fetus often dies because of the severity of placental hypoperfusion, in which case an absence of fetal heart tones and movement will be noted.
  • Uterine contractions stop and the fetus may be able to be palpated outside of the uterus if the rupture is large enough.
Tab: Nursing Care

The nurse monitors those most at risk and takes steps to detect and prevent uterine rupture when possible. The focus is on maternal and fetal stabilization and preservation of perfusion. The uterine rupture will be surgically repaired if possible, otherwise a hysterectomy may be required.

Uterine Inversion

Another type of uterine intrapartum complication is a uterine inversion in which the uterus turns inside out, either partially or completely, typically during the third stage of labor. While uterine inversion is uncommon, it is an emergency and can be fatal. When the use of fundal pressure is improperly used during or after birth, it puts the woman at most risk for uterine inversion. Uterine inversion can also occur when too much pressure is used to tug on the umbilical cord when the placenta is detaching after birth.

Section 16: Explore: Premature Rupture of Membranes

Premature rupture of membranes (PROM) refers to the rupture of the amniotic membranes before true labor has started. When this occurs prior to 37 weeks gestation, regardless of the presence of uterine contractions, it is referred to as preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM). The risks for the fetus when PPROM occurs relate to it being a precursor to preterm labor and birth. If PROM occurs but labor does not start, the risks to the fetus will be related to the risk of intrauterine infection.

PROM (occurring before labor starts) and/or PPROM (occurring prior to <37 weeks gestation) occur most often in the presence of fetal abnormalities, fetal malpresentation, overdistention of the uterus, maternal stress or low socioeconomic status, maternal nutritional deficiencies, and diabetes.

Once the amniotic membranes have ruptured, the clock starts ticking. There is a risk for infection for both mother and fetus associated with prolonged rupture of membranes, and the risks increase as time passes without delivery. Therefore, delivery should occur within 24 hours for the best outcome.

Tab: Verification

Ruptured amniotic fluid is often easily mistaken for the feeling of urinary incontinence or vaginal discharge. To verify that membranes truly have ruptured, the fluid needs to be tested. When ruptured membranes at any gestation are suspected and need to be verified, sterile vaginal exams are avoided until a sample of fluid can be tested to avoid skewing the results. It is also best practice to minimize sterile vaginal exams, particularly in earlier gestation when labor is not suspected, to minimize the introduction of bacteria to the vaginal canal.

A sterile speculum is used to visualize the presence of fluid pooling near the cervix, and then look at a sample under a microscope. If the fluid looks like the shape of a fern leaf, it is considered a positive fern test, and amniotic fluid rupture is confirmed. The cervical dilation is also estimated visually during the speculum exam to help make a labor determination.

The fluid can also be tested with pH paper. This is called the nitrazine test. The test strip will turn to dark blue when amniotic fluid is present.

An amnisure test may also be used to diagnose the rupture of the membranes. This test detects placental alpha-microglobulin in the fluid and its presence is considered evidence of rupture of membranes.

Tab: Treatment

Once PROM is confirmed, the provider must consider other factors to determine how to proceed. The loss of amniotic fluid brings with it the risks associated with infection plus the risks of oligohydramnios (umbilical cord compression, reduced lung volume, and deformities resulting from compression).

In the case of PPROM, the earlier gestation means the amniotic membranes may form a seal that stops further leaking and re-forms the protective barrier that keeps infection away, and amniotic fluid volume may replace itself. However, if the membranes continue to leak, the amniotic fluid volume will dwindle and the longer the pregnancy continues without the protective barrier of the amniotic sac and fluid, infection and oligohydramnios are becoming higher risks.

The treatment will therefore depend on how far along the pregnancy is, if contractions and/or labor (cervical change) are present, and how long the membranes have been ruptured.

Section 17: Explore: Impact of Premature Rupture of Membranes

Consider the following scenarios and review the impact if the pregnancy is term or preterm for each.

Transcript: Premature Rupture of Membranes

Scenario 1: A woman's membranes ruptured 2 hours ago, there are no signs of labor, everything is normal (no complications, no signs of infection, mother and fetus are stable).

  • If the pregnancy is term (>37+ weeks), the likelihood of a good outcome for the mother and fetus is high, and this may even be a sign of early labor. The provider will likely use medication to ripen the cervix, then induce labor, administer an antibiotic if more than 24 hours since rupture of membranes (ROM) occurred, and focus on minimizing infection risk. If induction is unsuccessful, cesarean delivery is likely because the risks increase as time goes by.
  • If the pregnancy is preterm (<36 weeks, PPROM), the risks at this point are related to delivering a newborn that is preterm and the priority is buying the fetus more time in utero until it is no longer safe to do so. The provider will likely administer lung-maturing steroids and antibiotics, monitor amniotic fluid volume, infection risk, and fetal status (ongoing nonstress tests, biophysical profiles, etc.). The provider may also place a cerclage suture that encircles the cervix to prevent cervical dilation and preterm birth.

Scenario 2: A woman's membranes have been leaking for an unknown time period because it was difficult to recognize the leaking as amniotic fluid and not urine. There are no signs of labor, no complications, but she has a fever, and the leaking fluid has an odor.

  • If the pregnancy is term (>37+ weeks), the risks are related to infection and oligohydramnios rather than fetal prematurity and the priority is minimizing infection-related complications. A cesarean birth is likely.
  • If the pregnancy is preterm (<36 weeks, PPROM), the risks are related to prematurity, infection, and oligohydramnios. The provider will have to weigh the maternal and fetal risks associated with buying more time for the fetus in utero versus the risks of delivering early.

When hospitalized care is needed following premature rupture of membranes (PROM) or preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM), the woman may find herself on bed rest and away from home for an extended period of time, during which medications, support, bed rest and constant monitoring for signs of maternal and fetal distress are provided. If she is stabilized and her care can be managed from home, the nurse will provide the following instructions to prevent further complications.

  • Abstain from all forms of sexual activity and vaginal penetration because it can increase infection risk and stimulate contractions. Breast stimulation may also stimulate contractions by releasing oxytocin.
  • Strictly adhere to prescribed activity restrictions.
  • Monitor for signs of infection and notify the healthcare provider of any temperature >37.8°C (100°F), uterine contractions, or vaginal drainage with a foul odor.

Section 19: Explore: Intrapartum Uterine Infection

Premature rupture of membranes (PROM) can lead to intrapartum uterine infection, and in return, an intrapartum uterine infection can cause PROM.

The longer the barrier surrounding and protecting the fetus from the outside world (and the organisms in the mother's vagina, anus, or urinary tract) is ruptured, the longer these organisms can proliferate and cause infection and inflammation. Once the amniotic membranes have been ruptured for more than 24 hours, the risks to both the mother and fetus increase more and more.

The clock begins ticking once the amniotic fluid has leaked. When it occurs, the nurse must monitor the increasing risk for uterine infection. The woman's temperature and heart rate will be monitored regularly because the increases may be the signs of infection. The woman's abdomen may become tender to touch. Foul-smelling vaginal discharge or leaking amniotic fluid may be present.

Tab: Causes of Uterine Infection

The organisms that cause a uterine infection known as triple I can lead to PROM by weakening the amniotic membrane which allows it to rupture.

  • Triple I is a term used to describe an intrauterine infection and it has specific diagnostic criteria and treatment protocols. Another term used for intrauterine infection includes chorioamnionitis (an infection of the chorion, the amniotic membrane or sac, and the amniotic fluid).
  • The nurse needs to be familiar with the diagnostic criteria for triple I for monitoring and the associated treatment protocols, order sets, and nursing implications. These criteria have specific parameters related to maternal temperature and timing of fever, fetal tachycardia baseline rate and timing, maternal white blood cell count level and associated factors, original and characteristics of cervical fluid drainage, and amniotic fluid testing results. All of the parameters within each of these criteria must be met as specified to be confirmed triple I.

It is best that the fetus is delivered before an infection sets in and increases the risk for additional issues for both the mother and fetus even long after delivery. Amniocentesis can diagnose chorioamnionitis if the membranes are not ruptured.

Tab: Minimizing Uterine Infection Risk

To minimize uterine infection risk, particularly after rupture of membranes (ROM):

  • The healthcare team will limit the amount of sterile vaginal exams used to check cervical dilation and effacement, and instead, recognize other cues to estimate labor progress. Healthcare providers can also limit the use of internal monitoring electrodes, particularly the spiral fetal scalp electrodes that penetrate the scalp, which also introduce bacteria into the uterine environment. Each vaginal exam and intrauterine intervention is an opportunity to introduce organisms into the vaginal canal, the uterus, and to the fetus.
  • Healthcare providers help prevent intrauterine infection by allowing the amniotic membranes to remain intact for as long as possible. Once ROM occurs, delivery needs to occur within 24 hours regardless of the presence of a known infection. As that deadline approaches, or after it has lapsed, antibiotic therapy will be prescribed. In the case of preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM), labor may be delayed to buy the fetus more time in utero as well by stopping an infection that could or did cause PPROM.
  • Throughout labor and delivery, the healthcare team must use excellent universal precautions and use of personal protective equipment (PPE) to minimize the spread of organisms from one body location to another. The nurse should keep the pads underneath the women as dry as possible and keep the perineum free of secretions by wiping them from front to back.
  • After delivery, the newborn may require additional testing and perhaps antibiotics to prevent or treat the infectious agent that reached them in utero or during delivery.

Childbirth preparation prior to labor helps the client learn self-help measures and what to expect during pregnancy and the birthing process. Enhanced knowledge leads to heightened confidence, increased pain tolerance, and improved satisfaction with the birth experience.

  • Bradley Childbirth Education teaches abdominal breathing to increase relaxation and emphasizes the avoidance of medications and interventions.
  • Lamaze Childbirth Education teaches concentration and relaxation to decrease contraction pain.

7. Explore: Childbirth Locations

Several delivery location options are available depending on whether the pregnancy is normal or complicated and the personal preference of the client and family.

Let's explore the benefits and disadvantages of each option.

Accordion — Hospitals Typically, childbirth occurs in the hospital setting with an obstetrician, family practice physician, or nurse midwife, and at least one registered nurse caring for the client and family.

Benefits:

  • Hospitals, depending on the acuity level, are equipped and prepared to handle any situation that may arise in the labor, delivery, and postpartum period. These include anesthesia and pain-control options, surgical suites in the event a cesarean delivery is needed, and special care units for the newborn in the case of medical complications.
  • Once admitted, some inpatient maternity units are designed to keep the client and family in one room throughout labor, delivery, and the postpartum period until discharge. These are often called LDRPs (Labor, Delivery, Recovery, and Postpartum).
  • In other hospitals, the labor, delivery, and recovery occur in one unit and the client is then transferred to the postpartum unit for the remainder of their hospitalization.

Disadvantages:

  • Hospital birth may increase stress and anxiety due to the highly clinical environment.
  • Clients may have fewer options, including freedom of movement, hydrotherapy, and birthing positions.
  • Some facilities limit the number of support persons who may be present during the labor and delivery process.

Accordion — Birthing Centers Birthing centers are childbirth care facilities that may operate independently or in affiliation with an acute care hospital, providing a more home-like environment. They are most appropriate for low-risk, normal pregnancies.

Benefits:

  • A home-like environment with a relaxed focus on the client and family during the childbirth process.
  • Client rooms are typically set up for labor, delivery, recovery, and postpartum (LDRPs).

Disadvantage:

  • There is often a lack of ability to provide more advanced care should an unanticipated need arise.

Accordion — Home Home deliveries were the only option until the shift toward hospital deliveries and the medical practice of obstetrics as a specialty developed. Even today, some clients still choose to deliver in the home environment. The labor and birth process is often overseen by a midwife.

Benefits:

  • The home environment allows clients more control over childbirth, including comfort measures that may be unachievable outside the home.

Disadvantages:

  • Healthcare providers do not typically attend deliveries at home. A client desiring a home delivery will likely seek care from a trained midwife. Limited technology is used in home births. Therefore, the ability to anticipate complications may be hindered.
  • In the event of complications that cannot be managed at home, the client must be transported to a hospital for childbirth and/or recovery.

8. Explore: Intrapartum Triage and Assessment

In the hospital's labor and delivery unit, nurses encounter pregnant women who will typically undergo a period of triage to determine whether admission is necessary. Women who are medically stable and are over 20 weeks gestation will usually be brought to the labor and delivery unit, rather than remaining in the emergency department or being admitted to a medical-surgical unit, even if the reason for seeking treatment is unrelated to pregnancy.

The labor and delivery nurse will assess maternal and fetal well-being, signs of potential complications, obstetric history, current labor symptoms, and psychosocial factors including the woman's labor support system. These assessment components include the considerations below.

Tab — Fetal Heart Rate and Pattern

The primary and most reliable way to assess the status of a fetus is to monitor the fetal heart rate and pattern. This can be done using continuous electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) or intermittently using an electronic doppler. Assessing the fetal heart rate and pattern is a priority during admission. The normal range for fetal heart rate is between 110 and 160 bpm. The pattern may include accelerations and early decelerations. However, variable and late decelerations are ominous and should be evaluated further.

The frequency of fetal monitoring during labor is determined by the client's level of risk. During active labor, the nurse should plan to monitor at least every 30 minutes.

When assessing the fetal heart rate and pattern, consider these questions:

  • What is the baseline fetal heart rate (FHR)?
  • What is the FHR variability?
  • Are accelerations in the FHR present?
  • Does the FHR meet reactive non-stress test criteria?
  • Are any decelerations present? If yes, what type of deceleration is occurring? Are interventions needed?
NOTE: Nurses who care for women during childbirth are legally responsible for correctly interpreting the fetal heart rate pattern, implementing appropriate nursing actions based on that pattern, and documenting the outcome of those actions.

(Image caption: An electronic fetal monitor showing a pregnant woman's fetal heart rate and pattern, as well as her contraction pattern in the report.)

Tab — Contraction Pattern

External electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) does not measure uterine tone unless an internal monitor is in place, so the nurse palpates the abdomen to assess uterine contraction strength manually. The palpable tone (strength) of contractions is described as mild, moderate, or strong.

When palpating the fundus, consider these questions:

  • What is the intensity (strength) of the contraction?
  • What is the intensity of the resting period after the contraction ends? The tone of the resting period should feel soft, like the upper lip.

Frequency, duration, onset, and regularity of contractions are assessed as part of the intrapartum admission nursing assessment. When assessing contractions, the nurse should consider these questions:

  • What is the frequency of the contractions? Frequency is the time between the beginning of one contraction and the beginning of the next contraction.
  • What is the duration of the contractions? Duration is the time from the beginning of the contraction to the end of the same contraction.

While evaluating contractions, the nurse should ask the client:

  • What is the pain level of the contractions?
  • What is a tolerable level of pain for the client?

Image — Palpable Tone of Contractions (transcript):

  • Mild contraction tone feels soft, like the tip of the nose.
  • Moderate contraction tone feels like the tightness of the chin.
  • Strong contraction tone feels like the forehead.
Tab — Vital Signs

Vital signs should be monitored frequently during labor. The nurse should plan on hourly assessments for blood pressure, pulse, and respirations unless complications or analgesia warrant increased frequency. Temperature will be monitored every 4 hours until the membranes are ruptured, then hourly. Abnormal parameters compared to the client's baseline should be reported to the healthcare provider and may change the frequency of evaluations.

Pain level should be monitored continuously as increased pain can elevate both blood pressure and pulse rate.

Tab — Amniotic Membranes

Amniotic membranes are assessed before the initial cervical examination to accurately determine if amniotic fluid is leaking. Yellow nitrazine paper may be used to detect amniotic fluid, which has a basic pH.

  • A blue color change is indicative of the presence of amniotic fluid.
  • No color change indicates lack of amniotic fluid (so no membrane rupture).

Ask the client these questions:

  • Has your water broken?
  • What time did it break?
  • What did the fluid look like?
  • About how much fluid did you lose? Was it a big gush or a small trickle?

If the amniotic membranes are ruptured, assess and document the time of rupture, amount, color, and odor of fluid (think of TACO).

  • T = Time that the amniotic membrane ruptured
  • A = Amount of amniotic fluid that came out
  • C = Color of the leaked amniotic fluid
    • clear—normal
    • greenish—meconium
    • yellow/cloudy—potential infection, called chorioamnionitis
  • O = Odor of the leaked amniotic fluid
    • odorless—normal
    • foul/strong odor—potential infection

Tab — Prenatal Care

The nurse reviews the prenatal record from the healthcare provider which includes important information, assessments, and laboratory testing completed throughout the pregnancy.

Consider these questions when examining the prenatal record:

  • When did prenatal care begin?
  • Has the client been admitted to the hospital during this pregnancy?
  • What is the estimated date of delivery?
  • What is the client's pregnancy history? (GTPAL)
  • Is there a history of pregnancy or labor complications?

Tab — Laboratory Tests

The client will be asked to provide a clean-catch urine specimen. Urine is tested for the presence of protein, ketones, glucose, WBC, and blood which may require intervention during labor.

Blood will be collected for several laboratory tests: blood type, Rh factor, and CBC. Other blood tests may be prescribed based on client history and presentation.

Tab — Other Assessments

The nurse should perform other assessments during the admission process, including:

  • allergies to medications and latex
  • history with problems with anesthesia
  • date/time of last food and fluid intake
  • current medications, including over-the-counter (OTC) and herbal supplement use
  • history of tobacco, alcohol, or illicit substance use
  • presence of a birth plan
  • preparation for childbirth
  • preferences or special requests
  • specific cultural needs
  • focused physical examination
Tab — Cervical Effacement and Dilation

Cervical dilation is a key nursing assessment for determining if the woman is in true labor.

If the facility's policy permits, a sterile vaginal exam (SVE) is often performed by the nurse as part of the intrapartum admission and ongoing assessment, which is to determine:

  • cervical dilation (the widening of the cervical opening)
  • cervical effacement (shortening and thinning of the cervix)
  • fetal station (the relationship of the presenting part to the ischial spines in the maternal pelvis)

A baseline sterile cervical exam, in conjunction with other assessment factors, can indicate labor progression.

NOTE: Vaginal exams should be performed only when necessary due to the increased risk of infection.

11. Explore: Nursing Care for the Labor Client

Once a woman is admitted to the labor and delivery unit, nursing care consists of additional considerations and interventions, including pain management, elimination, activity, nutrition, and metabolic pattern.

Tab — Pain Management

Childbirth is painful. The intrapartum nurse is an expert at pain management, with the extra skill set of managing pain while monitoring its impact on fetal well-being. The nurse works with the client to find what best manages pain using both pharmacologic and nonpharmacologic strategies.

Accordion — Anxiety and Fear An essential element of pain management in labor is to manage the client's anxiety and fear. This can be done through:

  • respecting the client's preferences and wishes
  • involving the client in decision making
  • keeping the client informed about what is happening, how things are progressing, and how the baby is doing

Accordion — Pharmacologic Pain Management Many pharmacologic pain management options are available to the laboring client, including systemic medications (meperidine, fentanyl, butorphanol, nalbuphine) and regional pain management techniques.

Special Considerations:

  • Medications taken by the client are likely to also affect the fetus. Example: Decreased fetal heart rate variability may occur after administration of an opioid analgesic.
  • Medications may affect the progress and length of labor. Example: Regional anesthesia may slow progress during the second stage of labor by diminishing the client's urge to push.
  • Medications that are used in labor can make clients drowsy, so caution should be exercised with ambulation to prevent falls.
  • Pregnancy complications may limit pharmacologic pain management choices. Example: Preeclampsia may result in a low platelet count that could prohibit the use of regional anesthesia.
  • Medications may have effects on pregnancy that do not occur in the nonpregnant client. Example: High levels of natural endorphins may modify pain perception and reduce the requirement for analgesia.
  • Clients who abuse alcohol or illicit substances may have fewer safe choices to manage labor pain. Example: Recent alcohol use increases the respiratory effects of opioid anagesics.

Accordion — Nonpharmacologic Pain Management Many nonpharmacologic pain management options are available to the laboring client who does not prefer pharmacologic intervention. In some cases, these strategies are necessary until pharmacologic intervention can be initiated or when rapid labor prevents the use of pharmacologic intervention. Nonpharmacologic strategies, which do not slow labor and have no maternal or fetal side effects include:

  • position changes
  • hydrotherapy (shower, tub, or whirlpool bath)
  • breathing techniques (slow paced, modified paced, pattern paced)
  • relaxation techniques (comfort, anxiety reducing strategies, progressive relaxation, neuromuscular disassociation)
  • cutaneous stimulation (massage, counterpressure, warm/cool compresses)
  • mental stimulation (imagery, focal point)

Accordion — Regional Pain Management Regional pain management provides pain control without the loss of consciousness. A main advantage is that the client can participate in the birth while maintaining good pain control. Pressure and some discomfort may be felt, but these sensations are greatly reduced. Fetal response may be affected by maternal response to administration but is typically not impacted directly by the medication.

  • Epidural: An intrathecal anesthetic agent and opioid are used to accomplish pain control during labor. A major adverse effect is maternal hypotension. Rapid IV bolus infusion of lactated Ringer's or normal saline before initiation of the block will offset vasodilation. Treatment includes IV fluid bolus, supplemental oxygen, and repositioning. Phenylephrine or ephedrine may be needed to promote vasoconstriction.
  • Combined spinal-epidural: Subarachnoid injection of opioids followed by continuous pain relief through an epidural catheter.
  • Local anesthesia: Small area of anesthesia that is used only for birth (perineal or pudendal).

Accordion — Nitrous Oxide A combination of 50% nitrous oxide and 50% oxygen can be used for labor pain management. Side effects include nausea, vomiting, and dizziness. The gas mixture is self-administered by the client during normal breathing activity.

Tab — Elimination

A full bladder competes for space with the contracting uterus in the abdominal cavity, which may cause damage to the bladder as well as impede fetal descent and labor progress. Therefore, it is important to assess for bladder fullness frequently and keep the client's bladder empty during labor.

Indwelling urinary catheterization may be ordered if ambulation to the bathroom is contraindicated, for example, when the woman has an epidural. Bladder distention and urinary retention are common side effects of epidural analgesia.

Diarrhea in early labor is possible. Bowel movements in the pushing phase of birth are very common due to bearing down.

Tab — Activity

Ambulation and/or physical movement in labor are crucial for labor progression. Movement promotes uterine contractions and the opening of the pelvis to encourage fetal descent. Additionally, the client may be more comfortable when mobile. The healthcare provider will determine the appropriateness of ambulation and activity in labor depending on various risk factors.

  • Ambulation may be contraindicated after the administration of systemic or regional analgesics due to the risk of falling and the need for continuous fetal monitoring.
  • If the membranes are ruptured and the presenting part is not fully engaged in the pelvis, the cord may prolapse out of the uterus during ambulation.
  • Movement during labor can take many forms. Clients may ambulate in the hall with a support person, stopping during contractions. In the room, the client may sit in a rocking chair, on the bedside, or on a yoga ball. Additionally, the client may take a shower or tub bath, if available. As labor advances, it may become too uncomfortable for the woman to ambulate; however, movement and position change often help with pain control.
  • When bed rest is warranted, the client should frequently change positions to encourage labor progression, fetal descent, and maternal comfort.
  • When fetal distress occurs, the nurse will assist the woman to a lateral (side-lying) position to promote uteroplacental circulation until the fetus recovers and the cause of distress can be treated.
Tab — Nutrition and Metabolic Pattern

Gastrointestinal (GI) motility and absorption slow during labor and delivery, which means that food eaten early in labor will remain in the GI tract and can cause nausea and vomiting. Solid food during active labor is usually withheld and the woman will likely lose her appetite. It is important for the nurse to assess when the client last had food and fluids in case cesarean birth is needed.

Nausea and vomiting, particularly as labor progresses, are very common.

  • Clear liquids (water and ice) are often provided for comfort.
  • Intravenous hydration with lactated Ringer's or normal saline is administered during labor to prevent dehydration.
  • Ondansetron IV may be used to prevent or treat nausea. Metoclopramide IV may be used to treat the symptoms of slowed peristalsis.

14. Explore: Labor Augmentation and Induction

Induction of labor is the chemical or mechanical stimulation of uterine contractions before the spontaneous onset of labor. Augmentation of labor is the stimulation of uterine contractions after labor has started but when progression is unsatisfactory. A favorable cervix is the most important criterion for successful labor induction and augmentation.

Tab — Indications/Contraindications

The healthcare provider may believe that it is best to induce labor rather than wait for it to occur spontaneously. Induction may be indicated for either maternal or fetal reasons, including:

  • Maternal factors:
    • premature rupture of membranes
    • preeclampsia/eclampsia
    • history of fetal stillbirth or demise
    • diabetes mellitus
    • history of precipitous (less than 3 hours) labor
    • chorioamnionitis
    • oligohydramnios
    • post-term gestation
  • Fetal factors:
    • intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR)
    • non-reassuring fetal heart rate and pattern
    • fetal complications, such as erythroblastosis fetalis and hydrops
    • significant congenital anomalies

However, induction may be contraindicated in some client conditions, including:

  • cord prolapse
  • placenta previa
  • transverse fetal lie
  • history of horizontal (classical) uterine incision
  • active herpes
  • significant acute fetal distress
Tab — Methods

Induction methods include one or a combination of amniotomy, misoprostol, prostaglandin, and oxytocin.

Accordion — Mechanical Stimulation Amniotomy is the artificial rupture of amniotic membranes by the healthcare provider, which can often cause uterine contractions to begin spontaneously. An accurate documentation of fetal gestational age should be considered.

Other means of mechanical stimulation to induce labor include:

  • breast stimulation
  • transcervical balloon catheter
  • membrane stripping
  • hydroscopic inserts (Laminaria)

(Image caption: Amniotic sac)

Accordion — Misoprostol Misoprostol (synthetic prostaglandin E₁) is traditionally used to prevent gastric ulcers but has been found to cause cervical ripening (soften) and contractions. Its use in pregnancy is currently considered off-label. Misoprostol should not be given to clients who have had a previous cesarean birth or major uterine surgery.

Misoprostol is available in 100 and 200 mcg tablets and the usual dose for induction of labor is 25 or 50 mcg inserted high in the vagina. An oral dose of 100 mcg is also effective. After vaginal insertion, the client should lie recumbent for at least 30 minutes.

A major adverse effect of misprostol is uterine tachysystole (hyperstimulation). When used to induce labor, continuous external fetal monitoring is required as well as quick access to cesarean birth if needed.

(Image caption: Misoprostal is inserted in the posterior vagina)

Accordion — Dinoprostone Dinoprostone (prostaglandin E₂) is one of the most commonly used medications that causes cervical ripening. It can be administered as an intravaginal or intracervical gel or a time-released vaginal insert. The client must remain recumbent for up to 2 hours after administration. The nurse must monitor for uterine tachysystole, though the incidence is less than with misoprostol.

Dinoprostone is often administered at night before labor induction with oxytocin in the morning. After dinoprostone has been inserted vaginally, the woman may experience uterine cramping and contractions.

(Image caption: Dinoprostone is placed near the cervix)

Accordion — Oxytocin Oxytocin is the most common drug given for induction and augmentation of labor. It is a very powerful medication requiring close maternal and continuous fetal monitoring.

Oxytocin is administered intravenously to cause uterine contractions once the cervix is favorable or when contractions need to be stronger or more regular to dilate the cervix. It is also used after birth to control postpartum hemorrhage.

  • Added to an intravenous (IV) isotonic solution, oxytocin is administered as a secondary infusion using an IV pump. Oxytocin is inserted into the primary IV line in the closest port to the venipuncture site.
  • Oxytocin is started slowly (1-2 mu/minute) and increased every 15-30 minutes until active labor ensues. Once active labor is achieved, the rate can be reduced.
  • Uterine activity and fetal heart rate pattern are monitored closely. If contractions become too strong and/or last too long, fetal distress may occur.

The nurse must closely monitor for adverse effects of oxytocin administration including:

  • blood pressure elevation (30% above baseline)
  • water intoxication
  • decreased urine output
  • uterine tachysystole
  • fetal hypoxia
  • cardiac arrhythmias
  • nausea and vomiting

(Image caption: Oxytocin (pitocin) is administered intravenously)

Tab — Bishop Score

Bishop Score is a rating system used to evaluate the readiness of the cervix for labor induction. There are 5 components of the assessment, with each receiving a score of 0 to 3. The higher the score, the more likelihood of a successful labor induction.

  • cervical dilation
  • cervical effacement
  • fetal station
  • cervical consistency
  • cervical position

15. Explore: Labor Augmentation and Induction — Nursing Care

Tab — Before Administration

Before administration of labor-inducing agents, the nurse should:

  • obtain baseline maternal assessment information to ensure safe administration and to monitor the medication's effects.
  • obtain baseline fetal assessment information, including position, presentation, and fetal heart rate and pattern.
  • determine the presence of contractions, including frequency, duration, and intensity.
Tab — Throughout Administration

As the induction or augmentation progresses, the nurse is continually assessing fetal well-being, uterine contractions, and labor progression. These assessments evaluate the client and fetal response to medications and determine whether induction or augmentation may continue.

Accordion — Fetal Assessment The nurse continuously monitors fetal well-being and tolerance by interpreting the fetal heart rate pattern and fetal response to maternal movements and increasing uterine contractions. Contractions should not occur more frequently than every 2 minutes to ensure adequate fetal oxygenation. Frequent uterine contractions may cause fetal hypoxia and distress.

Accordion — Maternal Assessment The nurse assesses the client's vital signs. The medications used for induction or augmentation may cause hypertension and tachycardia.

The nurse also monitors uterine contraction pattern (frequency and duration) and uterine intensity before, during, and after contractions. Soft uterine tone in between contractions is essential to fetal recovery and adequate oxygenation.

NOTE: A hypertonic uterus can rupture, which is a very dangerous situation for both mother and fetus. The medication should be stopped if the uterine resting tone is increasing, contractions are more frequent than every 2 minutes, or if the fetal heart rate shows distress.

Section 1: Introduction to Operative Delivery

The process of labor and delivery typically results in good outcomes for the mother and newborn. However, it is a very complex process consisting of multiple factors that impact outcomes. Sometimes, medical intervention is necessary. These factors and interventions will be discussed.

Section 2: Prepare: Operative Delivery

Question: The ______ and ______ of the woman's pelvis determine the ease with which the fetal head can pass through for vaginal delivery to occur.

Answer: size, shape (Correct!)

Feedback: The size and shape of the woman's pelvis determine the ease with which the fetal head can pass through for vaginal delivery to occur. Four different types of the pelvis can be assessed to determine if the pelvis is an adequate passageway: gynecoid, android, anthropoid, and platypelloid. The gynecoid pelvis is the most common and favorable for vaginal birth.

Section 3: Explore: Risks for Operative Delivery — The Five Ps of the Labor and Delivery Process

Labor is a diverse and individualized process with interacting, complex factors. Let's review the five factors of the labor and delivery process that must act in harmony with one another to produce positive outcomes: passageway, passenger, powers, position, and psyche.

So, what happens when one or more of the five Ps is dysfunctional and impacts the others? Labor may or may not progress safely and negative outcomes can result. There are several medical interventions that may be used to overcome dysfunctional labor and delivery.

We will discuss several: amniotomy, episiotomy, forceps, vacuum extraction, and cesarean section.

Section 5: Explore: Medical Intervention — Amniotomy

The obstetrician or certified nurse-midwife may determine that the amniotic membranes need to be ruptured artificially for various reasons. This procedure is called an amniotomy or an artificial rupture of membranes (AROM). The amniotic membranes (or amniotic sac) in which the amniotic fluid is encapsulated can be ruptured and drained using a sterile hook-shaped instrument that looks like a crochet needle.

One indication for an amniotomy is to accelerate labor when it has slowed and is no longer progressing (powers). Allowing the amniotic fluid to escape often accelerates uterine contractions and allows the fetus to descend into the pelvis. In addition, once the amniotic membranes are ruptured, an internal electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) probe can be placed into the uterus vaginally in situations where more accurate monitoring of the fetal heart rate or uterine contractions is necessary.

Nursing Implications

Tab: Fetal Heart Rate

The nurse closely monitors the fetal heart rate pattern before, during, and after the amniotomy. The gush of fluid can cause the umbilical cord to rush out ahead of the presenting part (cord prolapse) and become compressed, causing fetal hypoxia. This situation will likely necessitate an emergency delivery by cesarean section if fetal distress cannot be resolved quickly by relieving pressure on the cord in the vaginal canal.

Tab: Amniotic Fluid (Amount & Color)

The nurse notes the amount and color of the amniotic fluid. It should be clear, pale, watery, free of strong odor, and be around 1000 mL at the time of delivery. Each of these characteristics speaks directly to the health of the pregnancy, labor, and fetus.

A dark green or brown color indicates the presence of meconium or fetal bowel movement, which may be a sign of fetal distress during labor. Attempts to ensure that the fetus did not inhale any of the meconium-stained fluid during labor or delivery will be made and may require respiratory support.

Too much fluid (polyhydramnios) is associated with congenital abnormalities and too little amniotic fluid (oligohydramnios) is associated with kidney disorders of the fetus.

Fluid that is thick, has a foul odor, or is yellowish may indicate a uterine infection. Reddish fluid may indicate that the placenta is not intact or has begun to separate from the uterus, causing bleeding and fetal hypoxia.

Once the amniotomy has been performed, it is best if delivery occurs within 24 hours to avoid infection. After 24 hours, this situation is referred to as prolonged rupture of membranes (PROM).

Transcript: Amniotic Fluid

Normal amniotic fluid should be clear, pale, watery, free of strong odor. When the fluid appears light green, green, light brown, brown, or black, it indicates there is a risk for the well-being of the fetus, and medical intervention is needed.

Section 7: Explore: Forceps, Vacuum Extraction, and Episiotomy

Some delivery interventions can overcome dysfunctional Five Ps (passageway, passenger, powers, position, and psyche). For example, the interventions may effectively address if the fetus does not descend enough due to its position (passenger), the shape of the woman's pelvis is not ideal or the woman's perineum is not stretching enough to accommodate the fetus (pelvis/passageway), the contractions are weak (powers), or the woman is too tired to push effectively (psyche).

Tab: Forceps Delivery
  • Forceps look like spoons with curved handles and are placed on either side of the fetal head to aid in pulling the fetus out when labor has reached the point of the membranes being ruptured and the cervix being fully dilated, but delivery has stopped for some reason.
  • They are typically not used unless medically necessary, such as when the fetus is in distress and delivery needs to happen fast. Fetal heart tones are monitored closely.
  • After delivery, the newborn will likely have bruising and edema on either side of the face, and forceps marks can usually be seen.
Tab: Vacuum Extraction
  • Instead of using forceps, a vacuum extraction delivery may be used. A vacuum cup is attached to the fetal head and negative pressure is used to then gently pull the fetus out.
  • The fetus must be in vertex position, the membranes must be ruptured, and the cervix must be fully dilated for this method to be used. Again, fetal heart tones are monitored closely.
  • The newborn will often have a caput succedaneum (bump of fluid), a red ring, and edema on the head in the area of vacuum placement which subsides within a short period of time.
Tab: Episiotomy
  • Anesthetic is injected into the tissue of the perineum between the vagina and the anus, then a surgical incision is made in that area to widen the perineal opening. The episiotomy is repaired with sutures under continued anesthesia following delivery.
  • It may be deemed necessary or preferable by the healthcare provider in order to:
    • Create one controlled, uniform cut and avoid vaginal or perineal lacerations which can be deeper or more difficult to repair.
    • Accommodate a large fetus (macrosomia) or abnormal fetal presentation.
    • Lessen the risks associated with prolonged pushing (lacerations and future bowel and bladder complications).
    • Prepare for the use of delivery devices, such as forceps or vacuum extraction.

Transcript: Episiotomy is a surgical incision of the perineum, usually performed by an obstetrician, to widen the perineal opening to aid a difficult delivery during childbirth.

Section 8: Explore: Medical Intervention — Cesarean Delivery

When various labor and delivery interventions are unsuccessful and it is in the best interest of the woman and/or the fetus, surgical intervention may be necessary. A cesarean section (C/S) is a surgical procedure in which the fetus is removed through the mother's abdomen for a variety of reasons related to any of the dysfunctional Five Ps (passageway, passenger, powers, position, and psyche). It may be planned, unplanned, or done in an emergency.

Section 9: Explore: Nursing Implications — Cesarean Delivery

When a cesarean section (C/S) is deemed necessary, the nurse plays a major role in preparing the woman for surgery, assisting the medical team during the surgery, and caring for the woman after the surgery.

Pre-Operative Nursing Implications
  • The C/S may be planned (scheduled), unplanned, or emergent. The nursing implications depend somewhat on this factor, but the nurse will implement the provider's pre-operative C/S orders to ensure maternal and fetal readiness for surgery.
  • These will include the following: laboratory specimen collection and assessment of pre-operative hemoglobin, hematocrit, platelets, white blood cell count, and blood typing and screening; intravenous line placement followed by a bolus infusion of fluid and a prophylactic antibiotic; electronic fetal monitoring; placement of epidural or spinal-epidural catheter; and abdominal skin prep and shaving (if applicable).
  • A planned C/S may be scheduled when the fetus is breech or if the woman had a previous C/S and is a routine process for the intrapartum nurse.
  • An unplanned C/S may be necessary if vaginal delivery was attempted but is no longer an option (failure to progress, for example). If time is not a factor in terms of maternal or fetal well-being, the nurse will implement the usual pre-operative procedures.
  • An emergent C/S is necessary when maternal and/or fetal well-being is in immediate danger. This situation requires quick action by the entire healthcare team to prepare the mother for surgery safely but expeditiously.
Pre-Operative Teaching

For a planned C/S, pre-operative teaching will be like any planned surgical procedure in terms of how to prepare for the surgery the night before (skin prep, medications that are permitted, when to avoid food/liquids, etc.) and what to expect during and after the C/S in terms of delivery and care of her child, as well as information regarding her recovery.

Pain Management Options
  • Spinal (subarachnoid block) is frequently utilized for scheduled C/S.
  • General anesthesia is recommended for women with any noted contraindications for spinal anesthesia or in the case of an emergency when time and fetal well-being are in jeopardy.
  • Epidural can be used in both vaginal deliveries and C/S if it is placed while the patient is in labor and then requires a C/S.
  • Adjunct intravenous (IV) sedation may be applied by a nurse anesthetist or anesthesia provider to complement the woman's relaxation of the prior provided anesthesia.

Section 10: Explore: Nursing Implications — Cesarean Delivery Continued

Nursing implications for cesarean delivery include intraoperative nursing, uterine incisions, and vaginal birth after a cesarean (VBAC).

Intraoperative Nursing Implications
  • Clients with spinal or epidural anesthesia will not be able to urinate or feel bladder fullness. The indwelling urinary catheter is usually placed after the anesthesia has taken effect so that it is more comfortable for the client. The urine in the collection bag will be monitored during the procedure to ensure there is no blood in the urine, which may indicate the bladder was accidentally injured or cut.
  • The nurse will "circulate" in the operating room, following operating room (OR) protocols, assisting with moving the woman from the bed to the surgery table, ensuring equipment and supplies are in place, documenting the procedure, and providing emotional support to the woman and her significant other throughout the procedure.
  • The nurse will continue fetal monitoring before and during the procedure. Care for the infant upon delivery in the operating room is often a multidisciplinary approach that includes a neonatologist, a respiratory therapist, and a neonatal nurse.
Uterine Incisions for Cesarean Birth

(Transcript: Low transverse "bikini" incision — a horizontal incision in the lower abdomen. Low vertical incision — a vertical incision in the lower abdomen. Classic incision — a vertical incision in the mid abdomen.)

  • Low Transverse ("Bikini")
    • Advantages: unlikely to rupture during a subsequent birth, makes vaginal birth after cesarean (VBAC) possible for subsequent pregnancy, less blood loss, easier to repair, less adhesion formation
    • Disadvantage: limited ability to extend laterally to enlarge the incision
  • Low Vertical
    • Advantage: can be extended upward to make a larger incision if needed
    • Disadvantages: slightly more likely to rupture during a subsequent birth, a tear may extend the incision downward into the cervix
  • Classic
    • Advantage: may be the only choice in certain situations (implantation of a placenta previa on the lower anterior uterine wall, presence of dense adhesions from previous surgery, transverse lie of a large fetus with the shoulder impacted in the mother's pelvis)
    • Disadvantages: most likely of the uterine incisions to rupture during a subsequent birth, eliminating VBAC as an option for birth of a subsequent infant
VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Cesarean)

A woman who has had a previous cesarean delivery may not be able to safely deliver vaginally with subsequent pregnancies, depending on the reason for and outcomes of the cesarean section.

  • The healthcare provider will determine if it is safe to attempt a vaginal birth after cesarean (VBAC) with a trial of labor after a cesarean (TOLAC).
  • A successful VBAC is more likely if the reason for the previous cesarean was an isolated incident (such as breech presentation).
  • A successful VBAC is less likely if the reason for the previous cesarean is likely to recur (such as dystocia or stalled/ineffective uterine contractions causing a failure to progress).

Section 13: Explore: Initial Post-operative Nursing Implications

  • After the newborn is delivered, as with vaginal delivery, intravenous (IV) oxytocin is administered to aid in uterine contractions to control postpartum bleeding. The difference with cesarean delivery is that the placenta and pooled blood are manually removed from the uterus.
  • IV prophylactic antibiotics will be administered post-operatively due to a risk for infection related to surgery but also for infections that may result from complications of labor.
  • The indwelling urinary catheter will remain in place until the woman can safely ambulate to the bathroom. The nurse will continue to monitor the urine for signs of blood or infection.
  • Those who have undergone surgery are at increased risk for thromboembolism, but that risk almost doubles for the woman undergoing cesarean delivery. Pneumatic compression devices (EPC cuffs) are used during and after surgery for deep vein thrombosis (DVT) prevention.
  • The care of the woman after she has returned to her room from surgery is similar to that after a vaginal delivery. The addition of interventions for a postoperative client during the recovery period typically includes more frequent vital signs, monitoring for return of sensation and movement, use of a pulse oximeter and supplemental oxygen, monitoring the surgical dressing and urinary output, and the use of a bedside cardiac monitor.

1. Introduction to Pain and Labor

Labor and childbirth can be painful processes. Many medical, pharmaceutical, and non-pharmaceutical interventions can increase comfort, reduce pain, and decrease possible fear or anxiety during labor and childbirth.

This module will review some of these interventions and methods.

2. Prepare: Pain and Labor

Question — Analgesics in Labor (select all that apply)

Which statements describe the actions of analgesics given in labor? Select all that apply.

  • Analgesics are contraindicated.
  • Analgesics may depress newborn respiratory efforts. ✅
  • Analgesics cross the placenta barrier. ✅
  • Analgesics slow labor.

(Student answer — Correct.)

Feedback/Rationale: Analgesics cross the placenta barrier and affect the fetus. They can cause depressed respiratory efforts at delivery. Therefore, it is important to administer them cautiously and at the right time before delivery. Nursing assessment will focus on respiratory depression, drowsiness, and vital signs.

Analgesics are not given to slow labor and they are not routinely contraindicated in labor.

3. Explore: Pain and the 5Ps

All pain is subjective, difficult to define and quantify, and very personal. The pain associated with childbirth is unique for many reasons.

There are five components of labor that each impact the other throughout the labor and childbirth process: powers, passage, passenger, position, and psyche (5 Ps). (Interactive hotspot image; full transcript below.)

The Five Ps of Birth

Passenger: The size of the fetus's head and shoulders (in relationship to the mother's body) may increase the difficulty of delivery—causing an increase in pain. Compound presentations (the fetus's hand on the head) and posterior presentation are a few examples where the fetus may cause increased pain.

Powers: The force of uterine contractions and the fetus's body being pressed into the pelvis and through the birth canal creates a unique kind of pain. First, it is intermittent rather than constant. Even during late labor, a woman may be relatively comfortable between contractions. Also, childbirth pain is of two types: visceral and somatic.

  • Visceral pain is a slow, deep, poorly localized pain that is often described as dull or aching. Visceral pain dominates during the first stage of labor as the uterus contracts and the cervix dilates. A woman may describe a little discomfort with contractions during early labor, while others describe it as the worst pain imaginable.
  • Somatic pain is a quick, sharp pain that can be precisely localized. Somatic pain is most prominent during the late phase of the first stage of labor and during the second stage of labor as the descending fetus puts direct pressure on maternal tissues.

Position: Standing or walking during labor helps work with gravity and allows the pelvis to open and the fetus to move down into the birth canal, reducing pain overall. Leaning over encourages the fetus to move forward, taking the pressure off a woman's back.

Psyche: Several psychosocial variables influence a woman's experience of childbirth pain, including culture, anxiety, fear, previous experiences with pain, support system, preparation for childbirth, and expectations about pain control. For many, knowing that childbirth pain is part of a normal process, not related to injury or illness, brings comfort and strength.

  • Many take comfort in knowing that labor pain will result in the birth of their child. This expectation and the care of her baby often increase labor pain tolerance. However, those for whom the arrival of a child is not a happy occasion may be very anxious and have more difficulty managing the psychological aspect of their labor pain.
  • The time component of childbirth pain impacts the psyche in unique ways as well. In contrast to non-labor-related pain, the pregnant woman knows childbirth pain will happen and uses several months to mentally prepare for labor and to learn anxiety reduction techniques that will help her manage labor pain.
  • Childbirth pain is self-limiting and has a foreseeable end. Although it may be intense, a woman can expect her labor to end in hours, rather than days, weeks, or months. Other kinds of pain may also be brief, but the baby's birth brings a rapid decrease in pain.

Passageway: Pain can instinctively lead a woman to assume different positions in labor to allow room for fetal descent through her pelvis.

4. Explore: Labor Pain Management - Opioid Analgesic Medications

Many women choose to use analgesic medications for pain management in childbirth. Opioid analgesics are the most common systemic, parenteral, pharmaceutical options used to manage childbirth pain.

The opioid analgesics to be administered are prescribed by the provider. These medications cause central nervous depression, which decreases the woman's perception of pain but causes sedation and respiratory depression as well. Because these medications cross the placenta and affect the fetus, causing depressed respiratory efforts of the neonate at delivery, it is important to administer them cautiously and at the right time in relation to impending delivery. If the fetus delivers close to the administration of the medication, the newborn's respiratory efforts may be decreased and may need medical intervention such as opioid antagonist administration.

The most common opioid analgesic medications used during labor and their nursing implications are:

Nalbuphine Hydrochloride (Nubain) or Butorphanol Tartrate (Stadol)

  • These medications are not given to opiate-dependent women.
  • These medications are often given just before an epidural for mild pain relief until the epidural begins working.
  • Respiratory depression in the newborn may occur if these medications are given too close to delivery (monitor maternal and fetal effects closely).

Meperidine Hydrochloride (Demerol)

  • This is a synthetic, morphine-like compound.
  • If given too close to delivery, respiratory depression in the newborn may occur (monitor maternal and fetal effects closely).

Nursing Implications

  • The nurse will monitor maternal vital signs and for signs of hypotension and respiratory depression.
  • The fetal heart rate (FHR) pattern is also monitored closely by the nurse following the administration of parenteral, opioid analgesics. These drugs may impact the fetus indirectly if they cause maternal hypotension, or directly impact the fetal central nervous system. These impacts will often be displayed in the form of decreased FHR variability on the electronic fetal monitor.
  • After delivery, nursing assessment of the newborn will focus on respiratory depression, circulation, and neurologic impairment (which will also impact the initial respiratory efforts).
  • The antidote to these medications is naloxone (Narcan). It is an opioid antagonist that may be needed for the newborn to reverse the effects of a narcotic-opioid analgesic given too close to delivery to prevent narcotic-induced respiratory depression in neonates.

5. Explore: Labor Pain Management - Anesthetic Medications

Regional anesthetic pain management is commonly used at various points in the childbirth process. Unlike opioid analgesics, anesthetics provide pain relief along with a measure of numbness without impacting the level of consciousness or respiratory effort.

Epidural Nerve Block

An epidural nerve block is a common pain control method that provides both analgesia and anesthesia for vaginal labor and delivery as well as cesarean delivery. The epidural space is the area outside the dura mater, between the dura and the spinal cord.

(Slideshow transcript — 4 slides)

An anesthesiologist or certified nurse-anesthetist (CRNA) injects a particular concentration of local anesthetic and opioid combination into a tiny epidural space in the lumbar area of the spine for substantial vaginal labor and delivery pain relief. Breakthrough pressure may still be felt, but it is usually minimal and helps the woman when it comes time to push.

If a cesarean delivery is planned or becomes necessary after a failed labor, the level of the epidural on the spine can be extended upward to provide full anesthesia. The provider administers a higher concentration of anesthetic, resulting in loss of motor and sensory function when complete numbing is needed for a surgical abdominal incision.

After the medication is injected into the spine, the remaining thin catheter protrudes out from the insertion site in the back. The medication will likely continue as an infusion on a patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) pump with parameters ordered by the provider. A dressing is placed on top of the insertion site and tubing. The tubing is removed after delivery.

Maternal vasodilation and hypotension often result from the nerve blocking effects of the epidural medication, most likely within the first 15 minutes of initial or bolus administration, but may occur within the first hour. This may then be reflected in prolonged decelerations in the fetal heart rate pattern as the perfusion to the placenta is reduced. To prevent hypotension, a bolus of intravenous fluid will be infused prior to epidural placement and blood pressure will be closely monitored during and following epidural placement.

Other Anesthetic Medications

General anesthesia use is uncommon and typically only when an emergency cesarean is needed and there is no time for an epidural or spinal block to be inserted or if the woman is not a candidate for them.

Additional regional and local anesthetics may be used by the delivery provider in vaginal deliveries to cause numbness, for example, when an episiotomy will be done, or a laceration needs to be repaired. Also, a pudendal block may be used to numb the perineum for delivery.

7. Explore: Nursing Implications - Anesthetic Medications

Nursing care related to an epidural focuses on monitoring maternal and fetal status before, during, and after epidural placement and medication administration.

Prior to and During Epidural Placement

The nurse will ask the woman to empty her bladder one last time, ensure a pre-epidural bolus of the intravenous solution has been infused, continuously monitor maternal blood pressure and fetal heart rate pattern, assist the woman to maintain correct positioning during the procedure, and provide reassurance as the pain continues despite the need to hold still.

After Epidural Placement

Maternal and Fetal Stability

  • Assist the woman to a comfortable position that maintains ideal maternal and fetal circulation; continue close monitoring of maternal blood pressure and fetal heart rate pattern. Maternal hypotension may occur following epidural administration, which can affect fetal perfusion.
  • Monitor the medication's effects on maternal pain, motor, and sensory sensations.
  • Placement of an indwelling urinary catheter may be necessary to keep the bladder empty.
  • Occasionally, cerebral spinal fluid (CSF) leaks out if the dura is punctured during injection of the epidural, causing a spinal headache.
  • Although maternal fever following epidural anesthesia is common, the reasoning is not clear. It does not necessarily indicate an infection, which can be ruled out by the absence of additional infection assessment cues.
  • Other common side effects of epidural anesthesia include pruritis, shivering, nausea, and vomiting.
  • The nurse monitors and maintains the epidural dressing and site to ensure it remains clean, dry, and intact.

Impacts on Labor

The relaxation that occurs following epidural pain relief can sometimes accelerate the labor progress and fetal descent without the sensations that may alert the patient and the nurse to impending delivery. For example:

  • sudden fetal descent into the pelvis
  • increasingly strong contractions
  • sudden nausea and vomiting
  • complete cervical dilation and effacement
  • the feeling of pressure and/or an urge to push

Impacts on Delivery

Epidural may affect the ability to push during delivery because the patient has reduced feeling. The nurse will need to assist the mother with getting into a delivery position and provide additional interventions and guidance to aid in focusing on her pushing.

After delivery and possible perineal repairs, the epidural infusion is turned off and the catheter removed. The nurse should ensure that the catheter tip is intact and that a dressing is applied to the insertion site. The nurse monitors the woman's risk for falls, blood pressure, and for the presence of headaches.

9. Explore: Labor Pain - Nonpharmacologic Techniques and Support

Common Interventions

Although they may be appropriate for the well-being of a woman and fetus, common interventions often add discomfort to the natural pain of labor.

  • Intravenous (IV) lines cause pain when inserted and remain noticeable to many women during labor. Fetal monitoring equipment is uncomfortable for some women, but others want to hear the sounds. Both may hamper a woman's mobility, which she might use to assume a more comfortable position. A woman may expect routine electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) and tolerate the inconvenience for reassurance of her baby's well-being with technology.
  • A woman whose labor is induced or augmented often reports more pain and increased difficulty coping with it because contractions reach peak intensity quickly. Labor is more interventional with induction or augmentation. Vaginal examinations, amniotomy, and insertion of internal fetal monitoring devices also increase a woman's discomfort briefly because of vaginal and cervical stretching. Vaginal manipulation often stimulates a contraction because of a reflex known as Ferguson's reflex.

Medications and Nonpharmacologics

Women may choose to manage their labor pain with pain medications, nonpharmacologic techniques, or a combination of both. Nonpharmacologic techniques used in labor that can be calming and reduce pain include:

  • Relaxation techniques, such as tactile stimulation, music, distraction, hydrotherapy, mental stimulation, and breathing techniques (such as Lamaze).
  • The woman's partner, who is familiar with her likes and dislikes, can be instrumental in supporting these techniques and adjusting them as needed. This also helps the partner feel involved and helpful in the process.
  • Some women employ birth coaches or doulas to support them during labor and childbirth, instead of or in addition to a partner. Doulas are trained, experienced, childbirth support providers that some women, and even couples, hire to provide reassurance, touch, companionship, and comfort during labor. They do not perform any medical skills, but their care is shown to reduce pain, complications, and duration of labor in some cases.

Activity and Exercise

  • Ambulation and/or physical movement in labor, if permitted by the healthcare provider, may be a more comfortable pain control option for the woman in labor.
  • Ambulation may be contraindicated after the administration of analgesics in order to maintain continuous monitoring of the fetus and to assess for the impact of the medication on the fetus. Also, if the membranes are ruptured and the presenting part is not fully engaged in the pelvis, cord prolapse is a higher risk when ambulating.
  • Often, in the labor and delivery unit, women walk the halls with their support person, stop during contractions, go in their rooms, possibly rock back and forth while leaning forward on the bed, or perhaps take a bath or shower. When labor advances to a certain point, it may be too uncomfortable for the woman to ambulate.

12. Explore: Labor Pain - Nursing Care

Pain is personal and unique to each person, influenced by many variables. Labor and delivery nurses must be sensitive to the woman's cultural and religious values and beliefs, which may impact the woman's feelings about childbirth and how pain is expressed.

Expressions

The variations in expression are not quantifiable indications of pain level and may be affected by cultural norms. A woman who tends to express labor pain in an expressive manner is not necessarily in more pain than a woman who expresses labor pain in a stoic manner.

Also, the family's role in labor may vary based on cultural beliefs and practices. For example, a very quiet partner may seem uncaring to some, but it may be common practice in their culture. The nurse should not assume that a particular cultural background predicts how the woman will express pain, or how the family members will interact during labor.

Language Barrier

A language barrier in labor and childbirth can increase anxiety for women who cannot verbally express their needs and feelings to the healthcare team providing care or understand the explanations of procedures and reassurances that are provided.

Remember! Whenever possible, it is best that a bilingual nurse cares for the family with a language barrier. If that is not possible, a translator must be made available.

Cultural Sensitivity

Nursing considerations related to culturally sensitive pain control during labor involve encouraging women to express themselves in the way they feel most comfortable. Avoid praising expressions of pain that reflect one's own beliefs in how pain should be expressed (for example, praising a woman for being strong and brave when she is stoic and calm during contractions). Conversely, avoid correcting expressions of pain that reflect one's own belief of what constitutes inappropriate behavior (for example, assuming weakness is the reason a woman is being more expressive during contractions).

Physical Nursing Care

If welcomed by the laboring woman, physical nursing care in labor can include: (slideshow transcript — 5 slides)

  • Assisting with coaching of breathing during contractions to ensure adequate oxygenation and maximum calm and focus.
  • Deep, cleansing breaths before and after contractions, avoid rapid breathing during contractions.
  • Guidance during the second stage of labor to ensure the woman is pushing correctly to avoid complications.
  • Provide non-pharmaceutical comfort and pain relief measures such as guided imagery, calming environment, ice chips, cool cloths, and back rubs.
  • Continual monitoring of maternal well-being including emotional status.

1. Introduction to Nursing Care: Process and Stages of Labor

The process of labor and delivery is complex, yet mostly natural. Often, the results are positive without the need for intervention. However, multiple factors influence each other to make this happen. In this module, the stages of labor will be discussed along with the onset and signs of normal labor, the difference between false and true labor, and the response of the newborn to birth.

3. Explore: Signs of Impending Labor

Around 2 weeks before labor begins, the following signs may develop. (Interactive hotspot diagram — image saved at assets/Week4_Process_and_Stages_of_Labor/03-signs-of-impending-labor.png; full transcript below.)

  • Lightening: The fetus feels like it has settled or dropped into the pelvis, most often in first pregnancies.
  • Increased Urinary Frequency: An increase in urinary frequency is brought on by the pressure of the fetus settling in the pelvis and the pressure of the uterus.
  • Relief of Pressure & Fetus "Drops": It is easier to breathe once the fetus drops lower, which relieves pressure and allows more room in the abdominal cavity for respirations.
  • Leaking Amniotic Fluid in the Vagina: This may feel like leaking urine. Nitrazine paper is used at the bedside to determine the presence of amniotic fluid. If the paper turns blue, it is reactive and positive for amniotic fluid. If it does not change color, the fluid is probably urine. Typically, the amniotic fluid does not rupture or leak until after labor starts. It is important that the woman seeks medical attention if she believes her membranes are leaking or have ruptured.
  • Vaginal Examination: The exam performed by the healthcare provider late in pregnancy will find a cervix that is "ripening", beginning to efface (thin), soften, shorten, and dilate (open). Mucousy, blood-tinged vaginal discharge (called bloody show): There is a mucous plug that blocks the cervical opening during pregnancy. As the vascular cervix ripens and labor approaches, this plug may become dislodged and also be mistaken for amniotic fluid.
  • Backaches: Backaches along with uterine contractions become more frequent and intense but may or may not be regular and painful. This can make it difficult to determine true versus false labor. True labor is determined by regular, rhythmic contractions that result in progressive cervical dilation and effacement. Contractions that do not meet this criteria are called Braxton Hicks contractions and are false labor.
  • Other signs: Nausea, diarrhea, bursts of energy with the desire to clean and prepare for the baby (called nesting) are common.

4. Explore: The Onset of Normal Labor

The exact cause for the onset of labor, whether it is prompted by mechanical or hormonal changes, remains unknown. Some believe uterine stretching causes uterine contractions to start, but this is often not the case. Hormonal increases or decreases in either the mother or the fetus are also possible causes for the onset of labor.

  • Progesterone decreases in late pregnancy, allowing uterine contractions to strengthen.
  • Estrogen increases, making the uterus more sensitive to stimuli that cause contractions.
  • Oxytocin increases and stimulates uterine contractions.
  • Prostaglandins increase the strength of uterine contractions and further enhance oxytocin secretions.

A pregnancy is considered full term beginning at 39 weeks 0 days until 41 weeks 6 days. As the pregnancy continues beyond 40 weeks and into postterm (42 weeks gestation), the body goes through changes that affect the fetus. 5. Explore: The Five Ps of the Labor and Delivery Process

As a woman nears the end of pregnancy, specific clinical manifestations are linked to the onset of labor. Labor is a diverse and individualized process with complex, interacting factors. Five factors of the labor and delivery process must act in harmony to produce positive outcomes: passageway, passenger, powers, position, and psyche.

(Multi-slide presentation — Passageway slide image saved at assets/Week4_Process_and_Stages_of_Labor/05-five-ps-passageway-slide.png; full transcript below. Slide diagram notes: the fetus first passes through the pelvic inlet, designated by negative (-) station numbers; the narrowest diameter through which the fetus must pass marks the entrance to the pelvic outlet, designated as 0 station; the pelvic outlet is the smallest plane of the pelvic canal, designated by positive (+) station numbers. Gynecoid pelvis is the "classic" female pelvis and the best for vaginal delivery.)

The Five Ps of the Labor and Delivery Process

Passageway (pelvis): The size and shape of the woman's pelvis determine the ease with which the fetal head can pass through for vaginal delivery to occur. There are four different types of pelvis that can be assessed to determine if the pelvis is an adequate passageway. The gynecoid pelvis is the most typical and a flat pelvis is the most uncommon. The soft tissues of the pelvis also change in consistency and shape during labor:

  • The cervix thins (effacement) and dilates. A fully dilated cervix is 10 cm and can no longer be palpated.
  • Hormonal changes throughout pregnancy allow the vagina to stretch during labor.
  • The pelvic floor stretches and thins, and the anus may dilate and bulge.

Passenger (fetus, umbilical cord, and placenta): Normally, the fetal head is the presenting part in a vaginal delivery, and it must be able to exit through the woman's pelvis. There are several factors related to the fetus, which impact the head fitting through the woman's pelvis and dilating the cervix.

  • Fetal skull – The bones are not rigidly fused at birth. The plates or sections of the skull overlap one another (molding) as they move through the pelvis, allowing it to fit easily. Palpation of the fetal skull and the location of these plates, the spaces between (sutures), and the area where they meet (fontanelles), is used to determine the direction the fetus is facing.
  • Fetal attitude, position, and presentation: To take up minimal space and provide the best angle for fitting through the pelvis, there is an ideal position for the fetus in the pelvis at term.
    • Fetal Attitude: The fetal body is flexed.
    • Fetal Position: The back is bowed outward, the chin is down and tucked into the sternum, the arms are crossed on the chest, and the thighs flexed on the abdomen. Fetal position can be determined by vaginal exam, palpation of the abdomen using Leopold's maneuvers, and ultrasound.
    • Fetal Lie: The fetal body is longitudinal (fetal spine is parallel to the maternal spine) rather than transverse (perpendicular).
  • Fetal Presentation: When the fetus is in the cephalic (head down) position, it includes vertex (ideal attitude of the fetal skull), brow, face, and mentum (chin). Malpresentations, which often require a cesarean delivery, include:
    • Breech – when the buttocks or legs come in contact with the cervix rather than the head.
    • Shoulder – when the fetal head is lying diagonally rather than vertically.
  • Umbilical Cord and Placenta: The umbilical cord and placenta should deliver after the fetus. When the cord comes out first, the fetus is lying on it and decreasing its oxygen supply, called a cord prolapse.

Powers (contractions): For successful vaginal delivery, the uterine contractions (primary powers) and the mother's pushing at delivery (secondary powers) must be strong and regular enough to push the fetal head against the cervix, dilate it, and push the fetus out. The urge to push kicks in when the presenting part reaches the pelvic floor, but the client should not push until the cervix is fully dilated, otherwise it will become swollen and make the passageway smaller.

Position (of the mother): The anatomical positioning of the woman in labor impacts many factors. Positions that are more comfortable relieve fatigue and increase circulation. Also, certain positions use the force of gravity more effectively to allow the fetus to descend into the pelvis and put additional pressure on the pelvic floor, dilating the cervix. It is best for the woman to change positions frequently and remain physically active during labor when safe to do so.

  • Remaining upright improves circulation and minimizes venous compression while improving cardiac output, providing the mother and fetus with more needed blood flow and oxygen.
  • When the fetal occiput is posterior, getting on all fours (on hands and knees) helps relieve back pressure and may actually rotate the fetus into the occiput anterior (ROA).
  • Changing positions is also used when fetal distress is present. A left lateral position often relieves possible head and cord compression.
  • Several positions for birth are used depending on client preference and use of regional anesthesia.

Psyche (mother's psychological response): Anxiety, fear, and fatigue impact the woman's ability to relax and allow her body to do its job. Preparing mentally for childbirth through classes, having a labor partner, and a supportive healthcare team can all assist the mother in remaining calm.

In labor and delivery, these five factors—passageway, passenger, powers, position, and psyche—intermingle and influence each other to determine the outcome.

9. Explore: True Labor Versus False Labor

As we've discussed, the passageway, passenger, powers, position, and psyche (Five Ps) are the factors which determine the progression of normal labor and ultimately result in cervical change, fetal descent, and delivery of the fetus. Abnormalities in any of the five factors can alter or prevent labor progress, putting the mother or baby at risk.

Tab 1: True vs. False Labor

Nurses assess the Five Ps to distinguish between true and false labor. It is also important to educate clients regarding the differences between true and false labor. For example, clients who have never been in labor before may believe that the presence of contractions indicates true labor. However, Braxton-Hicks (irregular) contractions can be present weeks or even longer before true labor begins.

In general, contractions that result in cervical change (dilation and effacement) indicate true labor. Contractions without cervical change assisting with preparing the body for birth do not indicate true labor.

True labor contractions occur at regular intervals, gradually increase in duration and intensity, and shorten in frequency. Discomfort begins in the back and radiates to the abdomen. Intensity increases with activity, and comfort measures typically do not work.

NOTE: A cervical exam is the only definitive way to diagnose true versus false labor. True labor dilates and effaces the cervix.

False labor contractions are irregular with no change in duration, intensity, or frequency. Discomfort is in the lower abdomen. Activity has no effect on the contractions, and comfort measures typically lessen the pain.

Tab 2: Cardinal Movements

The seven cardinal movements of labor are the maneuvers, adjustments, and turns that the fetal head must make as it descends in the pelvis.

  • Engagement is when the largest diameter of the presenting part reaches or passes through the pelvic inlet (0 station).
  • Descent occurs when the fetal head enters the pelvic inlet in the occiput transverse (facing the side of the pelvis) position.
  • Flexion occurs when the fetal chin flexes downward onto the fetal chest.
  • Internal rotation occurs as the occiput rotates to fit the diameter of the pelvic cavity.
  • Extension occurs when the fetal head extends as it passes under the symphysis pubis.
  • External rotation occurs when the fetal head turns briefly to the position it occupied when it was engaged in the inlet (restitution), then turns further to one side to realign with the back and shoulders.
  • Expulsion occurs when the anterior shoulder is delivered before the posterior shoulder and the fetal body quickly follows.

Mnemonic for the order of the cardinal movements: "Every Darn Fool In Egypt Eats Raw Eggs." The beginning letter of each word represents one of the seven cardinal movements (Engagement, Descent, Flexion, Internal rotation, Extension, External rotation/Restitution, Expulsion).

Tab 4: Nursing Care

Nursing care for the first stage of labor may include:

13. Explore: Stages of Labor and Delivery — Fetal Delivery

The second stage of labor, fetal delivery, begins with complete (10 cm) cervical dilation and ends with the birth of the baby. This is when the client is pushing the baby out with each contraction and can last anywhere from 1 to 3+ hours, depending on the parity and whether the client has an epidural. Contractions occur every 2-3 minutes, lasting 40-60 seconds. Crowning occurs when the fetal head is encircled by the external opening of the vagina, signifying that birth is imminent. These contractions are slightly less intense than during the transition phase of Stage 1 Dilation.

Nurses play a key role in encouraging and supporting the client while pushing and ensuring rest between contractions.

Tab 1: Second Stage of Labor (interactive images — transcript)

Episiotomy and Lacerations

  • At some point in this stage, the healthcare provider may decide to cut an episiotomy. Anesthetic is injected into the tissue of the perineum between the vagina and the anus. A surgical incision is then made in the area to enlarge the opening which lessens the risk of prolonged pushing. The episiotomy is repaired with sutures under continued anesthesia following delivery.
  • Sometimes the perineum or vaginal walls tear during this stage of labor. These lacerations can be small or deep. They may be difficult to repair due to their lack of uniformity. An episiotomy may also be used when the fetus is large (macrosomia), in rapid deliveries when lacerations are more common, and with abnormal fetal presentations.

Fetal Delivery

  • After the fetal head extends and is delivered, another rotation occurs to allow the shoulders to be delivered followed by a quick expulsion of the newborn's body. The umbilical cord is clamped in 2 spots and cut in between the clamps.
  • The highest priority upon delivery is establishing the newborn's airway. Typically, the nares and mouth are suctioned out after birth. The newborn is then placed on the mother's abdomen for skin-to-skin. In the presence of fetal distress prior to delivery such as with meconium, or if the newborn is showing signs of distress at birth, the newborn may instead be placed in a radiant warmer where newborn nursing care begins. Breastfeeding may begin as soon as the newborn is stabilized and should be encouraged within the first hour.

14. Explore: Stages of Labor and Delivery — Placenta Delivery

The third stage of labor begins with infant delivery and ends with placental delivery. This stage typically takes 5 – 10 minutes regardless of gravity/parity.

Signs that the placenta is separating include:

  • change in the shape of the uterus
  • fundus moving upward
  • cord lengthening
  • gush of blood from the vagina

A placenta that has not delivered spontaneously within 30 minutes after birth is considered retained. Manual or surgical removal is necessary.

Following the delivery of the placenta, the uterus should contract firmly and remain contracted to close off the blood vessels exposed by the placental detachment. If the uterus does not contract adequately, hemorrhage will occur. Oxytocin infusion (or intramuscular administration if no intravenous access is established) is usually started to assist in uterine contracting.

15. Explore: Stages of Labor and Delivery — Stabilization

The fourth stage occurs immediately after delivery as the mother's body works to stabilize. It begins with delivery of the placenta and ends with the beginning of maternal physiologic readjustments. The client is monitored closely for 1 to 4 hours in the delivery room or recovery room. This stage often involves the mother being tired from labor and birth, as well as being excited about their newborn.

Key nursing roles are accurate assessment to identify any possible complications such as hemorrhage, providing comfort measures including pain management, and encouraging bonding. The newborn is often the most alert during this time, which is a good time to facilitate parental attachment and breastfeeding initiation.

Tab 1: Vital Signs

Vital signs are closely monitored in this stage. Decreases in systolic and diastolic blood pressure are expected during stabilization, as well as tachycardia, increased respiration, and slightly increased temperature. However, the client's vital signs should return to pre-labor baseline within 2-4 hours of delivery. If not, there may be complications present that require follow-up.

Post-birth danger signs include:

  • hypotension
  • extreme tachycardia
  • increased respiratory effort
  • uterine atony
  • excessive bleeding
NOTE: Blood pressure and pulse may not be reliable indicators of hypovolemic shock due to increased circulatory volume during pregnancy.
Tab 2: Uterine Tone

At this stage, the uterine fundus should be firm and midline with the umbilicus. Immediately after delivery, the fundus is palpated midway between the symphysis pubis and umbilicus. Within 12 hours, the fundus rises to approximately the level of the umbilicus. The fundus descends by approximately 1 cm per day. By 2 weeks after delivery, the uterus has returned to the pelvic cavity and can no longer be palpated.

The woman is taught to massage her fundus regularly. Breastfeeding and postpartum administration of oxytocin will help the uterus remain firm. If the uterus is boggy and/or displaced laterally, ensure that the bladder remains empty.

Tab 3: Lochia

(Figure: "Lochia rubra on a pad.")

The vaginal drainage will usually be heavier and bright red (rubra) at first and then gradually lessen. Lochia will continue to flow for several weeks postpartum and certain activities can increase the amount (breastfeeding and going from a lying to sitting or standing position). Some clotting is expected. However, clots larger than a quarter should be further evaluated. Foul smelling lochia suggests infection.

  • Lochia rubra is red in color and lasts for 3-4 days after birth.
  • Lochia serosa is pink in color and lasts from day 4-10 after birth.
  • Lochia alba is white discharge that lasts from day 10 up to 6 weeks after birth.

Once lochia flow stops, the cervix is considered closed and the chance of uterine infection is minimized.

NOTE: A perineal pad saturated in 15 minutes or less indicates excessive blood loss. This requires immediate assessment and intervention!
Tab 4: Perineal Tissue

Regardless of whether the perineum remains intact or there are lacerations and/or an episiotomy, the perineal tissue is observed regularly for swelling, approximation of sutures, abnormal bleeding, and signs of a developing hematoma.

NOTE: Marked fullness, excessive bruising, and/or constant perineal pain suggest the presence of a hematoma.

Transcript:

  • Labia: highly vascular tissue that protects the entrance to the birth canal
  • Perineum: mixture of soft tissue and muscle, highly prone to tearing during delivery
Tab 5: Nursing Care

Nursing care at this stage includes:

  • Ice packs are used for the first 24 hours after birth to decrease perineal swelling and pain.
  • Sitz baths (warm water bath) are used after the first 24 hours to promote circulation and decrease pain.
  • Mild analgesics may be administered. Opioid analgesics are used in rare situations.
  • Promote perineal care to prevent infection.

16. Explore: Nursing Process for Normal Labor — Interventions

Nursing interventions during labor include the following.

Accordion 1: Providing Comforting, Encouraging Physical Care
  • Assist with coaching of breathing during contractions to ensure adequate oxygenation and maximum calm and focus.
    • Use deep, cleansing breaths before and after contractions, and avoid rapid breathing during contractions.
  • Provide guidance during the second stage of labor to ensure the client is pushing correctly to avoid complications.
  • Provide non-pharmaceutical comfort and pain relief measures, such as guided imagery, calming environment, ice chips, cool cloths, and back rubs.
Accordion 2: Continual Monitoring of Labor Progress, Maternal and Fetal Well-Being
  • vital signs
  • uterine activity
  • fetal heart rate pattern
  • psychosocial status
  • signs of complications
  • labor equipment
Accordion 3: Maintaining Standard Precautions and Aseptic Techniques

This is particularly important in relation to exposure to blood and bodily fluids.

  • Sterile gloves are worn when doing a vaginal examination and performing intravaginal interventions.
  • The healthcare team will wear gowns, masks, and face shields or protective eyewear during a birth.
  • Clean gloves are worn when handling the newborn until after the first bath.

In addition, the nurse will:

  • Provide a safe and supportive environment for the client and family.
  • Explain the procedures performed and provide education.

Feedback/Rationale: Leaking amniotic fluid in the vagina may feel like leaking urine. Nitrazine paper is used at the bedside to determine the presence of amniotic fluid. If it turns blue, it is reactive and positive for amniotic fluid. If it does not change color, the fluid is probably urine.

Cervical dilation and effacement may occur without membrane rupture. Pressing downward on the fundus to observe for fluid gush is not an acceptable test for rupture. Assessing the color, consistency, and odor of the fluid is not a reliable indicator of the type of fluid.
