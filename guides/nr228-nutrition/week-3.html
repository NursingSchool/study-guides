Section 1: Introduction to Dietary Fats

Many individuals have a love-hate relationship with dietary fats. They love the way fats make food taste, but hate the impact eating fatty foods can have on their health. The key to a healthy diet is moderation of the type and quantity of fat that is consumed.

This learning activity will discuss the nutrition benefits and risks of lipids throughout the life span. By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Explore the link between fats and health.
  • Identify risk factors and conditions contributing to fat-related health alterations.
  • Recognize the complications from too much or too little fat in the diet.
  • Utilize knowledge of fats and health to promote a balanced diet.
  • Recommend how individuals can alter their lifestyle to decrease fat consumption.

Section 3: Explore: Types of Dietary Fats

The main difference between lipids and fats is that lipids are a broad group of biomolecules whereas fats are a type of lipid. Fat is stored in the adipose tissue and under the skin of animals. It is mainly used as an energy-storage molecule in the body. One pound of adipose tissue yields about 3500 calories. Fat provides 9 calories of energy per gram.

Tab: Triglycerides

Triglycerides are the largest class of lipids that are found in food and body fat. They contain three fatty acids and a glycerol molecule derived from carbohydrates. Triglycerides are identified as saturated, monounsaturated, and polyunsaturated. This identification metric indicates how many carbon bonds are used. For example, a saturated fatty acid has all carbon bonds filled with hydrogen. Conversely, a polyunsaturated fatty acid has multiple carbon atoms without hydrogen bonds attached to them. As a health professional, it is important to note that polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) are the best to consume.

The body uses triglycerides for energy storage, protection of organs, thermoregulation, and insulation of nerve cells.

Tab: Saturated Fatty Acids

Saturated fatty acids are triglycerides that have all their carbon atoms attached to hydrogen. It is the least healthy fat to consume. Saturated fatty acids are found in foods from animal sources. Plant sources that contain saturated fats are palm and coconut oils.

Monounsaturated fatty acids

Monounsaturated fatty acids (MUFA) are fatty acids that have two carbon atoms that are double bonded. This fat is healthier to consume than saturated fatty acids. Dietary sources for this MUFA are extra virgin olive oil, peanuts, and canola oil.

Polyunsaturated fatty acids

Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA) have multiple carbon atoms that are double bonded. This is the healthiest fat to consume. These fatty acids comprise the omega-3 (alpha-linolenic) and omega-6 (linoleic) fatty acids. The omega number represents the location of the double bond. The body is unable to produce these fatty acids and must obtain them via food consumption. PUFA is found naturally in sesame, sunflower, chia seeds, and fish. PUFA is added to some products during manufacturing, including margarine and processed cheeses. It is important to mention that omega-3 fatty acids may prevent cardiovascular disease.

Tab: Phospholipids

Phospholipids are unique in that they keep fat dispersed in fluids. These lipid molecules make up the cell walls and keep cell structures stable. Lecithin is an example of a phospholipid and is found naturally in egg yolks and soybeans. Phospholipids are also used as emulsifiers in food preparation. Mayonnaise and salad dressing are an example of how emulsifiers are used in food preparation.

Tab: Sterols

Sterols closely resemble fatlike lipids and are more complex than phospholipids and triglycerides. Sterols are present in plant- and animal-based foods. In the body, sterols are used by the liver to produce cholesterol, which is important for bile and vitamin D production. Cholesterol helps maintain the integrity of cell membranes. Because animals convert sterol to cholesterol, animal-based foods contain cholesterol which can increase the cholesterol levels of people.

Section 4: Explore: Know Your Fats!

When cooking with fats or adding them to uncooked foods such as sandwiches and salads, choose those that contain fewer saturated fats.

Review the Parent Tips: Cooking With Healthier Fats and Oils (https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/educational/wecan/downloads/tip-fats-and-oils.pdf) to make a better choice when determining the types of oil and fat for cooking.

Section 6: Explore: Calories From Fat

As a health professional, it's important to help clients to understand the calculation of calories from fat and why it's important to limit their intake of fat.

Tab: Consuming Fat

With 9 calories per gram, fat is the most calorie dense source of energy in the diet when compared to the 4 calories per gram in proteins and carbohydrates. To calculate the number of calories from fat in a food, multiply the grams of fat in the food by 9 calories.

For example, a one-half cup serving of vanilla ice-cream has 7.3 grams of fat. Multiply 7.3 by 9 to determine that 65.9 calories are from fat, which is almost half of the total calories!

Nutrition Facts (½ cup vanilla ice cream, 69g, 16 servings per container):

  • Calories: 137
  • Total Fat: 7.3g (47.9% DV)
  • Sodium: 53mg (2% DV)
  • Total Carb: 16g (46.7% DV)
  • Dietary Fiber: 0.5g (2% DV)
  • Sugars: 14g
  • Protein: 2.3g
Tab: Losing Body Fat

To lose a pound of body fat, a person needs to burn 3500 more calories than they consume. This means that an individual who wants to lose 20 pounds will need to burn 70,000 more calories than they consume (20 pounds times 3500 calories per pound).

While it seems to make sense that limiting fat intake is the best way to lose excess weight due to body fat, this must be done in moderation. Lowering the daily intake of fats to 10-15% of daily calories and consuming unsaturated fats is safer than eliminating fats altogether.

Section 7: Explore: Calculating Daily Calories From Fat Goal

To determine the number of calories and fat grams they need each day to lose, maintain, or gain weight, a person needs to consult with their healthcare provider or a registered dietitian. As a general guideline, to maintain the current weight, follow the 3-step formula below. Remember, everyone's body and metabolism are different, so these numbers could vary slightly for each individual and can change over time.

  • Step 1: Multiply total calories to be consumed each day by 30% to calculate calories from fat each day.
  • Step 2: Since each gram of fat has 9 calories, take calories from fat per day and divide those calories by 9.

Example: An athlete wants 30% of their 2700 calories consumed each day to be from fat. How many grams of fat should they consume each day?

  • Step 1: 2700 x 0.3 = 810 calories from fat each day.
  • Step 2: 810 calories ∕ 9 calories per gram = 90 grams of fat per day.

Section 9: Explore: Why People Eat Fats

Fat, in the form of cocoa butter, is high in saturated fat. Chocolate also contains steric acid, a saturated fat that does not raise low density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol, making it relatively healthy when consumed in moderation.

Overall, fats help food taste great, and its smooth texture feels good on the tongue. Fats provide satiety, or fullness, and provide a sense of contentment, or calm. Since fats are slower to digest than other food components, the feeling of fullness lasts longer and helps to maintain a normal serum glucose level. Fats are used by the body for organ protection, hormone synthesis, and energy storage.

Healthy fats are found in many foods, including:

  • Avocados
  • Cheese
  • Dark chocolate
  • Whole eggs
  • Fatty fish
  • Nuts
  • Chia seeds
  • Extra virgin olive oil
  • Coconuts
  • Full-fat yogurt

Important! The key to health is to eat the right types of fats in moderation.

Section 11: Explore: Digestion of Fat

Fat digestion starts in the mouth and ends in the small intestine. Bile, pancreatic lipase, and mechanical digestion all play a role in breaking down fat.

Cholecystokinin (CCK) is a hormone released in the duodenum that controls bile secretion. Bile is important because it acts as an emulsifier that further breaks down (i.e., catabolizes, or digests) fat so it can be absorbed into the blood and transported to cells. In addition, bile salts assist with the absorption of fat into the body. The pancreas plays a role in fat digestion by releasing pancreatic lipase that further breaks down triglycerides.

Once absorbed, triglycerides are initially transported into the lymphatic system, then absorbed into the bloodstream to be used by cells. During this transportation process into the bloodstream, triglycerides are formed into chylomicrons. Glycerol and other short-chained fatty acids are absorbed.

As fats are delivered to individual cells, anabolism is used to create new structures (such as cell walls) and substances (like hormones) that contain fats.

Individuals with alterations of the small intestine that interfere with fat absorption are likely to experience symptoms such as abdominal pain, bloating, chronic diarrhea and steatorrhea (fatty stools) due to the high fat content. Since fats are not being absorbed, nutrients that rely on fat for absorption, such as vitamins A, D, E, and K, may also become deficient.

Section 13: Explore: Lipoproteins

A molecule of lipoprotein contains both lipids (lipo-) and proteins. Lipoproteins are responsible for the transportation of fat particles in the blood which are measured as serum cholesterol. Since proteins weigh more than lipids, lipoprotein molecules with a higher ratio of protein to lipids are dense, making them healthier. In the same manner, lipoprotein molecules with a higher proportion of lipid to proteins are less dense, and not as healthy.

Lipoproteins are categorized based on their density (in descending order of fat content), including:

  • ultra-low-density lipoproteins (ULDL)
    • Chylomicrons, also known as ULDL, are composed of triglycerides (85–92%), phospholipids (6–12%), cholesterol (1–3%), and proteins (1–2%). These molecules are primarily made up of triglycerides and a small amount of protein. Chylomicrons are responsible for transporting lipids from the intestinal tract to cells in the body.
  • very-low-density lipoproteins (VLDL)
  • intermediate-density lipoproteins (IDL)
  • low-density lipoproteins (LDL)
    • While IDL, VLDL, and LDL all increase the risk of cardiovascular disease, LDL is considered the "bad" cholesterol when treatment is prescribed.
  • high-density lipoproteins (HDL)
    • High levels of high density lipoproteins have shown to be protective against cardiovascular disease. This is why HDL is considered the "good" cholesterol.

Section 14: Explore: Essential Fatty Acids

Essential fatty acids (EFA) are fats the body cannot make. These fats must be consumed from one's diet. Examples of these lipids are triglycerides that are unsaturated, such as omega-3 fatty acids. Signs and symptoms of EFA deficiency are dry scaly skin, hair loss, weight loss, and poor wound healing (Mogensen 2017).

Clients most at risk for EFA deficiency include:

  • any client on an extremely low-fat diet
  • clients with fat malabsorption
  • clients receiving treatment for protein malnutrition
  • clients recovering from serious burns and accidents

Section 15: Explore: Fat Intake Across the Life Span

To date, there is no golden rule guiding daily fat intake. Generally, some fat intake, particularly intake of unsaturated fats, can have beneficial effects on the human body. Children especially need some fat in their diets to promote healthy growth and development. However, any type of fat, including these healthier dietary fats, can still have negative effects when consumed in excess.

All ages are encouraged to follow these healthy guidelines for fat intake (USDA & DHHS, 2020):

  • Consume less than 10% of daily caloric needs in the form of saturated fats.
  • Limiting consumption to less than 7% has been shown to reduce the risk of heart disease.
  • Replace saturated fats with unsaturated fats whenever possible.
  • Minimize consumption of trans fats.
  • Consume less than 300 mg of dietary cholesterol each day.

Section 17: Explore: Heart Healthy Facts About Fat

Whether lowering fat intake for heart health, weight loss, or to feel better, the principles are the same: increase unsaturated fats, limit saturated fats, and stop using trans fat, hydrogenated oils, and tropical oils.

Love It

Unsaturated (Poly and Mono)

  • It lowers the rates of cardiovascular and all-cause mortality.
  • It lowers bad cholesterol and triglyceride levels.
  • It provides essential fats the body needs but can't produce itself.
Limit It

Saturated

  • It increases the risk of cardiovascular disease.
  • It raises bad cholesterol levels.

Note: Saturated fats are found primarily in meat products, palm oil, and coconut oil. It is important to note that trans fats are processed by the body like they are saturated fats. This is why trans fats are unhealthy and can lead to heart disease.

Lose It

Artificial Trans Fat, Hydrogenated Oils, and Tropical Oils

  • They increase the risk of heart disease.
  • They raise bad cholesterol levels.

Note: It is important to note margarine can be consumed in moderation. Margarine is made from monounsaturated fats. It is important to mention that some margarines can contain high amounts of trans fat, so it is vital to read the food labels.

Section 18: Explore: Rethinking Fats

Knowing the facts about fats is the first step toward meeting nutritional health goals. This knowledge must be followed with actions, which can be challenging. Here are some tips on how to start rethinking fats (MyPlate, n.d.).

Tab: Involve Everyone Who Shops for Food and Prepares Meals

These same individuals should also be part of meal-planning that promotes healthier fats and balanced meals for all who eat in the same home.

Tab: Check the Label First

Read the nutrition facts label on packaged foods. Choose products that are lower in saturated fat, since these types of fat are less healthy.

Tab: Eat Foods With Healthy Fats

Eat nuts, seeds, and fatty fish like tuna, salmon, and sardines. These foods, as well as vegetable oils like olive and canola, are good sources of unsaturated fat—a healthier fat option.

Tab: Limit Saturated Fat

Build meals around protein foods that are naturally low in saturated fat, such as beans, peas, and lentils, as well as soy foods, skinless chicken, seafood, and lean meats.

Tab: Skimp on "Solid Fats"

"Solid fats" such as butter, shortening, and fat from meats are high in unhealthy saturated fats. Switch to olive or canola oil for cooking and trim the fat when possible.

Tab: Swap the Spread

Switch from butter and cream cheese on your toast to nut butter or a spread of avocado and a squeeze of lemon. These spreads contain healthier unsaturated fats.

Tab: Customize Your Order

Order baked or steamed options instead of fried foods, especially deep-fried. A dash of hot sauce or a spoonful of salsa adds flavor without adding fat.

Section 1: Introduction to Dietary Proteins

Proteins are macronutrients needed to maintain good health. Proteins consist of amino acids and one nitrogen molecule. To maintain optimal health, the body digests proteins and restructures the amino acids to use for cell growth or energy. Like most foods, sources of protein can be both beneficial and harmful if not eaten as part of a balanced diet.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Identify components and types of protein.
  • Select sources of protein.
  • Identify factors that impact the ingestion and digestion of protein.
  • Discuss how proteins are used in the body.
  • Describe the protein needs of individuals.

Section 3: Explore: Protein Structure

Amino acids are the building blocks of protein. Proteins are the organic compounds formed when amino acids combine. The function of a protein depends on its structure and composition. Specifically, it is the way the amino acids are folded and the location of the amino acids that dictates their function in the body.

There are 20-22 amino acids that the body requires—the number reflects various ways amino acids are named. Nine (8 to 10, depending on how they are named) of the amino acids are considered essential because the body must obtain them from food. The liver can make the remaining amino acids using nutrients from a person's dietary intake. The liver can store every amino acid to ensure the body has a constant supply.

Protein Structure:

  • Primary structure: Amino acid
  • Secondary structure: α-Helixes
  • Tertiary structure: Polypeptide chains
  • Quaternary structure: Complex of protein molecule

Section 4: Explore: Proteins in the Diet

Proteins are important in our diets because they help us grow, maintain our bodies, and create the multitudes of enzymes and hormones needed for the countless metabolic reactions in our bodies.

Not all proteins contain the same type or quantity of amino acids. There are two types of protein.

  • Complete proteins contain all the essential amino acids for growth and maintenance.
    • Examples: fish, poultry, eggs, beef, pork, dairy, and soybeans.
    • Examples of plant-based complete proteins: chia, quinoa, buckwheat, and hemp seeds.
  • Incomplete proteins lack some essential amino acids.
    • Examples: amaranth, beans, and lentils.

Foods that have high-quality proteins are expensive, such as meat (including poultry) and eggs. People in less-developed countries have fewer opportunities to eat these foods. Due to their high cost, many people cannot afford appropriate amounts of protein. Because plant-based proteins (nuts, seeds, and legumes) are less expensive and can be grown at home, plant proteins are the primary source of protein for many people and cultures worldwide. Those who eat a primarily plant-based diet can obtain all the essential amino acids by eating a variety of foods throughout the day. For example, both peanut butter and bread are incomplete proteins but they can combine to provide all essential acids. So, whether eaten together in a sandwich, or separately as toast in the morning and peanut butter on celery for an afternoon snack, they provide all elements of a complete protein.

Section 6: Explore: Digestion and Absorption of Protein

Digestion

Protein digestion starts in the mouth where two enzymes in the saliva, amylase and lipase, break down food. Once the protein reaches the stomach, hydrochloric acid and other enzymes called proteases break the food down further into smaller chains of amino acids. From the stomach, these smaller chains of amino acids move into the small intestine. The pancreas releases enzymes that reduce the acidity of the digested food, which allows more enzymes to work on further breaking down the amino acid chains into individual amino acids.

(Protein is first broken down into polypeptides then amino acids during digestion.)

Absorption

Protein absorption happens in the small intestine, which contains microvilli. Microvilli are small, finger-like structures that increase the surface area for absorption in the small intestine. Once amino acids are absorbed, they are released into the bloodstream and taken to cells in the body where they start repairing tissues and building muscle.

(Amino acids are absorbed through the villi in the small intestine.)

Section 7: Explore: Protein Metabolism—Catabolism

The liver is the principal site of amino acid metabolism, but other tissues, such as the kidney, the small intestine, muscles, and adipose tissue, take part. Generally, the first step in the breakdown of amino acids is the separation of the amino group from the carbon skeleton, usually by a transamination reaction. This breakdown of proteins is referred to as catabolism. Catabolism is enhanced by hormones secreted by the adrenal cortex.

Amino acids are removed from the blood by the liver cells and deaminated (the amino or nitrogen group is cleaved or taken off the molecule). The carbon chain remaining after this deamination process is a keto acid. Some keto acids are rearranged into the glucose molecule and can be used as a fuel source or stored as fat. Some other keto acids are rearranged chemically into acetyl coenzymes and can be used as a fuel. Still, other keto acids may be rearranged into fatty acids and stored as fat. However, the amino group (nitrogen section) is toxic and is usually excreted from the body in the form of urea. Urea diffuses into the blood and is then excreted by the kidneys.

A healthy adult should have a zero-nitrogen balance. This means a person will excrete nitrogen at the same rate of intake.

  • A positive nitrogen balance occurs when more nitrogen is retained in the body than is excreted. A positive nitrogen balance occurs in growing children and pregnant women who are protein deficient.
  • A negative nitrogen balance occurs when more nitrogen is excreted than retained from dietary foods. This occurs because of aging, physical illness, stress, or starvation.

Section 9: Explore: Protein Metabolism—Anabolism

The major use of proteins is anabolism, the making of new cells. Anabolism centers around growth and building—the organization of molecules. In this process, small, simple molecules are built up into larger, more complex ones. An example of anabolism is gluconeogenesis. This is when the liver and kidneys produce glucose from noncarbohydrate sources.

Anabolism derives its energy from the catabolism process. Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is used in this process, fueled by anabolic hormones.

Other anabolic processes include bone mineralization and muscle mass growth. In some instances, synthetic hormones have been used as a shortcut to the body's own metabolism. Anabolic steroids are one example of this. Using anabolic steroids increases muscle mass by stimulating protein synthesis and muscle growth.

Section 10: Explore: Function of Proteins

How the Body Uses Protein
  • Growth and Maintenance: The body requires a constant supply of amino acids to promote growth, and replace or repair cells.
  • Immune System Response: The body uses protein to produce antibodies that can respond to bacteria and viruses.
  • Enzymes: The body uses amino acids to make enzymes, such as trypsin, chymotrypsin, and carboxypeptidase, which are used to digest protein.
  • Fluid and Electrolyte Regulation: In the body, proteins and minerals attract water, thus creating osmotic pressure. The pressure produced by proteins and minerals keeps water in a state of equilibrium between the compartments.
  • Acid-Base Balance: Proteins are used by the body as a buffer, promoting a healthy internal pH.
  • Transportation: Proteins transport nutrients in and out of the cells. For example, hemoglobin and myoglobin are proteins that store oxygen.

Section 11: Explore: Protein Needs Across the Life Span

The recommended daily allowance (RDA) for protein for an adult is 0.8 grams per kilogram (grams/kg). For the average male adult, that is about 56–63 grams per day and 46–50 grams per day for the average female adult. Factors that influence a client's daily protein requirements include age, physiologic state, and level of physical activity.

  • Age: Childhood protein requirements are higher (1 gram/kg) due to rapid growth. Current evidence (Hruby et al., 2020) supports that the decrease in total body weight that occurs with aging supports that increased protein intake (over 64 grams per day, or 1–1.5 grams/kg/day) helped middle-aged, high-functioning U.S. adults maintain their physical function better over two decades than those with normal or low protein intake.
  • Physiologic state: Women who are pregnant or lactating require additional intake of protein. In addition, wound healing, fevers, and physical illness increase the body's need for extra proteins.
  • Athletes: It is common for some athletes to consume up to 1.2–2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight (grams/kg). However, it is not recommended to exceed double the recommended daily allowance (or 1.6 grams/kg/day) of protein.

Section 12: Explore: Protein Serving Sizes

The serving size for animal-based protein is 1 cup, which is about the size of a deck of playing cards or the palm of an average-sized adult's hand. Plant-based protein portions will vary by the food. Individuals consuming most of their protein from plant-based sources should be aware of the total protein content per serving.

Using the body is good for teaching portion control:

  • A clinched fist is about a cup
  • An open or cupped palm is used to estimate the size of a protein serving
  • 2 palms are about 2 cups
  • A thumb is used to indicate an ounce

Section 14: Explore: Protein Excess

As good for the body as protein is, eating too much can be harmful, as well. Although high protein-based diets promote weight loss by eliminating a food group (carbohydrates), they also represent an unbalanced diet that may lack essential nutrients for health. As the body digests and metabolizes large amounts of protein, keto acids increase in the body, which may lead to ketoacidosis, a potentially dangerous acid-base imbalance.

Excessive protein intake is contraindicated in clients living with decreased renal function, including chronic kidney disease and renal impairment, as their bodies are unable to excrete the urea created by protein metabolism.

Even when an individual's total protein intake per day is not excessive, the quality of the protein can greatly impact their health. Because many animal-based proteins contain high amounts of saturated fat, the incidence of diseases such as atherosclerosis and hypertension may increase. For this reason, a variety of protein sources should be consumed in a well-balanced diet.

Section 16: Explore: Protein Deficiency

Protein deficiencies can be seen in clients suffering from starvation. This means the client is lacking the intake of food for protein and/or energy. Protein deficiencies can be the result of poor diet or alterations in protein absorption. Both marasmus and kwashiorkor are diseases related to protein malnutrition that result in severe changes in the body.

Health conditions, medications, and physical and social factors can cause protein deficiencies. In America, populations at greatest risk for protein insufficiency are the low income, homeless, and elderly clients. In addition, clients with abnormal metabolism, cystic fibrosis, renal failure, cancer, heart failure, end-stage liver disease, and a fatty diet can be at risk for protein deficiencies (Marcel, 2017). The progression of physical changes related to protein malnutrition occurs along a continuum. The early signs continue as the lack of dietary protein continues.

Individuals with less severe decreases in protein intake may exhibit the following signs and symptoms:

  • skin, hair, and nail problems
  • loss of muscle mass
  • increased risk of bone fractures
  • bigger appetite and increased calorie intake
  • risk of infections
  • fatty liver
  • slower growth in children

Globally, more than one third of childhood deaths are connected to undernutrition (LibreTexts, 2020). Marasmus (starvation) affects more than 50 million children under age 5 worldwide. Marasmus is characterized by an extreme emaciated appearance, poor skin health, hair loss, muscle loss, and growth retardation, with symptoms including acute fatigue, hunger, and diarrhea. Kwashiorkor is a disease brought on by a severe dietary protein deficiency. Its symptoms include edema (swelling) of the legs and feet, hair loss, anemia, emaciation (loss of muscle and fat) with a pot-belly, slow growth (children), and shiny skin.

Section 1: Introduction to Energy

Energy takes many forms. The body primarily uses chemical energy to maintain homeostasis. The process of converting this ingested energy into a usable form is called metabolism. The body utilizes energy for many different purposes, but most energy consumption is used just to maintain basic life support. This is called basal metabolism. When sleeping, eating, exercising, or doing other physical activities, more energy may be needed. Upon completion of this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Compare and contrast the two energy pathways.
  • Explain the three main sources of energy.

Section 3: Explore: What Is Energy?

Energy in the human body must be converted from nutritional sources into chemical energy. It is the progression of associated chemical reactions that are interdependent. There are two main energy pathways: anabolism and catabolism. These two processes together are called metabolism. They are interdependent and are closely regulated in the body by hormones. Both energy pathways work together to produce energy for life-sustaining functions.

The primary source of fuel for energy is food. The measurement of energy released from food is called kilocalories or calories. Determining the number of kilocalories produced can be calculated in two ways: using a bomb calorimeter or proximate composition analysis.

Tab: Bomb Calorimeter

A bomb calorimeter is a device that measures the energy produced when a sample of food is burned, but it is more accurate and efficient than the human body. Therefore, values that you see on nutritional labels are adjusted to represent the limitations present in humans.

Tab: Proximate Composition Analysis

Proximate composition analysis uses an estimation of the amounts of the total grams of carbohydrates, fats, proteins, water, and ash to calculate the energy of a specified food.

Section 4: Explore: Anabolic and Catabolic Pathways

Both the anabolic and catabolic pathways work together to supply energy, but the length and intensity of energy required, fitness level, and types of food dictate the source.

Tab: Anabolic Pathway

The anabolic pathway or anabolism is simply defined as building up. In this process, smaller molecules are combined to make larger ones. It is a process that requires energy, or adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the body's source of fuel. One or more molecules change themselves into end products using enzymes. Specifically, glucose uses energy to convert it to glycogen. Examples include the following:

  • Glycerol + Fatty Acids = Lipids
  • Amino Acids combine = Dipeptides

A mental image that might assist in remembering this concept would be that of a bodybuilder. These individuals focus on building up their muscles for competitions. Therefore, a mnemonic for this could be used. Anabolism starts with an A, so you could remember, "A bodybuilder builds up muscle."

Tab: Catabolic Pathway

Catabolism, or the catabolic pathway, is very simplistically described as breaking down. Larger molecules are broken down into smaller ones, and energy is released. In this process, glycogen is converted to glucose and produces ATP. Larger compounds become simplified. Examples:

  • Cellular Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) + 6O₂ (Oxygen) → 6CO₂ (Carbon Dioxide) + 6H₂O (Water) + ATP (energy)
  • Simple Sugars → Disaccharides + H₂O

One way to remember this concept is to think of a destructive cat. CATabolism—cats who tear things apart and break them down.

Section 6: Explore: Energy Sources

In the human body, foods provide a great source of energy, but they must be transformed. After foods are ingested, the processes of digestion, absorption, and metabolism occur. The three energy-supplying macronutrients that we receive from food include:

  • carbohydrates
  • fats
  • proteins

These three nutrients are essential in supplying the body's needs. As each of them is broken down (catabolism), energy is released, supplying fuel for essential functions.

Section 7: Explore: Carbohydrates as Energy

Carbohydrates can be classified as simple or complex, but both produce sugar when broken down. Simple carbohydrates are those that are quickly digested and synthesized to become glucose and short-term energy. Foods such as white bread, cakes, and cookies are examples. Conversely, complex carbohydrates are those foods that take longer to be synthesized and produce energy.

Conversion of carbohydrates is the most complicated of the three food sources. There is a 3-step process that occurs to provide energy: glycolysis, the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation.

Tab: Glycolysis

Glycolysis is the first step, and it is an anerobic process that converts glucose to carbon compounds. When the carbon compounds are made, pyruvic acid and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) are produced. For this process to occur, the body must have adequate levels of B complex vitamins (like niacin). The energy produced in this step is quickly produced but is used for the short term. Examples include exertion-based activities like sprinting and running from dangerous situations. Since this energy is used in a hurry, it does not get used completely and results in the production of pyruvic acid, which is converted to lactic acid. If you've ever exerted yourself and experienced pain that caused you to stop, you have experienced a buildup of lactic acid in the muscles. After a short time, additional oxygen becomes available to break down the lactic acid, relieving the discomfort. Glycolysis occurs in 1 of 2 places in the cell—the cytoplasm or the mitochondria. The jelly-like substance in the cells—the cytoplasm—is where the anerobic glycolysis happens and the short-term energy is produced.

Tab: Tricarboxylic Acid (TCA) Cycle

Long-term energy requires aerobic (needs oxygen) glycolysis and takes place in the powerhouse of the cell, the mitochondria. Pyruvic acid reacts with coenzyme A (CoA) and becomes acetyl CoA, moving to the TCA cycle. Once the TCA cycle is complete, carbon dioxide and ATP are produced. This aerobic pathway provides energy for longer-lasting activities like most activities of daily living (ADL).

Tab: Oxidative Phosphorylation

The final phase of making energy is oxidative phosphorylation, which is simply a fancy term for transporting a phosphate group from point A to point B. This process is very similar to a courier service, taking a product from one area to another. After this and other steps occur, water and ATP are produced.

Section 8: Explore: Fats as Energy

There are four types of fats with two being considered bad and two classified as good.

  • The unhealthy fats—saturated and trans fats—are unhealthy and can raise your low-density lipoproteins (LDL), or the "bad" cholesterol. When at room temperature, these types of fat are solid. Lard, chocolate, and margarine are examples.
  • The healthy fats—monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats—can lower your LDL level when consumed appropriately. The good types of fats are often liquid at room temperature, and examples include avocados and olive oil.

The reason fats can be deposited in and detrimental to the body is that they are not broken down in the liver before moving into the bloodstream. As fats are digested in the small intestine, they are absorbed into the lymphatic system. From there, fats move into the cardiovascular system and eventually reach the liver to be broken down. Conversely, carbohydrates and proteins are absorbed via the portal vein and carried to the liver before circulation.

Once fats are consumed, they are broken down into usable forms of energy. This process begins with the breaking down of fat by water, turning the fats into glycerol and 3 fatty acids. Glycerol is converted to pyruvic acid, which is used for energy. The three fatty acids are further broken down into acetyl coenzyme A (CoA) and move into the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle like the acetyl CoA from glucose.

Section 9: Explore: Proteins as Energy

Proteins are the third nutrient used for energy. Since proteins are composed of amino acids joined together by peptide bonds, it is easy to understand that they are broken down into these components using hydrolysis. Amino acid groups that contain nitrogen are processed in the liver. Other amino acids move into various parts of the protein pathway to create energy. Some are converted to pyruvic acid while others are part of the acetyl groups or part of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle.

Another important point to remember is that amino acids can be used for protein synthesis rather than the creation of energy. The body makes most amino acids, but there are several that humans only get from protein-rich foods. Like other nutrients, it is important to eat proteins in moderation, as excessive amounts can contribute to hyperlipidemia, coronary artery disease, and renal damage.

Section 11: Explore: Using and Storing Nutrients

Carbohydrates, fats, and proteins can be used for the creation of energy, but they can also be stored as fat all over the body. If the body does not need the nutrients because it has met its energy needs, the excess nutrients must be stored. Glycogen is the first stored type of energy used and is followed by energy in the adipose or fat cells. Proteins are typically stored and used for the repair of tissues and cells as needed. However, only some glycerol can result in glucose, which the brain constantly needs. Continual use of stored fat results in ketosis. A diet high in carbohydrates leads to increased glycogen storage, but the amount depends on the amount of activity level.

Section 13: Explore: Energy Balance and Needs

The amount of energy required by the body depends on various factors. Activity level, illness, injuries, and chronic diseases are important considerations for determining the amount of nutrients for a client. The recommendations from MyPlate, Healthy People 2030, and the United States Department of Agriculture's Dietary Guidelines for Americans are suggested guidelines for intake across the lifespan and based on light to moderate activity levels. It is essential to consider the activity level of the client and the size of the client to determine their accurate intake for healthy weight and energy needs.

To formulate a plan for each client, three areas should be considered for energy needs. These include total energy expenditure, physical activity, and the thermic effect of food. We will examine each of them individually and provide further clarification.

Section 15: Explore: Components of Total Energy Expenditure

Multiple factors affect the body's daily energy requirements, and the first is the amount of energy required to maintain essential functions such as breathing, circulation, heartbeat, and secretion of hormones.

Tab: Energy Calculation

There are two ways to determine how much energy is needed.

  • Basal metabolic rate (BMR): It is the most used method. Two methods can be used to determine BMR, including:
    • measuring body heat given off by a client
    • using a caliometer to measure the levels of oxygen and carbon dioxide exchanged

To calculate BMR, you would need to know the client's kilocalorie per kilogram per hour. Remember, 1 kilogram = 2.2 pounds. You would multiply that number by 24 hours to get the BMR per day.

  • Resting energy expenditure (REE): This component accounts for 70% of the total energy used in the body.

BMR/REE is the amount of energy needed to complete life-sustaining functions and is measured when the client wakes for the day before completing any physical activity. It is again completed 12-18 hours after the client's last meal.

Tab: Influencing Factors

Factors affecting BMR/REE include:

Non-Modifiable Factors

  • Age: Younger clients who are growing have a higher metabolism than adults over the age of 35. With age, lean muscle mass decreases.
  • Gender: In general, females have slower metabolisms than men, since men are typically larger.
  • Growth: Infants and children require more energy and have a higher BMR/REE due to growth and temperature regulation.
  • Hormonal controls: Controlled by the nervous and endocrine systems, the BMR/REE rate can change based on these influences (e.g., thyroid function – hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism).
  • Genetic influences: Hereditary factors can slow or raise your BMR/REE.
  • Body temperature: Being very hot or cold requires the body to use more energy and increases the BMR/REE.

Modifiable Factors

  • Lean muscle tissue: Muscle mass burns energy quickly and more efficiently than other sources.
  • Body fat: Higher levels of body fat burn less energy.
  • Physical activity: Increased activity requires energy for the muscles, and regularly exercising increases the BMR/REE, even at rest.
  • Diet: Strict reduction of caloric intake, as often seen in fads, crash diets, starvation, and fasting, slows the BMR to conserve energy. While many see short-term results on the scale or notice changes in the mirror, long-term reduction in metabolism can result in weight gain. Additionally, dietary deficiencies can slow BMR/REE.
  • Infection or illness: With these conditions, the body needs more energy for repair, so metabolism is increased.
  • Drugs: Stimulants like caffeine or nicotine can also increase metabolism.

Section 17: Explore: Physical Activity

Physical activity is the second component in estimating one's energy needs and accounts for 20-30% of an individual's total energy expenditure. The definition of physical activity is any body movement by the skeletal muscles that requires energy.

The amount of physical activity varies from person to person, based on duration and intensity. Even moderate activity can become a means of high energy expenditure if it's done for a long time.

Body size is the most influential factor that affects energy expenditure. The heavier the client, the more energy it takes to complete an activity. For example, a 110-pound client uses less energy than a 210-pound client to perform the same activity.

Section 18: Explore: Thermic Effect of Food

The third and final component of estimating energy needs is the thermic effect of food. This is the energy necessary to digest, absorb, metabolize, and store food that is eaten. While playing a critical role in the production of energy, it is only 7–10% of the body's total energy needs. After food is ingested, the body's cells increase their activities, and this is called the thermic effect of food or diet-induced thermogenesis. The increased activity to break down the food produces energy and heat, hence the name.

The body continually seeks to maintain homeostasis, and it must adjust to changes in physical and biological environmental situations. The energy used by the body to do this is called adaptive thermogenesis. Examples include physical and emotional trauma, extreme changes in diet lasting several days, and illness and recovery. As with other factors, the expenditure of energy depends on the client and the situation.

Section 3: Explore: The Deal With Fat

Fats are complex chemical structures that provide vital nutrients and energy to the body and make foods taste good. The downside of fats is that not all types of fat are good for the body.

Watch the video, The Deal With Fat [5:53], for a brief overview of the chemical facts of fats. (SciShow, 2015, December 21. The deal with fat [Video]. YouTube. https://youtu.be/mvvx2yQRbzQ)

Transcript:

In a saturated fat, every carbon is bonded to two hydrogens except the last, which is bonded to three. This chemical structure makes saturated fat tend to pack together easily, which can form plaques in the arteries and interact with cholesterol, making plaques build up in the bloodstream. This chemical structure also makes saturated fat tend to be solid (e.g., butter, coconut oil).

Unsaturated fat is not saturated because there is at least one double bond between two carbons in the chain. In nature, a double bond in a fat chain almost always locks it into a kink, which prevents the fat molecules from stacking into lattices and so don't form plaques, making them more likely to be liquid (e.g., olive oil, omega-3 acid).

Section 4: Explore: Lipid Profile

A lipid profile is a blood test used to assess a person's risk of developing cardiovascular disease (screening) and to evaluate treatment of unhealthy lipid levels (monitoring). Although most lipid profiles include total cholesterol, high-density lipoproteins, low-density lipoproteins, and triglycerides, the exact fats measured on a lipid profile may vary by laboratory.

Total Cholesterol Test It measures all the cholesterol in all the lipoprotein particles.

  • Normal Range: < 200 mg/dL

High-Density Lipoproteins (HDL) Test It measures the cholesterol in HDL particles. HDL is often called "good" cholesterol because HDL-cholesterol (HDL-C) takes up excess cholesterol and carries it to the liver for removal.

  • Normal Range: Male: 45 mg/dL | Female: 55 mg/dL

Low-Density Lipoproteins (LDL) Test It calculates or measures the cholesterol in LDL particles. Usually, the amount of LDL-cholesterol (LDL-C) is calculated using the results of total cholesterol, HDL-C, and triglycerides. LDL is often called "bad" cholesterol because it deposits excess cholesterol in walls of blood vessels, which can contribute to atherosclerosis.

  • Normal Range: < 100 mg/dL

Triglyceride Level Test It measures all the triglycerides in all the lipoprotein particles. Triglycerides are most in the very low-density lipoproteins (VLDL), which carry triglycerides in the blood.

  • Normal Range: 40–180 mg/dL
  • Note: Ranges and values may vary depending on laboratories, guidelines, the client's age, and comorbidities.

Section 3: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Screening Tools

Most nutritional screenings are triggered by weight loss in clients experiencing physical symptoms of malnutrition that occur over time (chronic malnutrition) and include the physical signs common with protein deficiency. Chronic protein malnutrition is often accompanied by other nutritional imbalances that must also be addressed to provide the body with the nutrients needed to prevent long-term complications and restore health.

Acute weight gain is often caused by fluid retention associated with a known alteration in health, such as heart or renal failure, and is treated as such.

Every client experiencing sudden weight gain or loss should receive a nutrition screening. The Joint Commission (TJC), an organization that accredits healthcare facilities, recommends that all clients whose condition could be related to altered nutrition be screened within 24 hours of admission (TJC, 2022). Each care facility may define the conditions that trigger the screening and the information to be gathered.

To assist with nutrition screening, many facilities develop tools based on the requirements outlined in their policies. One example can be seen in the nutrition care algorithm (American Society for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition).

Section 5: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Dietary Recall

There are several methods for collecting information regarding actual and habitual dietary intake. Most commonly, data is collected using dietary recall methods (historical/retrospective) or documented food records (current/prospective). Each method has its pros and cons, so it is important to choose a method suitable for the type of information needed.

Data is collected to provide information about calories (kcal), protein, carbohydrate, fat, vitamins, minerals, and fluid intake. Results can be calculated manually with food composition tables or analyzed by computer software. More than 100 computer programs are available to analyze dietary intake.

To complete the dietary recall assessment, the examiner asks the client to report all foods and beverages consumed during the past 1–3 days. Detailed descriptions of all foods, beverages, cooking methods, brand names, condiments, and supplements, along with portion sizes in common household measures, are included. Food models, measuring cups, life-size pictures, or abstract shapes (squares, circles, and rectangles) are used to assist the client in estimating the correct portion sizes of foods consumed. This method is useful in screening or during follow-up to evaluate adaptation of or compliance with dietary recommendations.

The advantages of dietary recall assessment are quick (only 15 to 20 minutes is needed) and applicable for most age groups. Because it is retrospective, the client does not modify their actual intake. The information can be obtained by face-to-face interview, telephone interview, or client self-reporting. However, the dietary recall relies on an individual's memory and awareness, so the information may not be accurate. And since dietary recall is assessed over the past 1 to 3 days, it may not provide a clear understanding of the client's current diet.

Section 7: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Food Journals

Keeping a food journal has become an established means of tracking foods eaten. It is a listing of all that is consumed, including portion sizes and time of day. Studies indicate that more success occurs when someone attempts to normalize food consumption and keeps written records of their food intake. Food journals, when used daily, provide a more accurate record of nutritional intake than dietary recall.

Food journals can be a single page, written in a diary or notebook, or electronically kept on a computer or mobile device. When caring for an individual with protein malnutrition, recommending the client track both what they have eaten and how they are feeling physically, emotionally, and cognitively provides both the nurse and client with a better understanding of how well their diet is supporting their health.

The discussions that occur when helping a client start a food journal and when reviewing a completed journal provide the nurse with cues regarding the client's dietary intake and opportunities to provide the client with nutritional education.

Section 9: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Laboratory Values

The three main proteins in serum plasma are albumins, globulins, and fibrinogen. Although globulins and fibrinogen are commonly reviewed as part of a nutritional assessment, decreased levels contribute to the physical changes associated with protein malnutrition, risk for infection (decreased serum globulins), and bleeding (decreased fibrinogen).

Key Laboratory Findings
  • Albumin: Maintains plasma oncotic pressure; transports small molecules
    • Normal: 3.5-5 g/dL (grams per deciliter)
    • Depletion: Mild 3.0-3.4 g/dL; Moderate 2.4-2.9 g/dL; Severe <2.4 g/dL
  • Prealbumin: Carrier protein for thyroxin; transports vitamin A when combined with retinol-binding protein
    • Normal: 16-40 mg/dL (milligrams per deciliter)
    • Depletion: Mild 10-15 mg/dL; Moderate 5-9 mg/dL; Severe <5 mg/dL
  • Hemoglobin: Carries oxygen to cells; makes blood cells red
    • Normal: Females 12.1-15.1 g/dL; Males 13.8-17.2 g/dL
    • Depletion in general: Mild anemia 10-12 g/dL; Moderate anemia 8-10 g/dL; Severe anemia <8 g/dL

Section 11: Explore: Assessing Protein Status—Cultural & Social Influences

People around the world associate food with social interaction. Whether dining with family, friends, colleagues, or others, most meals are shared with others. And the people with whom food is shared tend to influence an individual's dietary preferences and habits. The nurse is responsible for discovering how the client's cultural and societal influences impact their overall nutritional status, including consumption of healthy protein.

Tab: Culture

It is important that the nurse understands how an individual's cultural knowledge and attitudes guide their food choices, particularly those customs with symbolic or religious implications. Since it is important to understand the food practices of diverse groups, asking the right questions is key.

  • Which food practices are influenced by the client's culture?
  • Who shops for and prepares the food?
  • If fasting is required, what does that mean? Can any nutrients be consumed during the fast?
  • Does the client abstain from certain foods (alcohol, meat, sweets)?
Tab: Economics

Determine if the client has the resources to get healthy foods, either by purchasing or through community resources (food pantry, community kitchen, governmental assistance).

Tab: Education

Assess the client's understanding of protein: How it supports health, sources of lean proteins, restriction of proteins related to current health status.

Tab: Neighborhood / Environment

Ask the client if they feel safe when getting groceries and if they are accessible to the client. Some neighborhoods, known as food deserts, do not have grocery stores. For individuals without independent transportation, getting to the store to purchase food can be difficult.

Tab: Social Context

Changes within a client's family or social group, such as loss of a loved one or going away to college, impact individuals differently, and may interfere with the desire to eat due to social isolation.

Section 12: Explore: Nursing Process—Protein Imbalance

For clients with protein deficit, the main nursing diagnosis focuses on the alteration in nutrition, with actions and outcomes selected to promote and monitor their progress toward an optimal nutritional balance.

Additional nursing diagnoses may be added to address physical alterations, such as increased bleeding or infection, that result from the nutritional deficit.

Nursing Diagnosis for a client with protein malnutrition following the death of a spouse.

Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements related to inability to access protein, decreased appetite while grieving the loss of spouse as evidenced by 15-pound weight loss in the last month, hair loss, increased bruising, frequent urinary tract infections, and lethargy.

Tab: Expected Outcomes
  • No further weight loss
  • Steady weight gain of 1-2 pounds per week until reaches ideal body weight
  • Consumes 80-90% of all meals and snacks
  • Agrees to plan for accessing quality foods, including protein, after discharge.
Tab: Nursing Actions
  • Provide companionship during meals.
    • Rationale: Attention to the social perspectives of eating is important in both hospital and home settings.
  • Offer high protein supplements based on individual needs and capabilities.
    • Rationale: Such supplements can be used to increase calories and protein without conflict with voluntary food intake.
  • Validate the client's feelings regarding the impact of their current lifestyle, finances, and transportation on their ability to obtain nutritious food.
    • Rationale: Validation lets the client know that the nurse has heard and understands what was said, and it promotes the nurse-client relationship.
  • Help the client establish a written plan to obtain and consume a variety of foods.
    • Rationale: Grief can be overwhelming, leaving individuals without the ability to make even simple choices. Creating a written plan for nutrition provides the client with directions and eliminates the need to make decisions.

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Energy

Nurses play a large role in client education. Promoting a healthy lifestyle, weight, and energy balance is no exception. Although there are so many diet myths today, nurses can positively influence a client's well-being.

Upon completion of this learning activity, you will gain the skills needed to:

  • Describe the nurse's role in promoting a healthy lifestyle and energy balance.
  • Differentiate positive and negative lifestyle behaviors that affect energy.
  • Assess a client's energy level and the factors that contribute to it.
  • Explain nursing diagnoses related to energy.
  • Plan, implement, and evaluate care for clients with energy imbalances.

Section 3: Explore: Client Energy Sources

Clients have multiple factors that influence energy levels. It is important for nurses to complete a thorough history to obtain both subjective and objective data to gain an overall picture of health. Because people get vital nutrients from the foods they eat, it is essential to evaluate the client's dietary intake and nutritional status.

Energy in the human body must be converted from nutritional sources into chemical energy. It is the progression of associated chemical reactions that are interdependent. There are two main energy pathways: anabolism and catabolism. These two processes together are called metabolism. They are interdependent and closely regulated in the body by hormones. Both energy pathways work together to produce energy for life-sustaining functions. The primary source of fuel for energy is food. The measurement of energy released from food is called kilocalories or calories.

In the human body, foods provide a great source of energy, but they must be transformed. After foods are ingested, the processes of digestion, absorption, and metabolism occur. The three energy-supplying macronutrients that we receive from food are carbohydrates, fats, and proteins.

Section 5: Explore: Amount of Energy Needed

The amount of energy required by the body depends on various factors. Activity level, illness, injuries, and chronic diseases are important considerations for determining the amount of nutrients for a client.

The recommendations from MyPlate, Healthy People 2030, and the United States Department of Agriculture's Dietary Guidelines for Americans have suggested guidelines for intake across the lifespan for light-to-moderate activity levels. It is essential to consider the activity level of the client and the size of the client to determine the accurate intake for healthy weight and energy needs.

To formulate a plan for each client, three areas should be considered for energy needs. These include total energy expenditure, physical activity, and the thermic effect of food.

Section 6: Explore: Nurses' Role in Client Education on Energy

Nurses can play a role in explaining the concept of fueling the body for energy purposes and focusing on health promotion and disease prevention. With the general population being bombarded with advertisements for multiple diets and "healthy foods," providing accurate information is essential.

Nurses can provide support and work as part of an interdisciplinary team of dietitians, healthcare providers, behavior therapists, and psychologists. Help clients understand that a healthy lifestyle can include enjoying food and making choices about how much, when, and where foods are eaten.

The traditional approach of exercising more and eating less is not the method that is currently promoted. In the past, dietitians have been consultants; now they are coaches and counselors, assisting clients with lifestyle changes. The focus of the long-term approach should be client-centered, rather than being on a "diet." Accepting genetics and the concept that wellness is the focus of health will also aid in the lifestyle change process.

Section 8: Explore: Assessment of Energy

Energy requirements vary throughout the lifespan, among genders, and also with varying medical conditions. The nurse needs to use assessment skills to determine the appropriate energy needs for the client. Estimated calorie needs are determined by using the Estimated Energy Requirements formula involving heights and weights. For the most part, levels of activity vary from sedentary to very active, with different scales providing different classifications. One such scale is presented here.

Additionally, clients should be assessed to determine the following:

  • Typical dietary intake — Identify protein-calorie malnutrition, vitamin deficiencies, or iron deficiencies.
  • Ability to perform activities of daily living — Can the client meet his/her needs?
  • Perception of energy — Does the client feel that they have adequate energy each day? Does sleep each night provide rest and energy?
  • Medical diagnoses — Evaluate medical history to determine possible implications for change in energy. Conditions like heart failure, kidney disease, cancer, and pregnancy can alter energy. Evaluate labs such as blood glucose, electrolytes, BUN, creatinine, and oxygen saturation (resting and with activity).
  • Medications — Certain medications can cause an increase or decrease in energy.
  • Sleep disorders — Sleep apnea, insomnia, and night terrors can interfere with energy levels, the amount of sleep received each night, and the need for sleep aids.
  • Physical activity — What is the client's activity status? What physical exercise is done and how often?
  • Emotional status — Depression and other mental illnesses can cause alterations in energy.

Formulating nursing diagnoses depends on the information obtained in the assessment. Various reasons can cause the client to not have the energy needed. Possible nursing diagnoses include:

Section 10: Explore: Planning and Interventions for Energy

Planning and interventions for clients with energy needs include the following:

Tab: Nutritional Needs and Access
  • Determine client-specific nutritional needs based on age and developmental stage (e.g., infants need essential nutrients in formula and baby foods).
  • Explain nutritional needs and plan dietary consultations and healthy meal resources.
  • Establish a dietary plan for each client including detailed information on portion control, moderation, focusing on healthier alternatives, etc. This can be used for clients with excessive or inadequate intake.
  • Determine and educate clients on nutritional resources in the area for access to food, like food banks, church food pantries, and government assistance, to meet dietary needs despite economic ability.
Tab: Activity and Exercise
  • Implement energy-saving strategies for clients who are unable to be active due to medical conditions (e.g., delegating tasks to others and planning daily activities).
  • Provide activities and establish a plan for clients who are unable to exercise due to medical conditions (e.g., stretching and hand weights).
Tab: Sleep and Distress
  • Develop and teach strategies to promote healthy sleep patterns (e.g., routine, no electronics at night, and resources for finding a safe place to rest).

Section 1: Introduction to Weight

Weight is often associated with the presence or absence of fat, but there are many factors that contribute to an individual's weight. When you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills to:

  • Describe components of a healthy weight.
  • Explain measuring body fatness.
  • Identify methods to interpret weight.
  • Distinguish between body types and genetic influences on body size and shape.
  • Compare appropriate essentials of weight.
  • Determine essentials of fitness.
  • Differentiate food requirements for athletic performance.

Section 3: Explore: Weight Explained

Weight is an essential component of healthcare when caring for a client. Today's focus is on promoting and assisting clients to maintain a healthy weight. This is defined as a client who can perform basic physical activities of daily living, move comfortably, maintain a weight without restrictive diet or excessive exercise by following healthy eating guidelines, and not experience weight-related disorders. Clients can struggle with weight issues such as being underweight, overweight, or unable to maintain a healthy weight throughout their life.

Weight alone does not determine a person's level of health. Someone can have a high body weight with significant amounts of lean muscle contributing to the weight. Likewise, someone can have a low body weight, yet a high percentage of fat to lean muscle tissue. Weight is used because of the ease and convenience of measurement. There are several methods to determine more accurate body composition (lean body mass plus fat).

In clients who have impaired renal function or premenstrual syndrome, fluid retention can cause a change in weight. Additionally, clients who take diuretics or visit the sauna can have a rapid reduction in fluid loss, which will also affect the number on the scale and not actual body composition.

Section 4: Explore: Body Composition Measurement Methods

Besides weight, here are some ways to measure body size and composition:

Tab: Densitometry or Hydrostatic Weight

Densitometry or hydrostatic weight involves underwater weighing of the client and is the standard to assess other measures. However, having clients submerged in water is not always practical or available. The apparatus is bulky, expensive, and not quick to use. Additionally, many clients do not want to be fully submerged underwater.

Video: Watch the video, "How to Perform Hydrostatic Weight" [3:23], to learn how this weight measuring method is performed. PolyFit CP. (2012, October 23). How to perform hydrostatic weight [Video]. YouTube. https://youtu.be/NEUSzn1Jc88

Tab: Bioelectric Impedance Analysis (BIA)

Bioelectric impedance analysis (BIA) is a more widely used and practical method for determining body composition. It is measured in one of two ways. One is a handheld monitor where the client's height, weight, and age are entered, and they grip the handles. Another method involves placing electrodes on the wrists and ankles.

Both methods use transmission of a very mild electrical current throughout the body. Body fat percentage is determined based on the ease of transmission of the current. The higher the fat percentage, the slower the transmission of the current.

Tab: Body Mass Index (BMI)

Body mass index (BMI) is another way to interpret weight. It is measured by dividing the client's weight in kilograms by the square of the client's height in meters. As demonstrated in the image, there is a continuum that ranges from underweight to extremely (morbidly) obese.

While this calculation can apply equally well to both males and females, it does not take lean muscle mass into consideration. For example, someone who has very little muscle mass can be considered "normal," while someone who has a high muscle mass, like an athlete, can be categorized as obese or extremely obese.

Section 5: Explore: Body Fat Functions

Body fat serves various vital functions in the body. The most well-known function is that of storage fat, which can be utilized as needed for energy. The minimal, protective layer under the skin is also essential.

In men, essential fat accounts for approximately 3–8% of body weight, and the appropriate total storage and essential fat range is 15–20% of body weight. Measuring the waist to hip circumference ratio is a good indicator of body fat distribution in males. This ratio should be less than 0.95 to 1. Additionally, waist circumference alone is a good indicator of fatness as well.

In females, there is a larger amount of gender-specific fat. Healthy totals of essential and storage fat range from 25–35% of total body weight. A healthy hip to waist circumference ratio should be 0.8 or less.

Athletes may have fat levels below these ranges, but they should be monitored. This is especially important in women, where menstrual irregularities and amenorrhea (no menstrual cycle) are correlated with osteoporotic bone loss and fractures.

Section 7: Explore: Changes in Fatness

The shape of the body can influence characteristics of fat and potential risk factors. Here are some common body shapes and characteristics that affect health:

Tab: Android Body Type (Apple)

Characteristics:

  • It is largest around the waist, the middle.
  • It is common in both men and women during menopause.
  • Clients have a higher waist circumference than recommended.
  • It leads to an increased risk for weight-related diseases and disorders.
  • Visceral abdominal fat is dangerous, but it is also easier to lose.
Tab: Gynoid Body Type (Pear)

Characteristics:

  • Fat is primarily stored in the lower body (hips, thighs, buttocks).
  • More common in women, especially before menopause.
  • Clients have higher levels of estrogen, with a risk of insulin resistance.
  • Fat is more difficult to lose.

Regardless of body type, genetics plays a role in body shape and fat distribution. While exercise and activity can decrease the amount of fat, the distribution is based on individual DNA. Fat is mostly stored in adipocytes, special cells that are designed to hold fat droplets. This fat is in the form of triglycerides, produced from glucose, glycerol, fatty acids, and amino acids. The amount of stored fat is dependent upon energy balance. If energy is needed, the fat is broken down for transport to cells; and if it is not needed, the fat becomes stored. At birth, adipocyte numbers are relatively low and grow during phases of development and growth.

A pound of body fat is approximately 3,500 kilocalories. This means that an excess intake and positive energy balance of 3,500 calories can cause a weight gain of 1 pound, and a negative energy balance or expenditure of 3,500 kilocalories can cause a weight loss of 1 pound. This energy balance is determined by the intake to energy expenditure ratio.

Section 9: Explore: Influences on Weight

Despite the size and amount of adipocytes, energy expenditure, and genetic makeup, a client's weight is significantly impacted by nutritional, psychological, economic, and social factors. All these factors should be considered when evaluating a client's weight.

Slide 1: Nutritional and Financial Factors

The nutritional content and financial ability to buy food can impact the client's weight. If the client lives in a food desert, does not have financial resources, or does not have transportation to access a variety of foods to meet the recommended MyPlate Guidelines, the client's weight will be impacted. In addition, the convenience and low cost of prepackaged and fast foods are often appealing and much less expensive than foods that are rich in nutrients.

Slide 2: Psychological Factors

Psychological factors also play a role in the weight of a client. It is important to monitor the emotional and behavioral status of clients to evaluate for possible eating disorders or traumas. Weight-related abuse can be detrimental to one's intake. Pregnant women are especially vulnerable to abuse due to weight changes. Altered body image and cultural views on weight can also impact the client's weight.

Slide 3: Social Factors

Social factors are also large influences on weight change and intake. Food is often seen as a social event or incorporated into celebrations. Likewise, food can become solace for those who are depressed or even bored. Cultural implications can also be included in social factors, as food plays a large role in many cultures as well. The environment of the client is also important. If the client is in an unsafe area where exercise outside is restricted, it can be classified as an obesogenic environment, one that contributes to obesity.

Section 10: Explore: Physical Activity Versus Sedentary Lifestyle

Physical activity is a core component of maintaining a healthy weight. Just as we promote ambulation for gastrointestinal (GI) motility and recovery from illness, getting moving is essential! When you choose a sedentary lifestyle, you are putting yourself at risk for serious health consequences. Being physically active provides a great deal of benefits for both your physical and emotional health.

Benefits of Physical Activity

  • Increases muscle and bone strength
  • Increases heart rate and respiratory effort
  • Decreases depression
  • Increases the ability to move around
  • Increases self-esteem
  • Promotes better sleep and rest
  • Promotes healthy weight

Increased Risks With Sedentary Lifestyle

  • Heart disease
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Depression
  • Stroke
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Weight gain
  • Loss of strength

Section 12: Explore: Wellness Approach to Weight

Although your readings, MyPlate, and Healthy People 2030 provide recommended daily food intake and energy expenditure, there is no "quick fix" or magic formula that changes everyone's size or shape to that desired. The overarching goal of these recommendations is not a diet but a lifestyle and attitude change. Weight can fluctuate, and no one eats and exercises perfectly all the time. Adopting a diet is often done for a short amount of time, and people return to their bad habits. Instead, wellness must be the focus for success. Fueling our bodies with the correct nutrients and being physically active provides benefits that are more than just externally visible.

Strategies for maintaining healthy weight success include:

Tab: Adopt an Active Lifestyle
  • Increase physical activity such as walking and gardening.
  • Choose to do body movement that is produced by the skeletal muscles, requiring energy.
  • Exercise does not have to be strenuous to be beneficial. Small changes have big impacts, like walking rather than driving, parking in a further parking space, increasing steps per day, or choosing the stairs rather than the elevator.
Tab: Establishing Realistic Goals
  • Identify individual needs.
  • Examine the limit and control one has over the body and amount of stored fat, considering nonmodifiable factors such as genetics and fat distribution.
Tab: Changing Behavior
  • The most important goals to establish are related to changing behavior to find habits that can be lifelong and integrated into the lifestyle.
  • Examples include planning meals, grocery lists, limiting access to junk foods, and so forth.
Tab: Normalizing Eating
  • Realize that eating is a normal and necessary process that is enjoyable.
  • Establish a good relationship with all foods to learn balance and moderation.
  • Use hunger cues to guide eating rather than "cleaning the plate" or eating because it's time.
Tab: Minimizing Food for Emotional Needs

Address emotions and any emotional eating that is associated. Try journaling, doing exercise, eating without guilt, and arranging safe circumstances for eating.

Tab: Eating Regularly and Frequently

Space meals and balance nutrients to fuel the body's energy needs all day long rather than one or two large meals per day.

Section 15: Explore: Food and Athletic Performance

As with any physical activity, athletes need food as a source of fuel for energy. Just as a car cannot run without fuel before it stops working, the body needs the appropriate fuel for the task. These are important components of ensuring that all nutritional needs are met when planning for the athlete:

Tab: Kilocalories

Minimum requirements: 1,800–2,000 kilocalories/day with the following criteria:

  • Carbohydrates: 45–65% of kilocalories
  • Protein: 10–35% of kilocalories
  • Fat: 20–35% of kilocalories

Athletes should eat at least the minimum number of servings from each group above daily. Depending on body size, some athletes need more.

Tab: Hydration

Requirement: water and/or electrolyte replacement drinks

Water is the most crucial nutrient for athletic performance. Lack of hydration can present itself in less than 1 hour. Loss of 1 pound of body weight is equivalent to 480 mL (2 cups) of water that needs to be replaced.

Sports with moderate-heavy exercise and continuous activity longer than 90 minutes can benefit from sports drinks with electrolytes to prevent hyponatremia from water alone.

Tab: Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates provide glycogen that delays the onset of fatigue and allows for longer activity. Both simple and complex carbohydrates are needed.

Carbohydrate loading, increasing the number of carbs eaten, can be beneficial if the amount or length of training is increased, increasing glycogen stores for muscles.

Tab: Protein

With athletic activity, protein use is increased. Many athletes eat more protein than recommended, so the excess protein can be broken down into amino acids then used for energy or stored as fat.

Tab: Fat

The amount of fat used during exercise varies based on duration and intensity of the activity, prior level of training, and composition of diet.

Tab: Vitamins and Minerals

A balanced diet provides the necessary vitamins and minerals. Many athletes use supplements, but it is not necessary or proven to be beneficial. Toxicity can occur if both the supplement and the proper diet are maintained.
