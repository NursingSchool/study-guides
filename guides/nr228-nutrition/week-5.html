Section 1: Introduction to Adult Nutrition

Nutrition plays a large role in successful aging. Consuming a healthy diet is critical in meeting physical and psychological needs as well as preventing disease.

George (pronouns: he/him/his), 77 years old, and Diane (pronouns: she/her/hers), 72 years old, struggle with nutrition. In this concept, you will follow this couple and learn about their nutritional requirements as well as factors that affect their nutrition.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Explain the nutrient requirements of adults.
  • Discuss the influence of diet on reducing health risks.
  • Describe how factors, including oral health, appetite, senses, swallowing, depression, finances, mobility, and culture, influence adult nutrition.

Section 3: Explore: Adult Nutrient Requirements

According to the latest Dietary Guidelines for Americans (USDA & DHHS, 2020), small changes in daily eating habits are encouraged to improve health. The focus is on following healthy combinations of food and drink over time, increasing nutrient-dense foods, limiting calories from sugars and fats, and decreasing sodium.

Watch the video "What's Your Healthy Eating Style" [1:45] (external supplementary resource), which illustrates the above guidelines for ways of choosing healthy food and maintaining a healthy lifestyle.

George and Diane reflected on what they normally eat and their sedentary lifestyle. Like many Americans, they don't get enough servings of fruits and vegetables and eat above the recommended amount of grains. When estimating or calculating the amount of calories for themselves, George and Diane need to consider factors, such as age, sex, height, weight, and level of physical activity, that impact the total number of calories.

Section 4: Explore: Nutrition and the Older Adult

As George and Diane get even older, their nutrient requirements remain the same. According to Volkert et al. (2018), the older adult's body will continue to decrease the ability to absorb and synthesize nutrients. The tabs below include risk factors that decrease older adults' nutrition intake and absorption. In their 80's and 90's, it will be important to make sure they do not become malnourished and dehydrated.

Risk factors attributed to older adults' nutrition health and the corresponding interventions:

Chewing Problems — Interventions:

  • Oral care
  • Dental treatment
  • Texture-modified diet, if adequate

Swallowing Problems (Dysphagia) — Interventions:

  • Professional swallowing evaluation
  • Swallowing training
  • Texture-modified diet, according to swallowing evaluation

Impaired Upper Extremity Function — Interventions:

  • Physiotherapy, occupational therapy
  • Adequate help with eating and drinking (e.g. cutting food, hand-feeding)
  • Provision of adequate eating and drinking aids
  • Finger foods
  • Shopping/cooking aid, meals on wheels

Restricting Mobility, Immobility — Interventions:

  • Physiotherapy
  • Resistance training
  • Group exercise
  • Shopping/cooking aid, meals on wheels
  • Family-style meals in institutions

Depressive Mood, Depression — Interventions:

  • Eating and drinking with others/shared meals
  • Pleasant meal ambiance/eating environment
  • Group activities, occupational therapy

Loneliness, Social Isolation — Interventions:

  • Eating and drinking with others/shared meals
  • Group activities

Poverty — Intervention:

  • Social programs

Acute Disease, (Chronic) Pain — Intervention:

  • Adequate medical treatment

Adverse Effects of Medication (e.g., xerostomia, apathy) — Interventions:

  • Check medication for potential side effects
  • Reduce the dose of medication
  • Replace or stop the medication

Restricted Diets — Intervention:

  • Revision and liberalization of dietary restrictions

Section 6: Explore: Oral Health Considerations

Oral health can have a large impact on nutrition and food consumption. According to Eilender (2016), in the population of older adults 65 and older:

  • 40% suffer from periodontal disease.
  • Only 22% have dental insurance.
  • 25% do not have natural teeth.

George has difficulty chewing since he has some missing teeth. He cannot afford partial dentures or implants. Where he grew up, there was no water fluoridation or use of products with fluoride. George first visited a dentist when he was 19, since his family did not have money for dental visits. In addition, George takes a medication that causes dry mouth. Therefore, he tends to eat foods that are soft and drink more flavorful beverages like sweet tea. He knows he is at risk for choking and asphyxiation due to his reduced chewing ability.

To promote oral health, George's dentist suggested these oral health tips (CDC, 2021):

  • Limit sugar intake.
  • Drink fluoridated water and brush with fluoride toothpaste.
  • Brush teeth twice a day and floss daily.
  • Visit the dentist at least once a year.
  • Do not use any tobacco products and limit alcohol consumption.

Section 8: Explore: Physical Considerations

The senses of smell and taste play a key role in appetite, food choices, and food intake as it promotes appetite and allows recognition of flavors (Boyce & Shone, 2006). As the body ages, the sense of smell and the ability to discriminate between smells declines. The function of taste also declines and coincides with lack of smell. Some medical conditions and medications affect these senses. Medical conditions can also affect swallowing abilities or mobility, causing difficulties with eating. It can restrict the ability to prepare food as well as eat the food.

Diane has Parkinson's disease, which is a medical condition that can diminish smell and taste. The medications she takes also cause nausea if not taken on an empty stomach. She knows that most people with Parkinson's disease eat less and lose weight. Since the disease is progressive, she may eventually have problems with swallowing. The tremors may make it difficult for her to eat certain foods and drink liquids. Her healthcare provider suggested that she try spicier foods and herbs to stimulate her appetite. She should eat foods that are easy to swallow and high in water content to stay hydrated. A dietitian may be useful if her symptoms change (Parkinson's Foundation, n.d.).

Section 10: Explore: Psychological Considerations

Ever since his wife has been diagnosed with Parkinson's disease, George has had depression. He has a loss of appetite, sadness, and decreased motivation to participate in activities. He consoles himself mainly with food. He knows that certain foods, like french fries and cookies, are not good for him, but when he is feeling down, it seems to cheer him up.

His healthcare provider started him on an antidepressant and supplements. Commonly, clients with mental disorders are low in omega–3 fatty acids, B vitamins, minerals, and amino acids, which are precursors to neurotransmitters such as serotonin (Rao et al., 2008).

Section 11: Explore: Financial Considerations

George and Diane are one of the 13.7 million households in the United States that deal with food insecurity (Economic Research Service, 2022). They live off Social Security benefits but are left with no extra money at the end of each month. Like 3 out of 5 adults over the age of 60, they qualify for the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) but are not enrolled (National Council on Aging, 2021). George believes that even with financial assistance to buy food, they would still have difficulties getting to the store, which would cost more money. He also doesn't want his family to know that they cannot afford the food they need.

Overall, seniors are more likely to suffer from food insecurity if they live in a southern state, have a disability, are younger than age 69, live with a grandchild, and are African American or Hispanic (National Council on Aging, 2021).

Section 13: Explore: Cultural Considerations

For many people, food is based on culture. Most people acquire nutritional habits from childhood learning and the environment in which they grew up (Reddy & Anitha, 2015). The way food is selected, prepared, and stored is also culturally influenced. Religion is also a factor in nutrition, as some practices incorporate fasting or eating of particular foods. Healthcare providers need to be aware of cultural influences and adapt diets appropriately. Most times there are combinations of foods that will give the same nutritional results.

Both George and Diane grew up in rural Louisiana eating Creole foods. Most Creole dishes, like Cajun, consist of "the Holy trinity" of onions, celery, and green bell peppers. Creole dishes also use cream, butter, seafood, tomatoes, and herbs. They are less spicy than Cajun dishes. Diane used to do most of the cooking and was quite popular for her homemade Shrimp Étouffée. However, with her change in medical conditions and their lack of income, George and Diane struggle to maintain their food culture.

Reference

Reddy, S., & Anitha, M. (2015). Culture and its influence on nutrition and oral health [Special Edition]. Biomedical and Pharmacology Journal, 8, 613–620. https://dx.doi.org/10.13005/bpj/757

Section 1: Introduction to Childhood and Adolescent Nutrition

During childhood, children learn about healthy eating habits and form attitudes about food and food choices. There is incredible cognitive and physical development during this time.

This learning activity will cover the preschool years (ages 2-6), the school-aged years (ages 6-12), and adolescents (ages 12-18). You will follow a family with four children—Jake (aged 17), Josh (aged 15), Amanda (aged 12), and Karisa (aged 7).

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Describe nutrient requirements for early and middle childhood.
  • Describe nutrient requirements for adolescents.
  • Identify factors that influence nutrition in childhood.
  • Discuss school and community programs available for children.

Section 3: Explore: Nutrient Requirements of Early and Middle Childhood

Compared to infants, who triple their birth weight in the first year, young children have a slow down in growth. Children grow in spurts of height and weight followed by periods of little to no growth.

Tab: Measuring Growth

Growth is measured by body mass index (BMI) charts that are age and gender-specific. The BMI is compared to that of other children of the same gender and age. The chart is divided into percentiles that indicate whether a child has a healthy weight, is underweight, overweight, or obese.

View these resources from the CDC, Boys Stature Age Weight Growth Chart and Girls Stature Age Weight Growth Chart, as examples to understand how a child's growth is measured at each stage of life.

Unfortunately, most children do not follow these recommendations. Tab: Nutritional Concerns

Teenagers require key micronutrients and macronutrients to support growth. However, research studies show that most teenagers do not get adequate amounts of calcium and vitamin D (Eilender, 2016). Calcium and vitamin D during this stage in life are critical to building bone mass. By the time they reach age 18, adolescents have developed almost all of their bone mass. After that, bones get denser with age. Adolescents also need more iron for growth, blood volume, immunity, and cognitive development.

Nutritional guidance is important to develop a healthy adult lifestyle, prevent weight problems, reduce disease risk, and ensure growth needs are met. The American Academy of Pediatrics (2009) encourages healthcare professionals to address five nutritional concerns with adolescents:

  • low consumption of fruit and vegetables
  • low consumption of whole grains
  • low consumption of calcium and low-fat dairy foods
  • high consumption of sweetened beverages
  • frequent consumption of fast food

Section 9: Explore: Nutrition and Athletics

During childhood and adolescence, many children are involved in sports, in which weight and nutrition are emphasized.

Tab: Weight Control and Concerns

Weight control, gain or loss, can be perceived as advantageous for the individual athlete and/or the team. This table lists the sports that have different emphases on weight control.

However, there are dangerous ways to reduce weight including "food restriction, vomiting, overexercising, diet-pill use, inappropriate use of prescribed stimulants or insulin, nicotine use, and voluntary dehydration," which can entail fluid restriction, spitting, and the use of laxatives or diuretics (Committee on Sports Medicine and Fitness, 2005, p. 1557). Since these practices jeopardize nutritional needs and hydration, they should be prohibited at all ages.

  • delayed physical maturation
  • oligomenorrhea and amenorrhea in females
  • development of eating disorders
  • potential permanent growth impairment
  • increased risk of infectious diseases
  • changes in the cardiovascular, endocrine, gastrointestinal, renal, and thermoregulatory systems
  • depression

Tab: Calories Required for Athletes

The amount of calories an athlete needs is based on many factors such as body composition, age, state of growth, and level of fitness. Overall, athletes need to eat enough to cover the energy needed for the sport and daily living.

  • Weight loss, when necessary, should be gradual at 1 to 2 pounds each week.
  • To gain one pound of muscle, the calorie intake needs to be increased by about 2,000-2,500 kcal more than is being expended, the amount of protein consumed should be 1.5 g to 1.75 g of protein per kg of body weight per day, and strength training should be core fitness (Committee on Sports Medicine and Fitness, 2005).

It is important to emphasize to athletes and caregivers that nutritional needs for growth and development should be placed above athletic requirements.

Section 11: Explore: Body Image and Weight

Childhood and adolescence are times when the body image becomes defined. Select each tab to learn what research has found and what nursing interventions can help children with body image and weight concerns.

Tab: Negative Body Image

Jankauskiene and Baceviciene (2019) surveyed 579 adolescents of both genders. They concluded that children who overestimate body weight and have a higher body mass index (BMI) demonstrate increased body image concerns, poor eating habits, and low self-esteem. Despite dissatisfaction with weight or image, these children did not use physical exercise or healthy eating to address the issues. They admitted to skipping meals, especially breakfast, which can lead to eating disorders.

The results "support the idea that teaching about positive body image and adequate body weight estimation must be a part of all evidence-based healthy nutrition, physical activity, and weight control-related health education programs" (Jankauskiene & Baceviciene, 2019, Conclusions section).

Tab: Maternal Influences

It is essential that healthcare providers and educators do not shame a child who has body image or weight concerns. Many factors can play a role in these concerns. Rodgers et al. (2020) examined how the attitude, behaviors, and comments by mothers regarding body weight and eating impacted the thoughts of their children. They found that negative body and weight comments were associated with poorer child outcomes.

Their findings suggest that the family environment can play a role, and opinions about body image and nutrition are transferrable to children. Besides promoting positive body image, interventions should be directed at parent communication and the behaviors within the environment.

Morning Sickness:

Hyperemesis Gravidarum:

Section 3: Explore: Food Safety Risks

Foodborne illnesses can affect anyone at any age. They are caused by contamination from bacteria, viruses, parasites, or toxins. According to the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (2020), some people are at higher risk for serious foodborne illnesses. These groups are:

  • children under 5 years
  • adults aged 65 and older
  • people with weakened immune systems
  • pregnant women

Children Under 5 Years Old: Children are at an increased risk because their immune systems are still developing. Stomach acid that kills unsafe bacteria is produced less. Foodborne illnesses often cause vomiting or diarrhea. Children are at high risk for dehydration. E. coli is the most common foodborne illness.

Adults Aged 65 and Older: As we age, our bodies slow down. Stomach acid is less, thus allowing more bacteria to flourish. Peristalsis slows, holding onto bacteria in the gastrointestinal tract longer. The excretion of toxins by the liver and kidneys is decreased. The immune system's functions also decline. More than half of all Listeria infections occur in adults aged 65 and older.

People With Weakened Immune Systems: Some people have health conditions or take medications that decrease the body's immune response. If they get a foodborne illness, they tend to have more severe, longer illnesses and a higher chance of hospitalization or death.

Pregnant Women: Pregnancy changes the immune system of the mother and puts the unborn child at risk. Some foodborne illnesses can infect the fetus without the mother feeling sick. There are specific guidelines about food choices that pregnant women need to follow to decrease their risks.

Section 5: Explore: Obesity

Malnutrition includes nutritional deficiencies and excesses.

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, from 2017 to 2020, the prevalence of obesity in adults was 41.9% (CDC, 2022a) and 19.7% in children (CDC, 2022b). Obesity is related to many chronic health problems, including type 2 diabetes and heart disease. Healthy People 2030 has a goal to reduce obesity by encouraging healthy eating habits and physical activity.

Healthy People 2030 (n.d.) objectives include:

  • Reduce the proportion of adults with obesity.
  • Reduce the proportion of children and adolescents with obesity.
  • Reduce the proportion of adults who don't know they have prediabetes.
  • Increase the proportion of eligible people completing CDC-recognized type 2 diabetes prevention programs.
  • Increase the proportion of healthcare visits by adults with obesity, which includes counseling on weight loss, nutrition, or physical activity.
  • Reduce consumption of added sugars by people aged 2 years and over.
  • Increase the proportion of women who had a healthy weight before pregnancy.

Cut Out Added Sugars! (transcript)

Consuming too much can hurt your health and shorten your life. The American Heart Association recommends daily limits for added sugars:

  • Men: 9 teaspoons / 36 grams / 150 calories or less
  • Women and Kids: 6 teaspoons / 25 grams / 100 calories or less

Watch for added sugars in sugary drinks, syrups and sweets, sweetened breakfasts, sweet baked goods, and frozen treats.

Section 6: Explore: Undernutrition

There are four types of undernutrition: wasting, stunting, underweight, and deficiencies (World Health Organization, 2021).

Tab: Wasting

Wasting is defined as a low weight-for-height. This is mainly caused by a lack of food to eat or an infectious disease that causes diarrhea and weight loss. Treatment is possible but there is a high chance of death.

Tab: Stunting

Stunting is defined as a low height-for-age. It is caused by chronic undernutrition "usually associated with poor socioeconomic conditions, poor maternal health and nutrition, frequent illness, and/or inappropriate infant and young child feeding and care in early life" (World Health Organization, 2021, Undernutrition section). It can be the reason for physical and cognitive delays.

Tab: Underweight

Underweight is defined as low weight-for-age. A child considered underweight may be wasted, stunted, or both.

Tab: Deficiencies

Some people have an inadequate intake of vitamins and minerals. Iodine, vitamin A, and iron are the most common deficiencies.

Section 8: Explore: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

Over 30 million people in the United States have diabetes, which is also the seventh leading cause of death (CDC, 2021). Of those with diabetes, 90-95% have type 2 diabetes, which can largely be prevented through diet and exercise. If not managed, diabetes can cause kidney failure, vision loss, and decreased circulation leading to amputations. Type 2 diabetes is most often diagnosed in people over 45 but there has been a large increase in children and adolescents. In the last 20 years, the number of people with diabetes has doubled. It is estimated that over a third of the U.S. adult population is prediabetic and more than 84% are not aware they have it (CDC, 2021).

It is for this reason that Healthy People 2030 has a large focus on diabetes. There are 16 Healthy People 2030 (n.d.) objectives.

Healthy People 2030 Objectives Related to Diabetes (selected):

  • Reduce the number of diabetes cases diagnosed yearly.
  • Increase the proportion of adults with diabetes who have a yearly eye exam.
  • Increase the proportion of people with diabetes who get formal diabetes education.
  • Increase the proportion of adults with diabetes using insulin who monitor their blood sugar daily.
  • Reduce the rate of death from any cause in adults with diabetes.
  • Reduce the proportion of adults who don't know they have prediabetes.

Section 10: Explore: Food Asphyxiation

Choking from food is possible at any age. If the airway is not cleared, asphyxiation will occur. Toddlers and older adults tend to be more at risk. In a study examining adult food asphyxiation victims, the majority of victims were over 70 years old and only 7% survived to be discharged from the hospital (Inamasu et al., 2010). It was concluded that advanced age and pre-existing conditions made it difficult for this age group to recover.

Most choking and asphyxiation incidents with toddlers have to do with chewing and swallowing abilities. At ages 6 to 12 months, a child is learning how to eat but this also means they can choke. It is important to cut solid foods into small pieces, cook or soften foods, and avoid hard objects like raw fruits or vegetables. After 12 months, a child can feed themselves. Even though they can eat most foods, choking can still happen. Be aware of the following potential choking hazards.

Hazards for 6- to 12-Month-Old

Fruits/Vegetables:

  • Cooked or raw whole corn kernels
  • Uncut cherry or grape tomatoes
  • Pieces of hard raw fruit or vegetables
  • Whole pieces of canned fruit
  • Uncut grapes, berries, cherries, or melon balls
  • Uncooked dry fruit such as raisins

Proteins:

  • Whole or chopped nuts and nut butters such as peanut butter
  • Tough or large chunks of meat
  • Hot dogs, meat sticks, or sausages
  • Fish with bones
  • Large chunks of cheese, especially string cheese

Grain Products:

  • Cookies or granola bars
  • Potato or corn chips, pretzels, or similar snack foods
  • Crackers or breads with seeds, nut pieces, or whole grain kernels
  • Whole kernels of cooked rice, barley, wheat, or other grains

Sweetened Foods:

  • Hard candy, jelly beans, caramels, gum drops, or gummy candies
  • Chewing gum
  • Marshmallows
Hazards for 12- to 24-Month-Old

Fruits/Vegetables:

  • Carrot sticks
  • Whole grapes, cherry tomatoes
  • Large pieces of raw fruit or vegetables

Proteins:

  • Whole peanuts
  • Tough meat
  • Round slices of hot dogs or sausages
  • Seeds
  • Chunks of peanut butter

Grain Products:

  • Popcorn
  • Chips

Sweetened Foods:

  • Chewing gum
  • Hard candy

Section 12: Explore: Iron-Deficiency Anemia

Iron-deficiency anemia develops when the intake of iron is too low to support normal red blood cell production. It is the most common form of anemia and affects mainly children and pregnant women. It is estimated that "42% of children less than 5 years of age and 40% of pregnant women worldwide" suffer from anemia (World Health Organization, n.d.-a, para. 2). For children, low socioeconomic status is a significant risk factor for iron-deficiency anemia.

Anemia is a sign of poor nutrition and health. It impacts other nutritional areas such as undernutrition, low birth weight, and obesity. Since it is treatable, the World Health Organization has several programs to "increase dietary diversity, improve infant feeding practices and improve the bioavailability and intake of micronutrients through fortification or supplementation with iron, folic acid, and other vitamins and minerals" (World Health Organization, n.d.-b, para. 2).

Section 14: Explore: Lead Poisoning

Lead is a toxin found in old paint dust or chips, enameled porcelain fixtures (bathtubs), and soil or air from industrial and transportation pollution. It can be absorbed by the body in excessive amounts and distributed to major organs. It can be measured by a blood test. Young children are most vulnerable as they absorb 4 to 5 times more lead than adults and can suffer permanent health effects. Undernourished children will absorb even higher levels if other nutrients, like calcium or iron, are low. Exposure during pregnancy can cause miscarriage, stillbirth, premature birth, and low birth weight.

There is no safe level of lead. Even small concentrations may cause decreased intelligence, behavior problems, and learning disabilities in children. It is estimated that "nearly half of the 2 million lives lost to known chemical exposure in 2019 were due to lead exposure" (World Health Organization, 2022, Burden of Disease section).

Section 15: Explore: Government Programs

Through the U.S. Department of Agriculture's (USDA) Food and Nutrition Services, there are many programs aimed at ending food insecurities and obesity. There are 15 federal nutrition assistance programs that serve one in four Americans (Food and Nutrition Services, n.d.).

Tab: Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP)

This program supplies benefits to low-income individuals and families that can be used at stores to purchase healthy, SNAP-approved foods.

Tab: The Emergency Food Assistance Program (TEFAP)

This program provides low-income Americans with emergency food assistance at no cost and distributes the USDA foods through state distributing agencies such as food banks.

Tab: Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC)

This program provides low-income pregnant, breastfeeding, and non-breastfeeding postpartum women, and infants and children up to age 5 who are found to be at nutritional risk with supplemental foods, healthcare referrals, and nutrition education.

Tab: The National School Lunch Program (NSLP)

This federal meal program provides quality, low-cost or free lunches to children each school day in public schools, nonprofit private schools, and residential child care institutions.

Tab: Farmers' Market Nutrition Program (FMNP)

In addition to WIC benefits, WIC participants also are issued coupons to buy eligible foods from farmers, farmers' markets, or roadside stands that have been approved by the state agency.
