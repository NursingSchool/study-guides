Section 1: Introduction to Nutritional Assessment and Care

Nursing plays an essential role in all the stages of the nutritional care plan. The nurse can help identify risk factors, health-illness considerations, cultural, and religious needs, as well as counsel on genetic influences. Nurses work with others as part of a multidisciplinary healthcare team. The nurse's approach is holistic, thereby emphasizing health and wellness versus disease.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Discuss the nutrition care plan process.
  • Describe the types of nutritional assessments.
  • Explain the difference between enteral and parenteral nutrition.
  • Describe the role of nutrition in complementary and alternative medicine practices.

Section 3: Explore: Nutrition and the Nursing Process

The nursing process is widely used in nutritional health. The gathering and analysis of data are for the purpose of establishing nursing diagnoses related to nutritional needs.

An important step in the nursing assessment is knowing when to consult the registered dietitian (RD) in the process. Some of the main areas in the community where a registered nurse will be doing nutritional assessments are as a:

  • school nurse
  • home health nurse
  • long-term care nurse
  • postsurgical care nurse
  • public health nurse

Review the case study shown here as a quick example to see the nursing process in action as it relates to nutrition. Think about how you will use the nursing process to incorporate nutrition into a client's overall plan of care.

Case Study: Nursing Process and Diet Therapy

Patient Profile: Mrs. C is an 88-year-old woman brought to the hospital by her daughter. She is admitted with a diagnosis of dehydration, malnutrition, and electrolyte imbalance. On admission to the hospital, her daughter expresses frustration that her mother "just will not eat."

Subjective Data:

  • Daughter states her mother has been eating less and less
  • Daughter states she is very worried about the decreasing appetite and food intake
  • Daughter states her mother has been losing weight

Objective Data:

  • Appears pale, weak, tired
  • Is confused about place and time but is able to state her name intermittently
  • Does not recognize her daughter
  • Recently diagnosed with dementia
  • Lives with her daughter

Okay, so you have this information. What do you need to think about for each step of the nursing process?

Step 1: The nurse needs to collect more data regarding Mrs. C's nutritional status. A nutrition assessment is indicated. Nutrition assessment is a comprehensive approach to defining nutrition status that uses medical nutrition and medication histories, physical examination, anthropometric measurements, and laboratory data. The nurse needs to obtain a complete dietary history from the patient's daughter. The nurse needs to discuss Mrs. C's food preferences with the daughter.

Step 2: Other data to be collected include height and weight, BMI (body mass index), intake and output measurements, and daily weights (weight taken each day, at the same time, on the same scale, with the same type or amount of clothing, preferably with the bladder recently emptied). A calorie count and between-meal supplements should also be considered.

Step 3: A plan of care needs to be developed involving the nurse, dietitian, pharmacist, and physician. Discuss the impact of the medical diagnosis of dementia on Mrs. C's malnutrition. Review all medications she is taking for possible effects on nutrition and appetite. Review data obtained from calorie count. Review all data to determine the type of malnutrition and its cause.

Step 4: Appropriate nursing diagnoses include imbalanced nutrition; less than body requirements related to decreased ingestion; activity intolerance related to weakness, fatigue, and inadequate caloric intake; and deficient fluid volume related to decreased fluid intake.

Section 4: Explore: Nutritional Assessment

Data sources for assessment information include anthropometric data, food and nutrition history, physical examination, laboratory tests, and client history (Charney, 2016). Screening may be triggered by a change in medical condition, diet, intake, or nutritional support. The criteria can be remembered using the ABCDs of a nutritional assessment.

Tab: Anthropometric — Measurements of the Client
  • weight (including percent of weight change over a given time period)
  • height
  • BMI (body mass index)
  • head circumference (used in pediatrics)
Tab: Biochemical — Used to Determine Nutritional Deficiencies
  • liver function tests
  • diabetes mellitus (e.g., glucose, HgbA1C)
  • renal profile
  • lipids
  • anemia (e.g., iron, B12, thiamine)
  • protein (e.g., albumin, pre-albumin)
Tab: Clinical — Evaluation of Medical History and Current Status
  • medical condition (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, kidney disease)
  • treatment (e.g., dialysis, surgery)
  • medication (e.g., chemotherapy, antibiotics)
  • vital signs
Tab: Dietary — Recall of Foods and Frequency Including Food Influences
  • religious, ethnic, and cultural influences
  • 3-day food diary
  • 24-hour recall
  • diet history

When taking a food history, some use a 24-hour recall. This is an interview to obtain information about all foods and beverages consumed in the past 24 hours. However, sometimes this recall is inaccurate due to a client's memory, cooperation, and communication ability. It may not reflect someone's true diet. Another option is to have the client complete a food diary to record intake over a longer period of time. This allows the healthcare provider to see dietary patterns.

Section 6: Explore: Diagnosis and Planning

The nutrition care process model is very similar to the nursing care process, including assessment, diagnosis, planning, implementation, and evaluation.

According to the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (2018), nutrition diagnoses fall into four categories: Intake, Clinical, Behavioral, and Environmental.

Nutrition Diagnoses (categories):

  • Intake: More or less than body requirements
  • Clinical: Problems from a medical condition
  • Behavioral: Knowledge and beliefs
  • Environmental: Food access and safety

Planning nutrition intervention involves reviewing and prioritizing the client's nutrition diagnoses. For each diagnosis, the client-focused expected outcomes need to be defined. The provider must work with the client and/or caregivers and develop interventions that fit with the client's lifestyle and culture. Resources needed to make the plan successful should be included.

Reference

Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. (2018). NCP step 2: Nutrition diagnosis. Nutrition Diagnosis Snapshot. https://www.andeal.org/files/Docs/IDNT_Snapshot_NDe4_S2.pdf

Section 8: Explore: Nutritional Interventions

The purpose of a nutrition intervention is to resolve or improve the nutrition diagnosis by addressing the etiology. The intervention is a planned action intended to change nutritional behaviors, risk factors, nutrition-related knowledge, environmental conditions, or access to services. Interventions are customized to the client.

According to the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (2018), nutrition intervention can be classified into multiple categories:

  • Nutrition Education: Instructing a client in a skill or providing knowledge to help clients modify food choices, preparation, and/or quantity.
  • Nutrition Counseling: Providing a supportive environment with a counselor to establish nutrition-related goals to treat an existing condition and promote health.
  • Food and Nutrient Delivery: Individualized provision of food and specialty diets.
  • Coordination with Other Providers: Consultation with or referral to other healthcare providers, institutions, or agencies that can support the management of nutrition-related problems.
  • Population-Based Nutrition Action: Interventions focused on the nutritional health of a community.

Reference

Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics. (2018). NCP step 3: Nutrition intervention. Nutrition Intervention Snapshot. https://www.andeal.org/vault/2440/web/files/20140527-NI%20Snapshot.pdf

Section 10: Explore: Enteral Nutrition

One clinical intervention is enteral nutrition or "tube feeding." The tube, called a nasogastric (NG) tube, is inserted through the nose down to the stomach or small intestine. Another option is to place a percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG) or jejunostomy tube (PEJ) which deposits nutrition directly into the stomach or small intestine.

This intervention is used when the upper gastrointestinal (GI) system needs to be bypassed. This can be used for the inability to consume enough nutrients, difficulties swallowing, or surgery that interferes with eating. The client must have at least a partially functioning gastrointestinal system.

Enteral nutrition can be used with people of all ages and there is a wide variety of enteral nutritional formulas. Feedings can be continuous or intermittent and are counted as intake in an I&O assessment. However, they are not without complications.

  • GI complications: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal distention, cramps, diarrhea, malabsorption/maldigestion
  • Mechanical complications: Aspiration, tube malposition, tube clogged
  • Metabolic complications: Hyponatremia, hypernatremia, dehydration, hyperglycemia, hypokalemia, hyperkalemia

Before administering any nutrition or medication through the tube, proper placement must be verified. If the tube is placed in the stomach, the amount of residual should be checked to prevent over-inflation of the stomach. The client should be in a semi-Fowler's position during feedings and afterward to prevent aspiration. Frequently, nasogastric feedings are used for a short duration, while PEG and PEJ feedings are longer term. When the condition has resolved and the client is able to consume enough by mouth, the tube is removed.

Section 11: Explore: Parenteral Nutrition

Parenteral nutrition is delivering nutrients through the bloodstream via an intravenous solution. The solution "may include protein, carbohydrate, fat, minerals and electrolytes, vitamins and other trace elements for patients who cannot eat or absorb enough food through tube feeding formula or by mouth to maintain good nutrition status" (American Society for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition, n.d., para. 1). This type of nutrition can be total, supplying all nutrients, or partial, supplying a portion of nutrients.

Parenteral nutrition can be used as long as needed. It bypasses the gastrointestinal (GI) system completely and is used for patients who have a non-functioning GI system or require complete bowel rest. Some conditions include short bowel syndrome, cancer, bowel disorders, and severe pancreatitis.

Parenteral nutrition is usually administered through a central line, such as a peripherally-inserted central catheter (PICC) line. Infection of the catheter is the most common and serious complication of parenteral nutrition. Sterile technique is required with the insertion and maintenance of the central line. Other complications are blood clots and electrolyte imbalances.

Section 13: Explore: Complementary and Alternative Medicine Practices

Nutrition plays a large role in complementary and alternative medicine (CAM) practices. These practices involve healing approaches and therapies that are used together with, or instead of, conventional Western medicine.

Dietary supplements are the most used CAM therapy. They are readily available, relatively inexpensive, and can be bought without a prescribing healthcare provider. Dietary supplements can contain vitamins, minerals, herbs, amino acids, or hormones. Currently, manufacturers of dietary supplements do not have to prove product safety or efficacy (Shane-McWhorter, 2020).

Chinese medicine originated over 2,000 years ago and is centered around the philosophy that disease results from the improper movement of the life force, called "Qi." Qi is restored by balancing the opposing forces of yin and yang. This is done through medicinal herbs and acupuncture. Herbs can be used in a single form or as a mixture.

Clients with cancer have one of the highest rates of using nutritional CAM since it may improve their quality of life. Research has found that herbal CAM usage was 59% among clients receiving radiation and 22% among those receiving chemotherapy (Bayir & Guney, 2019). As a healthcare provider, it is important to ask clients if they are taking any dietary or herbal supplements. Despite their benefits, mixing certain supplements with prescriptions can cause interactions or treatments to be less effective.

Section 1: Introduction to Food and Medication Interactions

Since the introduction of computerized pharmacy systems, there has been a decrease in drug-drug interactions. However, food and medication interactions are also a common cause of adverse drug reactions and are more challenging. Food consumption is not normally documented in a client's chart. Clients sometimes don't disclose their use of herbal medicines and dietary supplements. Food, herbs, and supplements may interfere with a drug's absorption, metabolism, or excretion. This may increase the drug's toxicity, produce a new effect, or decrease effectiveness (Abdollahi et al., 2018).

This concept will look at the risk factors for food and medication interactions, the effects of food and medication, screening for food and medication interactions, and the importance of educating clients.

As you complete the concept, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Identify risk factors for food and medication interactions.
  • Describe the effects of food, herbs, and supplements on medication.
  • Describe the effects of medication on foods.
  • Explain how to screen for food and medication interactions.
  • Discuss the importance of educating clients about food and medication interactions.

Section 3: Explore: Types of Interactions

Pharmacokinetic Interactions

Pharmacokinetic interactions result in changes in drug absorption, distribution, metabolism, and elimination. The interactions lead to a decrease or increase in the drug's effect as a result of the concentration in the body. Pharmacokinetic interactions are the most common interactions and the cause of mild to severe side effects.

Example: Many drugs are metabolized with an enzyme system called the cytochrome P450 system in the liver. Grapefruit juice can block some of these enzymes, causing the drug to stay in the circulating bloodstream. This can lead to toxic levels and adverse effects of the drug.

Pharmacodynamic Interactions

Pharmacodynamic interactions occur when food or herbs directly change the effect of the drug. It does not change the blood concentration, and adjusting the dose of the drug will not counter a pharmacodynamic interaction.

Example: Ingestion of tyramine-rich foods in conjunction with monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs) can cause a hypertensive crisis. MAOIs reduce the breakdown of tyramine thus helping relieve depression. However, if MAOIs and tyramine are taken together, the vasoconstriction effects are increased.

Section 5: Explore: Risk Factors for Drug-Nutrient Interaction

Clients at increased risk for drug-food interactions include:

  • Older adult clients
  • Clients with chronic disease
  • Clients on long-term drug therapy
  • Clients with polypharmacy
  • Clients who are malnourished
  • Clients with increased nutritional needs

Older adult clients are the highest risk group. This population takes the majority of prescription medications, such as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, antihypertensives, and antidepressants, which have interactions with certain foods. Most older adults also have some type of underlying chronic conditions that require long-term therapies and nutritional interventions. The multiple therapies lead to polypharmacy.

In addition, the older adult population are more likely to suffer an adverse effect related to the interaction.

Case Study: A 77-year-old male client with a history of heart arrhythmias was placed on digoxin. The client was warned of decreased medication absorption and possible failure of medication associated with the consumption of calcium. The client remained adherent to taking the digoxin with water for several months and was showing improvement.

At a recent clinic visit, the client was told that his calcium level was low. He was encouraged to consume more calcium and a supplement was added to his medications. A medication review was not completed, so the link between calcium and digoxin was missed. Two weeks later, the client presented to the emergency department with a diagnosis of digoxin toxicity.

Section 6: Explore: Effects of Drugs on Nutrition

Since drugs and nutrients use similar organs for absorption, metabolism, and excretion, drugs can interfere with nutritional status. Drugs can also impact the actual intake of food by influencing appetite, taste, and smell. The drugs that have the greatest impact on nutrition are those that are taken for prolonged periods of time, have dietary requirements or regulations, or compete with nutrient-binding sites.

Appetite Changes

  • Drugs may suppress hunger, leading to weight loss. Examples: Some antibiotics, bulk agents, and narcotics like morphine decrease appetite.
  • Drugs may stimulate hunger, leading to weight gain. Examples: Alcohol, corticosteroids, and megestrol acetate increase appetite.

Taste and Smell Alterations

  • Some drugs may alter the ability to taste and smell certain foods which affects food intake. Examples: Allopurinol and lithium may cause a metallic taste, while aspirin and levodopa may cause a bitter taste. ACE inhibitors, beta blockers, and calcium channel blockers may cause altered smell.

Nutrient Absorption

  • Certain drugs may increase, decrease, or prevent nutrient absorption in the gastrointestinal system. Example: Orlistat decreases the absorption of fats.

Nutrient Metabolism

  • Drugs may increase or decrease the metabolism of certain nutrients. Example: Sulfonylureas and lithium decrease the uptake or release of iodine by the thyroid.

Nutrient Excretion

  • Drugs can increase or decrease the excretion of nutrients. Example: Diuretics, especially loop and thiazides, can increase the urinary loss of potassium, magnesium, sodium, and calcium.

Section 7: Explore: Drug-Food Interactions

The effect of a drug may be different than expected if it interacts with a food or beverage. It may also cause the drug to not work at all. One of the most common drug-food interactions is with grapefruit and grapefruit juice.

There are over 40 medications that can have an adverse effect with grapefruit. The video below from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration explains the details.

Video: "Don't Take This With That: Grapefruit, Drug Interaction" — FDA (YouTube: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FQJ4NO6SDqg)

Section 9: Explore: Timing of Medication Administration

Some medications need to be taken before meals, with meals, or after meals. Researchers found that most clients, over 89%, take their medications at inappropriate times and without regard for meals. The following are examples of why the timing of medication administration is important:

Before Meals
  • Help the body balance blood glucose: Rapid-acting insulin should be injected within 15 min before a meal. Short-acting (regular) insulin can be given 30 minutes before a meal.
  • Ensure proper absorption: Levothyroxine should be taken on an empty stomach 30-60 minutes before meals to avoid unpredictable absorption of the hormone. Bisphosphonates, such as alendronate and ibandronate, should be taken alone with a full glass of water.
With or After Meals
  • Reduce nausea or vomiting: Medications, like allopurinol, may cause nausea or vomiting. Take these after a meal to reduce these side effects.
  • Reduce stomach irritation: Aspirin, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and steroid medications can cause stomach irritation, indigestion or ulcers. Food or milk can coat the stomach thus reducing these effects.
  • Treat reflux or indigestion: Antacids are used to treat reflux after the consumption of food. It is most effective if taken immediately after a meal.
  • Ensure the medicine is not washed away: Liquid nystatin is used for oral thrush and must be used after meals. The client cannot eat or drink anything for 20-30 minutes after application.
  • Ensure proper absorption: HIV medicines, such as ritonavir, saquinavir and nelfinavir, require food in the gastrointestinal system in order for the body to absorb them properly.
  • Help the body process the meal: Enzyme supplements should also be taken with food to help the body process the meal.

Section 11: Explore: Herb and Drug Interaction

Rashrash et al. (2017) examined the prevalence of herbal use in over 26,000 Americans. Approximately 35% used herbal supplements. Of those that used supplements, almost 38% percent used prescription medications, and 42% used over-the-counter medication. The prevalence of use centered around people with chronic medical conditions such as stroke, cancer, and arthritis.

In a study by Alsanad et al. (2014), 806 clients with cancer were surveyed and 53.7% were reported to be taking herbal supplements. Drug combinations and herbal supplements taken were examined and potential herb-drug interactions were identified. It was discovered that 13.9% of clients were at risk of interaction. The most common herbs involved included garlic, green tea, mistletoe, Chinese herbal tea, iron, and Saint John's wort. Interactions included altered drug metabolism, decreased absorption, and hypersensitivity. It was also found that most of the clients did not discuss their herbal supplement use with healthcare professionals.

St. John's wort is most often used for depression but can be used for menopausal symptoms, attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and topically for wound healing (Mayo Clinic Staff, 2017). However, it can interact with medications by increasing or decreasing the effects. Examples include:

  • Antidepressants
  • Birth control pills
  • Barbiturates
  • Cyclosporine
  • Digoxin
  • Certain immunosuppressive drugs
  • Omeprazole
  • Protease inhibitors
  • Certain chemotherapy drugs
  • Narcotics
  • Certain statins
  • Warfarin

Section 13: Explore: Client Education

Educating clients about interactions is part of a healthcare provider's role. It is especially important if the client takes several different medications, sees more than one healthcare provider, and/or has multiple health conditions.

Here are some client education tips and suggestions:

  • Screening for interactions can be done in all healthcare and home settings.
  • Ask clients about all prescription drugs, over-the-counter drugs, and herbal supplements.
  • Ask clients to describe how and when they take the medications and supplements.
  • Remind clients that over-the-counter drugs may interact with other drugs, herbal supplements, and food as well.
  • Teach clients how to read drug and supplement labels.
  • Provide clients with educational materials and resources.

Section 5: Explore: Types of Food Poisoning

Food poisoning is mostly caused by bacteria and viruses. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2020), the top five most common foodborne infectious agents in the United States are:

  • norovirus
  • salmonella
  • clostridium perfringens
  • campylobacter
  • staphylococcus aureus

There are others, such as Clostridium botulinum (botulism), listeria, Escherichia coli, and vibrio that are less common but, when they cause an infection, it tends to be more severe.

Food poisoning can also be caused by parasites and chemicals.

Case Study

A group of six men returned from elk hunting in the mountains of Colorado. All were symptomatic with diarrhea, fatigue, and abdominal pain. Stool samples revealed they had giardia, a microscopic parasite. After further investigation, it was found that all of the hunters drank water from the mountain streams thinking it was "clean and pure." What the hunters did not realize was that the water was contaminated by animal feces upstream. They were treated with antiparasitics and all recovered.

Section 7: Explore: Food Poisoning Signs and Symptoms

Signs and symptoms of food poisoning include intestinal cramping, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and fever. They may differ depending on which organism was ingested. It may take hours to days for signs and symptoms to develop.

Tab: Signs and Symptoms

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2022) recommend that people should see a healthcare provider if symptoms are severe, including:

  • bloody stools
  • fever over 102 °F (38.9 °C)
  • frequent vomiting
  • signs of dehydration
  • diarrhea lasting more than 3 days
Tab: Treatment

Treatment depends on the severity of the symptoms. Some people recover with rest, fluids, and medications after a few hours or several days. Other cases require hospitalization. Severe cases can result in long-term effects such as chronic arthritis, brain and nerve damage, and kidney failure from hemolytic uremic syndrome.

All suspected cases of food poisoning should be reported to the local health department to help officials identify a possible outbreak. It is considered an outbreak if two or more people get the same illness from the same contaminated food or drink.

Section 10: Explore: Preventing Food Poisoning

Food poisoning can be prevented by four easy steps—"clean, separate, cook, and chill."

Clean

  • Wash your hands often
  • Wash surfaces and utensils after each use
  • Wash fruits and vegetables, but not meat, poultry, or eggs

Separate

  • Use separate cutting boards and plates for produce, meat, poultry, seafood, and eggs
  • Store produce, meat, poultry, seafood, and eggs separately from each other

Cook

  • Keep food hot (140 °F [60 °C] or above) after cooking
  • Microwave food thoroughly (165 °F [73.9 °C] or above)

Chill

  • Refrigerate perishable foods within 2 hours

(Source: U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 2022)

Section 13: Explore: Food Safety Challenges

Antibiotic Resistance

Although the United States food supply is considered safe, infection outbreaks do still occur. Infections may be resistant to antibiotics. So how does this happen?

If antibiotics are used to treat animals, the animal can develop antibiotic resistant bacteria. This bacteria can be spread in the animal waste. Meat can become contaminated during the food processing stage or foods, such as fruits and vegetables, can be contaminated by animal waste through soil or water. The bacteria may also be spread through contact with the animals.

Culture-Independent Diagnostic Tests (CIDTs)

CIDTs are rapid diagnostic tests that can determine the cause of an infection without culturing. This means faster treatment for the client but, without the culture process, public health officials cannot define the organism's strains, characteristics, or patterns. This information is required "to detect and prevent outbreaks, track antibiotic resistance, and monitor disease trends to know if prevention measures are working" (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2022, para. 3). The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention are encouraging laboratories to continue culturing harmful bacteria.

Preventing Contamination

There are several steps that food takes from farm to table: production, processing, distribution, and preparation. Contamination from food mishandling can occur at any point. However, further mishandling of the food during the preparation phase can make illness more likely.

Section 1: Introduction to Special Diets

Diets can be ordered by a healthcare provider if a client needs certain restrictions or has a health condition that requires a special diet. These diet orders can be found in the client's medical record. For clients with strict restrictions, nutrition counseling is sometimes ordered to assist the client in transitioning to a different diet and/or food preparation techniques.

As you complete the concept, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Identify common specialty diets used in healthcare.
  • Explain the purpose of specialty diets.
  • Identify what foods are restricted or allowed in specialty diets.

Section 3: Explore: Regular Diet

A regular diet would be prescribed for any client that has no restrictions or health conditions that require a special diet. It is also referred to as a general diet. The purpose is to provide a well-balanced diet that provides adequate nutrition.

In most facilities, a client on a regular diet will be able to order meals off a menu independently or with assistance if needed.

Section 4: Explore: Nothing By Mouth

Nothing by mouth (NPO), a Latin abbreviation for nil per os, is a diet order that means "nothing by mouth." This refers to a time, usually before a procedure or surgery, when the client cannot eat or drink anything in order to prevent aspiration. If the stomach is not empty, the contents can come up the esophagus and into the lungs. The length of NPO time varies based on the situation. Commonly, clients are NPO prior to and after surgery.

Clients may also be NPO in cases of reduced consciousness, gastrointestinal problems, or unsafe swallowing reflexes. In these cases, alternative hydration and nutrition is implemented.

NPO status must be documented and discussed with the client to improve compliance. If the client needs to take medications during this time, they should be reviewed with the healthcare provider. The healthcare provider will determine which medications should be taken and if sips of water are allowed.

Section 5: Explore: Vegetarian and Vegan Diets

Vegetarianism is the exclusion of meat, poultry, and fish from the diet. Vegetarianism is regarded as a lifestyle rather than a diet, since there are many plant-based diets. People may choose vegetarianism due to religious beliefs, environmental reasons, or for animal rights. Benefits include a reduced body mass, lower cholesterol, and lower glucose levels. However, vegetarians may be deficient in calcium, vitamin B12, iron, and zinc.

According to Frey (2013), there are several types of vegetarians:

  • Vegans: This is the strictest of the vegetarian lifestyles. Vegans exclude all animal products from the diet and use of all animal products. For example, vegans will not consume honey or dairy products and abstain from wearing leather and wool.
  • Ovolactovegetarians: Vegetarians who will exclude meat, poultry, and fish but will consume eggs, milk, and other dairy products since these foods are not obtained by killing animals.
  • Ovovegetarians: Vegetarians who include eggs in the diet but not milk products, meat, poultry, and fish.
  • Lactovegetarians: Vegetarians who use milk and milk products but not eggs, meat, poultry, and fish.
  • Semivegetarians or Pesce/Pollo Vegetarians: People who include fish or chicken in the diet but only minimally. They abstain from red meat.
  • Fruitarians: Vegetarians who eat only fruits, nuts, seeds, and other plant matter that can be harvested without harming the plant.
  • Flexitarians: People who prefer a vegetarian diet but are willing to eat meat, fish, or chicken on exceptional occasions.
  • Freegans: Vegans who seek to avoid participating in any consumer practices they regard as abusive of humans or the environment. Freegans obtain their food by growing it themselves, bartering, or foraging in refuse bins and restaurant trash receptacles for discarded food. This practice is called "dumpster diving" in the United States.

Section 7: Explore: Liquid Diets

Liquid diets can be used as a partial or full meal replacement option. They can be used after certain surgeries or procedures as well as for weight loss. Liquid diets for weight loss should only be used by clients with large amounts of weight to lose. They supply 500 to 800 calories per day, which is a very low-calorie diet. For this reason, clients using liquid for weight loss should be closely monitored by a healthcare provider (Davidson et al., 2013). The liquid supplements are usually in the form of a shake that provides a balance of vitamins and minerals. However, the longer the liquid diet, the higher the risk of deficiencies. The two most common are full liquid and clear liquid diets.

Clear Liquid Diets

Clear liquid diets are used to maintain fluid balance and minimize digestive impact. They are used post-surgically and following acute vomiting or diarrhea. Clear liquid means the client can consume any liquid or a food that melts into a liquid that is clear. Allowed foods include water, fat-free broth, bouillon, coffee, tea, carbonated beverages, clear fruit juices, gelatin, fruit ices, and popsicles. Foods to avoid include milk and milk products, fruit juices with pulp, and cream soups. If you can see through it, it's a clear liquid.

Full Liquid Diets

Full liquid diets are used to provide an adequate diet for clients who cannot chew or who are being progressed off of a clear liquid diet. All clear liquids are allowed as well as smooth-textured dairy products such as ice cream, strained or blended cream soups, custards, refined cooked cereals, vegetable juice, pureed vegetables, fruit juices, sherbets, puddings, and frozen yogurt. Foods not included are solids such as nuts, seeds, fruit, vegetables, and meat.

Section 8: Explore: Dysphagia Diets

Dysphagia diets, used for clients with impaired swallowing, were once based on a three-level scale — Level 1: Pureed, Level 2: Mechanically Altered, and Level 3: Advanced. However, these stages did not meet many clients' unique needs and clumped together foods with beverages.

In 2019, the International Dysphagia Diet Standardization Initiative (IDDSI) provided common terminology to describe food textures and drink thicknesses. To implement, the healthcare provider or dietitian would complete an assessment and make recommendations for foods or drinks for a client. The IDDSI framework consists of 8 levels (0-7). Drinks are measured from Levels 0–4, while foods are measured from Levels 3–7. Clients with a level 0-2 are on liquids with no form of solid foods. Only after level 3 can clients consume foods. The framework describes the progression from liquids to regular solid foods.

Section 10: Explore: Gluten Free Diet

Gluten is a protein in grains such as wheat, oats, rye, and barley. A gluten-free diet is consumed by people with celiac disease and gluten intolerance. Celiac disease is an inflammatory immune response to gluten which damages the small intestine. Consuming gluten may cause a medical crisis in these clients. Some clients may have sensitivity to gluten, which causes gastrointestinal symptoms.

In addition to avoiding grains, there are a large variety of gluten-containing foods. It is important to check the food labels. Foods that could contain hidden gluten include:

  • Soups
  • Processed foods
  • Candy
  • Salad dressings
  • Gravy
  • Soy sauce
  • Imitation bacon and seafood
  • Beer

Gluten-free foods are available as substitutes, but they can cost 3-5 times as much. About 30% of Americans claim to follow a gluten-free diet (Sims et al., 2017).

Section 11: Explore: Diabetic Diet

The American Diabetes Association (2020) nutrition recommendations focus on the Diabetes Plate Method. Non-starchy vegetables, such as broccoli, carrots, green beans, and leafy greens, make up half of a 9-inch plate. Lean proteins, like chicken, turkey, and eggs, make up one quarter while limiting carbohydrates, like whole grains, fruits, and dairy, to the other quarter plate. Beverages should be water or a low-calorie drink.

Other diabetic plans include carbohydrate counting or carbohydrate measurement, since carbohydrates have the greatest impact on blood glucose levels. Sugars should also be limited.

Nutritional Recommendations Per Plate (Diabetes Plate Method)

  • ½ Nonstarchy vegetables
  • ¼ Protein foods
  • ¼ Carbohydrate foods
  • Water or low-calorie drink

Section 13: Explore: Low Sodium Diet

A low sodium diet can be classified as a no added salt diet (4g sodium diet), low sodium diet (2g or 1g), or severe sodium restriction diet (500mg sodium diet). Clients with kidney disease, heart disease, and diabetes may be required to follow a low sodium diet. Since 77% of the average American's daily sodium intake comes from processed or prepared foods, it is important to read nutrition labels and have clients cut back on added salt (Tish, 2013).

Foods with high sodium include:

  • Smoked meats (sausages, ham)
  • Frozen dinners
  • Canned entrees (ravioli, spam)
  • Salted nuts
  • Soy sauce

The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet is recommended by the National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute for lowering blood pressure and sometimes accompanies a low sodium diet. The DASH diet consists of fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy foods, and reduced saturated fats. Results of studies have shown that combining a lower sodium intake with the DASH diet had a greater benefit than just the DASH diet or a low salt diet alone in hypertensive clients (Parker et al., 2013).

Section 14: Explore: Renal Diet

Clients with kidney disease or kidney failure have special dietary needs. They require a diet that limits sodium, potassium, phosphorus, protein, and fluid intake in order to decrease symptoms and reduce toxins. Since these nutrients are found in many foods, the diet can be challenging for clients. Client education about substitutes is key. Section 15: Explore: Neutropenic Diet

A neutropenic diet is sometimes referred to as a low-bacterial diet. It is prescribed for clients with suppressed immunity such as cancer and HIV/AIDS. The diet protects them from acquiring foodborne illness. It should be noted that the neutropenic diet is controversial. Some researchers argue that limiting fresh fruit and vegetables as well as dairy and meat compromises nutritional intake. Also, research shows that adhering to safe food handling practices provides adequate protection (Wolfe et al., 2018).

Clients on this diet are to refrain from:

  • All fresh fruits and vegetables
  • Raw or rare meat, fish, and eggs
  • Salads and deli meats
  • Raw nuts and oats
  • Unpasteurized dairy and soft cheeses
  • Yogurt with live or active cultures
  • Tap water

Wolfe, H., Sadeghi, N., Agrawal, D., Johnson, D., & Gupta, A. (2018). Things we do for no reason: Neutropenic diet. Journal of Hospital Medicine, 8, 573–576. https://doi.org/10.12788/jhm.2985

Section 17: Explore: High Fiber Diet

Dietary fiber is defined as the indigestible carbohydrate component of plants. There are two types of fiber: insoluble fiber and soluble fiber.

  • Insoluble fiber, which is in whole grains and raw vegetables, helps the body with peristalsis.
  • Soluble fiber, which is in many fruits, slows digestion and promotes a sense of fullness. Soluble fiber also decreases cholesterol levels, lowering the risk for heart disease.

Besides improving heart health, a high fiber diet can prevent digestive issues such as constipation and hemorrhoids. Converting to a high fiber diet should be done gradually to prevent abdominal pain and diarrhea. It is also important to tell clients to consume at least eight glasses of water and other fluids daily. A high fiber diet is contraindicated for those on a fluid restriction (Tish, 2013).

Section 18: Explore: Low Fiber Diet

A low fiber diet, also known as a low residue diet or restricted fiber diet, consists of easily digested foods such as white flour pastas and breads, casseroles, moist tender meats, and canned cooked fruits and vegetables.

The purpose of a low fiber diet is to reduce the bulk of stool in the intestine for easier elimination. This diet may be prescribed for clients after bowel surgery or for an irritated, inflamed gastrointestinal tract. Depending on the condition of the client, the diet may be more or less restrictive.

When educating clients about a low fiber diet, learning how to read food labels is important. They should select foods with no more than 1 gram of fiber per serving.

Section 19: Explore: Cholesterol Restricted Diet

Originating from the American Heart Association, a low cholesterol diet is designed to decrease the risk of cardiovascular disease by limiting cholesterol to less than 300 mg per day. There are a few ways to reduce cholesterol in the diet. One way is to read food labels.

Cholesterol free foods have fewer than 2 mg of cholesterol while low cholesterol foods have no more than 20 mg of cholesterol (Davidson et al., 2013). When preparing meals, clients can decrease cholesterol by choosing lean meats, baking or broiling meats, avoiding solid fats, and cooking with olive or canola oil.
