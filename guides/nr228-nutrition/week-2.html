Section 1: Introduction to Dietary Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are an essential nutrient needed by the body to maintain good health. Carbohydrates consist of sugars, starches, and fiber and are mainly found in plant sources. Healthcare providers must be aware that carbohydrates provide the body with vital energy to function properly.

In order to educate clients about maintaining good health, it is vital for healthcare providers to identify the many foods that make up carbohydrates and their cultural influences.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Review carbohydrates and their relationship to energy.
  • Review the foods that contain simple and complex carbohydrates and their health implications.
  • Discuss the pancreas and its role in glucose regulation.

Section 3: Explore: Basic Carbohydrate Science

Carbohydrates originate from plant sources. They are organic compounds consisting of bonded molecules of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. Carbohydrates take the molecular form of either sugars, starches, or fiber, and the body needs all three sources to maintain good health.

Here is a visual diagram explaining the impact different carbohydrates have on blood glucose levels over time.

Transcript:

  • Carbohydrates are classified as either simple or complex.
  • Simple carbohydrates are sugar molecules called monosaccharides and disaccharides. A monosaccharide is a one sugar molecule, and a disaccharide is a two-sugar molecule. Glucose is an excellent example of monosaccharides. It is important to note that glucose is the simplest form of sugar, and it is rapidly used by any cell in the body. Glucose does not have to go through a digestion process.
  • Food examples of simple carbohydrates are honey, milk, and fruits.
  • These food items will raise blood sugar very fast. The measurement of how fast blood sugar rises after consuming a carbohydrate is called the glycemic index. Refined white bread has a high glycemic index of 100. Factors that affect glycemic index in fruit are fiber content and ripeness.
  • Complex carbohydrates are called Polysaccharides. These are many sugar molecules bound together.
  • Polysaccharides consist of starches and fibers. Starches are broken down and used as an energy source, while fiber is used to assist with digestion. It is important to note that it takes the body longer to digest starches and fiber.
  • Food examples of complex carbohydrates are grains, legumes, and starchy vegetables. These types of complex carbohydrates provide additional vitamins and minerals.
  • Since it takes the body longer to digest complex carbohydrates, these food items will gradually raise the blood sugar. Complex carbohydrates have a low glycemic index.

Section 5: Explore: Facts About Fiber

Fiber provides bulk to chyme, which allows it to move through the digestive system easily. Also, fiber assists the small intestine with vitamin synthesis. Fiber is an integral part of a healthy diet because of its ability to aid in glucose regulation, slow the absorption of cholesterol, and speed the passage of chyme. Fiber also makes one feel full longer after eating.

Soluble and insoluble fibers are two types of fiber from polysaccharides. These two types of fibers both pass through the body without elevating blood glucose and aid blood glucose control and insulin response. The major differences between these dietary fibers are that soluble fiber (e.g., pectin, psyllium, β-glucan, and guar gum) dissolves in fluid, while insoluble fiber (e.g., cellulose and lignin) does not dissolve in fluid.

Benefits:

  • Lowers blood cholesterol

Foods High in Soluble Fiber:

Section 7: Glucose Regulation and Nursing Implication

The primary goal for carbohydrates is to maintain the body's blood glucose (sugar) levels between 70–100 mg/dl. Blood glucose levels are regulated by a pancreatic hormone called insulin. Insulin is responsible for lowering blood glucose levels by mediating the absorption of glucose into cells and enhancing the mediation of glucose into glycogen. Other pancreatic hormones help regulate blood glucose levels.

Section 8: Explore: Case Study—Mikey

Mikey (pronouns: he/him/his) is a 17-year-old type 1 diabetic who checks his glucose levels throughout the day to ensure his blood glucose level stays between 70–100 mg/dl. Mikey recognizes cues to alert him that his blood sugar is low:

  • Diaphoresis
  • Anxiety
  • Headache

He also keeps a careful record of his dietary intake of carbohydrates. Knowing how many carbohydrates he eats helps him figure out the right amount of insulin he may need.

Case Analysis

Since Mikey's pancreas doesn't function properly, he runs the risk of becoming hypoglycemic at night because he may not respond to the same cues while he is sleeping. When the body needs glucose overnight and the serum glucose level is low, the liver will signal cells to convert glycogen to glucose for energy. After glycogen stores are depleted, the body will start breaking down fat and then protein. If Mikey eats an evening snack with a low glycemic index, the drop in blood sugar at night can be avoided.

It is important for healthcare providers to ensure the blood sugar levels of diabetic clients are closely monitored. Some diabetic clients may need to consume a low-glycemic, high-fiber food item (e.g., apple) prior to bed. While the client sleeps, the high-fiber food item will be digested slowly and will maintain the client's blood sugar throughout the night. While the client sleeps, the carbohydrates and fiber in the apple will be digested slowly to maintain a consistent blood sugar and prevent the body's use of glycogen for energy.

Section 10: Explore: Carbohydrates—Nutrition Recommendations and Implications

Carbohydrates are one of the macronutrients. As you learned earlier, carbohydrates provide energy for the body, and 90% of its nutrients are absorbed by the body. It is important to consume the appropriate amount of carbohydrates to remain healthy. Therefore, the Institute of Medicine (IOM, 2000), the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS, 2020) establish carbohydrate dietary intake recommendations to prevent disease associated with carbohydrate consumption.

Tab: Recommendations for Carbohydrate Intake
  • It is recommended that healthy individuals consume 45–60% of their daily calories from carbohydrates.
  • Based on a 2,000-calorie diet, 900–1,300 calories should come from carbohydrates.
  • The recommended daily allowance for carbohydrates is about 130 grams per day.
  • Carbohydrates provide the body with approximately 4 calories per gram.

Complex Carbohydrate—Fiber: The IOM (2020) recommends that adult men and women consume 38 and 25 grams of fiber a day, respectively.

Simple Carbohydrate—Sugar: The USDA and DHHS (2020) recommend that added sugar is no more than 10% of daily calorie intake and should be limited as much as possible.

Simple Carbohydrate—Sugar Alcohol: Sugar alcohol is an ingredient that can be used as a substitute for sugar. These are found naturally in some foods and can be found in plant products like berries and fruits. They offer half to one-third fewer calories than regular sugar. They are also converted to glucose more slowly, resulting in a slower rise in blood glucose and a lower glycemic index.

Tab: Effects of Inappropriate Carbohydrate Intake

Consuming Too Many or Too Few Carbohydrates: Consuming too many carbohydrates can lead to obesity and increase the risk of developing diabetes and tooth decay. Conversely, consuming too few carbohydrates can cause fatigue, low body mass index score (BMI), and increase the risk of skin breakdown and muscle wasting.

Consuming No Carbohydrates: If the body does not have carbohydrates as an energy source, it can convert protein molecules to energy. Carbohydrates prevent the body from breaking down protein for energy. This is called the protein-sparing effect. It is essential to note, energy from protein is not as efficient as energy from glucose.

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Dietary Carbohydrates

We know the difference between simple and complex carbohydrates. We also understand how starches, fiber, and sugars are required by the body to maintain good health. There are many nursing interventions focused on preventing and treating diseases associated with the over-consumption of carbohydrates.

By completing these learning activities, you will gain knowledge and skills regarding:

  • Carbohydrates as an energy source for the body.
  • Carbohydrates across the lifespan.
  • Carbohydrates that should be limited.
  • Interpreting carbohydrates and food labels.

Mrs. Smith and her daughter are in the health clinic to learn more about carbohydrates. They wish to receive additional information about carbohydrates in the educational session.

Family Members

Section 4: Explore: Client Education - Carbohydrate Basics

During the first meeting, the nurse plans to discuss basic carbohydrate nutrition and consumption.

Nurse's Consultation Transcript:

Mrs. Smith, welcome. Let's discuss carbohydrates.

Mrs. Smith, carbohydrates provide 4 calories per gram. Fiber is the only carbohydrate that does not provide calories. Be sure to always keep the same activity level. If your activity level declines and you consume the same amount of carbohydrates, you will gain weight. Your body will want to store the extra calories for later.

Using the MyPlate guidelines (USDA, n.d.), 3/4 of the plate should contain carbohydrates. Carbohydrates are fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. It is important to mention that if these food items cannot be obtained fresh, it is okay to purchase them canned or frozen. It is also very important for you to consume as many whole foods as possible.

Fruit Juice Versus Fruit

When fruit juice is extracted from whole fruit, the sugars from carbohydrates increase while the dietary fiber decreases. The overall effect is that fruit juices have a higher glycemic index than the fruit itself. While many of the other nutrients, such as vitamins and minerals, are less impacted, the benefits of fiber from whole fruit, even if it is canned or frozen, are greater than those from fruit juice.

Section 9: Explore: Carbohydrates and Healthy Eating

Some helpful tips on how to incorporate healthy carbohydrates into your diet include:

  • Start the morning meal with whole grains. Try a hot cereal, such as oatmeal, since it is high in fiber and will keep you feeling full longer.
  • Use whole grain breads for lunch or snacks. This is an easy way to obtain added vitamins and minerals from foods.
  • Choose whole fruit instead of juice, so your blood sugar rises more slowly due to the fiber in the fruit.
  • Pass on potatoes and instead bring on the beans. Beans have more vitamins and minerals and provide insoluble fiber that can lower your cholesterol.
  • Add wild rice or barley to soups, stews, casseroles, and salads when possible.

Section 10: Explore: Risks of Low Carbohydrate Intake

Cindy: "I hear and read about low-carbohydrate diets. Are those diets okay for me?"

Nurse: "Cindy, often you hear about fad, low-carbohydrate diets. If you remove carbohydrates from your diet, you will be limiting your intake of fruits, vegetables, and grains. These food items contain a lot of nutrients needed to maintain good health. Before considering any new diet, always make sure you maintain the appropriate caloric intake for your energy needs and consume nutrient-dense foods."

Risks of Low Carbohydrate Intake:

  • Bad breath – Related to the body breaking down ketones for energy
  • Rash – Related to the reduction of vitamins and minerals in the diet
  • Constipation – Related to reduced fiber intake
  • Weakness – Related to reduced glucose stores in muscles
  • Headache – Related to low glucose stores

Section 16: Explore: Carbohydrates Across the Lifespan

It is important to remember that carbohydrate energy demands change based on our lifestyle and aging. The energy demands of an infant, middle-aged adult, and older adult are different. Nurses understand that older adults must consume carbohydrates that match their energy demands or they will gain weight. Conversely, infants require a nutrient-dense energy source to maintain rapid growth and development.

Infants and Breast Milk

Lactose is the principal carbohydrate found in breast milk. Carbohydrates account for approximately 40% of the total calories provided in breast milk. It is important to note that lactose helps to decrease a large number of unhealthy bacteria in the infant's stomach, which improves the absorption of calcium, phosphorus, and magnesium. It also helps to fight diseases and promotes the growth of healthy bacteria in the stomach.

Human milk contains another sugar called oligosaccharides. These are non-nutritive sugars for the infant. Oligosaccharides are important because they provide a probiotic effect that promotes beneficial bacteria growth in the digestive tract.

It is also important to mention that infants need at least 400 IU of vitamin D each day to maximize calcium for strong bone growth.

Section 17: Explore: Social Determinants of Health - Effects on Carbohydrates

In comparison to other food groups, carbohydrates are often inexpensive and readily available. Unfortunately, the higher quality carbohydrates, such as whole grains and items to which sugar has not been added, may cost more and be less affordable to individuals with a stricter budget and those living in food deserts (areas with limited grocery stores).

SDOH and Carbohydrates (Transcript):

  • Economic Stability: The client has the financial ability to purchase carbohydrates to maintain good health. For example, the homeless or indigent population may not have the financial means to purchase healthy carbohydrates. Often, clients purchase processed foods because they are cheaper.
  • Education: The client has the health literacy to understand adequate carbohydrate consumption. For example, will a client know how to read a food label or calculate carbohydrates in food?
  • Health and Healthcare: The client has access to healthcare resources. For example, clients have access to pre-diabetes and follow-up care with their healthcare provider.
  • Neighborhood and Built Environment: The client has access to healthy carbohydrates. For example, clients that live in a food desert may not have access to complex carbohydrates. Clients that live in food swamps may only have access to high-fat, low-fiber food. Food swamps are areas that have a high density of fast foods and junk foods relative to healthy food options.
  • Social and Community Context: The client is safe while in the community. For example, are there safe areas to have community gardens?

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Water and Wellness

Water is vital for health and wellness. Unfortunately, access to clean water for cooking, cleaning, and bathing is not available to everyone. This lesson explores the role of the nurse in working with individuals to promote the adequate intake and use of water as part of their health and wellness routine.

By completing these learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Model Chamberlain Care when communicating with clients, families, and other healthcare providers.
  • Assess a client for altered fluid balance.
  • Identify nursing diagnoses for clients who have altered fluid balance.
  • Develop and implement a plan of care to address and intervene in clients with altered fluid balance.
  • Evaluate the outcomes of clients with altered fluid balance.

Section 3: Explore: Health History: Water Intake

When assessing a client's water intake, the nurse seeks cues related to adequate consumption of water, risk factors for fluid imbalance, and factors that contribute to fluid imbalance. For example, lactating women require approximately 700 ml of extra fluid daily to support water loss during breast milk production (Bardosono et al., 2016).

Recommended Water Intake for Adults
Male15 cups3.7 liters120 ounces
Female9 cups2.1 liters72 ounces

Although fluid requirements remain the same through all stages of adulthood, physiologic changes—such as altered pituitary function and renal disease, level of activity, dietary intake, and body composition—can greatly impact fluid needs and balance.

One nutrient that influences fluid balance is sodium, which promotes fluid retention when consumed in high amounts. When obtaining the health history of a client with a fluid imbalance, the nurse should obtain information on potential sources of "hidden sodium" (processed foods, salting food prior to tasting, home water-softener). In addition to assessing sodium intake, the nurse should determine the client's knowledge of dietary sodium, its relationship to holding water in the body, and how to monitor their intake, incorporating education into the nursing plan of care as needed.

Section 4: Explore: Nursing Care: Fluid Volume Excess (FVE)

Fluid volume excess (FVE), also referred to as fluid volume overload or edema, is a condition in which a person experiences increased fluid retention. This may result from excessive sodium or fluid intake, as well as physiological changes in normal body processes. Since increased sodium intake is a big factor in fluid volume overload, this section will review edema related to sodium intake. The American Heart Association recommends sodium intake should be about 1500 mg per day to maintain heart health (AHA, 2020).

Tab: Contributing Factors

Fluid retention, or FVE, commonly occurs due to:

  • Increased sodium levels in the blood (hypernatremia)
  • Excessive fluid intake
  • Excessive sodium intake
  • Liver disease
  • Malnutrition
  • Kidney disease
  • Heart failure
Tab: Assessment Cues
  • Weight gain
  • Altered electrolytes
  • Changes in blood pressure
  • Changes in hemoglobin (Hgb) and hematocrit (Hct) values
  • Edema
  • Polyuria
  • Shortness of breath, orthopnea, or dyspnea
  • Tachycardia
  • Bulging fontanelles in infants
Tab: Nursing Actions

The primary nursing actions involve managing physiological changes (edema, shortness of breath, tachycardia) caused by the excess fluid. When FVE is severe, alterations in serum electrolytes may lead to emergent changes in respiratory, cardiovascular, or neurologic function.

Routine nursing actions include:

  • Obtain daily weights
  • Monitor fluid intake and output
  • Implement fluid restrictions, if prescribed
  • Monitor vital signs
  • Assess extremities and areas of dependence for edema
  • Monitor lung sounds and oxygenation status
  • Administer medications, including diuretics and electrolytes, as prescribed
  • Provide client education concerning the causes, symptoms, and treatment of FVE
Tab: Evaluating Outcomes

During treatment for FVE, the nurse continues to monitor the client for cues related to over-hydration, anticipating that these will decrease as fluid balance is achieved. Client education regarding the risk factors for FVE and lifestyle changes (reducing sodium intake and adhering to prescribed therapies for known health alterations) is key to long-term control.

It is important to mention a large percentage of dietary sodium comes from the consumption of processed foods while a smaller amount comes from seasoning with salt (American Heart Association, 2020).

Section 8: Explore: Nursing Care: Fluid Volume Deficit (FVD)

Fluid volume deficit (FVD) occurs in the vascular and intracellular spaces when fluid output exceeds fluid intake (Wayne, 2022). Electrolytes within the fluid are lost in equal proportion, resulting in hypovolemia.

Tab: Contributing Factors

Fluid loss commonly occurs through the:

  • gastrointestinal tract (vomiting or diarrhea),
  • renal system (polyuria)
  • increased perspiration (fever, high environmental temperatures)

Decreased oral intake of water and other liquids also contributes to dehydration if the intake is less than output, particularly in infants and older adults. For example, older clients with memory impairment may forget to drink water, while those taking diuretics may purposely decrease fluid intake to decrease the number of times they urinate. People of all ages who are unable to get their own drinks (for example, infants or those confined to bed) rely on others to provide their fluids.

Tab: Nursing Actions

The primary nursing action for clients in FVD is to provide fluids.

  • For clients who can drink, the nurse provides fresh water and beverages of choice, balancing intake of sugar and/or caffeinated beverages that may contribute to dehydration with water.
  • If the healthcare provider orders the client to "force fluids," the nurse educates the client on why it is important to increase water consumption (Wayne, 2022). A schedule that divides the fluid intake through the day should be created and shared with the client to increase their compliance with rehydration.

Other nursing actions include weighing the client daily, monitoring intake and output, reminding the client to drink, assisting the client to the toilet, as needed, and notifying the healthcare provider if the prescribed fluid intake is not being achieved through oral intake. If the client is unable to consume the required amount of fluid, intravenous fluids may be prescribed.

All clients at risk for dehydration should be educated on prevention. For clients without limitations due to health, this includes:

  • Drinking mostly water
  • Eating foods with high amounts of water like fruits and vegetables
  • Avoiding or limiting drinks with caffeine like coffee, teas, and soft drinks
  • Avoiding or limiting drinks with alcohol, which inhibits vasopressin, increasing urine production
  • Avoiding or limiting sugary beverages, which can also increase urine production
Tab: Evaluating Outcomes

During treatment for FVD, the nurse continues to monitor the client for cues related to dehydration, anticipating that these will decrease as fluid balance is achieved.

Section 12: Explore: Nursing Care: Water Intoxication

The term "water intoxication" is generally used to refer to hyponatremia that occurs due to the consumption of excess water without adequate replacement of sodium, as may happen during strenuous exercise. Water intoxication is also possible when there is extensive loss of electrolytes because of dehydration, and rehydration is accomplished using only water without the addition of replacement electrolytes. Water intoxication results in physical changes in the body that, left untreated, can progress to behavioral changes, loss of consciousness, and death. Many of the behavioral changes result from hyponatremia in the brain.

Tab: Contributing Factors
  • Intense physical activity (e.g., running a marathon or military conditioning)
  • Forced water consumption, often in cases of abuse
  • Voluntary water consumption due to an underlying mental health condition
  • Use of the drug MDMA (ecstasy or molly), often used at parties/raves where users dance, the environment is hot, and users consume a great deal of water
  • Clients receiving total parenteral nutrition (TPN)
Tab: Assessment Cues
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Fluid overload: abdominal distention, periorbital edema, general swelling
  • Head pain
  • Muscle spasm, cramping, or weakness
  • Hypotension
  • Tachycardia
  • Drowsiness
  • Fatigue
  • Anger, irritability
  • Seizures
  • Loss of consciousness
Tab: Nursing Actions

The primary nursing actions involve managing physiological changes (edema, shortness of breath, tachycardia) caused by the excess fluid. When FVE is severe, alterations in serum electrolytes may lead to emergent changes in respiratory, cardiovascular, or neurologic function.

Routine nursing actions include:

  • Obtain daily weights
  • Monitor fluid intake and output
  • Implement fluid restrictions, if prescribed
  • Monitor vital signs
  • Monitor labs: plasma and urine osmolarity, serum sodium
  • Assess extremities and areas of dependence for edema
  • Monitor lung sounds and oxygenation status
  • Administer medications, including diuretics and electrolytes, as prescribed
  • Provide client education concerning the causes, symptoms, and treatment of FVE
Tab: Evaluation Outcomes

The goals of treatment for water intoxication include preventing brain hernia from swelling (severe cases), relieving the symptoms experienced by the client, preventing further declines in serum sodium level, and providing client education and support to prevent recurrence.

Section 1: Introduction to Water and Wellness

Did you know that humans can live for weeks without food, but only days without water? The human body is 50-75% water, which is found in lean muscle, fat, bone, and blood. Water is lost from the body in perspiration, urine, digestive juices, stool, and even respiratory exhalation. And since the body does not store water, it needs to be replaced each day. The amount of water a person needs depends upon their level of activity, body size, metabolism, foods ingested, and even the weather.

Water helps moisten mucous membranes, dissolve minerals and nutrients, and reduce the burden on the kidneys and liver by flushing out waste products.

This lesson explores how water supports health and well-being, as well as the nursing care of individuals experiencing alteration in water (fluid balance) within the body.

By completing these activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Identify the importance of water to health.
  • Assess clients for risk factors and conditions contributing to water-related health alterations.
  • Select a nursing diagnosis for clients with altered fluid balance.
  • Prioritize independent and dependent nursing actions to promote health in clients with altered fluid balance.
  • Evaluate the client's response to care.

Section 3: Explore: Functions of Water

Water has several major functions in the body. Water provides the structural components of cells, regulates body temperature, and cushions body organs. Water can also be a source of trace minerals. For instance, hard water contains high amounts of calcium and magnesium. You may have noticed mineral deposits on faucets. The taste of water can be impacted by the minerals, even when it is safe to drink. For this reason, many individuals prefer to drink filtered water at home.

Water filtration systems, sometimes referred to as "water softeners," are used to reduce the hard water effects by replacing minerals in the water with sodium chloride. This "soft water" tastes better while also adding a small amount of sodium with each glass.

Section 5: Explore: Water as a Nutrient

How Much Water Is Enough?

Water is an inorganic, essential nutrient. The adequate intake (AI) for water is 15 cups a day for men and 9 cups a day for women, in addition to the water consumed in food. Unfortunately, many U.S. residents consume less than the recommended amount. A daily intake of at least 500 mL (about 2 cups) of water is needed to maintain organ function.

Digestion & Absorption

Water is readily absorbed into the body by the small intestine without being digested. Although water is absorbed in the small intestine, it is regulated within the large intestine. For example, if the body is dehydrated, the large intestine extracts water from the colon back into the body. If dehydration is severe or prolonged, constipation can result as water continues to be extracted from the colon.

Metabolism

Water is vital to maintain metabolic processes within the body. For instance, water is required for the adenosine triphosphate (ATP) cycle to produce energy. Conversely, water is the byproduct when glycogen and fat stores are accessed by the body for energy.

Section 7: Explore: Water in the Body

Water resides in three major compartments within the body. They are the interstitial, intracellular, and extracellular compartments. The interstitial is the fluid between the cells, and the intracellular is the fluid within the cells.

The extracellular fluid includes all interstitial fluid and fluid outside the cell, including plasma.

Fluid and Electrolyte Homeostasis: Homeostasis means stable equilibrium or balance. Homeostasis is the body's ability to maintain a physiological state of balance. The body achieves this balance by distributing fluids (think: water), electrolytes, and minerals between body compartments to maintain a state of equilibrium.

Image Transcript (fluid volumes):

  • 4-5 liters of blood (intravascular)
  • 28 liters of fluid in cells (intracellular)
  • 11 liters of fluid between the blood and the cells (interstitial fluid)

Section 8: Explore: Understanding Thirst

Thirst is the first sign of dehydration and is the body signaling it requires fluid in order to return to a state of homeostasis. Feelings of thirst, or a dry mouth, stimulate a person to drink fluids. People who are young, or who suffer from cognitive deficits, may be unable to express their thirst and are at higher risk of dehydration. People who do not have access to clean water may also be at risk for dehydration.

When dehydration occurs:

  • The sodium level in the blood rises.
  • The body pulls water from salivary glands to compensate for the increased sodium.
  • There is a sensation of a dry mouth (thirst sensation occurs).
  • Fluid is ingested.
  • Homeostasis returns.
Thirst Mechanism

When the thirst mechanism is faulty, as it may be during illness, physical exertion, or aging, regulatory mechanisms help conserve water by reducing urine output.

Section 9: Explore: Nursing Care: Fluid Volume Deficit (FVD)

Fluid volume deficit (FVD) occurs in the vascular and intracellular spaces when fluid output exceeds fluid intake. Electrolytes within the fluid are lost in equal proportion, resulting in hypovolemia.

Slide 1: Contributing Factors

Fluid loss commonly occurs through the:

  • gastrointestinal tract (vomiting or diarrhea),
  • renal system (polyuria), and
  • increased perspiration (fever, high environmental temperatures).

Decreased oral intake of water and other liquids also contributes to dehydration if the intake is less than output, particularly in infants and older adults. For example, older adult clients with memory impairment may forget to drink water, while those taking diuretics may purposely decrease fluid intake to decrease the number of times they urinate. People of all ages who are unable to get their own drinks (for example, infants or those confined to bed) rely on others to provide their fluids.

The primary nursing action for clients in FVD is to provide fluids.

  • For clients who can drink, the nurse provides fresh water and beverages of choice, balancing intake of sugar and/or caffeinated beverages that may contribute to dehydration with water.

Section 11: Explore: Nursing Care: Fluid Volume Excess (FVE)

Fluid volume excess (FVE) is a condition in which fluid retention increases in the body. Fluid retention is most often the result of increased sodium intake.

Tab: Contributing Factors
  • Conditions that decrease cardiac output, such as heart failure
  • Altered liver functioning, such as cirrhosis
  • Altered kidney function, such as renal failure and nephrotic syndrome
  • Conditions that alter hormonal balance, including menstruation and pregnancy
  • Excess sodium or fluid intake
Tab: Assessment Cues
  • Polyuria (increased urine output)
  • Weight gain
  • Edema of the extremities or face, with or without discomfort
  • Discomfort in the torso, with or without swelling (cramping, headache, or bloating)
  • High blood pressure caused by excess fluid in the bloodstream
  • Shortness of breath caused by extra fluid entering your lungs and reducing your ability to breathe normally
  • Heart problems resulting from excess circulating fluids including bradycardia and tachycardia, chest pain, and cardiomyopathy
Tab: Nursing Action

Nursing actions should include diet education related to limiting sodium intake. Client education should always include reading dietary labels and identifying the amount of sodium content in foods and snacks.

Tab: Evaluating Outcomes

Successful treatment of FVE resolves the client's initial signs and symptoms. Swelling and pain become less, weight gain decreases, and other symptoms resolve to the client's baseline, or normal.

Untreated hypervolemia can cause several complications, some of which are life-threatening. The underlying cause is decreased perfusion as a result of the pressure created by the fluid accumulation in the interstitial compartments, often referred to as "third spacing." The interstitium can hold up to 10% more water than is usually within the body. Complications of significant edema include:

  • Pericarditis, or swelling of the heart tissues
  • Heart failure
  • Delayed wound healing
  • Tissue breakdown
  • Decreased bowel function

Section 13: Explore: Safety of Water at Home

In 2014, the town of Flint, Michigan faced a water emergency when an unacceptable level of lead was found in the drinking water supplied from the main treatment plant. This was an emergency because high lead levels in children can cause severe health issues such as behavior and learning problems, slowed growth, hearing problems, and anemia. In adults, increased exposure to lead can cause reproductive problems and decreased kidney function.

Other factors that affect water contamination in the home include water temperature, the amount of corrosion in water pipes, how long the water remains in the pipes, and the presence of sediment within the pipes (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, 2020).

Other barriers to safe drinking water include homelessness (no access to safe water), low income (unable to replace older, lead pipes), obtaining water from wells (contamination from the surrounding ground), and manufacturing and farming chemicals impacting the water supply (DeMyers et al., 2017).

Section 14: Explore: Outdoor Water Consumption

The water in lakes, rivers, and streams should always be considered not suitable for consumption due to potential contamination. This contamination can come from animals, algae, infectious organisms, and chemicals used in manufacturing and farming. Even if the water looks clean, it can be harmful (Frank, 2020). It is important to plan water needs for each person in a group since potable (safe) water may not always be available. Potable water should be used for drinking, oral care, hand hygiene, and food preparation.

Promoting Safe Drinking Water Outdoors

When involved in activities like camping, hiking, boating, or other outdoor activities, bring bottled water for consumption. If bottled water is unavailable or in low supply, surrounding water can be purified by boiling or using a water purification system. Chemical and filter combinations are available for this use.

Section 15: Explore: Nursing Implications: Water Safety in the Home

Nurses must assess and address the unique barriers each client may encounter while trying to access fresh, safe water (U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, 2015). Recommendations to improve the safety of drinking water in the home should be taught to clients at risk for water insecurity or water contamination in their place of residence.

  • Do not allow babies and children to drink bathwater.
  • Use only cold water for cooking and making baby formula.
  • Run cold water throughout the house up to 5 minutes every morning to flush pipes.
  • Clean the aerator on the faucet often; it can collect sediments.
  • Place an osmosis filter at the tap so it will filter the water in the pipes of the house.
