Section 1: Introduction to Dietary Standards

Dietary standards are guidelines created by various organizations or governments to recommend food combinations that keep the populations they represent healthy. In the United States, the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) sets standards for the types and amounts of foods the American public should consume daily. These guidelines include unhealthy foods to limit or avoid, foods that should be consumed daily, and even standards for vitamin and mineral amounts. These standards help those who want to maintain their health by offering guidance for healthy eating.

Using primary prevention, these guidelines do not address special diets for those with chronic conditions like diabetes or renal disease. Other organizations, however, offer recommendations for these groups.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Apply the metrics that make up dietary standards, such as dietary reference intake (DRI).
  • Define and explain daily values.
  • Evaluate food labels.
  • Review the nursing implications related to dietary standards.

Section 4: Explore: What Are Dietary Standards?

Consider this question when using dietary standards: Are groups, populations, or individuals eating enough nutrient-rich foods?

Dietary standards focus on providing nutrient recommendations to reduce the risk of long-term diet-related diseases (e.g., type 2 diabetes, cancer) and prevent nutrient deficiency diseases. For example, vitamin C is an essential nutrient. Humans need about 90 mg/day to maintain good health. If a population or individual has a deficit in vitamin C, it can lead to scurvy or poor wound healing. Scurvy is still prevalent today, especially in lower socioeconomic societies or countries.

What about a person who has been unhoused and has no access to fresh fruit or food fortified with essential nutrients? They are at risk for vitamin and mineral deficiencies that could significantly impact their health.

By providing information about the nutrients needed to maintain health, organizations can help people better by targeting the amounts and types of foods that may be missing from their environments.

Section 5: Explore: Dietary Standard Values

Dietary standard recommendations are made by the National Academy of Sciences, an institution within the National Institutes of Health (NIH). The actual nutrient standard recommendations are expressed as dietary reference intakes or DRI.

These dietary reference intakes (DRI) are based on current research into the nutrient and consumption levels within the population, group, or individual. The DRIs include:

  • Acceptable macronutrient distribution ranges (AMDRs) — the safe ranges for nutrient intake of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats
  • Recommended dietary allowance (RDA) — the recommended amount needed to meet basic physiological needs, based on research
  • Adequate intake (AI) — the amount assumed to be needed when no research is available to determine the recommended dietary allowance
  • Upper intake level (UL) — the maximum amount of a nutrient ingested that is unlikely to cause an adverse reaction

Review the most current standards on the NIH Office of Dietary Supplements website: https://ods.od.nih.gov/HealthInformation/nutrientrecommendations.aspx

Reference: National Institutes of Health, Office of Dietary Supplements. (n.d.). Nutrient recommendations and databases. https://ods.od.nih.gov/HealthInformation/nutrientrecommendations.aspx

Section 8: Explore: The Use of Dietary Reference Intakes

Dietary reference intakes (DRIs) are used as a reference when planning and assessing nutrient intake. DRIs can be used to evaluate the diets of groups as well as individuals. A client's nutritional status is observed through physical assessment, health history, and laboratory studies. View the image shown here to learn about some common uses of DRIs in healthcare.

The Common Use of Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs) in Healthcare:

  • Developing dietary guidelines and food guides (For example, suggesting food that is fortified with vitamins for pregnant women or evaluating the goals in the Healthy People program)
  • Planning and tracking nutrition-related public health programs and diets (For example, are meals providing adequate nutrition to military personnel or those in the Women, Infants, and Children (WIC) food program? WIC is used to prevent maternity-related complications.)
  • Creating patient and consumer educational programs (For example, providing diet guidelines to reduce heart disease for all men over 75 years old) (ODPHP, n.d.)

Section 9: Explore: Micronutrient Fortification

Not all people have access to foods that are rich in essential micronutrients. This may be due to where they live, what foods are available to purchase, how much money they have to spend on food, or their own lifestyle choices. To help everyone get the needed nutrients, many foods are fortified with essential micronutrients.

Tab: Fortified Foods

Fortification of food is the process by which a nutrient is added to the food to assist the population in meeting its nutrient needs. Some examples of fortified foods include milk (vitamin D and calcium added), salt (iodine added), and flour (folate added).

If food is fortified, it is usually specified right on the label. In cases where there is limited access to fortified food, supplements are available so people can take these nutrients as pills.

Tab: Nutrients Added to Foods

Here are some nutrients that are needed and how someone can add them to their diet.

  • Vitamin A: This nutrient is needed to maintain healthy eyesight and immune function; often added to milk.
  • Folate: This nutrient is needed for fetal development; often added to flour.
  • Zinc: This nutrient promotes immune functions; added to some breakfast cereals.
  • Iron: This nutrient is needed for motor and cognitive functions; often added to cereals or flour.
  • Iodine: This nutrient is needed for thyroid health and cognitive development in infants; often added to salt.
  • Vitamin D: This nutrient modulates the immune system and helps the body hold calcium; often added to milk.

Section 11: Explore: Daily Value—Bringing It All Together

How do we know how many nutrients are consumed from a food item?

How will the nurse help a client with diet planning?

How will the client's entire diet be assessed?

Nurses use food labels to answer all these questions.

All packaged food must have a food label. That label contains information called a daily value (DV). DV is the percentage of a nutrient in a food item based on a 2,000-calorie diet. The DV percentage is based on one serving, not the entire food package.

DV information is important because it allows for various food items to be compared. In addition, DV provides information about how food can be mixed to achieve a nutritional balance.

Section 12: Explore: Nurse's Role

Dietary standards provide nurses with a way to assess and plan interventions related to a client's diet. Dietary standards serve only as a baseline guide for healthy individuals. The nurse must understand what additional nutrients a client will need if they are ill.

Using the daily values (DV) on package labels, the nurse will communicate to the client the amount of each nutrient contained in one serving. In addition, DV can be used in comparing nutrients in foods.

The nursing assessment considers the following:

  • diets, vegetarian vs. vegan
  • religious, geographical, cultural, socioeconomic factors, health literacy, and activity
  • access to food or nutrients

Nursing interventions include:

  • patient-centered education toward dietary regimens
  • patient-centered diet planning
  • role-modeling behaviors

Often, healthcare teams may have a registered dietitian that can assist with diets. Regardless of the healthcare setting, nurses have a unique role in ensuring client education and diets are patient-centered.

Section 14: Explore: Package Labels

The nurse uses food labels to identify foods that are high or low in a particular nutrient.

Mr. Johnson (pronouns: he/him/his) has heart disease and is on a low-sodium diet. He can quickly identify that one serving of lasagna has 37% of sodium in one serving. This food item is high in sodium.

Is a lower % DV always healthier? If a food has 5% DV or less of a nutrient per serving, it is considered low in that nutrient. If it has 20% DV or more of a nutrient per serving, it is considered high in that nutrient. Low or high can be either good or bad—it depends on whether you need more of a nutrient (like dietary fiber) or less (like saturated fat) (National Institute on Aging, 2022, "Understanding Percent Daily Value [% DV]" section).

Sample Label for Frozen Lasagna

This product is high in these nutrients (over 20% DV):

  • saturated fat, 23%
  • sodium, 37%
  • calcium, 25%

This product is low in these nutrients (under 5% DV):

  • added sugars, 0%
  • vitamin D, 0%

Section 1: Introduction to Digestion and Nutrition

How does the food we eat give us energy? How do the nutrients from broccoli get absorbed into the muscle cells?

This concept discusses the processes within the gastrointestinal system that are responsible for breaking down nutrients. By completing these learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Review the process of digestion, absorption, and metabolism.
  • Identify how age-related changes and diseases can ultimately affect the absorption of nutrients into the body.
  • Discuss the nursing implications for the client with alterations in nutrition.

Section 3: Explore: Digestion

We do not eat nutrients, we eat food. The gastrointestinal system processes food so the body can extract and use the nutrients. The process by which the gastrointestinal system breaks food down into its nutrient components is called digestion.

When food enters the mouth, two forms of digestion immediately occur: chemical and mechanical digestion.

  • Chemical digestion occurs when enzymes begin to break down food.
  • Mechanical digestion occurs when food is torn and macerated by teeth.

In addition, the tongue assists in mechanical digestion by forming the food into a bolus and assisting with swallowing.

Tab: The Stomach

Mechanical and chemical digestion occurs in all parts of the stomach. Parietal glands secrete Hydrochloric acid (HCl) to further break down the bolus into chyme. In addition, HCl lowers the stomach pH, which aids in nutrient absorption.

The vagus nerve is responsible for gastric peristalsis and the opening of the pyloric sphincter. The vagus nerve controls how long the bolus is held in the stomach before it passes into the small intestine as chyme. The type of food that is digested will affect the vagus nerve response time. For example, high-fat foods are held in the stomach longer. It takes approximately 5 hours for food to move through the stomach into the small intestine.

Tab: Digestive Organs

The pancreas and liver both produce bile and enzymes needed to further break the food down into individual components. The gallbladder stores the bile produced by the liver to offer a larger amount of bile all at once to help with digesting fats. Both the pancreatic enzymes and the bile are expressed through the same duct (called the common bile duct) into the duodenum (the first part of the small intestine).

Tab: Small Intestine

Once chyme enters the small intestine, the pancreas and liver will release enzymes to further assist with digestion. Bile is secreted from the gallbladder into the small intestine to further break down fats. Bile is made in the liver.

At this point in the digestive process, the nutrients are still outside the body. Nutrients now must pass through the intestine wall and make their way to the cells.

Tab: Large Intestine

The large intestine absorbs fluid from food byproducts. This causes the stool to harden into the form of the large intestine. As peristalsis moves the stool through the ascending, transverse, and descending colon, the stool becomes more formed in preparation for expulsion from the body. The pressure exerted on the intestinal walls by the packed stool causes the urge to defecate.

Section 4: Explore: Alterations in Digestion

The food we eat takes about 5 hours before it is ready or able to be absorbed by the body. Alterations in digestion will ultimately affect nutrient absorption.

Gastric bypass is one condition that can affect nutrient absorption. Since there is a smaller stomach that remains after surgery, mechanical and chemical digestion are altered.

Case Study

Tony had a gastric bypass for weight loss. Since Tony's stomach is surgically altered and smaller, he can only digest small portions of food.

Nursing interventions to support Tony include dietary education that includes eating small portions of soft, nutrient-rich food. Nutrient-rich food such as cottage cheese, yogurt with berries, protein shake, or veggie omelet will provide a dense source of protein and complex carbohydrates. The nutrient density per cup of legumes and beans is higher than other vegetables. Healthy meat options that are low in saturated fat are fish and chicken. Other helpful recommendations that can help Tony reduce his sugar intake include the use of non-calorie sugar substitutes to sweeten foods and flavoring water with a slice of fruit to increase the mineral content of the water. (Johns Hopkins Bayview Medical Center Clinical Nutrition Department, 2020).

Image Transcript — Gastric Bypass Surgery

The normal digestive cycle goes through the stomach to the duodenum and then the small intestine. After gastric bypass surgery (in which a portion of the stomach is partitioned into a gastric pouch), the digestive cycle bypasses the majority of the stomach and the duodenum, rejoining the normal cycle at the jejunum.

Section 6: Explore: Absorption

After the bolus completes the digestive process, the nutrients are ready to be absorbed into the body. Absorption is the process by which the nutrients are taken into the body.

Most nutrient absorption from the chyme occurs within the microvilli in the small intestine. Several examples of processes that bring nutrients into the body are osmosis, active/passive diffusion, and pinocytosis. Remember, it takes energy for some nutrients to be absorbed into the body.

Factors Affecting Nutrient Absorption

Vitamin and mineral absorption are dependent upon the body's ability to store the nutrients and the immediate need for the nutrients.

On the other hand, mostly all fats, carbohydrates, and proteins digested are absorbed by the body. This is what leads to unintentional weight gain.

Section 8: Explore: Alterations in the Absorption of Nutrients

Drugs

Drugs can alter the absorption of certain nutrients. For example, antacids alter the stomach pH, thus preventing calcium from being fully absorbed into the body. Decreased calcium can lead to osteoporosis.

Increased Fiber

Too much fiber can decrease the absorption of several nutrients by moving food too fast through the digestive tract. For example, extra fiber can slow the absorption of cholesterol.

Section 10: Explore: Metabolism

The nutrients that were once in the bloodstream or lymphatic system now move into the cell. Metabolism is the process by which absorbed nutrients are used by the body. The organs that have first access to the absorbed nutrients for their metabolism are the liver, heart, and brain.

Cells utilize nutrients for energy (catabolism) and some cells utilize nutrients for growth and regeneration (anabolism). Either way, there is a by-product or waste that occurs from metabolism.

The lungs excrete carbon dioxide and water. The kidneys excrete metabolic waste and excess vitamins and are responsible for the reabsorption of nutrients. In turn, the large intestine excretes dead cells, water, bacteria, dietary fibers, pigments, and dyes.

Section 11: Explore: Alterations in Metabolism

Many factors, such as age, illness, and activity level can affect metabolism. Individuals whose bodies are growing or healing (children, pregnant women, those with severe illnesses) require the energy produced by a higher metabolism. People with active lifestyles, whether through exercise, sports, or a physically demanding job, also require more energy and a higher metabolism.

Those with lower energy demands due to increasing age, decreasing activity, or a sedentary lifestyle, tend to have a slower metabolism.

Section 15: Explore: Nutrient Imbalance and Clinical Signs and Symptoms

The link between the gastrointestinal system, nutrient absorption, and the signs and symptoms of nutritional deficiencies is crucial to identifying and correcting nutritional imbalances. For health professionals, knowing where a nutrient is absorbed and the symptoms of it not being absorbed are key to helping people stay healthy.

For example, an individual who is dehydrated may be unable to absorb water from the stomach, the large intestine, or both due to nausea (not drinking), vomiting (loss of fluids), or diarrhea (loss of fluids).

Section 7: Explore: Dimensions of Health

Health is a complex concept best described by physical, intellectual, emotional, social, spiritual, and environmental dimensions. You may be familiar with the World Health Organization's (WHO, 2020) original definition of health, "Health is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease and infirmity." However, as a healthcare provider, you should explore all dimensions of health to create a holistic health picture.

Let's reflect on the Six Dimensions of Health, as defined by Grodner et al. (2020).

Physical Health: It refers to the efficiency of the body to function appropriately, to maintain immunity to disease, and to meet daily energy. Physical health depends on the amount of nutrients available to the body. The human body is composed of nutrients in various combinations and their distribution is highly individualized to meet the physical health needs of the individual. For example, to support wound healing, a client would need extra protein-rich foods.

Intellectual Health: It refers to the use of intellectual abilities to learn and to adapt to changes in one's. Nutritional imbalances can affect intellectual health, as occurs with iron-deficiency anemia. A lack of iron-rich food consumption, especially in children, could lead to possible learning difficulties.

Emotional Health: It refers to the capacity to easily express or suppress emotions appropriately. Poor eating habits during times of stress may include overeating or undereating the appropriate amounts of food. These eating habits can lead to fluctuations in blood glucose levels.

Social Health: It refers to the ability to interact with people in an acceptable manner and sustain relationships with family members, friends, and colleagues. Nutritional status is sometimes affected by the quality of a person's relationships with family and friends. For example, do you enjoy eating with others, do you restrict certain foods based on who is present, or are you unable to eat in certain social situations?

Spiritual Health: It refers to the cultural beliefs that give purpose to human existence, found through faith in the teachings of organized religions, in an understanding of nature or science, or in an acceptance of the humanistic view of life. Several religions forbid the consumption of specific foods. For example, individuals practicing the Jewish faith adhere to the dietary laws of their religion, which do not permit the consumption of pork products.

Environmental Health: It refers to the external factors that affect a person's health and well-being, including the physical environment within which one lives and works as affected by determinants of ethnicity, education, income, and occupation, and extending to the larger environment of safeguarding natural resources to reduce exposure to preventable hazards.

Section 8: Explore: Health Dimensions and Disease Prevention

As you reflect on the six health dimensions, understand that nutritional concepts are foundational in each. As a healthcare professional, you will be able to discuss these with your client as each relates to personal health goals, their health condition, and overall well-being.

By exploring the various dimensions of health (not solely physical health), a client can then use available resources to help restore health and well-being and mitigate ways to prevent illness and disease.

The client needs to recognize dangers to health that could be reduced or alleviated through specific actions or changes in nutritional behaviors. Review common prevention strategies and relate them to nutritional concepts.

Think about nutrition strategies as they relate to the levels of prevention:

  • primary prevention: nutritional strategies to prevent the initial development of a disease, illness, or disorder
  • secondary prevention: involving early detection to stop or reduce the effects of a disease, illness, or disorder
  • tertiary prevention: nutritional support and educational guidance after a disease, illness, or disorder develops

Section 13: Explore: The National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey

The National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) is a program of studies designed to assess the health and nutritional status of adults and children in the United States. The survey is unique in that it combines interviews (demographic, socioeconomic, dietary, and health-related questions) and physical examinations (medical, dental, and physiological measurements, as well as laboratory tests) administered by healthcare providers.

NHANES is a major program of the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS), part of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), and has the responsibility of producing vital health statistics for the nation.

NHANES provides important data on the nation's overall health. However, it does not focus specifically on populations at high risk for disease. Standardization over time provides useful data on nutritional trends and changes.

For more information related to the examination survey, review the CDC's introduction of the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey: https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/about_nhanes.htm

Section 14: Explore: Role in Health, Nutrition, and Wellness

Nutrition care is diagnosed and prescribed by primary care providers, which includes interdisciplinary collaboration with registered dietitians/nutritionists (RDNs), as part of a multidisciplinary healthcare team. Although generalist nurse providers do not prescribe dietary treatment regimens, they do offer recommendations and often educate clients about their prescribed dietary treatment plans.

Healthcare providers have a critical role in educating clients about health, nutrition, and wellness. Wellness is a lifestyle (i.e., a pattern of behaviors) that enhances a person's level of health. Food consumption should be viewed and reinforced as a positive way to nourish the body and to promote overall health and wellness.

Section 15: Explore: Health Literacy

As healthcare providers, we have to consider health literacy, the client's ability to acquire, comprehend, communicate, and utilize basic nutritional health information and services and integrate them into their own health decisions.

In terms of health literacy, there are various ways for teaching and learning through:

  • formal education, which is purposefully planned for implementation in an educational setting
  • nonformal education, which takes place through organized teaching and learning events in hospitals, clinics, and community centers
  • informal education, which encompasses a variety of educational experiences that occur through daily activities

Understanding nutritional concepts and health literacy is foundational to supporting personal health and wellness goals (e.g., Chamberlain Care® including taking care of yourself), and providing nursing care to clients in need of nutritional intervention to promote health and support optimal health outcomes.

Section 1: Introduction to Nutritional Factors

Many factors influence the foods our clients eat, such as culture, religion, and economics. Some factors are individualized, including dietary habits or the presence of chronic disease processes. External factors can include the type and amount of food availability. For example, food deserts, areas where nutritious food may be scarce, can significantly affect a client's nutritional status. Fortunately, there are many resources available to reduce food shortages and promote healthy food habits.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Discuss personal and community factors that affect nutrition.
  • Identify local and national resources that help improve nutrition.
  • Review the nursing care of clients with nutritional imbalances.

The decisions that clients make about food choices determine their health. Nurses must have a basic understanding of how certain factors overlap and intersect to influence our clients' food choices.

Section 4: Explore: Organizations That Help

Many organizations fill a void by preventing malnutrition and improving health through better food choices. Some agencies take a global perspective (like the World Health Organization), while others focus on individual communities or states. These agencies may be funded by governments, private foundations, and/or local donations.

Here are some areas agencies address to affect food availability and choice.

Tab: Globally

Various organizations funded by governments and private entities address food shortages and availability. Issues include working with governments and organizations that may be located in areas of war and famine. The primary goal is food supply and reduction in disease (addressing food storage, potable water, etc.).

Tab: Nationally

The U.S. Institute of Medicine (IOM) makes nutrition recommendations. The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) incorporates these recommendations into a set of dietary guidelines, such as Healthy People. The Department of Health Services identifies goals through Healthy People 2030 that address food shortages and nutrition.

Other national organizations, like the American Dietetic Association, publish nutrition-related guidelines as well. In the community environment, we can observe how a community addresses issues such as food deserts and lack of access to fresh foods. Section 5: Explore: Social Determinants of Health and Nutrition

Social determinants of health are conditions in the environments in which people are born, live, work, play, worship, and age that affect a wide range of health, including nutrition (Alliance for Strong Families and Communities, 2020). Nurses must assess all aspects of the client's environment to address any barriers the client may encounter while trying to maintain good health. Once the client's barriers to nutrition are identified, the nurse can implement interventions and recommendations that are client-centered.

Let's review how a client's assessment intersects with the social determinants of health.

Transcript — Social Determinants of Health:

  • Economic Stability: Assess the client's employment and their ability to purchase food to maintain a healthy diet. The ability to consistently acquire food is the most critical social determinant influencing healthy eating.
  • Neighborhood and Physical Environment: Assess the client's neighborhood for access to parks and activities for the client to stay active.
  • Education: Health Literacy: Assess if the client has the capacity to obtain, process, and understand basic health information.
  • Community and Social Context: Assess the client's community support structure, such as community gardens.
  • Food: Assess the client's access to healthy food options.
  • Healthcare System: Assess the client's access to healthcare resources such as dietitians, personal trainers, or nutritionists.

Section 7: Explore: Community Nutrition

As healthcare professionals, when we think about community nutrition, we think about a group of people maintaining good health. Examples of groups of people are older adults in a living center, children at an elementary school, or all pregnant women in one state.

As an example, let's consider all the healthy individuals in the U.S. as one group. The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS) provide dietary guidelines and lifestyle recommendations for healthy individuals in the U.S. In addition, the USDA and DHHS provide education and resources that help guide individuals and communities in making healthy food selections. The USDA's and DHHS's overarching goals are to prevent diet-related diseases resulting from a nutrient deficiency or excess. Four major guidelines (USDA & DHHS, 2020) are provided for communities with a blueprint to follow.

As nurses, it is important to help clients incorporate these guidelines into their lifestyles. Nurses must perform thorough assessments to ensure diet planning, recommendations, and goals are client-centered.

Transcript — Dietary Guidelines for Americans 2020-2025:

  • Guideline 1: Follow a healthy eating pattern at every life stage.
  • Guideline 2: Customize and enjoy food and beverage choices to reflect personal preferences, cultural traditions, and budgetary considerations.
  • Guideline 3: Focus on meeting food group needs with nutrient-dense foods and beverages, and stay within calorie limits.
  • Guideline 4: Limit foods and beverages higher in added sugars, saturated fat, and sodium, and limit alcoholic beverages.

Section 10: Explore: Food Portions

Follow-Up Question

The nurse is incorporating the United States Department of Agriculture's (USDA) food guide recommendations into the Johnson family education session. Mr. Johnson (pronouns: he/him/his) asked his second question.

Let's review a few of the important details.

  • Fruits—Focus on whole fruits. Use canned or frozen when fresh fruit is not available. Fruits provide added fiber and minerals to your diet. Make half your plate with fruits and vegetables.
