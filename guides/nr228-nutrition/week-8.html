Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition and Cardiovascular Health

Cardiovascular disease is a group of diseases and conditions that affect the heart and blood vessels. It is currently the leading cause of death in men and women throughout the United States among all ethnic and racial groups. Diet is an important risk factor in coronary heart disease. Food-related risk factors include obesity, high blood pressure, uncontrolled diabetes, and diets high in saturated fats. A low-saturated fat, high-fiber, high plant-based diet can substantially reduce the risk of developing heart disease.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Utilize prior knowledge of theories and principles of nutrition and cardiovascular health.
  • Implement the nursing process to determine appropriate nutrition for clients with cardiovascular disease.
  • Demonstrate clinical decision making as it pertains to cardiovascular health.

Section 3: Explore: Cardiovascular Disease

Cardiovascular disease remains one of the world's leading causes of death due to genetic and lifestyle influences. Selecting a heart-healthy diet and adopting an active lifestyle will help decrease the risk for development of cardiovascular disease that can lead to multiple health complications.

The nurse plays a large role in client teaching, promoting, and modeling a healthy lifestyle and preventative behaviors.

Section 4: Explore: Assessment

Assessment of the client at risk for cardiovascular disease includes identification of risk factors through health history and physical examination. Understanding the pathophysiology of cardiovascular disease also assists the nurse in identifying the risk factors. Select the tabs below to learn more.

Tab: Health History
  • chest pain, heaviness, shortness of breath, weakness
  • diet and exercise patterns
  • smoking and alcohol intake
  • history of heart disease, hypertension, diabetes, or other cardiovascular problems
Tab: Physical Examination
  • vital signs
  • weight, body mass index, and weight to hip ratio
  • peripheral pulse assessment
  • heart sounds
  • skin color and temperature
  • presence of diaphoresis
  • anxiety
  • respiratory assessment—lung sounds, rate, and effort
  • oxygen saturation
  • electrocardiogram and cardiac rhythm
Tab: Pathophysiology of Cardiovascular Disease

It is important to remember that foods and nutrients absorbed from the gastrointestinal (GI) tract enter the bloodstream first before being broken down in the liver. It is especially important to know about fats, as the fatty deposits collect along the arterial walls forming plaque. This leads to hypertension, coronary artery disease, and eventually other cardiovascular issues if left untreated or modified.

Section 5: Explore: Nursing Diagnosis and Planning

Clients who are at risk for nutritional and cardiovascular alterations may have the following nursing diagnoses:

  • Acute pain related to angina pectoris after eating a large meal
  • Ineffective health maintenance due to unhealthy dietary choices and lack of physical activity
  • Knowledge deficit related to cardiovascular disease and nutrition

Appropriate planning for clients with nursing diagnoses related to cardiovascular disease (CVD) and nutrition include the following:

  • Provide teaching for modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors.
  • Evaluate dietary intake and plan consultation with the dietitian for heart-healthy diets such as Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH).
  • Identify lifestyle changes to decrease the risk for CVD (e.g., stopping smoking, changing diet, and increasing activity).
  • Regulate blood pressure through modifiable behaviors and appropriate medication administration.

Section 6: Explore: Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension Diet

DASH stands for Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension. The DASH diet is a lifelong approach to healthy eating that is designed to help treat or prevent high blood pressure (hypertension). The DASH diet plan was developed to lower blood pressure without medications.

Tab: Reducing Sodium

The DASH diet encourages you to reduce the sodium in your diet and eat a variety of foods rich in nutrients that help lower blood pressure, such as:

  • potassium
  • calcium
  • magnesium
Tab: Prevention

The DASH diet is also in line with dietary recommendations to prevent:

  • osteoporosis
  • cancer
  • heart disease
  • stroke
  • diabetes
Tab: Versions of DASH

There are two versions of the DASH diet:

  • Standard DASH diet—2,300 milligrams of sodium a day
  • Lower-sodium DASH diet—1500 milligrams of sodium a day

Section 7: Explore: Evaluation

Evaluating the nutritional needs of clients with cardiovascular disease (CVD) includes the following:

  • adherence to medication
  • dietary modifications
  • increase in activity as physically able
  • improvement of vital signs and decrease in cholesterol and lipid levels
  • cessation of chest pain, improvement of tissue perfusion, and adequate circulation (PaO2 and PaCO2 normal levels)
  • maintenance of systolic blood pressure, pulse pressure, and cardiac rhythm within normal parameters
  • demonstration of decrease in or absence of complications from CVD

Section 9: Explore: Hypertension

Complications of cardiovascular disease include both primary and secondary hypertension. Explore each further below.

Tab: Primary Hypertension

Primary hypertension has no single known cause, but several factors are linked to variations in blood pressure control. These are:

  • genetics factors
  • diet especially increased salt (sodium chloride) intake
  • obesity
  • insulin resistance
  • excess alcohol
  • aging
  • increased stress
  • sedentary lifestyle
Tab: Secondary Hypertension

In secondary hypertension, blood pressure is raised due to a known underlying cause. These are:

  • renal disorders (e.g., chronic pyelonephritis, diabetic nephropathy).
  • vascular disorders (e.g., coarctation of the aorta).
  • endocrine disorders (e.g., primary hyperaldosteronism).
  • drugs (e.g., alcohol, cocaine)
  • miscellaneous causes (e.g., scleroderma, obstructive sleep apnea)

As a nurse, it is important to teach clients about ways to decrease cardiac disease risk, which includes eating and maintaining a healthy diet. Knowing factors that are linked to an increased risk of cardiovascular disease can help direct clients into making healthy choices to support heart health.

Section 11: Explore: Dyslipidemia and Diet

Altered serum lipids lead to dyslipidemia. Dyslipidemia can refer to levels that are either higher or lower than the normal range for those blood fats.

  • Dyslipidemia most often occurs as a result of diet and lifestyle.
  • Dyslipidemia may also be triggered by intermittent hypoxemia from sleep apnea.
Tab: Trans Fat

Trans fats increase low-density lipoprotein (LDL, "bad" cholesterol) levels and decrease high-density lipoprotein (HDL, "good" cholesterol) levels; therefore, they should be closely monitored and/or restricted. Encourage clients to read nutrition facts on labels of packaged foods which identify trans fat amounts.

Trans fats can be found in:

  • some margarines (especially stick margarine) and shortening
  • foods with hydrogenated or partially hydrogenated fat or oil
Tab: Hydrogenated Oils

Many commercially made foods contain partially hydrogenated oils:

  • bakery goods—cakes, pies, cookies, and crackers
  • premixed products—pancake mix and chocolate drink mix
  • fried foods—corn chips, french fries, fried chicken, and fried fish
  • snack foods—chips (e.g., corn, tortilla, potato) and microwave popcorn
Tab: Saturated Fat

Saturated fat is also a type of dietary fat. It is one of the unhealthy fats, along with trans fat. Foods like butter, palm and coconut oils, cheese, and red meat have high amounts of saturated fat.

Section 14: Explore: Tips for Eating Healthy

Ten heart-healthy diet and lifestyle recommendations from the American Heart Association (n.d.) include:

  • Start by knowing how many calories you should eat and drink to maintain your weight.
  • Increase the amount and intensity of your physical activity to match the number of calories you take in. Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate physical activity or 75 minutes of vigorous physical activity—or an equal combination of both—each week.
  • Follow the Mediterranean or DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) eating plan. Eat a variety of nutritious foods from all the food groups, and focus on the following:
    • a variety of fruits and vegetables
    • fiber-rich whole grains
    • low-fat dairy products
    • skinless poultry and fish, prepared in healthy ways
    • nuts and legumes
    • olive oil and non-tropical vegetable oils
  • Eat fewer nutrient-poor foods: saturated fats, trans fat, sodium, red meat, sweets, alcohol, and sugar-sweetened beverages.
    • Alcohol can alter the effectiveness of some cardiovascular medications, such as beta-blockers (e.g., atenolol, esmolol, and metoprolol sulfate).
  • Eat a variety of fresh, frozen, and canned vegetables and fruits without high-calorie sauces or added salt and sugars. Replace high-calorie foods with fruits and vegetables.
  • Eat fish at least twice a week, especially sources of omega-3 fatty acids (salmon, etc.).
  • Select fat-free (skim) and low-fat (1%) dairy products.
  • Avoid foods containing partially hydrogenated vegetable oils.
  • Choose low sodium foods and prepare them with little or no salt.
  • Use alcohol in moderation: one drink per day (women) and no more than two drinks per day (men). Red wine contains resveratrol, a protective substance. (American Heart Association, n.d.)

Reference

American Heart Association. (n.d.). The American Heart Association diet and lifestyle recommendations. https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/healthy-eating/eat-smart/nutrition-basics/aha-diet-and-lifestyle-recommendations

Section 16: Explore: Eating Away From Home

Once a person understands their dietary restrictions and can make healthy choices at home, their next challenge is often understanding how to select healthy items when dining outside their home, where they do not control which ingredients are used to prepare and cook the food. Even when people limit how often they eat out, a small amount of the wrong food could exacerbate their cardiovascular disease.

Fortunately, dietary restrictions do not need to interfere with eating healthy outside one's home when people take time to plan. Some tips that can be shared by the nurse or registered dietitian include:

  • bringing a healthy dish to gatherings with friends or neighbors
  • asking that high-sodium ingredients be left out of a dish
  • removing high-fat items, such as cheese, from a salad
  • going online to look at menus and nutritional information to know what foods are most aligned with their dietary needs

For the client's overall sense of well-being and being a part of a community, it is important for them to understand that staying at home for all meals can limit interactions with friends and relatives. The key to eating away from home is to create a plan that works for them.

Section 3: Explore: Case Scenario: Cancer

Cory (pronouns: he/him/his) is a 25-year-old male who works at a local construction company as a heavy equipment operator. He loves his job and the team he works with. He recently got engaged to his long-time girlfriend who he considers the love of his life. Money is tight because they are saving money to buy a house and have a beautiful wedding.

Cory's friends are mainly his co-workers as he is not very close with his family. He doesn't really have a best friend but will go out with the guys after work. He regards his fiancée's family as his.

One evening, Cory's fiancée mentioned to him that there was a lump on his testicle, and he should see a healthcare provider. He reassured her that it was nothing and he was fine. A month later, he noticed that it was larger and made an appointment. He didn't tell anyone because he didn't want them to be worried.

An ultrasound revealed that it was a solid mass, and blood tumor marker tests were positive for testicular cancer. The healthcare provider recommended that Cory undergo a radical inguinal orchidectomy, removing the testicle. They would stage the cancer, and he may need chemotherapy. Cory was devastated but scheduled the surgery. The healthcare provider discussed the importance of good nutrition prior to surgery.

That night he told his fiancée.

Section 6: Explore: Coping With Diagnosis

After the surgery, Cory was diagnosed with Stage II testicular cancer. Tests also found that the cancer had spread to the abdominal lymph nodes, so the healthcare provider discussed retroperitoneal lymph node dissection. It is a major surgery that would affect fertility. He also began four cycles of chemotherapy.

Cory felt embarrassed about having only one testicle and now, with finding out that it would be more difficult to have children, he became depressed. The chemotherapy made him tired and he lost his appetite for food. He began to lose weight. His fiancée was heartbroken by the news. Her dream was to have a large family. Although the healthcare provider assured them both that they could have children by other means, it was too much for her. She broke off her engagement and left Cory.

He felt like he wasn't worth anything. Without any support from work, he had thoughts of giving up and stopping treatment. As a result, he barely ate and didn't take care of himself.

Section 9: Explore: Cancer and Decreased Nutrition

Due to his decrease in weight, the healthcare provider discussed the situation with Cory. Cory needs to be healthier in order to proceed with the retroperitoneal lymph node dissection. The provider suggested that Cory see a counselor and a dietitian. The counselor would help Cory with coping skills, while the dietitian would help with methods to increase calorie intake.

The provider also discussed ways to help with the anorexia and any taste abnormalities that Cory had from the chemotherapy. Another option discussed was to use enteral nutrition to provide nutrition. Cory would spend a few days in the hospital to regain nutrition and energy.

The provider stressed to Cory that he has a very good chance of survival if the dissection is completed and chemotherapy is finished. The side effects are temporary and most resolved 3–4 weeks after treatment. Cory agreed to the enteral nutrition. In the hospital, he realized that he needed to fight the cancer and get his life back. Some of his co-workers came to visit and that inspired Cory. They offered to help him out so he could get the surgery and recover.

Section 10: Explore: Case Scenario: Human Immunodeficiency Virus

Now, consider the same client, but replace cancer with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV).

Cory started to get sick a lot and was slowly losing weight. Cory found out that he contracted HIV from a previous partner. His fiancée was devastated and left him. Because he was so weak, he lost his job. His life was falling apart, and he wanted to give up.

At first, Cory refused treatment due to his depressed mood. The healthcare provider warned him that if he did not start treatment, he would put himself at risk for acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS). Once he had this wasting syndrome, the chance of dying is high. So, Cory started on antiretrovirals. He noticed side effects like nausea and vomiting. He had mouth sores that made it difficult to eat. Since starting the medications, Cory had to change his lifestyle to live with these side effects and a suppressed immune system.

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition and Renal Health

The kidneys play a key role in maintaining homeostasis by filtering out the toxins in the blood. When the kidneys are not functioning properly, it can lead to severe system complications.

There are various types of renal disorders and diseases and based upon the type, it is important for the nurse to understand the recommended nutritional support. Proteins, fluid, and electrolytes are very important to monitor for renal complications, and diets will be highly individualized.

Section 5: Explore: Chronic Kidney Disease Progression

Viola contacted her healthcare provider 6 months after her initial diagnosis of Stage 2 of chronic kidney disease (CKD), because she noticed swelling in her hands and feet. She is also having back pain that she had not had before, is always tired, and is not urinating as much.

Serum blood testing showed a decrease in her estimated glomerular filtration, rated from 60 at the time of diagnosis to 51 now, which means she is now in CKD Stage 3.

Based on her current condition, the healthcare provider (HCP) changed Viola's blood medication from a beta blocker to an angiotensin II receptor blocker, which will help reduce her serum protein levels. The HCP referred Viola to a registered dietitian for assistance with meal planning.

Viola loves to cook, and it is a challenge for her to abide by any diet restrictions. The dietitian stressed that healthy food choices could slow the progression of the disease and discussed ideas to overcome the challenges of adhering to a diabetic diet while also lowering sodium and protein intake. Here are the interventions:

  • Avoid processed or packaged foods.
  • Eat less potassium-rich foods.
  • Limit animal proteins, including dairy products.
  • Use herbs and spices to flavor foods, instead of salt.

Section 8: Explore: Renal Failure

Viola was doing well by following her diet and health regimen until her mother passed away. Viola was close to her mother and found it difficult to complete the activities of daily living. Friends and neighbors, unaware of her medical history, brought over meals and groceries for her. Viola didn't want to disappoint or offend them, so she ate the food provided.

Her husband (pronouns: he/him/his) became worried about her. She had obvious weight loss and fatigue. One week later, he noticed that she was confused about simple things and wasn't eating. She said she was nauseated and had muscle cramps. He noticed she was itching her arms a lot. After some convincing, he took her to the emergency department (ED).

The healthcare provider diagnosed her with:

  • renal failure
  • uremia
  • metabolic acidosis
  • hyperglycemia
  • hypertension

Viola has orders to be admitted and begin hemodialysis immediately.

Section 10: Explore: Dialysis

Viola was upset because she felt like she couldn't eat anything. The dietitian stated that a plate should contain 1/2 vegetables, 1/4 carbohydrate-rich food, 1/4 high-protein food, and a piece of fruit. The biggest change is which fruit and vegetables Viola can eat.

Viola started on dialysis and met with a dietitian again. Although the dialysis filters out the toxins, it is vital that the meal plan is followed to avoid complications between dialysis sessions. Viola will have to continue her diabetic diet and consume:

  • More high-quality proteins
  • Less foods with high sodium
  • Less potassium-rich foods
  • Less foods containing phosphorus
  • Less fluids

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition and the Stress Response

Complicated stress plays a pivotal role in susceptibility to disease. Stress is unhealthy when individuals cannot adapt or when nutritional resources are depleted. Individuals we care for can show symptoms of stress and these symptoms often require healthcare interventions. One of our roles is to assess for signs and symptoms (i.e., clinical manifestations) of stress and intervene by providing nutritional therapy to promote overall health and well-being.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Review nutritional needs in times of severe stress.
  • Review a case example of severe stress (e.g., burn) and nursing care through nutritional support.

Prior to this learning activity, take the time to review foundational nutritional and stress concepts and pathophysiological effects of stress from a hormonal and physiologic response, to include clinical manifestations.

Section 3: Explore: Nursing Care—Complicated Stress: Burns

Complicated stress, such as in a client who has been burned, leads to not only an increased risk of injury, but injury impairs nutritional status, further decreasing immunity. It is a vicious cycle. The road to recovery from burns is complex and clients have many nutritional needs.

Nutritional therapy in burn care is a collaborative, interdisciplinary team approach to meet the complex needs of these clients. This learning activity will provide an overview of the specific needs of a burned client with complicated stress and associated nursing care.

Section 4: Explore: Nutritional Needs for Burns

One of the most stressful physical traumas is burn injuries. In addition to pain control and infection management, nutritional support is one of the most significant healthcare interventions for these clients. The nutritional needs for burn injury are highly individualized and complex and can cause severe stress on the body.

According to Grodner et al. (2023), overall goals for burn nutritional therapy aim to:

  • Support the skin as a barrier against pathogens.
  • Meet accelerated nutritional therapy needs (adequate calories and protein, maintain pre-burn weight, supply visceral proteins, and maintain lean body muscle mass).
  • Prevent nutrient deficiencies.
  • Prevent Curling's ulcer (gastric stress ulcers secondary to severe burns are known as Curling's ulcer, causing increased acid production and ischemic changes, which increases the risk for bleeding).
  • Manage hyperglycemia.

Nursing care involves supporting these nutritional therapy goals.

Reference

Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach (8th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 6: Explore: Energy Needs

A client with a burn injury has highly individualized energy needs. The most accurate method to determine specific energy needs is using indirect calorimetry.

Indirect calorimetry (IC) is considered as 'the gold standard' to determine energy expenditure, by measuring pulmonary gas exchanges. It is a non-invasive technique that allows clinicians to personalize the prescription of nutrition support to the metabolic needs and promote a better clinical outcome. (Delsoglio et al., 2019, Abstract).

When indirect calorimetry is not available, then other predictive models can be used. One of the simplest ones to use is the Curreri formula.

(25 kcal x kg) + (40 kcal x % total body surface area) energy

Energy needs will vary according to the size of the burn. It is not unusual for a burn client to need 4,000–5,000 calories a day to meet energy needs (Grodner et al., 2023).

Section 8: Explore: Fluid Replacement—Nursing Care

Burns disrupt fluid distribution in the client, which can reduce circulatory volume and hinder recovery. Fluid replacement is critical to prevent dehydration, maintain circulation, and prevent further complications. Nursing care includes supporting the client's fluid replacement needs.

  • Specific fluid replacement is individualized based on the client's age and weight and total surface area burned. The "Rule of Nines" is a commonly used assessment tool with percentage estimates of total body surface area (TBSA) burned (see the image below). Nursing care includes calculating fluid replacement needs based on an assessment tool. Identify in your clinical facility which assessment tool is used to calculate TBSA burned.
  • Large water losses are also possible from vomiting, hemorrhage, fever, infection, or diuresis.
  • Intravenous (IV) fluids are used initially.
  • Provide oral fluids as soon as possible. Remember, older adults require special attention as they have depressed thirst mechanisms.

Once fluid resuscitation has been initiated, it is important to identify if appropriate perfusion, hydration, and renal function are present. Strict intake and output (I/O) is reported. It is also important to weigh the client daily because fluid loss from perspiration and/or wound drainage is difficult to calculate.

Section 10: Explore: Intervention—Vitamin Replacement

Vitamins are critical for immune function, wound healing, clotting, and energy. Here are some examples:

  • Vitamin C builds connective tissue and capillary walls and is important for collagen synthesis and immune function.
  • Vitamin A is important for immune function and cellular repair.
  • B vitamins metabolize protein and energy.
  • B-complex vitamins build hemoglobin.
  • Vitamin K promotes blood clotting.
  • Vitamin B12 helps the body convert food into glucose, providing energy.

Exact supplemental requirements have not been identified with burn clients, however, they are commonly recommended due to the increase in energy demands.

Intravenous (IV) administration of high dose vitamin supplements improves vitamin levels. Nurses may administer medications as prescribed to support vitamin replacement needs.

Mineral replacement is equally important for burn nutritional therapy. Some examples include:

Section 13: Explore: Interventions—Methods of Feeding

Routine intravenous (IV) fluids supply hydration and electrolytes, but do not meet energy and nutrient needs. The options for providing nutrition to a client with high energy needs to promote optimal healing, include oral, enteral, and parenteral. Read further for more information.

Methods of feeding:

  • Oral: method of choice if it can be tolerated, if the burn is less than 20% total body surface area (TBSA), and the burn does not interfere with the oral site.
  • Enteral: when oral feedings are not tolerated, next is the enteral route.
    • Correct placement of the gastrointestinal (GI) tube is verified by gastric content aspiration, pH testing, radiography (i.e., x-ray), and less often by auscultation.
  • Parenteral: nourishment through peripheral veins or central veins, bypassing the GI tract.

Section 15: Explore: Nursing Action—Enteral Feedings

Enteral feedings are prescribed by the primary care provider and the clinical registered dietitian and administered by the nurse.

There are a wide variety of commercial formulas available for enteral feeding use. Feedings are regulated by amount or rate. The rate can be continuous, usually measured in mL per hour, or by a specific amount immediately infused (bolus).

Nurses will monitor for enteral feeding-related complications: diarrhea is the most common complication. Several mechanisms have been proposed in explaining enteral-nutrition related diarrhea.

Adding fiber-rich formulas may improve bowel function and help reduce diarrhea.

Section 18: Explore: Parenteral Feedings

The parenteral method of feeding involves infusing nutrients and fluids directly into the venous system, bypassing the gastrointestinal (GI) tract completely. This is used when a client with a severe burn cannot tolerate food or formula through the GI tract.

  • Peripheral parenteral nutrition is generally used for less than 14 days—for short-term nutritional support.
  • Central parenteral nutrition is for large nutrient needs or full nutrition support for longer periods of time.

Remember, enteral feedings are preferred over parenteral feedings because they promote normal nutrient metabolism and maintain integrity of the gut. Parenteral nutrition bypasses the GI system.

A team of specialists including primary healthcare providers, registered dietitians, pharmacists, and registered nurses work closely together during the administration of parenteral nutrition therapy.

Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Cardiovascular Health

Eating nutrition-rich food is essential to staying healthy. From the moment a person is born, a healthy diet supports growth while reducing the risk of chronic disease. The National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion (2022) reports that "adults who eat a healthy diet live longer and have a lower risk of obesity, heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers" (para. 1). Section 3: Explore: Nutrition and Cardiovascular Health

Two of the leading causes of heart disease and stroke are high blood pressure and high blood cholesterol (National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion, 2022). Consuming too much sodium can increase blood pressure and the risk for heart disease and stroke. The 2020–2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommend getting less than 2,300 mg a day, but Americans consume more than 3,400 mg a day on average (as cited in CDC, 2021, "Facts").

The average American consumes too much sodium, saturated fat, and sugar, increasing their risk of chronic diseases. For example:

  • Fewer than 1 in 10 adolescents and adults eat enough fruits or vegetables.
  • Six in ten young people aged 2 to 19 years and five in ten adults consume at least one sugary drink on any given day.
  • Over 70% of the sodium that Americans eat comes from packaged, processed, store-bought, and restaurant foods (National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion, 2022, para. 3).

This over consumption of foods is linked to negative health outcomes and will have consequences throughout the person's lifetime. Eating foods low in saturated fats and high in fiber and increasing access to low-sodium foods, along with regular physical activity, can help prevent high blood cholesterol and high blood pressure.

Section 5: Explore: Risk Factors

When looking at risk factors, it is important to identify which factors you can and cannot control. For example, if looking at cardiovascular disease, the risk factors are grouped into two categories: controllable and uncontrollable.

Controllable risk factors are those which you can take steps to change or influence:

  • diet
  • smoking
  • excessive alcohol consumption
  • inactivity

Uncontrollable risk factors are those which you cannot influence:

  • age
  • family history
  • ethnicity
  • gender

Early identification of risk factors as well as actions to change or improve your controllable risk factors will help ensure that you can enjoy a long and active future.

Section 6: Explore: Lipids

Lipids perform three primary biological functions within the body: they serve as structural components of cell membranes, function as energy storehouses, and function as important signaling molecules.

Lipids are classified into 3 categories. These categories include:

Tab: Triglycerides
  • The largest class of fats makes up more than 95% of lipids in the diet.
  • They are found in fried foods, vegetable oil, butter, whole milk, cheese, cream cheese, meats, avocados, olives, corn, and nuts.
  • They can be semi-solid (fats are room temperature) or liquid (oils)
Tab: Phospholipid (Lecithin)
  • component of dietary lipids
  • acting as an emulsifier (soluble in water and fat at the same time)
  • found in plants (e.g., soybeans) and animals (e.g., egg yolks)
  • crucial for building a protective barrier, or membrane, around your body's cells
  • transporting lipids within the body
  • not essential
Tab: Sterol (Cholesterol)
  • least common type of lipid
  • necessary to bile, vitamin D, sex hormones, cells in the brain and nerve tissues
  • not essential
  • only found in animal products
  • being considered bad for health, but cholesterol is essential for life

While it may seem that eliminating all triglycerides from the diet is in the best interests of one's health, that is not the case. Triglycerides are used to create cell walls, transporting lipids within the body, and synthesizing bile, vitamin D, and sex hormones. Following a balanced diet that increases the number of plant-based food sources and limits the number of fats and red meats, such as the DASH diet, provides enough triglycerides to maintain overall health and decrease cardiovascular disease risks.

Section 7: Explore: Screening for Dyslipidemia

A diet high in "bad" fats contributes to dyslipidemia (increased serum triglycerides) and is associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular disease. For most individuals, changing their diets to include fewer "bad" and more "good" fats is a risk factor they can control. Because cardiovascular disease risk increases as people age, screening for dyslipidemia provides data that can motivate what may be an unwanted dietary change.

Triglyceride levels are divided as follows:

  • Normal—less than 150 mg/dL (1.7 mmol/L)
  • Mildly increased—150 to 499 mg/dL (1.7 to 5.6 mmol/L)
  • Moderately increased—500 to 886 mg/dL (5.6 to 10.0 mmol/L)
  • Very high—greater than 886 mg/dL (10.0 mmol/L)

Section 11: Explore: Nutritional Therapy

The dietary changes that support heart health focus on controlling existing cardiovascular problems by decreasing the rate of atherosclerosis, lowering the overall cholesterol triglyceride levels, and reaching or maintaining a healthy weight. This often includes altering the types and amounts of the fats, fiber, carbohydrates, and proteins.

Fats

  • Replacement of saturated and trans fats with monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats can help decrease LDL cholesterol levels and increase HDL cholesterol levels.
  • Use more olive and canola oils and nuts, such as walnuts, almonds, or pistachios.
  • Fish oils may also reduce inflammation.

Soluble Fiber

  • 5 to 10 g of soluble fiber per day
  • 2 to 3 g of plant sterols or stanol esters per day

Carbohydrate

  • Eating more complex carbohydrates (e.g., whole grains) increases soluble fiber intake, improves serum lipid levels, and helps control blood sugar levels.
  • 45%-65% of daily calories should be consumed as complex carbohydrates.

Protein

  • Substitute plant proteins for animal proteins.
  • Use fish more often.

Section 6: Explore: Anorexia and Cachexia as a Result of Cancer

Common causes of malnutrition in clients with cancer are anorexia and cachexia. Anorexia is the loss of appetite for food and occurs with almost all cancers. Cachexia is the weakness and wasting of the body with a weight loss of greater than 5% in 6 months. Most of the time, it is common with cancers that effect eating or digestion, but it can occur if the body is not storing fat or maintaining muscle due to tumor growth. Cancer causes the release of cytokines, which leads to hypermetabolism requiring increased nutritional requirements. Most clients with cancer do not have the nutritional base to compensate for this, so the body begins to break down fat and muscle. Cachexia can lead to death, so if needed, parenteral or enteral nutrition may be used. Clients can have both anorexia and cachexia at the same time.

Etiology is usually multifactorial and can include:

  • side effects of treatment and/or medications
  • changes in taste and smell
  • decreased peristalsis
  • infections
  • psychological and emotional effects
  • hypermetabolism
Annette

Annette underwent a bilateral mastectomy and nearby lymph nodes were biopsied. Since the lymph nodes on the right side were abnormal, she was scheduled for radiation therapy. At first, she didn't feel like eating much because of the surgical pain, so she tried eating small amounts of fruits and vegetables throughout the day. She would add in protein-rich snacks like yogurt and peanut butter. The anorexia worsened after she had a few weeks of radiation. Her healthcare provider told her to eat something small every few hours and drink high-calorie, high-protein beverages like canned liquid supplements between the small meals instead of with the meals.

Nausea and vomiting are serious side effects of cancer and greatly impact nutrition. They can be caused by tumors, cancer treatments, slowing of bowels, electrolyte imbalance, infection, or anxiety (National Cancer Institute, 2018). Sometimes clients may have anticipatory nausea or vomiting due to an expectation of these symptoms related to treatment. This can cause clients to refuse or skip treatment. Nausea and vomiting can cause dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, fatigue, slow wound healing, and anorexia.

Section 12: Explore: Malnutrition as a Result of Human Immunodeficiency Virus

Section 13: Explore: Physical Impairments—Human Immunodeficiency Virus

Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) and resulting opportunistic infections affect every body system, causing physical impairments especially if left untreated. For example, infections in the eyes can lead to bleeding in the retinas and possible blindness. Some HIV medications can damage the heart, liver, and kidneys, yet the benefits outweigh the risks. Nausea, vomiting, mouth and esophageal lesions, and impaired dentition are common symptoms of acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS). All these physical changes impact nutrition.

Determining causes is crucial to successful intervention. It is important for people with AIDS to keep their weight up. They may need to increase their intake of protein and calories. Drinking extra water can reduce some side effects of medications and prevent dehydration. Healthcare providers may recommend vitamin and mineral supplements as well. If needed, parenteral or enteral nutrition may be used.

Section 14: Explore: Risk for Foodborne Illness While Immunosuppressed

Anytime the immune system is compromised, whether it be through cancer treatments or a condition like acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS), clients are more susceptible to food borne illnesses. It is important for this population to pay close attention to food safety rules. Developing a food borne illness can cause serious complications or lead to death.

Clients with suppressed immune systems should avoid:

  • cold meats such as deli lunch meat, hot dogs, and salami
  • unpasteurized dairy products and fruit juices
  • smoked fish
  • raw, unwashed fruits and vegetables
  • undercooked meats and poultry
  • raw or undercooked seafood and fish
  • undercooked eggs or foods with raw egg

Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Renal Health

The kidneys play a key role in maintaining homeostasis by filtering out the toxins in our blood. When the kidneys are not functioning properly, it can lead to severe system complications.

There are various types of renal disorders and diseases and based upon the type, it is important for the nurse to understand the recommended nutritional support. Proteins, fluid, and electrolytes are very important to monitor for renal complications, and diets will be highly individualized.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Describe nutrition restrictions for someone with kidney failure.
  • Explain ways to prevent kidney stones with nutrition.
  • Describe dietary considerations for dialysis clients.

Section 3: Explore: Importance of Nutrition and Kidney Health

The kidneys play an important role in the body. Diseases and conditions of kidneys, such as kidney disease, renal failure, and kidney stones, can affect other body systems and have serious consequences. These diseases can be caused or worsened by underlying conditions like hypertension and diabetes. Good nutrition can prevent or slow the progression.

For example, the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (2021) recommends that clients with chronic kidney disease in Stages 1–3 concentrate on weight management, control of diabetes and hypertension, and lowering cholesterol. It would be challenging for clients to deal with a new diagnosis and learn about nutritional requirements without professional assistance.

Clients should see a dietitian when there is a new diagnosis, a decline in kidney function, high potassium or phosphorus levels, hypertension, or if there are diet-specific questions. Research has shown that nutrition therapy can improve laboratory values, maintain kidney function, and increase knowledge of the condition. Surprisingly though, approximately 88% of clients with chronic kidney disease received no dietary consultation (Slinin et al., 2011). The same study showed that those clients that did see a dietitian had a 19% lower mortality rate.

Section 4: Explore: Kidney-Friendly Diets

Kidney-friendly diets are aimed at preventing damage from hypertension and diabetes. The following are diets recommended by the National Kidney Foundation (n.d.) to promote kidney health.

The DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) Diet

You may know this as a heart-healthy diet, but since it helps lower blood pressure, it is also a kidney-friendly diet. This diet can slow the progression of kidney disease and kidney stone formation. It is rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish, poultry, and low-fat dairy products. It limits sodium to less than 2,300 mg per day and discourages added sugars.

Mediterranean

In a study, clients following a Mediterranean diet reduced the risk of developing kidney disease, prevented the decline of kidney function, and promoted better kidney transplant outcomes (Gomes-Neto et al., 2020). Even though this is also considered a cardiac diet, it has a positive effect on the kidneys. Like the DASH diet, it is high in fruit, vegetables, and whole grains with lower amounts of meat and dairy products. A plant-based diet is not the same as a vegetarian diet. On a plant-based diet, clients eat mostly whole grains, fresh fruits, and vegetables, but they can also consume small portions of animal products. On a vegetarian diet, in addition to plant-based products, processed and refined foods, fast foods, and salty snacks are allowed. A plant-based diet can have many health benefits for clients with kidney disease.

Section 6: Explore: Preventing Kidney Stones

It is estimated that one in ten people will have a kidney stone over their lifetime (National Kidney Foundation, n.d.). Most stones occur from calcium combining with oxalate or uric acid accumulation in the kidneys. If a stone occurs, it can block the flow of urine and cause pain. If a stone occurs and is removed, testing will show what type of stone it is and further guide recommendations.

Tab: Drink Enough Fluids

One of the best ways to prevent a kidney stone is to drink enough water—about 2 liters per day. This dilutes substances that can lead to stones and keeps the excretion process. Citrus beverages, like orange juice, can help block stone formation as well. Episodes of dehydration can contribute to stone formation.

Tab: Increase Calcium Intake

One would think cutting back on calcium would decrease stone formation. However, this is a common misconception. Calcium is needed to balance oxalate and excrete it through stool. Low amounts of calcium cause oxalate to collect in the kidneys.

Tab: Reduce Sodium

Sodium can increase the amount of calcium in the urine. If the excess calcium does not bind to oxalate and excrete through stool, then it collects in the kidneys, thus increasing the chance of stone formation.

Tab: Limit Animal Proteins

Uric acid is a byproduct of protein metabolism. Therefore, limiting red meat, poultry, eggs, and seafood will decrease the levels in the body.

Tab: Avoid Foods That Form Stones

Food rich in oxalate, such as beets, chocolate, spinach, rhubarb, and nuts, can create kidney stones. If a client is prone to calcium oxalate stone formation, a dietitian or healthcare provider may recommend limiting or avoiding such foods. Food rich in oxalate should be eaten with a calcium-rich food, so binding occurs in the intestines and not in the kidneys.

Section 7: Explore: Limiting Sodium

Consuming less sodium is important for kidney health, especially in clients with chronic kidney disease or kidney failure. The kidneys are responsible for the sodium-water balance in the body. When the kidneys fail, the sodium can build up, causing:

  • increased thirst
  • systemic edema
  • high blood pressure
  • heart failure
  • shortness of breath

A low sodium diet is less than 2,400 mg per day. To limit sodium intake, do not use salt at the dinner table, reduce salt in recipes to half the amount stated, look for 5% or less sodium on food labels, and limit amounts of restaurant and packaged foods. Use herbs and spices to flavor food, but be cautious about salt substitutes. Many are high in potassium.

Section 9: Explore: Limiting Protein

Adequate protein is essential for muscle growth and energy. Protein is found in animal and plant sources. Animal sources of protein are considered high quality protein since they provide all the essential amino acids. Plant sources are lower quality proteins because they lack one or more of the essential amino acids.

Consuming more protein than needed makes the kidneys work harder and may make kidney disease worse. When protein is broken down, it produces a waste product called urea. If the kidneys are not functioning appropriately, urea is not removed and will build up in the body. High urea levels can cause fatigue and a poor appetite. It can also cause itchy, white deposits on the skin.

Clients with chronic kidney disease need to figure out the right combination of proteins and the right amount of proteins to eat. High quality proteins will keep the body healthy with limited portions. With that in mind, some high-quality proteins, such as dairy products, are also high in phosphorus, which is bad for the kidneys. Therefore, lower quality protein sources can be incorporated into the diet. Due to the complexity of protein intake, clients should discuss the intake of proteins with a dietitian or healthcare provider.

Section 11: Explore: Limiting Potassium

Potassium is needed for nerve and muscle health, including keeping a regular heart rate. The kidneys are used to maintain that potassium balance in the body. In kidney disease, toxins can build up in the blood and cause cardiac problems. Side effects of high potassium include weakness, numbness, and tingling. If the levels are too high, it can lead to an irregular heartbeat, heart attack, and even death.

According to the National Kidney Foundation (n.d.), a healthy adult consumes 3,500 mg to 4,500 mg of potassium per day. On a potassium-restricted diet, only 2,000 mg per day is allowed. There are many foods that are high in potassium, such as oranges, potatoes, and whole-grain bread. Foods like apples, carrots, and white bread are lower in potassium. The healthcare provider may also prescribe a potassium binder, which binds to potassium and is excreted through stool.

National Kidney Foundation. (n.d.). Potassium and your CKD diet. https://www.kidney.org/atoz/content/potassium

Section 12: Explore: Limiting Phosphorus

Phosphorus is a mineral that can combine with calcium. In kidney failure, phosphorus is not removed and remains in the bloodstream. It then pulls calcium out of the bones, making them weak. It can also damage blood vessels, eyes, and the heart. Signs and symptoms of high phosphorus levels include joint pain, weakness, muscle pain, itching, and red eyes. Therefore, it is important to limit phosphorus intake through diet and medications.

Phosphorus is in many packaged and popular foods. Clients with renal failure should avoid packaged foods that contain phosphorus or labeled with the word "PHOS" in the ingredients. Foods such as meats, dairy, beans, whole-grains, and dark sodas are also high in phosphorus.

Phosphorus binding medications can be taken with meals to lower the levels of phosphorus in the blood. Most of these medications are calcium-based. However, there are newer ones on the market. Clients on a phosphorus binding medication will still have to lower their phosphorus intake and watch their diet.

This is particularly important for clients who have received a kidney transplant.

Section 15: Explore: Vitamins and Minerals

Vitamins and minerals are essential to carry out certain functions in the body. In the case of kidney disease or failure, the body may not be getting enough of certain vitamins and minerals. Healthcare providers may recommend the following supplements:

  • B Complex works with iron to prevent anemia and helps facilitate energy.
  • Iron prevents anemia.
  • Vitamin C helps heal wounds and prevent infections.
  • Vitamin D facilitates calcium absorption into the bones.
  • Calcium keeps bones healthy.

There are some vitamins and minerals that those with kidney disease need to avoid. These include vitamins A, E, and K, because they are fat soluble, so they are stored in the body and cause dizziness, nausea, and even death. In addition, other vitamins can cause complications as well. For example, some people may take a low dose of vitamin C since larger doses can cause a buildup of oxalate which puts them at risk for kidney stones and other issues over time. The healthcare provider will determine if vitamin supplements are needed and what dosage.

Section 16: Explore: Fluids

Depending on the type and stage of kidney disease, the client may be under a fluid restriction. The more urine the kidney produces, the fewer restrictions there are on fluid consumption. When the kidneys cannot excrete water, the fluid will build up systemically, putting pressure on the heart and lungs. The client will have signs of fluid overload.

A client's fluid intake is calculated on an individual basis depending on urine output and dialysis settings. Fluids are considered any food that is liquid at room temperature. This includes ice cream, popsicles, gelatin, and soup.

Client education regarding fluid intake includes:

  • drinking no more than what is allowed
  • foods that are considered fluids
  • hidden fluids such as those used in cooking
  • how to manage thirst

For those clients that have large fluid restriction, thirst is a big issue. Encourage clients to limit sodium, suck on ice chips, sip fluids instead of gulping, take medications with applesauce, and maintain good oral hygiene. Clients should avoid mouthwash with alcohol since this will dry the mouth.

Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and the Stress Response

When considering stress, people often think of the psychological responses but not the interrelated body systems. Stress can have ill effects on the body. This includes the immunologic and metabolic processes and their nutritional counterparts. If the nutritional status is impaired, then it will further complicate the body's ability to recover in response to stress.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Identify immunologic and metabolic responses to stress and their relationship to nutritional status.
  • Discuss the effects of stress on nutrient metabolism.
  • Discover the effects of malnutrition and starvation.
  • Identify nutritional therapies to help minimize the effects of the stress response.

Prior to this learning activity, take the time to review foundational learning concepts of stress and the pathophysiological effects of stress from a hormonal and physiologic response, including clinical manifestations of stress.

Section 3: Explore: The Immunologic Response

One of the first body systems impacted by impaired nutritional status is the immune system, including mucous membranes, skin, gastrointestinal (GI) tract, T-lymphocytes, macrophages, granulocytes, and antibodies.

Once inflammation occurs, the body responds to stress with fluctuating hormonal and metabolic changes. To help maintain homeostasis, the immune system responds to protect the body. If the body is deficient in the nutrients needed, then clients will have decreased immunity.

Reactions are determined by the amount and duration of stress. For example, uncomplicated stress (e.g., nutrient deficiency secondary to malnutrition) is very different than the nutritional needs of a client who has trauma (e.g., broken bones), an infection, or has had surgery.

Impaired immunity leads to an increased risk of disease, which leads to the occurrence of disease, which then leads to compromised nutritional status, which then further impairs immunity. The body will try to compensate by increasing the metabolic needs demanded by the body. This cycle will continue if impaired immunity is not addressed.

Important! Recovery requires this cycle to be broken.

Section 5: Explore: The Metabolic Response

Let's review the stress response. When stress occurs, the body responds with a series of chain reactions within the central nervous and endocrine systems. The amount of response depends on the magnitude and duration of stress. Hormones play a key role in stress response, preparing the body to either respond or flee the stressor. Referred to as the "fight or flight" response, the responding hormones include:

  • Aldosterone is a corticosteroid that causes renal sodium retention.
  • Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) is referred to as vasopressin, which promotes conservation of water and salt to support circulating blood volume.
  • Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) causes the release of cortisol to mobilize amino acids from skeletal muscles.
  • Catecholamines (epinephrine and norepinephrine) stimulate hepatic glycogenolysis, fat mobilization, and gluconeogenesis.

Section 6: Explore: Uncomplicated and Severe Stress

There are stressful conditions that can complicate a client's nutritional needs or put them at risk. Let's look at how stress may be classified:

  • Uncomplicated stress is present when clients are at nutritional risk. Perhaps the client has a malabsorption issue, which puts them at nutritional risk. Once the malabsorption issue is corrected, then stress is alleviated.
  • Severe stress is associated with trauma, disease, infection, and some types of surgery. These clients have significant immunologic or metabolic responses to stress. Nutritional needs for these clients are increased to meet the body's physiologic demands.

Section 7: Explore: Ebb and Flow Phases

The body responds to severe stress in two phases—the ebb phase and flow phase—which helps explain the initial injury (ebb) and recovery (flow) phases of metabolic stress within the body. It helps the nurse identify specific metabolic responses to stress during each phase. The ebb and flow phases are generally discussed in response to trauma.

Ebb Phase

It occurs immediately after trauma and lasts from 24 to 48 hours. The main physiological role is to conserve circulating volume and energy stores for recovery and repair. Low body temperature, low blood pressure, and high blood glucose levels are characteristics of the initial injury phase.

Flow Phase

The flow phase is the recovery phase (that follows the ebb phase) approximately 3–10 days after an injury. During the recovery phase, energy expenditure increases. In the recovery phase, clients still have high blood glucose levels, but blood pressure normalizes and body temperature increases.

Please note, this is just a brief introduction to the phases as it relates to metabolic status. You will learn more about these phases as you progress in the program. It is important to know from a nutritional standpoint that nutrients and minerals, fats, proteins, and carbohydrates are in higher demand the longer the body is in a stressful or hypermetabolic response. Remember that a hypermetabolic response also decreases the client's ability to fight infection.

Section 12: Explore: Malnutrition: Metabolic Effects

Section 13: Explore: Starvation

Malnutrition occurs along a continuum from a severe lack of nutrients to an extreme abundance of nutrition. The response of the body to a lack of nutrients ranges from slight to severe. Decreased dietary intake can be voluntary (limiting intake to lose weight) or involuntary (lack of access to nutrient-rich foods). Prolonged lack of access to food is referred to as starvation (Grodner et al., 2023). Starvation involves clients having reduced protein stores, reduced metabolic rate, and severe nutrient deficiencies. During starvation, the body conserves protein and glycogen stores as much as possible, and all body tissues (other than the brain, nerves, and red blood cells) derive most of their energy from fat. After 24 hours of starvation, glycogen stores are depleted, so after 36 hours, the body will have to deplete amino acids and produce glucose by gluconeogenesis.

The body conserves energy during starvation by decreasing metabolic rate (hypometabolic), which will make the person feel colder than usual. Mental acuity would decrease as a result of decreased glucose supply. To conserve energy, the person is likely to sleep more than usual.

If the body cannot adapt successfully, then metabolic stress and effects occur. Failed adaptation will continue to deplete any protein and fat stores, further suppressing the immune system and ultimately leading to death.

Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Trauma

Complicated stress can be caused by trauma, illness, disease, and various types of surgery. It leads not only to an increased risk of disease but impairs nutritional status and further decreases immunity. It is a vicious cycle.

By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Review energy requirements and other nutritional needs in times of severe stress.
  • Identify the effects of complicated stress on metabolism.
  • Discuss high-risk populations and the nutritional needs of clients with complicated stress.

Section 3: Explore: Severe Stress

View the slideshow using the arrows for an explanation of severe stress.

Slide 1 / 2

The severe stress caused by trauma, complex illness or disease, and surgery complicates the client's underlying health and recovery. Some examples of traumatic problems that lead to complicated stress are burns, fractures, malabsorption issues, and high-risk surgeries such as hip replacement, open-heart surgery, and bariatric surgery.

In an ideal world, all clients who are to undergo surgery will have optimal health and nutritional status. However, the client may have nutrient deficiencies secondary to the underlying health concern causing the need for surgery. These clinical manifestations include, but are not limited to:

  • nausea
  • vomiting
  • fatigue
  • anorexia
  • fever
  • decreased oral intake
Slide 2 / 2

Severe stress may place the client in a hypermetabolic state. The road to recovery is to treat the underlying stress in collaboration with meeting their nutritional needs. This collaborative approach will support healing and decrease the risk of infection.

If the client has nutritional deficits prior to complicated stress, then this can further complicate the healing and recovery process. If the client does not have an immediate need for surgery, then meeting their nutritional needs will come first, prior to being a surgical candidate.

Section 5: Explore: Ebb and Flow Phases

Let's review the two phases of a severe stress response and examples of clinical manifestations.

  • Ebb phase (early phase)—immediately after injury
    • decreased oxygen consumption
    • hypotension
    • hypothermia
    • hyperglycemia
    • lethargy
    • increased circulating hormones (glucagon and cortisol)
  • Flow phase—36 to 48 hours after injury
    • increased oxygen consumption
    • normal blood pressure (normotensive)
    • hyperthermia
    • hyperglycemia
    • increased nitrogen excretion
    • increased circulating hormones (glucagon and cortisol)

The flow (recovery) phase can last for days, weeks, or months until the injury heals.

If the client has nutritional deficiencies or is in poor nutritional status prior to severe stress, then they are at a greater risk of infection and poor wound healing.

Section 7: Explore: Food and Nutritional Therapy

There is an increased need for protein, vitamins, nutrients, minerals, and fluids during a hypermetabolic state. Review the protein requirements and fluid needs shown here.

Pay close attention to the client's hydration/fluid status. Increased fluid losses are possible from fever, increased urine output, diarrhea, draining wounds, or diuretic therapy.

These specific formulas are estimates to help determine the energy and fluid requirements of clients experiencing hypermetabolic stress. It takes an interdisciplinary team approach, including primary care providers, registered nurses, and registered dietitians to calculate specific protein, fats, carbohydrates, and fluid needs within the plan of care.

Protein requirements:

  • normal: 0.8 g/kg body weight
  • moderate stress: 1.0 to 1.5 g/kg body weight
  • severe stress: 1.5 to 2.0 g/kg body weight

Fluid needs:

  • adults younger than 55 years: 35 to 40 mL/kg body weight
  • adults 55 to 65 years: 30 mL/kg body weight
  • adults older than 65 years: 25 mL/kg body weight

Section 10: Explore: Effects of Stress on Carbohydrates, Fats, Vitamins, and Minerals

During times of complicated stress, carbohydrate (CHO), fat, vitamin, and mineral metabolism can be affected. Select each tab to look at the effects of stress on CHOs, fat, vitamin, and mineral metabolism.

Tab: Carbohydrate Metabolism
  • Hepatic glucose production increases.
  • Insulin levels and glucose increase.
Tab: Fat Metabolism
  • Fat from adipose stores is mobilized for energy due to increased levels of catecholamines and a simultaneous decrease in insulin production.
  • Fat stores are rapidly depleted if hypermetabolic clients are not fed during this time.

Note: Resulting malnutrition increases susceptibility to infection. Malnutrition may contribute to multiple organ dysfunction syndrome, sepsis, and even death.

Tab: Vitamins and Minerals
  • The need for most vitamins and minerals increases.
  • If kilocalorie needs are met, the client is most likely to receive adequate amounts of most vitamins and minerals.
  • Special attention should be given to vitamins A, C, and zinc because they play key roles in supporting skin integrity, wound healing, and immune system functioning.

Section 12: Explore: Nutrition Needs - General Surgery Client

Nutrition needs are increased in clients undergoing surgery because nutrition deficiencies can develop. The nurse should pay careful attention to nutritional status before surgery and individual nutrition needs after surgery. Under-nutrition can lead to decreased protein synthesis, weakness/fatigue, multiple organ dysfunction syndrome, and even death. Clients who are malnourished or in danger of malnutrition must be recognized early so corrective action can be taken prior to surgery. This includes a swallow evaluation to determine any oral feeding difficulties.

Proper nutrition after surgery is essential for wound healing and recovery. An increased need for protein is key for the surgical client. Some risks of protein deficiency after surgery include:

  • poor wound healing
  • rupture of the suture lines
  • delayed healing of fractures
  • depressed heart and lung function
  • failure of gastrointestinal (GI) stomas
  • anemia
  • reduced resistance to infection
  • liver damage
  • extensive weight loss
  • muscle wasting
  • increased risk of death

Section 14: Explore: High-Risk Clients

Clients who are at higher risk of nutritional deficiencies include:

  • malnourished, starved, and obese clients
  • children, older adults, and pregnant women
  • clients with burns
  • clients with broken bones, hip replacement, open-heart, and bariatric surgeries
  • clients with multiple traumatic events
  • clients with traumatic brain injury (TBI) that are severely hypermetabolic and catabolic
  • clients with spinal cord injury (SCI) and those with multiple traumas in addition to SCI

A severe stress response stimulates the release of norepinephrine, epinephrine, and cortisol, resulting in a greater hypermetabolic response. Clients at high risk have significantly longer hospital stays and higher mortality rates than clients with an optimal nutritional status (Grodner et al., 2023).
