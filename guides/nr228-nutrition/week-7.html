Section 1: Introduction to Diarrhea and Constipation

Gastrointestinal (GI) disorders can produce significant nutritional problems. In many situations, diet is the foundation of therapy. Diarrhea and constipation are intestinal motility disorders that can disrupt nutritional health.

This learning activity focuses on the causes, clinical manifestations, and strategies to safely care for clients with diarrhea or constipation. By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Understand the causes of diarrhea and constipation.
  • Identify the clinical manifestations of diarrhea and constipation.
  • Develop feeding strategies to support the safety and nutritional intake of clients living with diarrhea or constipation.

Section 3: Explore: Gastrointestinal Health

Throughout the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, a variety of bacteria support optimal metabolic, digestive, and immune functions that keep the body healthy (Zhang et al., 2015). Referred to as intestinal flora, or microbiota, the type and balance of bacteria vary within the GI tract based on the digestive and absorptive functions in each location. While all GI bacteria serve a purpose, they can be divided into groups: beneficial, harmful, and opportunistic.

In a healthy person, the types of bacteria are balanced and digestion of food, absorption of nutrients, and elimination occur as expected. Dysbiosis, or a microbial imbalance, happens when the "bad" bacteria increase in number and inhibit the "good" bacteria.

Select each tab to learn about the groups of bacteria living in the GI tract.

Tab: Beneficial Bacteria
  • also known as "good" bacteria
  • examples: bifidobacteria, lactic acid bacteria
  • vitamin synthesis, digestion and absorption assistance, infection prevention, immunity stimulation
  • impact: health maintenance
Tab: Harmful Bacteria
  • also known as "bad" bacteria
  • examples: staphylococcus, clostridium perfringens, E. coli (toxic strain)
  • actions: intestinal putrefaction, production of bacterial toxin, production of carcinogenic substances, and gas production
  • impact: decreases health: speeds the aging process
Tab: Opportunistic Bacteria
  • examples: bacteroidetes, E.coli (nontoxic strain), streptococcus
  • actions: none, when in balance with "good" bacteria
  • impact: cause intestinal issues when out of balance

Section 4: Explore: Gastrointestinal Bacteria and Aging

Bacterial groups in the intestines change as people age, with the percentage of harmful bacteria increasing over time.

Because the womb is a sterile environment, newborns have no gastrointestinal bacteria. Bifidobacteria growth is fueled by the nutrients galactooligosaccharides and lactose in breast milk. While breastfeeding, the infant's stool is yellowish and does not smell because its intestinal environment is dominated by bifidobacteria.

Bacterial flora appear as the infant begins to eat and drink baby foods. In healthy adults, bifidobacteria account for 10–20% of intestinal bacteria. As aging continues, the balance between "good" and "bad" bacteria changes. This dysbiosis contributes to gastrointestinal issues that develop in older adults.

  • use of antibiotics and other medications
  • dietary intake
  • stress
  • chronic illness
Tab: Common Side Effects of Dysbiosis

Common side effects of dysbiosis include:

  • abdominal pain
  • nausea
  • diarrhea
  • constipation
  • bad breath

Section 6: Explore: Constipation

A healthy bowel eliminates stool between 3 times each day to 1 time every 3 days. A person is considered constipated when they have fewer than three bowel movements per week that are hard, dry, and difficult and painful to expel.

Constipation most commonly occurs when waste or stool moves too slowly through the digestive tract or cannot be eliminated effectively from the rectum, which may cause the stool to become hard and dry. Chronic constipation has many possible causes.

Factors that may increase the risk of chronic constipation include:

  • being a woman or an older adult
  • being dehydrated
  • eating a low-fiber diet
  • having limited or no physical activity
  • taking certain medications, including sedatives, opioid pain medications, some antidepressants or medications to lower blood pressure
  • having a mental health condition, such as depression or an eating disorder

Section 7: Explore: Constipation Causes

Increasing water intake is helpful for the prevention of constipation—approximately 8–10 glasses per day. Including fiber-rich foods like whole grains, fruits, and vegetables is also important. Chronic constipation can be distressing.

Care recommendations for clients experiencing constipation include:

  • Increase fiber intake in the diet to provide roughage and ease stool passage.
  • Use stool softeners, laxatives, and probiotics to aid in avoiding constipation.
  • Use medications, such as opioids, sparingly, and in conjunction with other methods to prevent constipation.
  • Encourage the client to walk and become more active.
  • Avoid skipping meals and form an eating routine.
  • Listen to the body's cues for bowel movements and avoid stress, which can decrease gastrointestinal (GI) motility.

Reasons for Constipation (transcript):

  • Drinking insufficient water
  • Unhealthy diet
  • Medicine
  • Pregnancy
  • Stress
  • A change of scenery
  • Age
  • Sedentary lifestyle

Section 9: Explore: Clinical Manifestations of Constipation

Clinical manifestations (i.e., signs and symptoms) of chronic constipation include:

  • passing fewer than three stools a week
  • having lumpy or hard stools
  • straining to have bowel movements
  • feeling as though there is a blockage in your rectum that prevents bowel movements
  • feeling as though you cannot completely empty the stool from your rectum
  • needing help to empty your rectum, such as using your hands to press on your abdomen and using a finger to remove stool from your rectum

Section 11: Explore: Nutritional Considerations—Dietary Fiber

According to the American Heart Association (as cited in University of California San Francisco, n.d.), the average daily fiber intake should be 25 to 30 grams a day. However, the average dietary intake of fiber among adults in the United States is estimated to be 15 grams a day. The lack of dietary fiber lends itself to an increased risk for constipation. The inclusion of fruits, vegetables, legumes, beans, grains, and cereals are easy ways to increase fiber and reduce the risk of constipation.

The types of fiber found in food are water-soluble and water-insoluble. Each has different properties and characteristics.

Water-Soluble Fiber
  • It dissolves in water.
  • Water-soluble fiber absorbs water during digestion.
  • It increases stool bulk and may decrease blood cholesterol levels.
  • Water-soluble fiber can be found in fruits (such as apples, oranges, and grapefruit), vegetables, legumes (such as dry beans, lentils, and peas), barley, oats, and oat bran.
  • It includes plant pectin and gums.

Bulk-forming laxatives include water-soluble fiber to increase water retention in the intestines to soften the stool, making it easier to pass.

Water-Insoluble Fiber
  • It does not dissolve in water.
  • Water-insoluble fiber remains unchanged during digestion.
  • It promotes normal movement of intestinal contents.
  • Water-insoluble fiber can be found in fruits with edible peels or seeds, vegetables, whole grain products (such as whole-wheat bread, pasta, and crackers), bulgur wheat, stone-ground corn meal, cereals, bran, rolled oats, buckwheat, and brown rice.
  • It includes plant cellulose and hemicellulose.

Reference: University of California San Francisco. (n.d.). Increasing fiber intake. https://www.ucsfhealth.org/education/increasing-fiber-intake

Section 14: Explore: Diarrhea—Types and Causes

Diarrhea is loose, watery stools three or more times a day. Diarrhea may be acute, persistent, or chronic.

  • Acute diarrhea is a common problem that typically lasts 1 or 2 days and goes away on its own.
  • Persistent diarrhea lasts longer than 2 weeks and less than 4 weeks.
  • Chronic diarrhea lasts at least 4 weeks and symptoms may be continual or may come and go.

Diarrhea has been linked to the following causes:

  • intolerance to foods (e.g., lactose intolerance to dairy or an allergy to gluten)
  • difficulty absorbing fats
  • tolerance to medications
  • medical treatments

It is important to identify the underlying cause of diarrhea and take action to control the impact it has on health and nutritional well-being.

Section 15: Explore: Diarrhea—Clinical Manifestations and Complications

The clinical manifestations and complications of diarrhea are similar for acute, persistent, and chronic occurrences. Review each tab to learn more.

Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Common manifestations common to all causes include:

  • abdominal cramps or pain
  • bloating
  • nausea
  • urgent need to have a bowel movement
  • vomiting

Manifestations associated with specific causes include:

  • fever (gastrointestinal infection, significant dehydration)
  • blood in the stool (bacterial infections, inflammatory bowel disorders)
  • mucus in the stool (chronic illnesses, including Crohn's disease and cystic fibrosis, gastrointestinal infection)
Tab: Complications

Although anyone can experience diarrhea, for some individuals, even acute episodes of diarrhea can lead to complications in young children, older adults, and those living with chronic medical conditions. Persistent diarrhea can lead to dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, kidney failure, and organ damage.

Clinical manifestations of dehydration that should be reported to the healthcare provider include:

  • concentrated urine (dark color, urine volume smaller than normal, or lack of urine output)
  • rapid heart rate
  • headaches
  • flushed, dry skin
  • irritability and confusion
  • lightheadedness and dizziness
  • severe nausea and vomiting

Clients experiencing chronic diarrhea are at risk for nutritional deficits in protein, iron, and vitamin D due to malabsorption.

Deficiency of vitamin B12 may occur as a result of dysbiosis (bacterial imbalance).

Section 16: Explore: Nutritional Support for Diarrhea

Click on each + to learn more about liquids, smaller meals, and additional food recommendations.

Liquids
  • Drink liquids 30 minutes before or after meals.
  • Limit the use of apple juice, which may aggravate diarrhea (especially in children).
  • Electrolyte solutions (Gatorade, Pedialyte, etc.) may be prescribed with medical advice to replenish lost sodium and potassium.
  • Avoid milk products. Milk products contain lactose, a natural sugar that may not be digested properly.
Small Meals
  • Include foods that are low in fiber (bananas, rice, applesauce, dry toast, crackers) during the acute phase.
  • Progress to foods that are high in potassium when tolerated. Include oranges, grapefruit, bananas, apples, potatoes, dairy products, apricot nectar, baked squash, and sweet potatoes.
Additional Food Recommendations

Avoid foods during periods of severe diarrhea, which are:

  • Very hot, which might increase the natural movement of the intestines
  • High in caffeine, such as coffee, colas, or chocolate
  • High in fiber, like whole-grain breads or cereals, raw fruits and vegetables, nuts, dried fruit, popcorn, salads, and bran
  • Gas-forming, like cabbage, onions, dried beans or lentils, carbonated beverages, broccoli, cauliflower, or chewing gum
  • Avoid polyols, which are carbohydrates, found in apples, apricots, nectarines, pears, plums, prunes, and mushrooms. Other sources include additives, such as sorbitol, mannitol, xylitol, maltitol, and isomalt.
  • Products made with high-fructose corn syrup should be eliminated when not well tolerated. These are found in honey, apples, mangos, pears, and watermelons.

Section 1: Introduction to Dysphagia

When people think of gastrointestinal (GI) disorders, swallowing difficulties are not the first thing that comes to mind, but complications with swallowing can have great impacts on a client's nutritional status. Dysphagia is a swallowing disorder that causes difficulty or pain while swallowing or attempting to swallow.

This learning activity focuses on the causes, warning signs, and strategies to safely care for a client with dysphagia. By completing this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Understand the causes of dysphagia.
  • Identify the clinical manifestations of dysphagia.
  • Develop feeding strategies to support the safety and nutritional intake of clients living with dysphagia.
  • Identify possible causes of dysphagia.
  • Identify warning signs of dysphagia.
  • Identify feeding strategies for clients with dysphagia.

Section 3: Swallowing Phases and Dysphagia

Swallowing occurs using a series of complex events or phases.

Tab: Preparatory Phase (Not Pictured)

This phase involves the person getting the food to their mouth.

Clients with mobility issues in their upper extremities (e.g., tremors, weakness, decreased range of motion) may have difficulty getting food to their mouths and should be assessed by the nurse, even the client does not have dysphagia.

Tab: Oral Phase

This phase occurs when the food enters the mouth and ends when the food bolus enters the pharynx. The primary occurrences during this phase are chewing the food, mixing the food with saliva, keeping the food in a bolus, and beginning to swallow.

Symptoms of chocking that require immediate follow-up by the nurse include coughing, choking, sensation of food getting stuck, persistent drooling of saliva, gurgled (or wet sounding) speech, and bringing food back up (regurgitation).

Tab: Pharyngeal Phase

This phase begins as the bolus enters the oral pharynx and ends when it passes into the esophagus. Aspiration occurs during this phase due to the proximity of the openings to the trachea (anterior) and esophagus (posterior) in the neck, making this phase the least safe for the client. The epiglottis and other muscles involved with swallowing play a key role in preventing the food bolus from entering the trachea.

Tab: Esophageal Phase

This phase begins as the bolus enters the esophagus and ends when it passes into the stomach. The esophageal muscles contract in a rhythmic manner, referred to as peristalsis, to propel the food through the esophagus to the stomach.

Section 4: Types of Dysphagia

Dysphagia is a swallowing disorder that causes difficulty or pain while swallowing or attempting to swallow. Difficulty in swallowing can be caused by:

  • structural dysphagia
  • esophageal motility
Tab: Structural Dysphagia

Structural dysphagia results from changes to the actual structure of the esophagus can be caused by:

  • obstruction
  • inflammation
  • edema
  • surgery
Tab: Esophageal Motility

Esophageal motility (movement) issues can be caused by:

  • amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)
  • myasthenia gravis
  • cerebrovascular accident
  • traumatic brain injury
  • cerebral palsy
  • Parkinson's disease
  • multiple sclerosis

Section 7: Individualized Care

Thin liquids may be thickened to facilitate swallowing and decrease the risk of aspiration. No two clients with dysphagia are alike. Therefore, the diet must be individualized according to the swallowing ability and personal food preferences of the client. Food must be served in a form that fits the specific anatomic and functional needs of the client.

Section 9: Aspiration Precautions

When clients are diagnosed with dysphagia, there are specific recommendations for feeding to avoid aspiration.

  • Position the client upright, bent slightly forward, with the chin tucked and head tilted forward.
  • Eliminate distractions so the client can focus all their attention on the meal.
  • The feeding assistant should sit at or below the client's eye level while feeding.
  • Avoid asking them to talk while eating.
  • Instruct the client not to use liquids to clear the mouth of foods. Liquids should be used only after they have cleared food from the mouth.
  • Encourage frequent, dry swallows or coughing to help clear food from the mouth between bites.
  • Encourage small bites (1 teaspoon of solid food or about 10–15 mL of liquid), especially if the client's ability to manage food is impaired.
  • Allow adequate time to eat (for example, 20–30 minutes).
  • Use spoons rather than cups; clients have less difficulty getting food and liquid this way.
  • A straw may be used by some to drink liquids and thinned foods.
  • While eating, check for voice quality. A wet or gurgled voice indicates food may be resting on the vocal cords.

During meals, supervision is necessary to prevent or minimize swallowing problems. Clients should be reevaluated regularly to determine whether any changes need to be made to the consistency of fluids or food. Sometimes clients eat too quickly and then choke when trying to swallow. Staff can observe and supervise while clients eat to remind them to complete the swallowing sequence before taking the next bite of food.

Section 11: Feeding Techniques

Various feeding techniques may facilitate safe swallowing:

  • Serve small, frequent meals to help maximize intake.
  • Encourage clients with dysphagia to rest before mealtime. Postpone meals if the client is fatigued.
  • Give mouth care immediately before meals to enhance the sense of taste.

Section 3: Explore: Right Upper Quadrant Pain

Yolanda Rodriguez (pronouns: she/her/hers) is a 62-year-old Hispanic female presenting at the emergency department with reports of right upper quadrant pain radiating to the right shoulder lasting for more than 12 hours. She has been experiencing nausea and vomiting, fever, and tenderness to the right upper quadrant. Labs have been completed. Abdominal ultrasound confirms gallstone presence. She is scheduled for gallbladder removal (cholecystectomy).

Laboratory tests to evaluate for the presence of gallbladder disease include (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2017):

  • Complete blood count (CBC): evaluates blood cells; a high white blood cell count can indicate inflammation, infection, an abscess, or a ruptured gallbladder.
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) or erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR): elevated results indicate inflammation.
  • Liver panel: if someone has gallstones that block bile ducts, results for bilirubin may be high due to bile backing up into the liver. Liver enzymes, especially alkaline phosphatase (ALP), may be elevated in severe cases of gallbladder inflammation.
  • Lipase (the preferred test) or amylase: these pancreatic enzymes may be elevated if gallbladder disease has also caused pancreatitis.

Section 4: Explore: Gallbladder Assessment

The gallbladder is a small organ, located in the right upper quadrant under the liver. It stores bile and releases it into the duodenum to emulsify fats in the diet.

Alterations in the function of the gallbladder can occur for various reasons. Most commonly, there is the presence of gallstones, caused by changes in the composition of the bile and called cholelithiasis. These stones can become lodged in bile ducts and result in additional complications. Risk factors include pregnancy, hormonal birth control, diabetes mellitus, cirrhosis, pancreatitis, and celiac disease. Inflammation of the gallbladder is known as cholecystitis and can occur with long-term total parental nutrition or severe illness, which causes alteration in functionality. Incidence occurs with increasing age, especially in females, obesity, and is more common in Hispanic and American Indian cultures.

It is important that the nurse assesses for the following cues:

Tab: Abdominal Assessment
  • right upper quadrant pain with radiation to epigastric area (worsens after fatty or fried food intake)
  • abdominal distention
Tab: Gastrointestinal History
  • nausea and/or vomiting
  • Alterations in stool and urine color (changes in defecation [i.e., bowel movements]) often occur with gallbladder issues.
    • Stools may become light-colored or chalky if bile ducts are obstructed. Unexplained and frequent diarrhea after meals can be a sign of chronic gallbladder disease.
    • Changes in urination can occur as well. Clients may notice that the urine color has become darker than before. Dark or orange colored urine can indicate a blockage in the bile duct.
Tab: Integumentary
  • jaundice
Tab: Diagnostic Testing
  • complete blood count (CBC), c-reactive protein (CRP), lipase, amylase, and liver enzymes
  • gallbladder ultrasound, nuclear medicine scan, and abdominal ultrasound

Clients with biliary alterations can have the following nursing diagnoses (hypotheses):

  • Acute pain related to cholecystitis or cholelithiasis and subsequent surgical intervention
  • Alteration in nutrition: less than body requirements due to gastrointestinal (GI) disturbances related to gallbladder disease
  • Fluid volume deficit related to nausea and vomiting
  • Knowledge deficit related to risk factors, aggravating behaviors, and poor dietary choices

While planning and implementing care for the client experiencing gallbladder disease, the nurse should reflect on establishing priorities for care. For example, if a client rates their pain as 9 out of 10 and is not nauseated or vomiting, acute pain is the higher priority.

Another client may have been vomiting enough to have a fluid and electrolyte imbalance and be experiencing pain they rate as 10 out of 10. For this client, both problems are a high priority. The healthcare provider informs Yolanda that she needs to have her gallbladder removed. The most conservative treatment for cholecystitis involves:

  • a low-fat diet
  • medications to reduce pain and inflammation
  • antibiotics to treat infection (if present)

Tab: Surgical Interventions: Preoperative Care

Removal of the gallbladder can be performed with either an open cholecystectomy (via laparotomy) or laparoscopic cholecystectomy.

Before Yolanda has either surgery, she should have nothing by mouth (NPO) for at least 6 hours to decrease the risk of aspiration while receiving anesthesia. Additional interventions may include:

Tab: Surgical Intervention: Postoperative Care

Oral intake

Immediately after an open laparotomy cholecystectomy, clients are NPO until bowel sounds return and flatus is passed. Once that occurs, the client's diet is advanced, starting with clear liquids and progressing as tolerated. The goal is to eat a regular diet that is low in fat.

After a laparoscopic cholecystectomy, the client's bowel sounds are assessed and clear liquids provided, if present. The client is often discharged to go home with instructions to progress to a regular diet that is low in fat.

Tab: Interventions for Recovery at Home

After having either procedure, Yolanda's body will require time to heal and adapt to the lack of the gallbladder. This usually involves a gradual return to normal activities and a low-fat diet for several weeks.

The nursing care of clients experiencing alterations in biliary health with gallbladder disease includes:

  • administration of antiemetics for nausea related to the disease process

Section 10: Explore: Diet Teaching

The nurse is teaching Yolanda about her diet after the cholecystectomy. Although there isn't a required post-surgical diet, the following tips may help minimize problems with diarrhea.

Tab: Limit Fats

Avoid high-fat foods, fried and greasy foods, and fatty sauces/gravies for at least 1 week after surgery. Instead, choose fat-free or low-fat foods. Low-fat foods are those with no more than 3 grams of fat a serving. Check the labels and follow the serving size listed.

Tab: Increase Fiber in the Diet

This can help normalize bowel movements. Add soluble fiber, such as oats and barley. Increase the amount of fiber slowly, such as over several weeks, because too much fiber at first can make gas and cramping worse.

Tab: Eat Smaller, More-Frequent Meals

This may ensure a better mix with available bile. Healthy meal planning should include small amounts of lean protein, such as poultry, fish or fat-free dairy, along with vegetables, fruits and whole grains.

Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Application: Nutrition for Gastrointestinal Health

Adequate nutrition consists of the ingestion and utilization of water, essential nutrients, vitamins, and minerals to maintain and sustain health and wellness. Gastrointestinal (GI) health affects all other body systems. As nurses, it is essential to assess clients' nutritional well-being, identify the factors that influence nutritional outcomes, and direct the care provided based on the findings. This module will guide you in the care of individuals facing nutritional deficits related to alterations in gastrointestinal health.

As you complete the concept, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Describe the nurse's role in assessing the impact of gastrointestinal disorders on the client's well-being.
  • Differentiate nutritional deficits that occur as a result of GI disorders.
  • Assess a client's level of distress based on GI symptoms.
  • Explain nursing diagnoses related to GI disorders.
  • Plan, implement, and evaluate care for clients with dietary needs related to GI disorders.

Section 3: Explore: Six Dimensions of Gastrointestinal Health

When a client experiences a gastrointestinal (GI) disorder, it is essential that the nurse begin to recognize cues, identifying relevant data to the clinical situation. This is part of nursing assessment. It is also important to understand the profound, holistic impact from not only a GI perspective, but an alteration in elimination as well. When completing the assessment, identify data encompassing the following six dimensions of health (Grodner et al., 2020).

Tab: Physical Health

The client's physical health is most affected when the disorder intensifies over time, leading to weight loss and nutrient deficiencies, in addition to the pathology of the disease itself.

Tab: Intellectual Health

Since diet is so profoundly impactful on GI disorders, finding the right combination of foods or consistently restricting certain foods can be a challenge.

Tab: Emotional Health

Having physical symptoms like diarrhea, gas, and vomiting can be difficult to manage. Clients may struggle with acceptance. Poorly managed symptoms lead to decreased self-esteem and well-being and even decreased quality of life.

Tab: Social Health

It is necessary to assist clients in coping with changes that occur with some disorders like dumping syndrome and colostomies.

Tab: Spiritual Health

It is important to see the client holistically and provide strategies such as meditation and yoga or other spiritual support.

Tab: Environmental Health

Individuals living with an acute or chronic GI alteration often hesitate to be in public, fearing the loss of bowel control when they do not know the location of the closest restroom. This can impact both social and mental health.

Reference: Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). Nutritional foundations and clinical application: A nursing approach (8th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 4: Explore: Social Determinants of Health

The five social determinants of health are important to consider when assessing gastrointestinal (GI) alterations and developing a holistic plan of care.

Economic Stability

1 in 10 live in poverty and cannot afford healthy food, healthcare, and housing.

Education Access and Quality

  • Clients with higher levels of education have a longer life expectancy.
  • People with decreased income, students with disabilities, and those who are bullied are more likely to struggle with math and reading and are less likely to go to college.
  • Additionally, lower educational levels are more likely to have lower paying jobs, heart disease, diabetes, and depression.

Healthcare Access and Quality

  • 1 in 10 do not have access to healthcare providers due to lack of insurance.
  • These people do not get preventative care, screenings, and medications needed.
  • Sometimes clients live too far away from healthcare and/or do not have transportation.

Neighborhood and Built Environments

  • Many people live in unsafe situations, with a high rate of violence, unsafe air/water, and other health and safety risks.
  • Low-income clients are more likely to be exposed to hazards at work such as secondhand smoke, noise, and toxins.

Social and Community Context

  • Relationships with family, community, and co-workers impact a client's well-being.
  • Positive relationships can help decrease negative impacts such as unsafe living situations, discrimination, access to food, and lack of available resources.

Has the client recently had a change in weight? An increase or decrease in weight can be related to GI illness.

Section 6: Explore: Diabetes Management

The goal of dietary management in clients with diabetes is to stabilize their blood sugar levels in order to maintain function, prevent complications, and teach them to manage personal care of the disease. The most common nursing diagnoses that are associated with nutritional needs related to diabetes include:

  • Knowledge Deficit Related to Dietary Management of Diabetes
    • risk for ineffective therapeutic regimen management (due to knowledge deficit or acceptance of disease)
    • ineffective coping (due to recent diagnosis, denial, and lack of nutritional knowledge)
  • Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements
    • risk for unstable blood glucose (due to poor dietary habits)
    • risk for deficient fluid volume (due to poor dietary habits)
    • risk for impaired skin integrity (due to poor dietary habits and decreased cardiovascular perfusion)
    • risk for infection (due to poor dietary habits and decreased cardiovascular perfusion)
    • risk for injury (due to poor dietary habits and lack of sensation)

Section 7: Explore: Nursing Planning and Interventions

Select the tabs to learn more about a dietary and activity plan and how to monitor clients and medication administration.

Tab: Dietary and Activity Plan

The dietary plan focuses on:

  • evaluating nutrition
  • counseling and educating on nutritional guidelines
  • developing the client's appropriate diet
  • decreasing weight, blood pressure, and lipid levels as appropriate
  • demonstrating understanding of nutritional categories and appropriate food selection for each
  • providing adequate calories for achieving and maintaining a reasonable body weight, normal growth, and development

The activity plan focuses on:

  • teaching the client how exercise controls diabetes
  • assessing activity level and determining appropriate aerobic goals, step count, or exercises for clients who are bedridden
  • avoiding exercise when the insulin level is at its peak and waiting until the blood sugar is 100 to 200 mg/dL, 30 to 60 minutes after meals
  • avoiding exercise when the blood glucose level is greater than 250 ml/dL and ketones are present in urine
  • ingesting added carbohydrate if the glucose level is less than 100 mg/dL
  • monitoring blood glucose levels before and after exercise and learn to adjust food and insulin amounts
  • consuming added carbohydrate as needed to avoid hypoglycemia; keep carbohydrate-based foods available during and after exercise
Tab: Client Monitoring and Medication Administration

Through the client teaching, the client should be able to do the following:

  • Demonstrate appropriate blood glucose monitoring based on the type of diabetes and age of client.
  • Explains and return demonstrates proper foot care and inspection.
  • Describe strategies to reduce the risk of injury and infection.
  • Explains and demonstrates appropriate administration of prescribed medications.

Section 11: Explore: Nursing Evaluation

When clients return for evaluation and follow-up visits, it is imperative that nurses continue the education from the previous visit, evaluate current nutritional needs related to diabetes, provide appropriate resources, and consult the healthcare provider as appropriate.

Clients should also be monitored for additional complications such as:

Tab: Neuropathy

Neuropathy is a very common complication for those with a diagnosis of diabetes. Recent studies have shown that a combination of a vegan diet and exercise may help in treating neuropathy. The nurse should encourage exercise and proper nutrition.

Tab: Renal Insufficiency or Failure

Retinopathy is also a very common complication for those with a diagnosis of diabetes. Renal insufficiency and failure are also common complications from diabetes. Urine output, color, and renal function should be evaluated using urinalysis and renal function labs.

Tab: Sexual Dysfunction

Sexual dysfunction can also be present in both males and females. Males struggle with erectile dysfunction, and females experience decreased desire and vaginal lubrication. This difficult subject may not be shared openly by the client, so it is important to evaluate and provide education and resources as appropriate.

Tab: Vision Loss (Retinopathy)

Vision loss can be a problem due to uncontrolled glucose levels (retinopathy). Routine eye examinations should be encouraged for clients with diabetes.

Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Biliary Health

Wellness is influenced by the function of the body organs. Physical health is crucially dependent on the liver, pancreas, and gallbladder. One malfunction can devastate an individual's nutritional status. The biliary system is comprised of the:

  • gallbladder
  • the hepatic, cystic, and common bile ducts

In this learning module, you will explore the nutritional therapies for clients with alterations in their biliary system.

Section 3: Explore: Six Dimensions of Health

Review the six dimensions of health below.

Slide 1: Physical Health

Physical health is most affected when a gastrointestinal (GI) disorder is chronic and intensive. Weight loss and nutrient deficiencies pose health risks in addition to the primary disorder.

Slide 2: Intellectual Health

Intellectual health is tested as the patient tries to find food combinations and textures that are aesthetically pleasing. Some disorders require constant vigilance to restrict inadvertent consumption of problematic foods (e.g., gluten in celiac disease).

Slide 3: Emotional Health

Emotional health is taxed when patients struggle with acceptance of their dietary or physical limitations. Poorly managed symptoms diminish psychological well-being and overall quality of life.

Slide 4: Social Health

Social health strategies are needed to help patients handle the personal ramifications of colostomies, dumping syndrome, and other disorders.

Slide 5: Spiritual Health

Spiritual health can be enhanced through yoga and meditation, which are tied to mind and body wellness.

Slide 6: Environmental Health

Environmental health dimension may alter the sense of wellness due to the unexpected need for access to a bathroom at home, at work, and during commuting or traveling.

Section 4: Explore: Biliary Colic and Low-Carbohydrate Diet

Bile and digestive enzymes are carried by the bile ducts from the liver, gallbladder, and pancreas to the small intestine. The bile carries away wastes from the liver and plays a role in the digestion of fats. The gallbladder stores a portion of the bile. After an individual eats a meal, the gallbladder contracts, emptying the gallbladder's contents into the small intestine.

When a person has a gallstone that is blocking the bile duct, they can develop biliary colic with clinical manifestations such as

  • a sharp cramp or dull ache in the middle to upper right quadrant of the abdomen
  • fever
  • jaundice

Biliary colic is thought to be increased by diets that are high in calories, fat, and carbohydrates. Modification of diet—including reducing high-calorie foods and maintaining a low-fat and low-carbohydrate diet—contributes to the prevention of biliary colic.

Section 6: Explore: Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) has become the most common form of liver disease in the United States and is one of the most common comorbidities associated with obesity and metabolic syndrome. It is unclear why certain individuals accumulate and deposit more fat in their livers (Mayo Clinic, n.d.).

The excess fatty deposits are toxic to liver cells. Over time, the liver becomes inflamed and scar tissue develops, often progressing to cirrhosis.

A calorie-controlled diet limiting fats, fructose, and alcohol may be ordered. Adequate tracking of glucose and lipid levels will be needed. Whatever the cause, proper nutrition in the form of a well-balanced diet is important in reversing fatty infiltration.

Section 8: Explore: Gluten-Free Diet to Support Biliary Health

Celiac disease is a chronic autoimmune disorder in which the mucosa of the small intestine is damaged when food containing gluten is consumed, impacting the body's ability to absorb nutrients. It is a disease of malabsorption (nutrients are not absorbed) and an abnormal immune reaction to gluten. The recommended treatment for celiac disease is to adhere to a diet that is gluten-free.

Malnutrition is one of the most common complications linked to untreated celiac disease since the condition results in serious intestinal malabsorption. One of the most effective ways of treating malnutrition in individuals with celiac disease is to identify the cause—products containing gluten. Individuals with celiac disease should consume a gluten-free diet to decrease inflammation and support biliary health. With an increase in the number of individuals experiencing celiac disease, gluten-free products are now easier to find in stores, restaurants, and at special food companies.

Gluten is a protein found in:

  • Wheat
  • Rye
  • Barley
  • Cereal
  • Grains
  • Pasta
  • Processed foods

Gluten-free foods include:

  • Meat
  • Fish
  • Fruits
  • Vegetables
  • Rice
  • Potatoes

Section 11: Explore: Celiac Disease

Celiac disease is the most common disease for which dietary modification remains the main treatment to support biliary health. Individuals who have a diagnosis of celiac disease and/or who are not following a gluten-free diet experience altercations in the speed at which food is moved through the small intestine (Anderson, 2022). The lining of the small intestine is damaged, which leads to limited cholecystokinin production. This increases the risk of gallbladder issues due to the delay in the emptying of the gallbladder, especially after a fatty meal.

Section 12: Explore: Hepatitis

Hepatitis is inflammation of the liver. Acute hepatitis can occur as a result of infectious mononucleosis, cirrhosis, toxic chemicals, or viral infection. There are five types of viral hepatitis: A, B, C, D, and E.

Individuals with a diagnosis of hepatitis usually do not require a special diet. It has been found that eating healthily, maintaining a healthy weight, and avoiding alcohol are simple steps that individuals with a diagnosis of hepatitis can take to prevent complications.

Hepatitis can be caused by environmental factors and chemical or medication toxicities. Risk factors for contracting viral hepatitis include men who have sex with men, illegal drug users sharing needles, and persons who visit developing countries.

Section 13: Explore: Dietary Consumption and Hepatitis

Everything you eat and drink passes through your liver. Your liver changes food into stored energy and chemicals necessary for life. Your liver makes nutrients available so your body can use them to build cells, give you energy, and maintain normal body functions.

A good diet can improve liver health and lower the risks associated with hepatitis. If you have hepatitis, you usually do not need a special diet. Recommendations for those with hepatitis are:

  • eating regular, balanced meals
  • maintaining healthy calorie intake
  • eating whole-grain cereals, breads, and grains
  • eating lots of fruits and vegetables
  • getting adequate protein
  • limiting fatty, salty, and sugary foods
  • drinking enough fluids
  • reaching and maintaining a healthy weight
  • avoiding alcohol

The goal is to eat healthily, maintain a healthy weight, and avoid alcohol consumption.

Section 15: Explore: Gallstones and Risk Assessment

The gallbladder is a small, pear-shaped organ on the right side of the abdomen, beneath the liver. The gallbladder holds a digestive fluid that gets released into the small intestine called bile. Cholecystitis is inflammation of the gallbladder, usually caused by gallstones blocking bile from leaving the gallbladder. The other causes of cholecystitis include bile duct problems, tumors, serious illness, and certain infections.

Treatment for cholecystitis often involves gallbladder removal. However, diets high in fat and low in fiber may increase the risk of gallstones. To lower the risk, choose a diet

  • high in fruits,
  • high in vegetables, and
  • that includes whole grain foods.

Increased bilirubin can occur if the gallbladder is blocked and cannot drain properly. Increased cholesterol can cause the development of gallstones. The white blood cells would be elevated, indicating infection, and the bilirubin blood levels would be increased.

Populations at risk for gallstones include:

  • adults aged 60 and older,
  • pregnant women, and
  • women using hormone replacement therapy or oral contraceptives.

Section 16: Explore: Gallstones to Pancreatitis

Gallstone pancreatitis is a type of pancreatitis caused by a blockage of the pancreas duct by a gallstone. If a stone blocks the opening of the bile duct into the small intestine, it could block the flow of material from the liver and pancreas. This could result in inflammation and could turn into a life-threatening disease. When this occurs, material from the liver and pancreas is obstructed.

When this condition is diagnosed, the client is given the following instructions:

  • Avoid food and fluids until the inflammation subsides.
  • Intravenous fluids are provided during the initial phases.
  • In the case of severe pancreatitis and ongoing inflammation, intravenous delivery of nutrients is started.

Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition and Diabetes

Nutrition is critically important in preventing diabetes, managing existing diabetes, and at least slowing, the rate of development of diabetes complications. Diabetes is a group of diseases in which the body lacks insulin production or defects within the insulin receptors.

As you complete this learning activity, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:

  • Differentiate the types of diabetes.
  • Explain the characteristics and presentation of both types of diabetes.
  • Compare and contrast current and traditional nutritional approaches.
  • Identify appropriate nutritional interventions based on client needs.

Section 3: Explore: Types of Diabetes

Diabetes is a group of disorders and is classified into two types: diabetes mellitus type 1 and type 2. Explore each one in further detail below.

No one quite knows why this occurs, or what triggers it. Once these antibodies attack the pancreas and complete their destruction, the pancreas is no longer able to make insulin. Section 4: Explore: Diabetes Management

The primary goal of diabetes management is the control of blood glucose in an attempt to achieve normal or near-normal levels. Diet and exercise play an immense and critical role in the regulation of blood glucose levels.

Development of an active lifestyle works well in conjunction with selection of a healthy diet. Several nutrition interventions are often implemented to achieve this control.

Section 5: Explore: Nutritional Recommendations

Many healthcare providers prescribe a specific caloric level for clients with diabetes. However, the American Diabetes Association (ADA) does not endorse any type of caloric level or percentage of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. Instead, the ADA recommends that the dietary needs for each client should be individualized (as cited in Grodner et al., 2023). Specifically, the focus is no longer on a "diet" but nutrition therapy. Each client requires different nutrition needs to achieve treatment goals.

Tab: Nutrition Therapy

Diabetes self-management education is the focus—teaching the client to manage blood sugar levels based on current influences. In order to complete nutrition therapy, the client undergoes several steps:

  • Comprehensive nutritional assessment
  • Self-care treatment plan development
  • Evaluation of client's health status, learning ability, readiness to change, and current lifestyle
  • Tailored meal planning approach to fit the client's lifestyle and needs, designed to meet treatment goals
Tab: Nutrients Intake

Total fat, saturated fat, cholesterol, fiber, vitamins, and minerals should be the same as those recommended for the general population (Grodner et al., 2023). The only variance is in carbohydrate consumption, which will be discussed in detail later in this module.

Clients with diabetes should also use caution with alcohol intake and not omit foods. Alcoholic beverages should be consumed with food to prevent hypoglycemia. Moderate alcohol does not negatively impact clients with well-controlled glucose levels.

Another important factor to consider is the client's lipid and cholesterol levels. Protein can be 15-20% of daily kilocalories from animal and vegetable protein sources, which is important for clients to remember when selecting foods.

Reference: Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach (8th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 6: Explore: Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are a major component in diabetes management. Current nutritional recommendations for the management of diabetes include a diet that includes carbohydrates from fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, and low-fat milk.

Carbohydrate counting to control postprandial glucose levels is successful in most clients. However, research has shown that low-carbohydrate diets are an important source of energy, fiber, vitamins, and minerals, and they are important in dietary palatability (Grodner et al., 2023). Therefore, these foods are important components of the diet for individuals with diabetes.

The purpose of carbohydrate counting is to establish an appropriate balance between the amount of carbohydrates eaten and the insulin to regulate the blood glucose level. A registered dietitian (RD) is an essential part of the multidisciplinary team to help determine when and how much a client can eat. It is a flexible meal planning strategy that helps the client understand how food choices affect blood glucose levels. The carb's rating on the glycemic index determines the amount of insulin needed to balance blood glucose.

Reference: Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach (8th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 9: Explore: Fiber

Fiber is an important component in the control of blood sugar. People with diabetes are encouraged to choose a variety of fiber-containing foods such as legumes, fiber-rich cereals (≥5 g fiber/serving), fruits, vegetables, and whole grain products because they provide vitamins, minerals, and other substances important for good health (American Diabetes Association, 2008).

Whole grain–containing foods and dietary fiber have been associated with improved insulin sensitivity, independent of body weight. Dietary fiber has also been associated with improved ability to secrete insulin adequately to overcome insulin resistance for clients with diabetes type 2.

Reference: American Diabetes Association. (2008). Nutrition recommendations and interventions for diabetes: A position statement of the American Diabetes Association. Diabetes Care, 31(Supplement 1), S61-S78. https://doi.org/10.2337/dc08-S061

Section 12: Explore: Hypoglycemia

Hypoglycemia is a condition that occurs when one's blood sugar is lower than normal, usually less than 70 mg/dL (Mayo Clinic, n.d.).

Signs include:

  • hunger
  • nervousness
  • shakiness
  • perspiration
  • dizziness or light-headedness
  • sleepiness
  • confusion

If left untreated, hypoglycemia may lead to unconsciousness. Hypoglycemia is treated by consuming a carbohydrate-rich food such as a glucose tablet, juice, or honey. It may also be treated with an injection of glucagon if the person is unconscious or unable to swallow.

Reference: Mayo Clinic. (n.d.). Type 1 diabetes. https://www.mayoclinic.org/diseases-conditions/type-1-diabetes/diagnosis-treatment/drc-20353017

Section 13: Explore: Artificial Sweeteners

Artificial sweeteners can be used to reduce both calorie and carbohydrate intake. Sugar substitutes can also help curb those cravings individuals have for something sweet. The FDA has approved these five non-nutritive artificial sweeteners for use in the U.S. (as cited in Grodner et al., 2023). They are:

  • acesulfame potassium
  • aspartame
  • neotame
  • saccharin
  • sucralose

Before being allowed on the market, all underwent rigorous scrutiny and were shown to be safe when consumed by the public and more specifically, those individuals with diabetes.

Steviocide (Stevia) is a low-calorie sweetener that has both antioxidant and antidiabetic properties. Unlike artificial sweeteners and sugar, stevia can suppress your plasma glucose levels and significantly increase glucose tolerance. Steviocide is derived from a South American shrub, presents a sweet taste but cannot be marketed or sold as a sweetener in the United States. It is a natural herb.

Reference: Grodner, M., Escott-Stump, S., & Dorner, S. (2023). Nutritional foundations and clinical applications: A nursing approach (8th ed.). Elsevier.

Section 15: Explore: Special Considerations

For clients living with diabetes, special considerations consist of illness, eating disorders, diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), and hyperglycemic hyperosmolar nonketotic syndrome (HHNS). Review each of these below for more information.

Tab: Illness

Glucose levels can be elevated with illness and stress and can be caused by an increase in liver production of glucose stimulated by the illness or injury. Clients who are sick should be instructed to modify their activity. These strategies include more frequent monitoring of blood glucose levels, checking urine for the presence of ketones, drinking sugar-free liquids like water and tea, sticking to a normal meal plan, taking usual medications, and contacting the healthcare provider for intolerance of foods, fever, moderate ketones, and/or glucose levels over 250 mg/dL.

Tab: Eating Disorders

With type 1 diabetes, eating disorders can occur. When insulin therapy is started, the client gains weight and disordered eating presents itself. It is dangerous because clients will omit insulin to keep their weight down. Warning signs include changes in eating habits, excessive exercise, unexplained elevations in A1C levels, amenorrhea, or episodes of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). A mental health referral should be initiated.

Tab: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Life-threatening illness that occurs with missed insulin administration or pump failure. The body begins breaking down fat for fuel and produces high levels of ketones. The result is metabolic acidosis that results in osmotic diuresis, dehydration, and produces Kussmal's respirations. Without treatment, coma and death will result, and DKA is best handled in the intensive care unit.

Tab: Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic Nonketotic Syndrome (HHNS)

Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic nonketotic syndrome (HHNS) is also a life-threatening medical emergency that requires immediate intervention. It is caused by an insulin deficiency resulting in severe hyperglycemia. If untreated, the serum becomes hyperosmolar, and osmotic diuresis causes loss of electrolytes. Mortality rate is 10–25% and HHNS is also best handled by the intensive care unit.

Section 16: Explore: Review of Nutrition Therapy for Clients With Diabetes

When working with a client to develop nutrition therapy to control diabetes, the nurse must complete an individualized assessment of:

  • current eating patterns
  • food preferences
  • metabolic goals

Consider personal preferences (e.g., tradition, culture, religion, health beliefs, and socioeconomics) as well as metabolic goals when working with individuals to determine the best eating pattern for them. An individualized eating pattern considers the individual's health status, skills, resources, food preferences, and health goals.

Referral to a registered dietitian nutritionist (RD/RDN) is essential to assess the overall nutrition status of, and to work collaboratively with, the client to create a personalized meal plan. The meal plan will also align with the overall treatment plan, including physical activity and medication use.

Section 1: Introduction to Nutrition for Gastrointestinal Health

The human gastrointestinal (GI) system is very complex and disruptions to this homeostatic environment can lead to inflammation, pain, and multiple other GI symptoms. Nutrition significantly impacts a person's GI health and some GI disorders may have serious nutritional implications. Almost everyone has had some form of episodic GI symptoms, including indigestion, gas, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and constipation. Others may have more complicated disorders, such as dysphagia.

Evaluation of a client with gastrointestinal symptoms is holistic and it takes a multidisciplinary approach to help separate GI symptoms associated with dietary practices from those related to GI disease or dysfunction.

In this learning activity, we will discuss an overview of nutrition and its impact on GI health, and examples of different nutritional therapies utilized in the management of general GI symptoms. This learning activity will guide you in identifying the nutritional needs of individuals with GI disorders and in understanding the impact nutrition can have on their overall health.

Section 3: Explore: Nutrition for Gastrointestinal Health

Food is our main source of energy, and vitamins and trace minerals are essential components of our diet for nutritional health. Without these essential components, dietary deficiencies can develop and cause gastrointestinal (GI) symptoms and complicate GI diseases/disorders.

Common GI symptoms, such as vomiting and diarrhea, disrupt the homeostatic environment of the GI system and can lead to fluid/electrolyte and nutritional deficiencies. Dehydration is a concern with uncontrolled vomiting and diarrhea. The loss of fluid and electrolytes stresses body functions. Infants are at high risk for dehydration because their bodies contain a high percentage of fluids.

Malnutrition is very common in hospitalized clients and has been associated with prolonged hospitalization, longer intensive care unit (ICU) stays, higher hospital costs, and adverse health outcomes (Ukleja, 2018). Therefore, identifying clients at risk for malnutrition through conducting a nutritional assessment, close monitoring, and prioritizing nutrition interventions has been associated with improved clinical outcomes.

Section 4: Factors Impacting Gastrointestinal Health

The relationship between a person's health and the social determinants of health (SDoH) is complex. Many of the factors that affect gastrointestinal (GI) health, such as having enough money to purchase healthy food and having healthy food available to purchase, also contribute to the health of the entire body. When chronic gastrointestinal illnesses interfere with a person's ability to work, it can lead to a decreased income, making healthy foods even more difficult to attain.

Select each tab to learn more about how each social determinant factor impacts an individual's GI health.

Tab: Healthcare Access and Quality

Access to healthcare can be limited by a person's income, lack of insurance, unwillingness to seek healthcare, and a lack of healthcare providers nearby. Since nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea (common indicators of altered GI health) are common, many people delay seeking care while trying over-the-counter or home remedies.

Tab: Neighborhood and Built Environment

Where a person lives influences their food choices based on what fresh foods are available, the cost of those foods, and the ability of people to grow their own food. Those living in food deserts, or areas without grocery stores, may rely on unhealthy convenience foods, such as prepackaged and fast foods.

Another aspect of the built environment for those with GI disorders is having ready access to a restroom. Conditions, such as irritable bowel disease (IBD), that cause sudden urges to defecate, make going out risky unless the person living with the disorder is certain of the location of bathrooms at their destination.

Tab: Social and Community Context

Emotional health is taxed when an individual struggles to accept the limits caused by GI illness, whether dietary restrictions (e.g., low-salt diet) or physical changes (e.g., prolonged vomiting). Prolonged illness and social isolation can diminish psychological well-being and overall quality of life due to the loss of support provided by family, friends, coworkers, and others within the person's life.

A person's family and culture influence their food choices and cooking in ways that can either support or weaken their GI health, as well as their beliefs about seeking healthcare, in general. Certain GI diseases (e.g., colorectal cancer, helicobacter pylori infection, gallstones, and gallbladder cancer) have a genetic component and tend to occur within families.

Tab: Economic Stability

Short-term GI alterations that resolve without medical intervention can lead to missed days of work, decreasing the person's income. If the condition lingers or worsens and treatment is sought, insurance copays for healthcare provider visits, tests, medications, and even hospitalization can be challenging financially, particularly for those with lower incomes or no health insurance.

Tab: Education Access and Quality

Chronic GI tract illnesses can interfere with a person's education. Whether it is missed days at school or a lack of energy to attend class and complete homework, the nutritional intake during times of acute illness depletes the body's energy and can interfere with brain function.

Section 5: Screening for Gastrointestinal Disorders

Nutritional assessment techniques are noninvasive, inexpensive, and easy to perform. Nutrition screening is the first step in assessing nutritional status. Based on easily obtained data, nutritional screening is a quick and easy way to identify individuals with nutritional risks, such as those with weight loss, inadequate food intake, or recent illness. Parameters used for nutrition screening typically include weight and weight history, conditions associated with altered nutritional intake (e.g., difficulty chewing or swallowing), diet information, and routine laboratory data. Additional screening tools are available for populations with unique needs (e.g., pregnant women).

Malnutrition in hospitalized clients is associated with adverse health outcomes, including increased length of stay, increased readmission rates, and increased mortality (Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality, 2020). Nurses are often the first to assess a newly hospitalized client and identify those who are malnourished or at risk for malnutrition, referring them for a comprehensive nutritional assessment, including dietary history and clinical information, physical examination for clinical signs, anthropometric measures, and laboratory tests.

In addition, various methods for collecting current dietary intake information are available: 24-hour recall, a food frequency questionnaire, and a food diary. During hospitalization, documentation of nutritional intake is achieved through calorie counts of nutrients consumed or infused. However, the simplest method for assessing dietary intake is the 24-hour recall. The client completes a questionnaire or is asked to recall everything eaten within the last 24 hours. This allows insights into the client's dietary intake. However, there are drawbacks, including:

  • The person is unable to recall the type or amount of food eaten.
  • Their intake within the last 24 hours may be atypical of their usual food intake.
  • Some people may alter the truth for various reasons.
  • Items such as snacks, gravies, sauces, and condiments are often underreported, making dietary analysis incorrect.
  • It is also essential to prompt the individual to report liquid intake, as some will omit drinks and note only the food they ate.

Section 8: Diagnostics of Gastrointestinal Disorders

Image Description: Close-up of the controls of a colonoscope. This instrument is used for performing colonoscopies, or polyp removal. Progress is monitored, with selective focus, shallow depth of field, by a surgeon and assistant on a computer screen, and the client is under general anesthesia.

Clients experiencing symptoms of a gastrointestinal (GI) disorder (e.g., nausea, vomiting, cramping, anorexia, constipation, diarrhea, or blood in the stool) often need diagnostic testing to verify a specific diagnosis. These tests can be noninvasive (e.g., testing the stool for occult blood) or invasive (e.g., colonoscopy, barium enema, exploratory surgery). In addition to helping define the alteration in health, diagnostic testing reveals information about the underlying cause, symptoms, and severity of the client's illness.

Diagnostic tests of the GI system often require some preparation (e.g., fasting, medication to cleanse the bowel, abstaining from specific foods that may interfere with test results (e.g., food and drink that contains red dyes prior to a colonoscopy), removing all jewelry (magnetic resonance imaging [MRI]), or having a ride home after a procedure that involves sedation (e.g., colonoscopy or endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography [ERCP]).

Section 9: Nutritional Therapy

A healthy diet is a cornerstone of therapy for gastrointestinal (GI) health, regardless of the symptoms or disorder. Unusual dietary practices, nutritional inadequacies, or food intolerances can lead to GI symptoms or disorders.

Common diets that reduce inflammation in the GI system and are often recommended for individuals experiencing GI symptoms or disorders include:

  • Mediterranean diet
  • DASH diet

These diets offer antioxidant-rich foods that play an essential role in reducing inflammation in the GI system. They also focus on the prevention of various health alterations, which includes GI health. The goal is to have well-balanced food choices, weight management, and maintain healthy eating. The DASH diet has a special focus on controlling hypertension, as well.

Section 10: The Mediterranean Diet

The Mediterranean diet is based on traditional eating habits that are followed by residents of the countries bordering the Mediterranean Sea (American Heart Association, 2020). Dietary patterns in this region may vary because of differences in culture, ethnic background, religion, economy, geography, and agricultural production. However, their diets are mostly similar within the region, support optimal health, and help reduce inflammation in the body.

This diet includes:

  • plenty of fruits, vegetables, bread and other grains, potatoes, beans, nuts, and seeds
  • olive oil as a primary fat source
  • dairy products, eggs, fish, and poultry in low to moderate amounts

Section 11: Why the DASH Diet Works

While the dietary approaches to stop hypertension (DASH) diet is most well known in relation to preventing and treating heart diseases and hypertension, it is a well-balanced dietary plan that can also promote health in other body systems. With an emphasis on healthy fats, lean proteins, decreased sodium, and increased mineral intake, the DASH diet decreases inflammation.

As with any dietary plan, use of the DASH diet may not be recommended for use with all gastrointestinal (GI) disorders, such as those that require a low-residue (low-fiber) diet. Take a moment to review the science behind the DASH eating plan.

Transcript: Why the DASH Eating Plan Works

Scientific studies show that following DASH and eating less sodium can help lower your blood pressure and LDL cholesterol

What you choose to eat affects your chances of developing hypertension, otherwise known as high blood pressure. Blood pressure can be unhealthy even if it stays only slightly above the optimal level of less than 120/80 mmHg. The more your blood pressure rises above normal, the greater the health risk.

Scientists supported by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood institute (NHLBI) have conducted multiple scientific trials since the Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension – or DASH eating plan – was developed more than 20 years ago. Their findings showed that blood pressures were reduced with an eating plan that emphasizes vegetables, fruits, and whole grains and include fish, poultry, beans, nuts, and healthy oils. It limits foods that are high in saturated fat, such as fatty meats, full-fat dairy products, and tropical oils such as coconut, palm kernel, and palm oils. It is also lower in sodium compared to the typical American diet and reduces sugar-sweetened beverages and sweets.

The DASH eating plan follows heart healthy guidelines to limit saturated fat and trans-fat. It focuses on eating more foods rich in nutrients that can help lower blood pressure – mainly minerals (like potassium, calcium, and magnesium), protein, and fiber. It includes nutrient-rich foods so that it also meets other nutrient requirements as recommended by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine.

Section 13: Common Symptoms of Gastrointestinal Alterations

Gastrointestinal (GI) disorders can have significant nutritional implications on an individual's health by impacting the individual's ability to chew, swallow, digest, and absorb nutrients while passing substances on for elimination.

Disorders of the GI tract include those that affect the esophagus, stomach, small intestine, and large intestine (upper and lower GI system). Common client symptoms include gas, indigestion, nausea, and vomiting. GI disorders may include dysphagia, diarrhea, and constipation.

Section 14: Intestinal Gas and Flatulence

Intestinal gas occurs within the stomach, small intestine, and large intestine.

Tab: Gas Locations

How gas impacts a person depends on which location in the gastrointestinal (GI) system the gas is present.

  • excessive bloating or distention of the abdomen
  • release of swallowed air from the stomach via the mouth (eructation, belching, or burping)
  • expelling air produced by food digestion and fermentation by intestinal bacteria (passing gas or flatus)
Tab: Types of Gas Produced

A variety of factors contribute to gas production along the GI tract, including:

  • Bloating, or the sensation of having a full stomach, is not generally linked to a specific set of causes. Studies have demonstrated that clients with frequent bloating do not have an unusually large amount of air trapped in their stomachs (Mayo Clinic, 2022).
  • Stomach gas can be caused by carbonated drinks, eating too fast, smoking tobacco, sherbet (an alternative to ice cream that also contains dairy), chewing gum, or sucking on hard candies. These all allow air to enter the stomach and become trapped.
  • Intestinal gas (flatus) is most often caused by the fermentation of food by intestinal bacteria. Other causes include an imbalance of good and bad bacteria, constipation, poor absorption of carbohydrates, lactose intolerance, and consuming foods that contribute to gas production.

Simple diet changes can help reduce gastrointestinal gas and relieve symptoms. Clients with gas and flatulence may have a "full" feeling and may not want to eat, leaving their nutritional requirements unmet.

Although many GI disorders contribute to gas, a person's diet often has the most significant influence on the production of intestinal gas and flatulence. One can reduce or eliminate the symptoms by identifying, limiting, or avoiding the offending foods.

Section 16: Dysphagia

Dysphagia is a common gastrointestinal (GI) disorder that affects a client's ability to swallow and can lead to malnutrition. Just like breathing or the beating of the heart, swallowing is an involuntary action that occurs without thought. Therefore, systematic screening of the client's ability to swallow and a thorough assessment of their nutritional status is essential. If a client cannot swallow, there is a significant risk of malnutrition.

Any condition that weakens or damages the muscles and nerves used for swallowing or leads to a narrowing of the back of the throat or esophagus can cause dysphagia. It should be noted that dysphagia, though more common in older adults, is not a normal part of aging.

Tab: Risk Factors for Dysphagia
  • older age (normal wear and tear on the esophagus)
  • neurologic conditions
    • stroke
    • Parkinson's disease
Tab: Complications of Dysphagia
  • malnutrition
  • aspiration pneumonia (stomach contents enter the lung)
  • choking

The goal in managing clients with dysphagia is to frequently assess their nutritional status, swallowing ability, and adjust their diets, as needed. It is important to follow directions provided by the speech-language pathologist and healthcare provider regarding the consistency of foods that the client can safely swallow, monitor their eating (in case they choke or aspirate), and have the client eat in a fully upright position (aids swallowing).

Section 18: Nutritional Goals

An individual's dietary and nutritional goals are determined by their overall health, the foods they eat, and the specific gastrointestinal (GI) symptoms and disease. When dietary changes are included in the client's treatment plan, the goals are:

  • maintaining adequate intake using a well-balanced diet, supplementing as needed
  • protecting the airway of the client living with dysphagia
  • avoiding foods that have little nutritional value (e.g., processed foods, fast foods, items with high sugar content)

Steps to support these goals will vary by the client, but should generally include:

  • increasing the number and variety of fresh foods consumed
  • eliminating foods that worsen the client's condition
  • encouraging the client to eat at least three meals per day, as tolerated

For clients who cannot shop for or prepare their own foods, alternate assistance, like having a family member shop and prepare their food or a program that delivers healthy meals to the home, should be explored.
