## Section 1: Introduction to Pediatric Nursing

Pediatric nursing involves providing care for infants, children, and adolescents on a continuum from health to illness. The primary goal in pediatric nursing is to improve the quality of health for children and their families.

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients and how culture, communication, hospitalization, and play all influence the quality of care that is provided.

## Section 3: Explore: Health and Wellness - Pediatric Stages

**Framing Health and Wellness Across the Continuum**

Life span development influences our overall health and well-being. Nurses seek to understand how age, risk-behavior, and wellness contribute to optimal health outcomes.

Click below to review age-related differences, health risks, health promotion concepts, and how the nursing process is used across the life span development continuum.

### Infant (1 month to 1 year)

| Domain | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Physical | Birth weight doubles by 5 months. Height increases about 2.5 cm monthly during the first 6 months and then about 1.2 cm monthly until 1 year. Vision and hearing develop. Some newborn reflexes disappear (grasping, sucking, etc.). By 4 months, an infant can hold their head up. Learning to sit, stand and walk. |
| Cognitive | Develop in the sensorimotor period by *Piaget*. Explore through senses and learn through trial and error. Shake, throw, and put things in their mouth. Important milestone: Object permanence develops between 7-9 months (knowing things still exist even if not seen). Language develops from crying, cooing, and laughing to imitating sounds, comprehending the meaning of simple commands, and repeating words. |
| Psychosocial | Experience *Erikson's* period of trust vs. mistrust (learning to trust self and others through caregiver interactions). Begin to separate self from others. By 8 months, differentiates strangers from familiar people and close attachment to caregiver occurs. By 9 months, able to play simple games, such as peekaboo. |
| Health Risks | Injury: motor vehicle, aspiration, suffocation, falls and poisoning. Physical or emotional abuse or neglect. Sexual abuse. |
| Health Promotion | Quality and quantity of nutrition: Breastfeeding is recommended for the first year of life. Iron-fortified commercially prepared infant formula as an alternative. No solid foods until 6 months. After 6 months, cereals, fruits, vegetables and meats can be introduced one new food at a time. Ensure adequate iron and fluoride intake. The total daily sleep time is 15 hours with 1-2 daytime naps. Immunizations: Influenza (age 6 months and older annually); Hepatitis B (HepB): 3 dose series at birth, 1-2 months, and 6 to 18 months; Rotavirus: 2-dose series at 2 and 4 months, or 3-dose series at 2, 4, and 6 months; Diphtheria, tetanus, and acellular pertussis (DTap): 4-dose series at 2, 4, 6, 15 months; Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib): 4-dose series at 2, 4, 6, 12-15 months, or 3-dose series at 2, 4, 12-15 months; Pneumococcal conjugate (PCV13): 4-dose series at 2, 4, 6, 12-15 months; Inactivated poliovirus (IPV): 3-dose series at 2, 4, and 6-15 months. |
| Nursing Process | Assessment-opportunities for developing and using senses, developmental delays, parents' knowledge, abuse, and growth. Intervention-use stimulation strategies to optimize development and use of senses while conserving energy during hospitalizations, limit negative experiences, encourage parents to provide care during hospitalization, limit the number of caregivers, anticipatory parental guidance (nutrition, back to sleep, car seat safety, and immunizations). |

### Toddler (12 to 36 months)

| Domain | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Physical | Grows about 3 inches in height and 4-6 pounds in weight each year. Height at 2 years (24 months) is approximately 50% of their final adult height. Starts to participate in self-care such as eating, dressing, and toileting. Gross motor skills develop from walking to running, jumping, climbing, and riding a tricycle. Fine motor skills develop in drawing, stacking blocks, and feeding self. |
| Cognitive | Start of the Preoperational Stage of Development according to *Piaget* (covers the age span from 2 to 7 years). Recognize self as a separate being, but unable to consider other's views (egocentric). Imitate others' behavior. Language develops to include an ability to speak in two-word sentences and vocabulary up to 300 words by the age of 24 months. Reading to the toddler enhances their language development. |
| Psychosocial | Experience *Erikson's* period of Autonomy versus Shame and Doubt. Strive for independence. Explore the power of temper tantrums and negativism. Solitary play continues, but begin to engage in parallel play (playing *next to* someone instead of *with* someone). |
| Health Risks | Injury: poisoning, drowning, or motor vehicle accidents. |
| Health Promotion | Nutrition: Childhood obesity; limit milk to 2-3 cups/day; grains, fruits, vegetables, dairy and protein; may desire to eat one type of food repeatedly; serve healthy finger foods. Toilet training. Immunizations: Influenza (1 dose annually); Diphtheria, tetanus, & acellular pertussis (DTap): 4th dose at 15 months; Measles, mumps, rubella (MMR): 1st dose (12-15 months); Varicella: 1st dose at 12-15 months; Hepatitis A: 2 dose series 12-24 months; Hepatitis B (HepB): 3rd dose; Inactivated poliovirus: 3rd dose at 18 months. |
| Nursing Process | Assessment-safety of the environment. Intervention-Encourage parents to stay with the child and participate in care during hospitalizations, establish trust with the parents, provide anticipatory parental guidance (safety precautions including car seat safety, temper tantrums, negativism, reading, toileting, and eating). |

### Preschoolers (3-5 years)

| Domain | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Physical | Gain about 5lb/year. Grow about 2.5 to 3 inches/year. Toilet training is completed for most. By 5 years old, preschoolers are able to skip on one foot, jump rope, skate and swim. Visual acuity approaching 20/20. |
| Cognitive | *Piaget's* cognitive theory does not include a period specifically for children 3 to 5 years old. The preoperational is divided into two stages: the preconceptual (2 to 4 years), and intuitive thought (4 to 7 years). The main transition is a shift from totally egocentric thought to social awareness and the ability to consider other viewpoints. Thinking is more complex, and children are able to classify objects based on size, color, and by questioning. Aware of cause and effect. More social interaction. Magical thinking: Believe in lifelike qualities in inanimate objects and that thoughts have power. Fear of bodily harm. By age 6, children have a vocabulary of 8,000 to 140,000. "Why" questions are common. |
| Psychosocial | Experience *Erikson's* period of Initiative Versus Guilt. Acquiring a sense of initiative is the major developmental task. Develop new skills and explore their environment. Guilt arises when children attempt activities that are beyond their abilities. Allowing preschoolers to do things on their own, yet setting limits and providing guidance assists with this stage of development. Stress may revert preschoolers to a previous stage of development, such as bed wetting, thumb sucking or wanting assistance from parents on activities they usually are independent in, such as feeding, eating, or dressing. Play becomes more social and shifts from the parallel play of a toddler to an associative play of playing together. Pretend play is common and children may have imaginary friends. |
| Health Risks | Same as toddlers. Setting good parental examples is important. |
| Health Promotion | Nutrition: Consume about half of adult portions. The finicky eating of a 4-year-old changes to an interest in trying new foods at age 5. Sleep: average 12 hours at night. Regular vision screening, especially the presence of strabismus. Immunizations: Influenza (1 dose annually); Tetanus, diphtheria & acellular pertussis (Ddap): 5th dose (4-6 years); Inactivated poliovirus: 4th dose (4-6 years); Measles, mumps, rubella (MMR): 2nd dose (4-6 years); Varicella: 2nd dose (4-6 years). |
| Nursing Process | Assessment-opportunity to practice and learn new skills, the parents' knowledge. Intervention-Involve the child in care (for example through manipulation of the equipment), encourage expression of feelings, and provide anticipatory parental guidance (injury prevention, physical and developmental milestones). |

### School Age (approx. 6 years - 12 years)

| Domain | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Physical | Gain about 4-7 lb/year. Grow about 2 inches/year. Most girls exceed boys in height and weight by the end of the school age years. Large muscle coordination improves and strength doubles. Fine motor skills improve also. Children are able to draw in detail. Body: Modest and sensitive about being exposed. |
| Cognitive | According to *Piaget*, the transition to the concrete-operational period is around 7 years old. Around age 11, transition to the fourth and final stage, formal operations, beginning to understand abstract relationships, formulate hypothesis, and consider different options. Start to demonstrate logical and more concrete thinking. Able to see other's viewpoints: Are less egocentric and able to understand that others may not share the same thoughts or feelings. Concentration improves. Able to place objects in order according to their size. Are less likely to interpret words literally and able to understand their meaning within a context (understand jokes and riddles). |
| Psychosocial | Experience *Erikson's* period of Industry Versus Inferiority. Acquire more skills and competencies. When recognized for success, children develop a sense of worth. When faced with failure, children develop a sense of unworthiness. Develop self-concept and self-esteem. Group play, and group and personal achievements are important. Learn to collaborate and work towards a common goal. Prefer same-sex peers. |
| Health Risks | Accidents and injuries with bicycles and motor vehicles are the most common. Infections, such as the common cold. Poverty and access to health care. |
| Health Promotion | Screening: visual, auditory, and growth; scoliosis. Health education in schools to promote good health practices. Dental care. Safety, such as wearing bicycle helmets. Nutrition: Obesity. Immunizations: Influenza (1 dose annually); Meningococcal: 1st dose (11-12 years); Tetanus, diphtheria & acellular pertussis (Ddap): 11-12 years; Human papillomavirus (HPV): recommended for all adolescents ages 11-12 years (can start at age 9 years) and through age 18 years if not previously adequately vaccinated - age 9 through 14 years at initial vaccination: 2-dose series. |
| Nursing Process | Assessment-growth, visual and auditory status, posture for scoliosis, and identify stressors. Intervention-minimize stress and the child's stress response, provide for privacy for the child and anticipatory parental guidance for the parents; annual health visits. |

### Adolescence (usually 13-20 years)

| Domain | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Physical | Changes are rapid. Girls' height increases 2 to 8 inches and weight increases by 15 to 55 lbs. Boys' height increases 4 to 12 inches and weight increases by 15 to 65 lbs. Primary and secondary sexual characteristics develop and mature. Girls usually reach puberty earlier than boys. Any deviation in physical appearance from peers is difficult. |
| Cognitive | Experience *Piaget's* period of formal operations: understand abstract relationships, formulate hypothesis, and consider different options. Able to do abstract thinking. Able to understand problems comprehensively and work to problem solve solutions. Period of high risk behavior, feeling invincible. |
| Psychosocial | Experience *Erikson's* period of Identity Versus Role Confusion. The developmental task is searching for personal identity. Work to emotionally separate from parents, yet preserve family relationships. Develop own ethical and value system. Make choices related to career, education, and lifestyle. Seek group identity and may dress similarly. Underestimate the consequences of own actions, such as smoking. |
| Health Risks | Accidents are the leading cause of death among adolescents, with motor vehicle accidents being the most common. Violence and homicide. Human trafficking. Suicide. Substance use. Tobacco use, including e-Cigarettes. Eating disorders, such as anorexia and bulimia. Sexually transmitted infections (STI). |
| Health Promotion | Health education especially preventing accidents and substance/tobacco use. |
| Nursing Process | Assessment: Screen for violence and suicidality, substance use. Interventions: Health education. Immunizations: Influenza 1 dose annually; Meningococcal: 2nd dose (16 years); Human papillomavirus (HPV): recommended for all adolescents ages 11-12 years (can start at age 9 years) and through age 18 years if not previously adequately vaccinated - ages 9-14 years at initial vaccination: 2-dose series; ages 15 years or older at initial vaccination: 3-dose series. |

## Section 6: Explore: Growth and Development

With five pediatric life span groups (infant, toddler, preschool, school-age, and adolescent), it is often hard to imagine how anyone can learn everything for each age group. However, it is actually easy!

**Think About It**

Growth and development follow an orderly, yet individual, sequence guided by theoretical frameworks. If you learn the sequence (developmental milestones) and the theories, you will be able to apply that knowledge to the pediatric client you are caring for.

Knowledge of pediatric clients will allow you to adapt your teaching approach to the developmental level of the pediatric client. For instance, consider you are caring for a preschool child in a pediatric medical-surgical unit and you have an order for an intravenous (IV) catheter. Based on the fact that preschool children are egocentric, you would want to explain in simple terms that the IV is not a form of punishment, but something to help them feel better. In addition, knowing a child's developmental level allows you to tailor your nursing interventions to meet their needs.

## Section 8: Explore: Health Promotion

When you think about pediatric health promotion, think about the concept of wellness. Pediatric health promotion involves activities that enhance physical, psychological, and emotional wellness. In addition, disease and injury prevention is the main focus. Due to concerted global efforts focused on child health promotion, mortality and morbidity among children have significantly decreased over the years. In fact, the immunization of children against disease has been deemed one of the greatest accomplishments in modern medicine.

Check out the [CDC immunization schedule](https://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/?CDC_AAref_Val=https://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/schedules/index.html) (n.d.). Be sure to figure out which vaccine is given first and when live vaccines are given.

As front-line healthcare providers, nurses play a critical role in advocating for public health and educating caregivers on primary prevention strategies (i.e., immunizations). According to the American Nurses Association (ANA, 2015) policy statement supporting immunizations, "Current evidence and research show that immunizations are essential to the primary prevention of disease from infancy through adulthood" (p. 2). Nurses also have a responsibility to provide accurate information related to the safety and efficiency of vaccines, to include recommended immunization schedules, throughout an individual's healthcare continuum.

> While we may not persuade all of our patients to get vaccinated, we can, and we must do our part to spread truth about the importance of protecting ourselves and our children from disease and counteract misinformation. Every patient we vaccinate is a step toward stopping the spread of diseases we thought were left behind.
>
> (Cox, 2019, para. 8)

Encourage your patients and families to ask questions and seek clarification of information, address their concerns regarding vaccine safety, and educate them on recommended immunization schedules. A crucial part of pediatric nursing is the use of anticipatory guidance. Anticipatory guidance begins before the child is born and continues throughout the pediatric life span. The aim is to prepare the parents ahead of time to avoid disease or injury.

## Section 9: Explore: Social Determinants of Health

Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) refer to the environmental conditions where people are born, live, work, and play which impact their health, well-being, functioning, and quality of life. SDOH also contribute to health disparities and inequities. The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services created Healthy People in an attempt to address those health disparities and create social, physical, and economic environments that promote achieving health and well-being for all.

Healthy People identified many Leading Health Indicators (LHIs), or high priority public health issues and objectives, aimed towards the pediatric population in order to improve health and well-being among the most vulnerable in our society. Some of the LHIs addressed by Healthy People in the pediatric population include:

- reducing infant death rates
- oral health
- childhood obesity
- improving literacy rates

Nurses should be familiar with the LHIs and recommendations of Healthy People 2030 so that they can provide proper anticipatory guidance to caregivers who will aid in collectively eliminating health disparities and inequities.

For a full list of Leading Health Indicators (LHIs), visit the [Healthy People website](https://health.gov/healthypeople/objectives-and-data/leading-health-indicators) (n.d.).

## Section 10: Explore: Cultural Inclusivity

Cultural inclusivity in nursing addresses and supports the needs of people from diverse cultures and values their unique contributions to their care. It acknowledges the ethnic and cultural differences found in people of varied races or religious beliefs. Understanding what preferences are acceptable, preferred, or forbidden can assist the nurse in planning care that is most appropriate for their client's ethnic and cultural background.

**Fact:** Many cultural preferences and rituals do not impact medical care or pose harm to the client.

The most effective way to determine appropriate care is to ask the client or family about their preferences and taboos. Never make assumptions about client or family preferences, as some may not follow the traditional norms of their culture. Some nursing considerations when planning care for cultural inclusivity are to inquire about communication, physical touch, diet, and ritual preferences.

**Fact:** Supporting a client's or family's cultural norms can increase comfort and decrease anxiety.

## Section 12: Explore: Therapeutic Play

Play serves a vital function in the life of a child. Therapeutic play helps in the development of many types of skills such as intellectual, sensorimotor, social, creativity, self-awareness, and therapeutic awareness.

- Play allows children to express feelings, emotions, and fears.
- Play facilitates mastery of development stages.
- Play assists in the development of problem-solving abilities and coping strategies in a safe environment.
- Play allows children to learn socially acceptable behaviors.
- Play can be used to teach children, especially in a hospital, pediatrician's office, or other acute care facility, and assists in gaining cooperation for medical treatments.
- Play activities should be tailored and appropriate to each child's stage of development.

## Section 13: Explore: Therapeutic Play - Nursing Considerations

Utilizing therapeutic play with a pediatric client is one of the most effective ways to engage the child and gain compliance when performing procedures.

Some nursing considerations when using play to engage a child are:

- Select toys and activities that are age appropriate, safe for the child, and that enhance development.
- Consider isolation precautions and the child's specific illness in relation to toy selection, as those toys may not be allowed to leave the child's room once they have been introduced.
- Encourage caregivers to bring one favorite toy from home to help ease anxiety and fears.
- Allow the child to go to the playroom if possible and it is not contraindicated based on the child's activity level, mobility, and illness.
- Encourage peers to visit an adolescent client using appropriate precautions when indicated.
- Involve a child life specialist in planning activities or procedures.
- Utilize dolls or stuffed animals to demonstrate procedures before they are performed on the client. This is most effective for younger children who exhibit stranger danger or are fearful of health care personnel.
- Allow the child to manipulate and play with medical equipment.

## Section 16: Explore: Illness and Hospitalization

An illness and hospitalization can be traumatic and disruptive for a child and their family. A nurse needs to understand how an illness can impact a family and incorporate interventions to help the child and family cope. The level or degree of impact is directly proportional to the child's level of development.

### Tab: Infant

At this age, the child is unable to describe their illness, follow directions, or understand the need for therapeutic procedures. Therefore, stranger and separation anxiety develop, leading to feelings of protest (appears sad, agitated, inconsolable, or watching desperately for caregivers to return), despair (appears sad, hopeless, withdrawn, or acts ambivalent when caregivers return), and detachment (appears happy, interested in the environment, becomes attached to healthcare members, or may ignore caregivers).

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:
- Preserve the child's trust.
- Reassure the child that the caregivers will return.
- Encourage the caregivers to "room in" to facilitate caregiver-child attachment.
- Maintain normal routines and rituals when possible.
- Allow choices, when possible, to facilitate feelings of control with the child's caregiver.

### Tab: Toddler

At this age, the child has a limited ability to describe illness, a poorly developed sense of body image and boundaries, limited understanding of the need for therapeutic procedures, and limited ability to follow directions. Therefore, they will experience separation anxiety and exhibit a strong reaction to any type of procedure related to the intrusion of boundaries. Because of the toddlers' perception of poorly defined body boundaries, they fear that their body will be mutilated by common medical procedures (e.g. a blood draw). Simply telling them that the bleeding will stop after the procedure will not calm their fears, but applying a band-aid is much more reassuring to them. Toddlers are most likely to demonstrate regression behaviors (i.e., thumb sucking, bottle feeding, or elimination accidents in an already toilet-trained child) that are more appropriate to an earlier stage of development, often to cope with stress or anxiety. Caregivers should be educated on the changes in behavior and punishment should be avoided.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:
- Preserve the child's trust.
- Reassure the child that the caregivers will return.
- Encourage the caregivers to "room in" to facilitate caregiver-child attachment.
- Maintain normal routines and rituals when possible.
- Allow choices, when possible, to facilitate feelings of control with the child's caregiver.

### Tab: Preschool

At this age, the child has a limited understanding of the cause of their illness but knows what illness "feels" like. They have a limited ability to describe the clinical manifestations of their illness but can understand the cause and effect which can be inhibited by concrete thinking. Fears in this age group are related to magical thinking. Therefore, invasive procedures are interpreted as body mutilation. Illness is perceived as a punishment for their bad behavior. Some degree of separation anxiety exists, but the child may become uncooperative, withdrawn or aggressive, or develop nightmares.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:
- Encourage caregivers to participate in the child's care and play time.
- Allow children to express their feelings.
- Give simple explanations and avoid medical terminology (with pediatric clients and caregivers).
- Allow time for questions and maintain a trusting relationship with caregivers and the child.
- Continually praise the child, focus on desired behavior, and give rewards (e.g., stickers).

### Tab: School-age

At this age, the child has a beginning awareness of body functioning and can describe pain. They have an increasing ability to understand cause and effect. They have a major fear of losing control, pain, and bodily injury and will seek out information as a way to maintain a sense of control. They can sense when they are not being told the truth. They experience stress when separated from peers and their regular routine is disrupted.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:
- Communicate openly and honestly.
- Clarify misconceptions.
- Encourage participation in care to maintain a sense of control and independence.
- Facilitate visitation for siblings and peers.
- Provide explanations using visual aids (e.g., diagrams, models, and body outlines).
- Praise the child and focus on the desired behaviors.

### Tab: Adolescent

At this age, the child mostly understands cause and effect and their perceptions of illness severity are based on the degree of body image changes. Their major fears surround loss of independence, loss of identity, body image disturbance, and rejection by others. Separation from peers is a source of anxiety and they experience feelings of isolation. Physical appearance and peer acceptance have major importance to how adolescents perceive themselves, and they might not adhere to treatments or medication regimens due to body image concerns or peer influence.

Nursing considerations to address these concerns:
- Involve adolescents in the plan of care.
- Support relationships with family and peers.
- Provide consistent and factual explanations.
- Be supportive of emotional expressions and outbursts.
- Facilitate communication between the adolescent and their caregivers.
