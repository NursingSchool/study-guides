## Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Cystic Fibrosis

**Nursing Care of the Child with Cystic Fibrosis**

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients with cystic fibrosis (CF).

Cystic fibrosis is a lethal, genetic condition characterized by mucus-producing gland dysfunction. The disease produces multi-system involvement including the lungs, small intestines, pancreatic ducts, and bile ducts. Advancements in antibiotics, nutrition, and respiratory management have improved the life expectancy of clients with cystic fibrosis in recent decades.

## Section 3: Explore: Pathophysiology

Cystic fibrosis is an inherited, autosomal recessive disease caused by a mutation in the cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR) gene, which results in a disruption of the flow of chloride ions and water across cell membranes. In turn, this causes the lungs, pancreas, and other organs to produce thick and sticky mucus which clogs airways and ducts.

In the lungs, retained mucus serves as a medium for bacterial growth of organisms such as *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA), and *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, which then causes respiratory infections.

In the pancreas, thick secretions block the ducts which prevent pancreatic enzymes (e.g., trypsin, chymotrypsin, amylase, lipase) from reaching the duodenum, causing impaired digestion and absorption of nutrients, particularly fats, proteins, and, to a lesser degree, carbohydrates.

## Section 4: Explore: Clinical Manifestations

The clinical manifestations of cystic fibrosis vary widely and change as the disease progresses. The most common symptoms are respiratory, gastrointestinal, growth failure, and diabetes symptoms.

### Tab: Respiratory System

- Classic clinical manifestations include chronic coughing and wheezing.
- Mucus impactions cause obstruction, hyperinflation, areas of atelectasis, and infection.
- Advanced disease clinical manifestations include a barrel shaped chest, clubbing of fingers and toes, and repeated episodes of bronchitis and bronchopneumonia.

### Tab: Gastrointestinal System

- Some infants with cystic fibrosis will have a meconium ileus at birth.
- Lack of digestive enzymes causes excessively large, bulky stools of undigested food that are frothy and foul smelling (steatorrhea).
- Poor absorption of nutrients, vitamins, and fat and increased oxygen demands for pulmonary function results in children with cystic fibrosis having difficulty maintaining and gaining weight (failure to thrive).

### Tab: Reproductive System

- Females have delayed puberty and difficulty becoming pregnant and sustaining pregnancy.
- Males are often sterile.

### Tab: Integumentary System

- A child with cystic fibrosis has sweat with abnormally high sodium and chloride concentrations, so they are at risk for abnormal salt loss, dehydration, and hypochloremic and hyponatremic alkalosis during hyperthermic conditions.

### Tab: Endocrine System

- Pancreatic insufficiency can cause diabetes symptoms of hyperglycemia, polyuria, glycosuria, and weight loss.

## Section 5: Explore: Diagnostic Evaluation

Early detection of cystic fibrosis and early intervention are associated with better patient outcomes. Diagnosis is often early in infancy. Diagnostic evaluation of cystic fibrosis includes:

- **Newborn screening test:** all 50 states screen newborns for cystic fibrosis
- **Sweat chloride test:** sweat is stimulated, collected, and electrolytes are measured
- **Cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR) mutation:** detection of this mutation provides a high degree of certainty of the disease, but does not guarantee the child will have cystic fibrosis

## Section 6: Explore: Therapeutic Management

Therapeutic management of cystic fibrosis therapy includes minimizing pulmonary complications, ensuring adequate nutrition for growth, encouraging physical activity, and promoting a reasonable quality of life for the child and the family.

### Tab: Management of Pulmonary Problems

Goals: prevent and treat pulmonary infection by improving ventilation, removing mucopurulent secretions, and administering antimicrobial agents.

Airway Clearance Therapy:
- High-frequency chest compression:
  - A mechanical chest device (vest) is worn to assist with mucus breakdown and clearance
  - Nebulization therapies are generally administered during the vest therapy
- Positive expiratory pressure (PEP):
  - Performed by breathing out with a moderate force through a device such as a PEP valve, flutter, or acapella
- Chest physical therapy:
  - Percussion by another person to loosen mucus, then mucus is coughed out
- Huff coughing:
  - A technique to move mucus from the lungs; used in combination with other therapy

- Bronchodilators: opens bronchi for easier expectoration
- Nebulized saline: improves airway hydration and increases mucus clearance
- Physical exercise: any aerobic exercise that the patient enjoys should be encouraged to increase lung vital capacity, remove secretions, increase pulmonary blood flow, and maintain healthy lung tissue for effective ventilation
- Isolation precautions:
  - Contact precautions are implemented for all clients with cystic fibrosis
  - Client should wear a mask in communal areas of the hospital
  - Individuals with cystic fibrosis should be separated from others with cystic fibrosis to reduce the chance of cross-infection

2. Forcefully "huff" the air out. (Moves mucus from large airways and out of lungs.)
3. Cough and spit out mucus. (To clear mucus from the airway.)

### Tab: Management of Gastrointestinal Problems

- Pancreatic enzymes are administered right before or with meals and snacks to ensure that digestive enzymes are mixed with food in the duodenum.

### Tab: Management of Endocrine Problems

- Cystic fibrosis related diabetes is a combination of insulin resistance and insulin deficiency.
- Decreased nutrient absorption, anorexia, and severity of pulmonary illness cause increased risk of glucose management problems.

## Section 9: Explore: Nursing Management

Nursing care of the child with cystic fibrosis involves comprehensive assessment and interventions to address all affected systems, whether in an ambulatory or hospital setting. Most patients with cystic fibrosis will require hospitalization for treatment of pulmonary infection, uncontrolled diabetes, or a coexisting medical problem that cannot be treated on an outpatient basis.

### Tab: Assessment

- Assess respiratory status: lung sounds, cough, presence of sputum, evidence of fatigue, and pulse oximetry.
- Assess response to respiratory treatments.
- Assess the frequency and nature of stools.
- Assess for evidence of growth failure: weight loss, muscle wasting, pallor, anorexia, or decreased activity.
- Monitor for signs of depression, anxiety, and disturbed self-image.

### Tab: Medication and Enteral Feeding Administration

- Administer medications: pancreatic enzymes (pancrelipase); vitamins A, D, E, and K; oral antifungals for *Candida* infection; antihistamines; anti-inflammatory agents; cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR) modulators; and antibiotics.
- Administer oxygen as needed.
- Administer enteral feedings as needed to meet needs for growth; administer feedings during the night to minimize the disruption of daily activities.

### Tab: Collaboration in Care

- Collaborate with respiratory therapy regarding respiratory treatments and respiratory medication administration.
- Consult a dietitian for assistance in nutrition education and setting nutritional goals.
- Involve child in planning care and modify care to meet the preferences of the child; it is not uncommon for children with cystic fibrosis to rebel against their treatment regimen and refuse some interventions.

### Tab: Family Support

- Support family in coping with a diagnosis of a chronic, life-threatening disease which will require lifelong and complex treatment.
- Provide education for intravenous antibiotic administration at home.
- Refer to reputable resources such as the [Cystic Fibrosis Foundation](https://www.cff.org/) for support and education.
