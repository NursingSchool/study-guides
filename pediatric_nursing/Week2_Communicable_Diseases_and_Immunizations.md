## Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Communicable Diseases and Immunizations

This lesson provides an overview of communicable diseases and childhood immunizations. Communicable diseases are diseases that are transmitted from one person to another directly or indirectly. Many communicable diseases can be eradicated or lessened with immunizations. The nurse plays a critical role in health promotion and advocacy by educating about the importance of immunizations and following the recommended vaccination schedule.

"Vaccination" and "immunization" are terms that are used interchangeably.

---
## Section 3: Explore: Communicable Diseases

The prevalence of most communicable diseases in children has decreased as immunizations have been developed and become part of routine pediatric care. The use of antibiotics has further reduced the incidence of complications from such infections. Some common childhood communicable diseases for which immunizations are available include chickenpox, measles, mumps, rubella, and whooping cough.

Communicable diseases have common patterns:

- **Incubation period:** the initial stage after exposure before symptoms are apparent
- **Prodromal period:** when the symptoms first become apparent; symptoms may be vague and not specified to the pathogen
- **Acute illness:** as the pathogen replicates, more severe and specific symptoms are present
- **Decline stage:** symptoms gradually improve
- **Convalescence:** symptoms resolve

## Section 4: Explore: Infection Control

Hospital-acquired infections are preventable if health care personnel practice meticulous handwashing and implement precautions to stop the spread of disease. Various precautions are implemented based on the mode of transmission of the disease.

### Tab: Standard Precautions

- Use barrier protection such as gloves, goggles, gowns, and masks.
- The purpose is to prevent contamination from all body fluids, secretions, and excretions.

**Transcript (isolation signage):** Standard Precautions

Everyone must:
- Clean hands when entering and leaving the room
- Cover mouth and nose with arm or tissue when coughing or sneezing
- Wear gown, glove, and face protection for any risk of body fluid contact

Care providers and staff must:
- Wear appropriate mask, eye cover, gown, and gloves if contact with body fluids likely

### Tab: Respiratory Hygiene

- Cover mouth and turn head away when coughing and sneezing.
- Offer surgical mask for persons who are coughing.
- Use tissues to contain respiratory secretions.
- Keep distance from others when coughing.

### Tab: Airborne Precautions

- For illnesses transmitted by airborne particles.

**Transcript (isolation signage):** Airborne Contact Precautions (in addition to standard precautions)

Restricted visitation, Enter only if immune

Everyone must:
- Clean hands when entering and leaving the room

Care providers and staff must:
- Wear gown and gloves before entering the room
- Wear an N95 mask

Patient placement:
- Airborne infection isolation room required (negative air pressure)
- Keep door closed

### Tab: Droplet Precautions

- For illnesses transmitted by large particle droplets.

**Transcript (isolation signage):** Droplet Precautions (in addition to standard precautions)

Everyone must:
- Clean hands when entering and leaving room

And:
- Wear mask

Care providers and staff must:
- Wear eye protection with respiratory symptoms and standard precautions when contact with secretions likely

### Tab: Contact Precautions

- For illnesses transmitted by direct contact or contact with items.

**Transcript (isolation signage):** Contact Precautions (in addition to standard precautions)

Everyone must:
- Clean hands when entering and leaving room
- Put on gown and gloves at door

Care providers and staff must:
- Use patient-dedicated or disposable equipment
- Clean and disinfect shared equipment

More information: [CDC — Infection Control Basics](https://www.cdc.gov/infection-control/about/index.html?CDC_AAref_Val=https://www.cdc.gov/infectioncontrol/basics/transmission-based-precautions.html)

## Section 5: Explore: Chickenpox

Chickenpox (varicella) is a highly contagious viral respiratory disease transmitted through the air, direct contact, and through contaminated objects. Contracting chickenpox during pregnancy can possibly cause birth defects or a life-threatening illness.

### Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:
- 24 hours of low-grade fever
- Malaise
- Anorexia

Acute phase:
- Pruritic rash to face, trunk, extremities
- Rash appears as papules (raised red/pink bumps), then forms into vesicles (small fluid-filled blisters), then the blisters break and leak and form crusts and scabs
- Typically lasts about a week

*(figure: chickenpox rash on forehead, Herrmann 1966)*

### Tab: Therapeutic Management

- Antipruritic or antihistamine medications for itching
- Acetaminophen for discomfort or fever

### Tab: Nursing Management

- For hospitalization, implement airborne contact precautions until lesions are crusted.
- Educate parents about home care:
  - Isolate ill child from children at high risk and unvaccinated individuals until lesions are crusted.
  - Encourage skin care:
    - Apply pressure on itchy areas rather than scratching
    - Smooth and shorten nails
    - Place mittens on young children
    - Bathe daily without soap
  - Dress in lightweight, loose clothing to keep cool.
  - Encourage child to drink fluids.

Avoid administering aspirin (due to the risk of Reye's syndrome). ([NIH NINDS — Reye's Syndrome](https://www.ninds.nih.gov/Disorders/All-Disorders/Reyes-Syndrome-Information-Page))

**Transcript (summary card):**

Chickenpox (varicella)
- Type: viral
- Transmission Source: respiratory secretions
- Isolation Precautions: airborne contact
- Complications: secondary bacterial skin infections, encephalitis, pneumonia

**Reference:** Herrmann, K. L. (1966). [Chickenpox on forehead; Photograph]. *CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL)*. https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=21510

## Section 6: Explore: Measles

Measles (rubeola) is a highly contagious respiratory disease caused by a virus. It is spread through the air and by direct contact of the droplets of infected persons.

### Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:
- Fever
- Malaise
- Cough
- Conjunctivitis
- Koplik's spots (pinpoint red spot with white specks in buccal cavity) lasting 3-4 days

Acute phase:
- Irregular macular erythema rash appears beginning on face and behind ears and spreading to feet (lasts up to 7 days)
- Vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Anorexia
- Lymphadenopathy

*(figure: measles rash, Partin 1976)*

### Tab: Therapeutic Management

- Analgesics for pain
- Vitamin A

### Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:
- Maintain isolation until fifth day of rash
- Encourage rest
- Administer antipyretics for fever
- Dim lights for photophobia
- Clean eyelids with warm saline if secretions are present
- Use cool mist vaporizer

**Transcript (summary card):**

Measles (rubeola)
- Type: viral
- Transmission Source: respiratory secretions
- Isolation Precautions: airborne and contact
- Complications: otitis media, pneumonia, laryngotracheitis, encephalitis (rare)

**Reference:** Partin, B. G. (1976). [Measles; Photograph]. *CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL)*. https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=17639

## Section 7: Explore: Mumps

Mumps (parotitis) is a viral infection that can cause swelling in the salivary glands. It is spread through the air and by direct contact.

### Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:
- Fever
- Malaise
- Headache
- Anorexia for 24 hours

Acute phase:
- Unilateral or bilateral edema and tenderness of parotid gland (by day 3)
- Earache that is aggravated by chewing

*(figure: mumps parotid swelling, CDC 1975)*

### Tab: Therapeutic Management

- Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs to relieve pain, decrease inflammation, and reduce fever
- Analgesics for pain

### Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:
- Apply warm or cool compress for edema
- Encourage rest
- Administer analgesics for pain
- Encourage fluids and soft, bland foods

**Transcript (summary card):**

Mumps (parotitis)
- Type: viral
- Transmission Source: saliva
- Isolation Precautions: droplet and contact
- Complications: sensorineural deafness, encephalitis, myocarditis, arthritis, hepatitis, orchitis, oophoritis, pancreatitis, meningitis, sterility (rare)

**Reference:** Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (1975). [Mumps; Photograph]. *CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL)*. https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=14488

## Section 8: Explore: German Measles

German measles (rubella) is a viral disease spread through direct contact with nasopharyngeal secretions of infected persons.

### Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:
- Young children have no symptoms besides rash
- Adolescents have lymphadenopathy for 1 to 5 days
- Low-grade fever
- Headache
- Sore throat
- Anorexia
- Clear nasal discharge
- Cough

Acute phase:
- Within 3 days a pinkish red maculopapular rash begins on face and spreads to trunk, then to extremities

*(figure: German measles rash, CDC 1966)*

### Tab: Therapeutic Management

- Analgesics for pain
- Antipyretics for fever

### Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:
- Medicate for fever and comfort
- Avoid exposure to pregnant women

**Transcript (summary card):**

German Measles (rubella)
- Type: viral
- Transmission Source: nasopharyngeal secretions
- Isolation Precautions: droplet
- Complications: miscarriage or birth defects, arthritis (rare), encephalitis (rare)

**Reference:** Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (1966). [German measles; Photograph]. *CDC Public Health Image Library (PHIL)*. https://phil.cdc.gov/Details.aspx?pid=22145

## Section 9: Explore: Whooping Cough

Whooping cough (pertussis) is a bacterial respiratory disease. It is spread through the air.

### Tab: Clinical Manifestations

Prodromal phase:
- 1-2 weeks of upper respiratory tract infection signs and symptoms
- Cough
- Sneezing
- Little or no fever
- Headache
- Anorexia

Acute phase:
- 4-6 weeks of dry, hacking cough followed by prolonged inspiration ("whoop" sound most often at night)
- Vomiting of thick, stringy mucus

Convalescent stage:
- 1-2 weeks of decrease in coughing and whooping

Link: [Listen to a child with whooping cough](https://www.babycenter.com/health/illness-and-infection/sounds-of-whooping-cough_10316927)

### Tab: Therapeutic Management

- Antibiotic therapy

### Tab: Nursing Management

Educate parents about home care:
- Promote rest
- Encourage fluids
- Provide cool mist humidifier or humidified oxygen
- Observe for signs of airway obstruction

**Transcript (summary card):**

Whooping cough (pertussis)
- Type: bacterial
- Transmission source: respiratory secretions
- Isolation precautions: droplet and standard
- Complications: pneumonia, atelectasis, otitis media, seizures, hemorrhage, weight loss, dehydration

## Section 10: Explore: Nursing Management of Communicable Diseases

The nurse's role with communicable diseases is to help identify the infectious organism, prevent the spread, prevent complications, and promote comfort to the ill child.

### Tab: Identify the Infectious Organism

- History of recent known exposure
- Evidence of clinical manifestations
- Immunization history
- History of having the disease

### Tab: Prevent Spread

- Advocate for immunization as primary prevention.
- Report the incidence of communicable diseases to the local public health department.
- Implement measures to prevent the spread of communicable diseases:
  - Follow isolation protocols.
  - Follow strict handwashing.
  - Use cough/respiratory etiquette.

### Tab: Prevent Complications

- Most children recover without difficulty from communicable diseases; however, certain groups of children are at risk for complications.
- Children with immunodeficiency are at risk for viremia from the varicella-zoster virus; antivirals may be used to treat varicella infections in immunocompromised children.
- Children with hemolytic disease are at risk for aplastic anemia from fifth disease.
- An increased incidence of pertussis has prompted a recommendation for a booster vaccine.

### Tab: Promote Comfort

Itching:
- Cool baths
- Refrigerated lotions (ex. calamine)
- Lightweight clothes
- Administer antipruritic medications
- Keep nails short

Fever:
- Antipyretic medication
- Manipulate environment (fans)

Sore throat:
- Lozenges and analgesics
- Saline rinses

## Section 14: Explore: Communicable Diseases and Vaccinations

Perhaps one of the most controversial topics in pediatrics is the topic of immunizations. Vaccines have single-handedly decreased the pediatric infectious disease rates over the years for many communicable diseases with high mortality and morbidity rates. As with all medications and treatments, vaccinations have side effects, and it is the question of side effects that brings vaccines under scrutiny. However, the reality remains that vaccinations protect children from very specific and dangerous communicable infectious diseases.

For example, the antivaccination movement was deemed responsible for the 2019 measles outbreak in America. In 2019, 1,282 measles cases were confirmed in over 31 states, which is the greatest outbreak of measles since 1992 (Patel et al., 2019). The cause was linked to unvaccinated people contracting and spreading measles.

**Reference:** Patel, M., Lee, A., Clemmons, N., Redd, S., Poser, S., Blog, D., Zucker, J., Leung, J., Link-Gelles, R., Pham, H., Arciulo, R., Rausch-Phung, E., Bankamp, B., Rota, P., Weinbaum, C., & Gastanduy, P. (2019). National update on measles cases and outbreaks: United States, January 1–October 1, 2019. *Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report, 68*(40), 893-896. https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/68/wr/mm6840e2.htm?s_cid=mm6840e2_w

## Section 15: Explore: Immunization Schedules

In the United States, the immunization schedule recommendations are developed by the [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices](https://www.cdc.gov/acip/index.html) and the [American Academy of Pediatrics' Committee on Infectious Diseases](https://services.aap.org/en/community/aap-committees/committee-on-infectious-diseases/). The typical immunization schedule begins during infancy to help provide immunity before children are exposed to potentially life-threatening diseases. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention provides the full immunization schedule. Below are the recommended vaccines for birth to 6 years and 7 years and older.

The recommended schedule for childhood immunizations begins at birth and continues through adolescence.

Contraindications to receiving a vaccination include:
- Severe febrile illness (note that minor illness is not a contraindication)
- Immunocompromised children cannot receive live virus vaccines
- Known allergic response to a previously administered vaccine or substance in the vaccine

A vaccine reaction may occur a few hours to days after injection. Localized tenderness, redness, swelling at the injection site, low-grade fever, and irritability can be expected side effects.

## Section 16: Explore: Nursing Management of Vaccinations

Nurses have a responsibility to promote immunizations as a primary prevention strategy to protect the individual and the public from life-threatening and preventable diseases. Nurses are responsible for educating families about vaccines, recognizing contraindications to immunizations, and administering vaccines.

### Tab: Parent Education

- Provide accurate and user-friendly information including the disease the vaccine prevents and potential side effects such as fever, rash, and soreness at the injection site.
- Provide the [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's Vaccine Information Statement (VIS)](https://www.cdc.gov/vaccines/hcp/vis/index.html) as required by law.
- Be flexible and offer options regarding vaccination schedules (for example, spread out vaccines over different visits so the child is not receiving so many at once).
- Educate about ways to minimize unwanted effects of the vaccine (for example, topical anesthetic or acetaminophen before vaccine).
- Document required vaccination administration information:
  - Day, month, year of administration
  - Manufacturer and lot number of vaccine
  - Name and title of person administering vaccine
  - Site and route of administration
  - Informed consent was obtained
- Avoid judgmental language and respect the parent's wishes.

### Tab: Contraindications

- Administering a live virus to a severely immunocompromised child (live virus vaccines include measles, mumps, rubella [combined vaccine], rotavirus, and varicella)
- Administering a live virus when recently receiving passive immunity (for example, after immunoglobulin administration or from maternal antibodies)
- Administering measles, mumps, rubella, and varicella vaccines to a pregnant woman
- Administering vaccines if the client has an allergy to components of the vaccine

### Tab: Administration

- Ensure proper storage of vaccine to protect potency.
- Reconstitute per manufacturer's direction.
- Rotate site of injections when several vaccines are needed at once.
- Administer injections simultaneously when several are needed.
