## Section 1: Introduction to Pediatric Concepts

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients. In this lesson, you will learn about how to incorporate family-centered care into your nursing practice through health promotion and anticipatory guidance.

## Section 3: Explore: Family-Centered Nursing Care

Families are complex and dynamic institutions and there is no one-size fits all nursing approach. Each child and family must be cared for using an open, accepting mindset and each pediatric client will have a unique set of living and environmental circumstances to which the nurse must be acceptable.

A family is defined as whatever an individual considers it to be. Families can include many or very few individuals, with varying roles and responsibilities, who have a strong emotional bond and commitment to one another. The term caregiver is used to identify the primary person(s) caring for a pediatric client. This person may or may not always be the biological parent, but one who has been deemed legally responsible for the child's care and wellbeing. Due to the expanding concepts of family, the term household is sometimes used. Positive family relationships are characterized by caregiver-child interactions that demonstrate mutual warmth and respect.

In pediatrics, a family is viewed as a whole unit, instead of individual members. A change to one member affects the entire family unit. Too much or too little change can lead to dysfunction within the family. Families undergo stress which can be expected or unexpected, such as an unexpected illness or an expected, scheduled procedure. Both instances can add stress to the family unit, which can cause individual members to react to the stress in ways they would not normally.

## Section 4: Explore: Components of Family-Centered Care

Family-centered care applies to the planning, delivery, and evaluation of health care for children of all ages in any setting and includes the following components:

- mutually agreed upon partnerships between families, nurses, and health care providers through which the families and children benefit
- respecting cultural, spiritual, and social diversity and including family-specific views in the plan of care
- understanding the growth and developmental needs of children and their families or caregivers
- treating both children and their families as clients
- working with all types of families, family structures or compositions (i.e., traditional, nuclear, single-parent, blended, extended, foster, binuclear, communal family), and parenting styles (i.e., dictatorial/authoritarian, permissive, democratic/authoritative, or passive)
- collaborating with families and caregivers regarding hospitalization, home, and community resources
- allowing families and caregivers to serve as experts regarding their children's health conditions, usual and unusual behaviors in different environments and situations, and routine needs

## Section 6: Explore: Anticipatory Guidance

One component of providing family-centered nursing care is anticipatory guidance. Anticipatory guidance is the process of understanding upcoming developmental needs and then teaching caregivers to meet those needs.

Nurses should provide information about what caregivers can expect from children in each developmental stage. As children grow and develop, their basic needs evolve over time. Nurses should be prepared to discuss the following developmentally-appropriate health promotion items with caregivers:

- physical, cognitive, and psychosocial development, immunizations, health screenings, and age-appropriate activities
- injury and illness prevention
- nutrition
- sleep
- dental care
- sexuality

Health promotion through anticipatory guidance is vital in providing services proactively, with the goal of optimizing the child's level of functioning. It ensures the child is growing and developing appropriately and it promotes the best possible health of the child by teaching caregivers and children about upcoming developmental needs and expectations.
