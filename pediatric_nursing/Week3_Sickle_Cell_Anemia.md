## Section 1: Introduction: Nursing Care: Sickle Cell Anemia

# Pediatric Hematologic Alterations: Sickle Cell

This lesson provides an overview of how the nurse provides care for pediatric clients with sickle cell anemia. Sickle cell disease is a group of diseases in which abnormal sickle hemoglobin S (Hgb S) replaces normal adult hemoglobin (Hgb A).

Sickle cell anemia is the homozygous and most common form of sickle cell disease. Sickle cell anemia is an inherited, autosomal-recessive genetic disorder that affects the red blood cells (RBCs), which become acutely sickle-shaped, occluding small blood vessels (blocking blood flow) causing pain and decreased function. The child must receive the autosomal-recessive gene from both parents in order to have sickle cell anemia. If a child has sickle cell trait, then the child inherited the gene from one parent but is asymptomatic.

## Section 3: Explore: Sickle Cell Anemia

# Sickle Cell Anemia

Sickle cell anemia is a congenital hemolytic disease resulting from a defective hemoglobin molecule (Hgb S) that causes red blood cells (RBCs) to become sickle-shaped. Sickle cell anemia is a chronic illness with distress resulting from blocked and inadequate circulation and tissue/organ damage that causes pain over time, organ failure, and death.

**Pathophysiology**

The pathology of sickle cell anemia is that the RBCs become rigid, rough, and elongated, forming a crescent or sickle shape. This process is called sickling, which can produce hemolysis (cell destruction). The altered cells accumulate in capillaries and smaller blood vessels, making the blood more viscous. Normal circulation is impaired, causing pain, tissue ischemia and infarctions, and swelling.

**Risk Factors**

Sickle cell anemia is an autosomal recessive condition requiring genes from both parents. When both parents are carriers, each pregnancy presents a 25% chance that a child will be born with the disease and a 50% chance that the child will have the sickle cell trait.

Sickle cell anemia primarily affects African-Americans, but may also be seen in those of Mediterranean, Indian, Middle Eastern or Hispanic descent.

## Section 4: Explore: Sickle Cell Crisis

# Sickle Cell Crisis

Children with sickle cell disease have three types of problems or crisis episodes. A crisis episode can be the result of:

### Accordion: Vaso-Occlusive Crisis

- This is the most common, non-life-threatening crisis related to blocked blood flow (ischemia) of tissues and bone from sickling.
- Blood stasis causes ischemia and infarction. Seriousness of sequalae depends upon the site of the occlusive crisis.
- Acute (related to dehydration and decreased oxygen) vaso-occlusive crisis:
  - Severe pain, usually in bones, joints, and abdomen but can be located anywhere on the body (e.g. low back pain)
  - Hematuria
  - Obstructive jaundice
  - Visual disturbances
  - Tissue engorgement

### Accordion: Sequestration

- This is a potentially life-threatening crisis where there is excessive pooling of blood primarily in the spleen (splenomegaly) and sometimes in the liver (hepatomegaly) resulting in lethargy, pallor, and hypovolemia which can progress to shock.
- Hypovolemic shock signs include irritability, tachycardia, pallor, decreased urinary output, tachypnea, cool extremities, thready pulse, and hypotension.

### Accordion: Aplastic Crisis

- This results from an extreme drop/reduction in red blood cells (RBCs; bone marrow suppression– often a vital trigger) leading to anemia, pallor, lethargy, sleepiness, and dyspnea.
- Typically triggered by a viral infection.

## Section 6: Explore: Sickle Cell Anemia: Clinical Manifestations

# Sickle Cell Anemia: Clinical Manifestations

### Interactive Transcript: Sickle Cell Anemia

**Clinical Manifestations**

- Family history of sickle cell anemia or sickle cell trait
- Shortness of breath, fatigue, dizziness
- Pallor, pale mucous membranes
- Acute pain due to blocked blood vessels and tissue ischemia
- Brain- stroke, headache
- Eyes- retinopathy, blindness, hemorrhage
- Heart- heart failure
- Chest/Lungs- pain, chest syndrome, pneumonia, pulmonary hypertension, atelectasis
- Abdomen- pain
- Spleen- splenomegaly, splenic sequestration, autosplenectomy
- Liver- jaundice, hepatic coma
- Gall Bladder- gallstones
- Kidneys- hematuria, impaired function
- Genitourinary- painful erection (priapism), hematuria, hyposthenuria (dilute urine)
- Extremities- pain, hands and feet cool, dactylitis (hand-foot syndrome), avascular necrosis (shoulder & hip), osteomyelitis, chronic ulcers (rare in children)

**Assessment Findings by Age Group**

- Infants
  - Before age 4 months, symptoms are rare because of fetal hemoglobin
  - Colic from pain caused by abdominal infarction and/or splenomegaly from sequestered red blood cells (RBCs)
- Toddlers & Preschoolers
  - Hypovolemia and shock from sequestration of large amounts of blood in spleen
  - Pain at the site of the vaso-occlusive crisis
  - Possible functional asplenia by age 4, leading to an increased risk for infection
  - Constipation can lead to abdominal pain. Proper assessment is needed to determine the cause of the pain (e.g. diet vs. sickle cell related pain) so appropriate care can be provided.
- School Age & Adolescents
  - Enuresis
  - Extreme pain at the site of crisis
  - Priapism
  - Delayed growth and development and delayed sexual maturity
  - History of infections caused by atrophied spleen
  - Poor healing of leg wounds from inadequate peripheral circulation of oxygenated blood
  - Constipation can lead to abdominal pain. Proper assessment is needed to determine the cause of the pain (e.g. diet vs. sickle cell related pain) so appropriate care can be provided.

## Section 8: Explore: Sickle Cell Anemia: Nursing Care

# Sickle Cell Anemia: Nursing Care

Nursing care and considerations for clients with sickle cell anemia include:

### Slide 1: Pain control

- Administer sufficient pain medication to promote comfort.
- Administer analgesics (oral or intravenous) around the clock (ATC) when in crisis. Utilize patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) if appropriate.
- Apply comfort measures (i.e., warm packs to painful joints to promote vasodilation of vessels).
  - Do not apply ice as this increases the vasoconstriction of the vessels thereby exacerbating the symptoms.
- Remember: Opioids administered for pain may cause constipation, which can also cause pain. When administering opioids, the nurse should monitor bowel movements and implement interventions to prevent constipation.

### Slide 2: Blood Products

- Administer blood products (usually packed red blood cells [RBCs]) and exchange transfusions per facility protocol for severe anemia (replaces the sickled cells with normal blood cells).
- Observe for hypervolemia and transfusion reaction.

### Slide 3: Hydration

- Decreases blood viscosity, sickling and vascular blockage.
- Intense hydration therapy (intravenous [IV] fluids and oral intake) to prevent or correct dehydration while maintaining fluid and electrolyte balance.
- Monitor intake and output to avoid fluid overload and monitor kidney function.

### Slide 4: Oxygen

- Administer oxygen as prescribed if hypoxia is present to increase tissue perfusion and decrease tissue ischemia.

### Slide 5: Comfort and Mobility

- Promote and encourage rest to decrease oxygen consumption and avoid fatigue.
- Position for comfort and to decrease stress on painful joints.
- Mild range of motion to retain mobility.

### Slide 6: Infections

- Prevent infection if possible.
- Administer antibiotics.
- Perform frequent hand hygiene.
- Give oral prophylactic penicillin.
- Administer vaccines to avoid pneumonia (pneumococcal conjugate vaccine), meningitis (meningococcal vaccine), and *Haemophilius influenzae* type B vaccine (Hib).

## Section 11: Explore: Sickle Cell Anemia: Family Education

# Sickle Cell Anemia: Family Education

Keeping a child with sickle cell anemia healthy and happy takes a coordinated effort from the child, their family, and their health care team. The nurse should provide education that is evidence-based and appropriate for both the child's needs and the general health literacy of the caregivers.

### Interactive Transcript

- Teach family proactive care to prevent episodes/crisis and infection.
- Provide emotional support for grieving and coping during a crisis and on an ongoing basis.
  - Refer to social services if appropriate.
  - Emotional or physical stress can trigger a crisis episode.
- Ensure adequate fluid intake to prevent dehydration.
  - Dehydration increases blood viscosity which can trigger a crisis episode.
- Avoid infection by practicing good hand hygiene and avoiding individuals who are ill.
  - Receive early treatment for infections as fever can trigger a crisis.
- Moderate activity and adequate rest to avoid fatigue and hypoxia as this can trigger a crisis.
  - Any condition that increases the body's need for oxygen or alters oxygen transport can trigger a crisis.
- Observe for manifestations of impending crisis and infection and notify the healthcare provider immediately.
- Promote rest and adequate nutrition.
- Maintain up-to-date immunizations to prevent infection.
- Consider genetic testing and counseling to allow for family planning.
- Wear a medical-identification wristband or tag.
