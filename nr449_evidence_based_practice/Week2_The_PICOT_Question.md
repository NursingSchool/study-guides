## Section 1: Introduction to the PICOT Question

The research question guides research design and methodology. Therefore, carefully developing a research question is essential to constructing the rest of the research process.

The PICOT model helps the researcher develop a specific clinical question. PICOT stands for:
- **P**opulation/Patient Problem
- **I**ntervention
- **C**omparison
- **O**utcome
- **T**ime

This lesson will teach you how to formulate a research question to be used in evidence-based practice.

---
## Section 3: Explore: Research Questions: Where Do I Start?

Establishing a well-written research question is the most essential step in the research process. How the question is stated determines which measures will be used, which data will be collected, which analysis is appropriate, and which conclusions may be drawn.

**Transcript (presentation, 5 slides):**

1. **Answer the why question first.** When the researcher understands the reason for the study, the specifics of the question are apparent.
2. **Review the literature.** Replication of previous studies is essential. Do not finalize the research question without an extensive literature review.
3. **Write, refine, and refine again.** Write the research question. Then think about it and refine it. Ponder it some more, then refine it again. The effort spent to get the question right is worth the time.
4. **Do not wait for perfection.** The research question is a work in progress. As the design of the study becomes clear, so will the exact research question.
5. **Keep the research question focused.** Do not include more than one major concept or outcome per question. Multiple questions should be used instead of multiple parts to a single question.

## Section 5: Explore: Elements of a Research Question

A research question differs from a purpose statement in that it is stated as a question and gives specificity to what will be measured in the study.

The acronym PICOT outlines the elements of a good quantitative research question.

Sample PICOT Question: In adult surgical clients in the operating room (**population**), does the pre-incision use of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) (**intervention**) compared to the use of povidone-iodine (**comparison**) impact surgical site infections (**outcome**) in 30 days (**time**)?

Population (P), Intervention (I), and Outcome (O) are always present. Comparison (C) and Time (T) may or may not be present, depending on the question.

Example of an exploratory study without a Comparison (C) and Time (T): For terminally ill cancer patients (**population**), does massage therapy at bedtime (**intervention**) impact quality of sleep (**outcome**)?

**Transcript (interactive hotspots for the sample PICOT question):**

**P: Population**
- Who is the client?
- What is the problem?
- Consider age, sex, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, etc.
- *In adult surgical clients in the operating room*

**I: Intervention**
- What do you want to do for the client (treat, diagnose, observe)?
- What is the client exposed to?
- Therapeutic measure, medication, diagnostic test, etc.
- *does the pre-incision use of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG)*

**C: Comparison**
- Is there a standard or alternative intervention to compare with the planned intervention?
- Another intervention, placebo, standard of care, diagnostic gold standard.
- A comparison is not always present.
- *compared to the use of povidone-iodine*

**O: Outcome**
- What do you hope to accomplish, measure, improve, or affect?
- The desired outcome should be measurable.
- *impact surgical site infections*

**T: Time**
- What is the time period?
- The specific time period must be relevant to the question. Note the period over which the population is observed, or the outcome is measured.
- Many questions do not include a time.
- *in 30 days*

## Section 7: Explore: Qualitative Research Questions

A qualitative research question is less prescriptive and more general. It outlines the phenomena to be studied and who can best inform the inquiry. Measurements, interventions, and comparison groups are irrelevant in qualitative research, so they are not a part of the question.

Examples of qualitative research questions:
- What are the perceptions of pregnant adolescents (population) regarding accessing prenatal care (outcome)?
- How do pregnant women newly diagnosed with diabetes (population) perceive reporting blood glucose levels (outcome) to their healthcare providers during pregnancy?

## Section 8: Explore: PICOT and Purpose Statements

You will notice that the elements of the PICOT question are very similar to those in the purpose statement. However, many research articles will not explicitly state the PICOT question. When the research question is not specifically identified, the reader will need to infer the elements from the study design, methods, and sampling.

Let's review a [research study conducted by de Avila Meinberg et al.](https://chamberlain.primo.exlibrisgroup.com/discovery/fulldisplay?context=PC&vid=01CUCON_INST:DEFAULT&search_scope=MyInst_and_CI&tab=Everything&docid=cdi_pubmedcentral_primary_oai_pubmedcentral_nih_gov_4031818) (2012).

The purpose statement reads, "The aim of the present study was to evaluate the effects of an oral hygiene protocol (consisting of 2% chlorhexidine and manual brushing) in a heterogeneous population of ICU patients on the VAP incidence, duration of mechanical ventilation, and lengths of the patients' ICU and hospital stays" (de Avila Meinberg et al., 2012, para. 4).

**Inline practice question (drag-and-drop PICOT builder):** "In (population), does the use of (intervention) compared to (comparison) impact the rate of (outcome)?" — build the PICOT question from this purpose statement.

**Answer:** In ICU patients (population), does the use of 2% chlorhexidine with toothbrushing (intervention) compared to placebo gel with toothbrushing (comparison) impact the rate of ventilator-associated pneumonia (outcome)?

**Reference:** de Avila Meinberg, M. C., de Fatima Meinberg Cheade, M., Miranda, A. L., Fachini, M. M., & Lobo, S. M. (2012). The use of 2% chlorhexidine gel and toothbrushing for oral hygiene of patients receiving mechanical ventilation: Effects on ventilator-associated pneumonia. *Clinical Research, 24*(4). https://doi.org/10.1590/S

## Section 12: Explore: Research Question Appraisal

Now that you have learned the elements of a PICOT question, it is important to determine a good research question from one that lacks desirable characteristics. Unfortunately, creating questions and finding answers is seldom straightforward.

The acronym FINER provides a guide for evaluating the desirable characteristics of a research question. Researchers use this framework to consider the philosophical, logical, and scientific implications of writing research questions.

**Transcript — FINER:**
- **Feasibility:** Are there enough participants to power the study? Are the participants needed available? Is the scope narrow enough for the study?
- **Interesting:** Is the question interesting?
- **Novel:** Does the study confirm or refute previous findings or provide new findings?
- **Ethical:** Does the study cause unacceptable risk to participants? Does it invade privacy?
- **Relevant:** Is the question relevant to scientific knowledge, clinical and health policy, or future research directions?

## Section 15: Explore: From Research Question to Hypothesis

The research question guides the design of the study and the hypothesis suggests the statistical analysis. A research hypothesis is a specific statement that predicts the direction and nature of the results of a study. The way it is written determines which statistical tests are used and which outcome is expected.

Question: What is the relationship between nurse burnout and healthcare-associated infections?

Hypothesis: There is no relationship between nurse burnout and healthcare-associated infections.

Although stating that no relationship exists may seem counterintuitive, it is the only way to measure statistical significance. The researcher cannot be certain that a relationship exists but can calculate the probability that it does not.

### Tab: Elements of a Good Hypothesis

- Statement of an expected relationship (or lack of relationship)
- Identified direction of interest
- Includes the population, variables, and comparison
- A **null hypothesis** states that there is no difference between groups as a result of receiving the treatment or not receiving the treatment.
- An **alternative hypothesis** specifies an expected difference between treatment groups. There are two types of alternative hypotheses:
  - A **nondirectional** hypothesis is one in which the researcher is interested in a change in any direction.
  - A **directional** hypothesis is one in which the researcher is interested in only one direction of change.

**Worked example:**

Question: In adult surgical clients in the operating room (population), does the pre-incision use of chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) (intervention) compared to the use of povidone-iodine (comparison) impact surgical site infections (outcome) in 30 days (time)?

Null Hypothesis: In adult surgical clients in the operating room, there is no difference in the rate of surgical site infections with the use of CHG as compared to the use of povidone-iodine.

Directional Alternative Hypothesis: In adult surgical clients in the operating room, there is a reduction in the rate of surgical site infections with the use of CHG as compared to the use of povidone-iodine.
