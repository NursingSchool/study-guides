## Section 1: Introduction to Types of Research

In this lesson, you will learn about the differences between descriptive research, qualitative research, and quantitative research and how important these types of research are to the nursing profession.

---

## Section 3: Explore: Descriptive Research

Descriptive research is a type of research that is used to describe the characteristics of a population. It collects data that is used to answer a wide range of what, when, and how questions pertaining to a particular population or group. The primary focus of descriptive research is the exploration and description of phenomena in real-life situations. Descriptive research does not answer questions about why a certain phenomenon occurs or what the causes are.

Descriptive research plays an important role in nursing and can be invaluable in documenting the prevalence, nature, and intensity of health-related conditions and behaviors. The results of descriptive research studies are critical in the development of effective interventions. In nursing, the only way to understand the beliefs and values of different individuals and groups is to describe them. This descriptive knowledge helps nurses develop interventions that will benefit individuals, families, or groups and enable them to obtain desirable and predictable outcomes.

## Section 4: Explore: Descriptive Research Strengths & Limitations

A variety of data collection methods may be used alone or in combination to answer the types of questions guiding descriptive research. Some of the more common methods include surveys, interviews, observations, case studies, and portfolios. The data collected through these methods can be either quantitative or qualitative.

Each of the methods used to collect descriptive data has its own strengths and limitations. The following are some of the strengths and limitations of descriptive research studies in general.

### Tab: Strengths

- It provides high-quality data since the research is conducted in the respondent's natural environment. Study participants are questioned or observed in a natural setting (e.g., their homes, childcare, or educational settings).
- Study data can be used to identify the prevalence of particular problems and the need for new or additional services to address these problems.
- Descriptive research may identify areas in need of additional research and relationships between variables that require future study. Descriptive research is often referred to as "hypothesis-generating research."
- Depending on the data collection method used, descriptive studies can provide high-quality data and generate rich datasets on large and diverse samples.
- It provides thorough information since the data collected is qualitative and quantitative and gives a holistic understanding of a research topic.
- Data collected from descriptive research is robust and represents a larger population (or sample size), which makes it easier to analyze and use for decision making.

### Tab: Limitations

- Descriptive studies cannot be used to establish cause and effect relationships.
- Respondents may not be truthful when answering survey questions or may give socially desirable responses.
- The choice and wording of questions on a questionnaire may influence the descriptive findings.
- Depending on the type and size of the sample, the findings may not be generalizable or produce an accurate description of the population of interest.

## Section 5: Explore: Types of Descriptive Research Studies

Descriptive research studies can be conducted using a variety of study types. The researcher must ensure that they select the best type of study for their research to maximize the study results.

### Interactive Transcript: Types of Descriptive Research Studies

**Observational**

Researchers collect data based on their view of the behavior and characteristics of the respondent, with the respondents themselves not directly having an input. It is often used in market research, psychology, and some other social science research to understand human behavior.

**Survey**

Respondents answer through surveys or questionnaires or polls. They are a popular market research tool to collect feedback from respondents. Can be conducted online or offline, making it the go-to option for descriptive research where the sample size is extremely large.

**Cross-Sectional**

Cross-sectional studies can be thought of as providing a "snapshot" of the frequency and characteristics of a disease in a population at a particular point in time. These are very good for measuring the prevalence of a disease or of a risk factor in a population. Thus, these are very helpful in assessing the disease burden and healthcare needs.

**Longitudinal**

Involves looking at variables and gathering of data repeatedly over an extended period of time. This research can take place over a period of weeks, months, or even years. In some cases, longitudinal studies can last several decades.

**Case Study**

Involves in-depth research and study of individuals or groups. Case studies lead to a hypothesis and widen a further scope of studying a phenomenon. However, case studies should not be used to determine cause and effect as they can't make accurate predictions because there could be a bias on the researcher's part.

**Correlation**

Used to measure the relationship between 2 or more variables, with the researcher having no control over them. It aims to find whether there is a positive correlation (both variables change in the same direction), negative correlation (the variables change in the opposite direction), or zero correlation (there is no relationship between the variables). This does not explain cause and effect but can be used in making predictions about relationships. Correlation does not equal causation.

## Section 7: Explore: Qualitative Research

Qualitative research is a broad term that embraces several different methodologies. Qualitative studies use a scholarly approach to help us formulate an understanding of a phenomenon. In other words, qualitative research describes life experiences, cultures, and social processes from the perspectives of the persons involved in naturalistic settings. Qualitative research is explanatory, descriptive, and inductive in nature. It uses words, as opposed to numbers, to explain a phenomenon which allows us to gain insights without measuring concepts or analyzing statistical relationships.

Qualitative researchers assume that we can only understand things if we consider the context in which they take place, such as a client who struggles to take their antiretroviral medication, or the female client who has carefully thought through childbirth despite a debilitating illness, or social media's influence on adolescents' mental health, specifically suicidal ideation and thoughts.

The purpose of qualitative research is to bring about a description of a social experience that is so detailed and insightful that someone who has not experienced the phenomenon can understand and appreciate its distinctions.

## Section 8: Explore: Characteristics of Qualitative Research

Qualitative research is a systematic approach to understanding the experiences of others. Characteristics of qualitative research include the following:

- Seeks to answer difficult "why" questions
  - Explanatory and descriptive
  - Discovery oriented; explanatory, descriptive, and inductive in nature; uses words rather than numbers to explain a phenomenon
- Often used when just beginning to understand a phenomenon or if a new perspective is needed
- Phenomena studied in naturalistic settings
  - The setting that people live in daily
  - Important because qualitative researchers consider the context to be very important in understanding a phenomenon
- Attempts to interpret phenomena in terms of the meanings people bring to them
- Used when attempting to understand the nature of a phenomenon
- Does NOT test interventions
- Seeks to understand the client's experience
- May provide information necessary to pursue quantitative studies
- May be used for theory development or extension
- Uses inductive reasoning

## Section 9: Explore: Types of Qualitative Research

There are multiple design strategies available to the qualitative researcher and the particular type of research design is dependent upon the purpose and nature of the research. The following types are regularly used because they answer questions about human reactions and interactions.

### Tab: Case Research Methods

In-depth study of a single subject/person or a small group of subjects (e.g., family group, community, or other entity) over an extended period with the goal of describing the impact of health and illness on an individual. Multiple data collection methods may be used, including observation, interviewing, and instrumentation. Mixed methods, which combine qualitative and quantitative data collection, are also widely used. Case research is an appropriate method for studying the responses of individuals and small groups related to interventions, health behaviors, and perceptions of illness. It may be the only way to study individuals with rare conditions.

### Tab: Content Analysis

Technically not a specific qualitative research design, it is more accurately described as a data analysis method used for interpreting the meaning in verbal responses of respondents or in historical or written documents. This term is commonly attached to designs that rely on data collected via interviews or document analysis and that use interpretive coding to arrive at themes and patterns. Such designs are sometimes referred to as descriptive qualitative designs, but even that term is misleading because these studies may be interpretive as well. Content analysis is often used when a qualitative study does not neatly fit into one of the more formal classifications.

### Tab: Phenomenology

Concerned with the lived experiences of humans in relation to a shared phenomenon, and investigates the meaning of an experience among a group whose members have lived through it. Used to describe how unique individuals respond to the circumstances of their health and illness. It is useful for understanding how patients react and respond to both everyday experiences and unique events. It is also useful for obtaining evidence of nursing practices that support and enhance the ways patients respond to the challenges in their healthcare.

### Tab: Ethnography

Focuses on the culture of a group of people and studies the features and interactions of that culture. The assumption underlying this type of research is that every group of individuals evolves into a culture that guides the way members structure their experiences and view the world. The ethnographic researcher's aim is to learn from, rather than study, the members of a culture without changing it. Participant observation is very common. The researcher is more than an observer and actually becomes an active participant in the culture under study to more thoroughly understand its experiences and worldview.

### Tab: Grounded Theory

Aimed at discovering and developing a theory based on systematically collected data about a phenomenon. The intent is to discover a pattern of reactions, interactions, and relationships among people and their concerns. This leads to the development of a theory to explain the phenomenon.

## Section 13: Explore: Quantitative Research

In direct contrast to qualitative research, quantitative research means that nurses can practice based on what they know, rather than what they think. Quantitative research is generally reported using numbers and statistics, which is helpful because numbers, statistics, and mathematics are universal languages. Quantitative methods and procedures allow the nurse to focus on detecting the truth and describe what is known in a standard, universally understandable way.

Quantitative research:

- Enables the nurse researcher to determine what is known
- Develops knowledge by a hypothesis-testing process: state them, test them, disprove them, or confirm findings by retesting
- Systematically testing research questions and hypotheses
- Contains a plan or blueprint
- Provides the structure for maintaining control in the study
- Replication of results
- Uses deductive reasoning

The purpose of quantitative research is to use measurements to determine the effectiveness of interventions and to report these effects with an identified level of confidence. Quantitative designs are considered some of the strongest scientific evidence for nursing practice.

## Section 14: Explore: Characteristics of Quantitative Research

Quantitative research involves measuring objective characteristics or responses of subjects and is reported using numbers. Quantitative studies involve comparing groups of subjects in some way (either between groups or within a single group over time). Quantitative designs are intended to be used with representative samples so that the findings can be generalized to larger groups of people with a high level of confidence that the study's outcome will be the same.

Characteristics of quantitative research include:

- Relying on numbers to measure and quantify variables
- Studying objective characteristics and responses that can be measured
- Comparing groups of subjects in some way
- Applying interventions to samples to generalize the study results to broader populations
- Aiming to determine the effects of an intervention through a high level of control

## Section 15: Explore: Types of Quantitative Research

Quantitative studies address a considerable number of research questions, though these questions are usually focused on the effects of interventions, actions, risk factors, or events. Quantitative research studies focus on establishing a statistical relationship between variables and measuring the probability that error was responsible for the outcome. There are multiple design strategies available to the quantitative researcher and the particular type of research design is dependent upon the nature of the research question and the resources available to the researcher. Fundamentally, quantitative types of research can be classified in the following ways:

### Tab: Experimental Designs

Experimental designs use a highly structured study of cause and effect to answer questions about and determine the effectiveness of an intervention. These studies are considered the "gold standard" for evidence-based practice because they provide convincing support for the value of a treatment. Experimental designs are often referred to as randomized controlled trials because these designs have in common the random assignment of subjects and a high level of control.

Experimental designs provide clear evidence of the effectiveness of interventions, yet they are uncommon in nursing and healthcare in general. This rarity reflects the difficulty in achieving the high levels of control that are characteristic of experiments in an applied setting. In addition, it is not always desirable, or ethical, to manipulate an independent variable or to withhold treatment, so alternative designs must often be selected.

### Tab: Quasi-experimental Designs

These studies of cause and effect are similar to experimental designs but use convenience samples or existing groups to test interventions. These studies have many of the same characteristics of experimental designs except for one key feature: they do not randomize subjects to groups, but rather work with intact groups or convenience samples. Because of this single feature, control and generalizability are limited.

### Tab: Comparisons of Intact Groups

- **Ex post facto research** relies on observation of the relationships between naturally occurring differences in the intervention and outcome. Data is usually collected after the fact, meaning that both the independent and dependent variables have already occurred. This design weakens the capacity of these studies to support cause-and-effect conclusions because the researcher cannot prove temporality. In other words, the researcher depends on the subject's recall to determine if the independent variable preceded the dependent one.
- **Causal-comparative study** involves categorization of subjects into groups. An outcome of interest is measured, and differences are attributed to the differences in the classification of subjects. This design identifies cause-and-effect relationships by forming groups based on certain characteristics that the individuals inherently possess (e.g., gender, ethnicity, or geographic region). An outcome of interest is then measured to determine if the groups differ.
- **Case-control study** involves observation of subjects who exhibit a characteristic matched with subjects who do not. Differences between the subjects allow the study of relationships between risk and disease without subjecting healthy individuals to illness. These studies are common in epidemiology and public health because they enable researchers to study the links between causative agents and disease. Case-control studies are often used to judge the relationships between risk factors and disease states or between an event and an outcome.

### Tab: Time Series Designs

Time series designs answer questions about the effectiveness of interventions over time - a type of quasi-experimental design in which only one group receives the intervention. An outcome is measured repeatedly over time. The researcher is interested in both the effects of an intervention and the timing of its effects. Time series designs have only one group: the group receiving the intervention. A baseline is measured and an intervention applied, and then measures are taken periodically over a specified time period. In these studies, subsequent measures are compared to the baseline measure, and any changes that emerge are assumed to be due to the intervention. In such designs, subjects serve as their own comparison group, with subsequent responses being compared to data for the same subjects in the preintervention period.

## Section 16: Explore: How Nurses Use Quantitative Evidence

Nurses can use quantitative evidence to:

- Answer a research question
- Identify if assessment procedures are effective in detecting client conditions
- Determine the effects of an intervention
- Measure the relationships between variables
- Detect changes over time
- Draw conclusions about the relationships between interventions and outcomes
- Determine which outcomes can be expected from specific interventions
- Develop standard operating procedures to standardize the way client outcomes are measured, collected, and reported
