## Section 1: Introduction to the Research Process

Research involves the search for truth. The goal of the research process is to produce knowledge that is relevant and applicable to the body of nursing knowledge and that becomes evidence for practice. However, there are multiple ways to determine and describe the truth. Therefore, the researcher must carefully consider the research question, design, and philosophical foundation before moving forward.

This lesson will describe the research process, introduce you to two paradigms of nursing research (quantitative and qualitative design), and describe research classifications. Lastly, you will begin to learn how to read and critique research studies.

## Section 3: Explore: Nursing Knowledge: Ways of Knowing

The nursing profession is defined by the knowledge possessed and sought. Carper (1978) identified four patterns of knowing that form the structure of nursing knowledge: personal, empirical, ethical, and aesthetic. The practice of nursing is a holistic, human experience.

The ways of knowing allow nurses to develop a deep personal understanding of themselves and the practice of nursing.

**Transcript (slide presentation):**

**Personal Knowing** refers to knowledge obtained through observation, reflection, and experience. This knowledge of ourselves enables us to establish authentic, therapeutic relationships with others (Brugger & Madison, 2015).

**Empirical Knowing** is knowledge gained through research and objective facts. This knowledge is used through the employment of evidence-based nursing practice and is often called the "science" of nursing (Brugger & Madison, 2015).

**Ethical Knowing** is the sense of right and wrong that we use to develop our moral values. In the nursing profession, personal ethics are based on the obligation to protect and respect human life (Brugger & Madison, 2015). Nurses are guided in developing professional morals by the American Nurses Association's Code of Ethics for Nurses (2015).

**Aesthetic Knowing** is how we characterize the moment when we uncover something new, such as perspective. This "aha" moment is often called the "art" of nursing.

- American Nurses Association. (2015). *Code of ethics for nurses with interpretive statements.* American Nurses Association.
- Brugger, S. B., & Madison, H. E. (2015). *Nursing knowledge: The ways of knowing*. https://www.excelsior.edu/article/nursing-the-ways-of-knowing/

## Section 4: Explore: Phases of the Research Process

Research is systematic and rigorous, meaning that decisions are carefully made that form the foundation of the study. However, some characteristics are universal in all research studies, which make up the phases of the study.

Though this process may appear as steps, with the end of one phase leading directly to the next, research is a fluid process. Several things happen within the same time frame, such as searching for a theoretical framework, the literature review, and the development of the research question. Often the researcher will move on to design the study, but occasionally, new literature will be published that requires the research question to be redeveloped. This question revision is an example of when phases may be conducted out of sequence, or the researcher may move back and forth between steps.

Due to the variety of research, the process must be used as a general guide adapted to each unique situation.

**Transcript (phases diagram):**

1. **Define Problem**
   - Reflect on practice
   - Identify a gap in knowledge
2. **Scan Literature**
   - Review literature for basic knowledge, relevant evidence, and theoretical framework
3. **Determine Design**
   - Design should work with philosophical assumptions, nature of question, intent of researcher, and time dimension
4. **Sampling Strategy**
   - How subjects will be recruited and assigned to groups
   - How many subjects are needed
5. **Collect Data**
   - Use reliable and valid methods
6. **Analyze Data**
   - Use techniques appropriate for type of data
7. **Communicate Findings**
   - Disseminate to appropriate audiences
8. **Support Practice**
   - Link research to nursing practice guidelines

## Section 6: Explore: Research Process Compared to Nursing Process

There are many similarities between the nursing process and the research process.

Once the process is complete, we must communicate our findings. Whether with the interprofessional team to enhance client care or with the medical community to improve quality outcomes, communication is the final step in both the nursing and research process.

## Section 7: Explore: Research Paradigms

A paradigm is an overall belief system that strives to make sense of reality and is the basis of knowledge. Two broad research paradigms reflect research methods: qualitative and quantitative studies.

Both types of research have similar characteristics, including:
- Disciplined, rigorous approach based on external evidence
- Methods that require samples and the cooperation of individuals
- Focus on the rights of human subjects and ethical guidelines
- Ultimate aim of discovering new knowledge used to improve nursing practice

### Tab: Quantitative Research

Quantitative research is the traditional approach to scientific research that assumes an objective reality and that events are not random but linked to an associated cause. It involves identifying the variables that represent characteristics of interest and then measuring them in a reliable, valid way.

The phases of the research process are tightly controlled, from the way subjects are selected to the study design to eliminate bias. Statistical analysis is used to establish confidence in the results and rule out the effects of random error.

The study of cause and effect in nursing is essential to evidence-based practice. Therefore, quantitative research is helpful to answer questions about how nursing actions can influence outcomes.

### Tab: Qualitative Research

Qualitative research is a naturalist approach to inquiry that represents a view of reality constructed by the individual. Reality is not fixed but exists based on participant beliefs, resulting in multiple views of reality. Thus, the researcher develops a relationship with the subject and bias is inherent.

Qualitative methods focus on understanding the meaning of an experience from an individual's perspective. Data is collected through observations, interviews, case studies, or social interactions. Data is analyzed based on verbal descriptions and observable behaviors from which themes are identified.

Examples of qualitative research include perceptions of quality of life, the meaning of a life event, and the willingness to endure side effects for a therapeutic result. The goal is to incorporate client preferences and values as a guide for nursing practice.

## Section 9: Explore: Mixed Methods Research

Mixed methods research is when researchers collect and analyze both quantitative and qualitative data within the same study. By drawing upon the strengths of both types of research, the researcher can explore diverse perspectives.

The quantitative data enables the researcher to identify and measure relationships, while the qualitative data provides a deeper understanding of human experience. Both are essential to support quality client care.

Examples of studies using mixed methods:
- Describe the rate of hand washing on a nursing unit (quantitative) and explore the nurses' perceptions of the importance of hand washing (qualitative).
- Measure the bacteria on hands after washing (quantitative) and observe hand washing technique (qualitative).

Study example: [mixed methods study](https://bmcnurs.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12912-018-0285-z)

## Section 10: Explore: Classifications of Research: Intent of the Researcher

Research is classified in several different ways. The first type of classification is based on the intent of the researcher.

Both basic and applied research may be conducted using quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods.

**Provide new knowledge for the foundation of nursing (basic research).** Basic research is commonly called theoretical, pure, fundamental, or bench research. This type of research builds knowledge that forms the foundation for practice but does not directly apply to the practice setting.
- Example: Studying the effects of circulatory volume on neonatal cardiac function.

**Provide knowledge that practitioners can immediately apply to the practice of nursing (applied research).** Applied research has a single goal of improving nursing practice. The findings are intended to contribute to the modification of nursing practice.
- Example: Determining the efficacy of a specific counseling technique after the death of a spouse.

## Section 11: Explore: Classifications of Research: Nature of the Design

The nature of the design can also classify research.

Experimental research refers to studies of cause and effect, which usually explore the effectiveness of an intervention. Participants are chosen and assigned to groups randomly, then the researcher manipulates a treatment and compares the outcomes to a group that received no treatment. If the outcomes are different, researchers can assume that the difference is due to the treatment, since all other variables have been controlled.

Quasi-experimental research is like an experimental design, but the selection and assignment of subjects are not randomized. Instead, convenience samples are often used.
- Example: The population studied is residents in two nursing homes. One group will receive treatment and the other group will not receive treatment. However, the subjects are not randomly assigned to the nursing homes and they already reside in one of the two homes, creating a convenience sample.

Nonexperimental research cannot test cause and effect.
- Descriptive research involves the study of a particular situation or event that already exists. No variables are manipulated.
- Correlation research focuses on existing relationships between variables.
  - Example: Do teenagers with mothers who had a teenage pregnancy have a higher adolescent pregnancy rate themselves?
- Predictive research searches for relationships in which the values of one variable can be used to predict the values of another. For example, predictive research involves determining whether a risk factor will lead to a particular health condition.
  - Example: Do specific family characteristics predict the risk of adolescent pregnancy?

## Section 12: Explore: Classifications of Research: Time Dimension

A final way that research can be classified is by the time dimension chosen for the study.

### Tab: Retrospective

Retrospective studies focus on the past because the data used has already been collected about events that have already happened. This type of data is termed secondary.
- Example: Using medical record data to determine differences in the rate of ventilator-associated pneumonia between clients who received oral care every 4 hours and those who did not.

### Tab: Prospective

Prospective studies focus on the future, with data being collected by the researcher.
- Example: A study involving oral care and ventilator-associated pneumonia by experimenting with different time periods, methods, and durations of oral care.

Prospective studies are more reliable since the researcher has greater control over the data collected. However, when using retrospective data, the researcher must rely on the accuracy and completeness of the originally collected data.

### Tab: Longitudinal

Longitudinal studies are conducted over time, usually over very long periods, typically to study the emergence of disease or the long-term effects of treatments. Data collection occurs at prescribed intervals during the study. A significant disadvantage of longitudinal studies is subject attrition.
- Example: Monitoring the children of smokers over time to measure the emergence of pulmonary disease.

### Tab: Cross-sectional

Cross-sectional studies collect data at a single point in time, often across multiple populations, to identify the existence of a phenomenon in the present, not on how it will look in the future. Though able to be completed quickly, the effects of time are not analyzed, leading to the primary disadvantage of this type of study.
- Example: Determining the distribution of pulmonary disease in children who have a caregiver who is a smoker in the home at a given point in time.

## Section 16: Explore: Reading Research for Evidence-Based Practice

The ability to read and interpret research is an essential task for nurses. The American Nurses Association Code of Ethics (2015) states in Provision 7 that "all nurses must participate in the advancement of the profession through knowledge development, evaluation, dissemination, and application to practice" (p. 27).

By engaging in scholarly inquiry and reading or participating in research, nurses expand the body of knowledge that advances the theory and practice of nursing. However, reading and interpreting a research study can be challenging. Learning to critique research studies is a foundational task necessary to translate evidence into practice (Kaplan, 2012).

Before deciding to read a research article, determine if the research report addresses a question of interest or can be used in your nursing practice. Then begin to answer the questions listed below (adapted from Kaplan, 2012):

### Tab: Does the title of the article accurately describe the research?

An informative title conveys key concepts and perhaps provides clues to the methods used or variables explored.

### Tab: Is the abstract representative of the article?

Start with reading the abstract, which provides a brief overview of the study's purpose, research question, methods, results, and conclusions.

### Tab: Does the article introduction make the purpose of the study clear?

A good introduction provides the basis for the article. It should include a statement of the problem, the rationale for the study, and the research question.

### Tab: Is the literature review relevant to the study and comprehensive? Does it include current research?

The literature review provides the context for the research study. It establishes what is currently known about the problem and what gaps in knowledge may exist.

### Tab: Does the methods section explain how researchers addressed the research question?

The methods section tells you how the study was conducted and should provide enough detail to allow the study to be replicated. Use this information to identify the independent and dependent variables. The researcher should describe how subjects were recruited and selected for participation. Additionally, any data collection instruments should be described, including how reliability and validity were accounted for during data collection.

### Tab: Is data analysis consistent with the study question and research design?

Data analysis must relate to the research design. For example, a quantitative research study may use descriptive statistics to summarize data and other tests, such as t-tests or regression analysis, to compare or evaluate data. A qualitative study may use coding or content analysis to interpret data.

### Tab: Are the results presented clearly in the article?

Results should be presented in a manner that is easy to read, such as tables or figures, then summarized in the text.

### Tab: Are the limitations presented and their implications discussed?

Researchers must state the limitations of the study. These factors explain why the results may not be relevant in all situations, may only be generalized to specific situations, or may be less robust than anticipated. Examples of limitations include low survey response rates or having a high attrition rate during a longitudinal study.

### Tab: Does the discussion explain the results in relation to the research question and study significance?

The discussion provides an opportunity to link study results back to the original research question, theoretical framework, and literature review. The results should be interpreted and explained. A discussion is also a place for the researcher to make recommendations for nursing practice and future research. The reader must determine if the recommendations are supported by the data or seem to be the researcher's opinions.

### Tab: After reading the article, how can you use this research to improve nursing practice?

Consider what you have learned from the article. Then, if the information is relevant to your practice, determine how you can implement the findings into client care at your institution.
