## Section 1: Introduction to Research and the Law

The duality of law and ethics exists in research. Law is considered the minimum standard with ethics being above the minimum behavior. Most researchers and providers provide ethical care but, if not, the legal system is there to address the situation.

This lesson will describe how the nurse can maximize the legal and ethical protections afforded to the subjects.

---
## Section 3: Explore: Four Categories of Law

Law is divided into four broad categories: Common, Administrative, Statutory, and Tort.

**Common Law**

Common law is the part of English law that is derived from custom and judicial precedent, rather than statutes.

Example: Medical Malpractice Cases

**Administrative Law**

Administrative law generally refers to the laws and legal principles governing the creation, administration, and regulation of government agencies at the federal, state, and local levels.

Example: (HIPAA) Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

**Statutory Law**

Statutory law in the United States consists of laws passed by the legislature. For the federal government, statutory laws are acts passed by the United States Congress. These acts are designated as Public Laws or Private Laws.

Example: Denying a client based on financial status - Congress passed broad rights to healthcare for the elderly, disabled, people living in poverty, and children by establishing Medicare and Medicaid.

**Tort Law**

A tort is an act or omission that gives rise to injury or harm to another and amounts to a civil wrong for which courts impose liability.

Example: Using an instrument that is known to be defective.

## Section 5: Explore: Legal Issues

There are a variety of potential legal issues to navigate during the nursing process.

### Tab: Communication

A researcher needs to be aware of what they share with others related to the study and its participants.

- Defamation is a false and harmful oral statement.
- Libel is a false and published (or written) statement.

The intent should be to distribute correct information.

### Tab: Emotional Safety

A researcher must protect subjects against inflicting mental or emotional distress. Sometimes a statement about a risk of emotional distress is added to consent forms to reduce the legal risk of this occurring.

### Tab: Client Cells/Tissue

There is ongoing discussion about whether consent is required to use an individual's tissues. Clients may not even be aware that they have contributed their cells to research. If a profit is made by selling tissues, some researchers don't feel that the client should be compensated.

### Tab: Privacy

Subjects have the right to privacy. Subjects have the right to have their health information kept confidential, released only to authorized individuals, and to have their bodies protected from public view.

### Tab: Consent

Researchers should ensure that subjects who are underage, disabled, or members of a vulnerable population are represented by their legal guardians. Additional examples of vulnerable populations include low-income, homeless, LGBTQ, and the elderly. Additionally, some clients live in more rural locations and do not have access to jobs and healthcare.

- The language must be simple enough to understand.
- The consent must not be coerced.
- Consent must be documented.
- The subject must have had time to review the document.

## Section 7: Explore: Institutional Review Board (IRB)

Institutional Review Boards (IRB) are required by law in any organization that allows research involving people.

This requirement evolved from early protection programs such as the Belmont Report and the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research.

Goals of an IRB include:

- Safeguarding against the inhumane treatment of individuals
- Ensuring that research is ethically acceptable
- Ensuring that the welfare and rights of research participants are protected

The U.S. Food & Drug Administration (FDA) has a [detailed website about IRBs](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/institutional-review-boards-frequently-asked-questions) which provides additional information.

There are three categories of research review by an IRB:

- An **exempt review** is limited to research studies that pose no risk for the subjects. For example, the researcher may survey participants or collect data from medical records. Though the study does not need full IRB approval, the researcher must still adhere to ethical principles.
- An **expedited review** is used when a study poses minimal risk for the subjects. The probability of physical or emotional discomfort from participation is no greater than that experienced during daily life. These studies are reviewed by one or two IRB members who will approve or deny the research proposal.
- A **full review** is necessary when research poses more than minimal risk to subjects. These studies must be reviewed by the entire committee. Federal funds are often received and publication of research findings is anticipated. Methods of data collection, the consent process, and selection of subjects are closely inspected.

## Section 10: Explore: Animal Testing

Research that has benefited humans often began with research conducted on animals. There are many animal advocacy organizations that have pushed for better treatment of animals and have campaigned for humane treatment.

Multiple guidelines now include:

- Injuries should be limited to unavoidable levels
- Analgesic and other pain relief agents should be used during testing
- Replace animal subjects with non-animal alternatives
- Reduce the number of animals used in experimentation

Experimentation raises concerns about necessity, purpose, risks, benefits, as well as the subject's capacity for suffering.

Some states have laws against animal testing and consider it abuse. Researchers must identify the parameters they must follow and uphold ethical practices.

## Section 12: Explore: Research and the Internet

Gathering data on the internet requires additional standards, including ethical standards and attention to avoid legal repercussions.

- Over the internet, the ability to observe the potential participant while they are reading the informed consent document is absent, so it is difficult to know with certainty that the participant read the document. This inability to observe the process might allow more room for the participant to later claim that their consent was not informed.
- Other steps might need to be taken by the internet researcher to increase confidence that the participant has read and understands the material related to the research. One possibility is to have an e-mail conversation with the participant after they submit the informed consent document to verify that they have read the document and to answer any questions or clarify any concerns.

Nurse researchers must:

- Identify why online is more effective and more beneficial than in person.
- Ensure subjects have a safe, private space to respond.
- Support the ethical acquisition of web addresses; ensure that the list owner has endorsed the study.
- Evaluate the vulnerability of subjects.
- Implement a process to ensure subjects are of legal age.
- Provide contact information so participants can easily communicate with researchers as needed.

The possibility of a lawsuit brought by a participant outside the researcher's home state opens the possibility of multiple jurisdictions in which a lawsuit could be filed. Because it is state-regulated, malpractice is generally a state court issue. However, if the participant is in one state and the researcher/provider is in another state, the lawsuit could be brought in the participant's home state or, if the participant and the researcher are from different states, the case could be brought to federal court.
