## Section 1: Introduction to Sources and Levels of Evidence

Research produces evidence. It is essential to understand the strength of that evidence to decide the quality of research and the need for further study. By understanding, grading, and identifying strong sources of evidence, the nurse can make better decisions about the quality of research and the need for more answers.

After completing this module, you will be able to:
- Describe different sources of evidence.
- Determine the strength of evidence.
- Apply a grade or strength to examples of evidence.

---
## Section 3: Explore: Evidence

Evidence is a set of available facts or information that supports the answer to a question. The entire purpose of research is to produce evidence. Once enough evidence exists to answer a research question, researchers can be confident that the question is answered. However, in answering the question, other questions often arise which require additional research.

Not all evidence is equal. While some forms of evidence can answer a question, others leave doubts. This uncertainty is considered the strength of the evidence. There are two ways to ensure enough evidence exists. The first is to produce more evidence. The second is to grade the evidence.

Identifying the levels of evidence involves creating a ranking system that can identify the strength of evidence and better identify how likely the evidence answers the research question.

---
## Section 5: Explore: Evidence Strength

The strength of evidence can indicate the accuracy of the research question. The strength of evidence comes from the source used to produce that evidence. It is important to get a large and/or strong amount of evidence before deciding to implement research in a healthcare practice environment.

There are various ways of deciding the level or strength of evidence. Some use scoring and grading techniques to determine the strength of research. This divides the different types of evidence and ranks them based on their factual accuracy.

Just because the evidence is weak or the source is questionable does not mean that the research is invalid. It does mean that more information may be needed to better support the research findings. The risks and benefits of the facts may determine whether the evidence is useful in certain situations.

## Section 7: Explore: Sources and Uses of Evidence

We use evidence in many different aspects of nursing. There are many tools used for patient care management that are based on evidence. Tools like the Braden Rating Scale or the Hamilton Depression Scale are based on different forms of evidence. Other tools, including critical pathways, care maps, or protocols are often based on evidence.

**Transcript:**

**Nursing Process:** Within the nursing process we use evidence when assessing a client condition, diagnosing a client problem, and planning a client intervention. This is evidence that we will usually collect but may also be research we use when planning or intervening.

**Public Policy:** We use evidence in public policy to guide law changes, establish policies and procedures for care, or to determine the cost and feasibility of implementation of evidence-based care.

## Section 8: Explore: Grading Evidence

In determining strength, a list of evidence can be created and categorized from the strongest to the weakest form.

Aggregated (pulled together) evidence is among the strongest, using a specific process of criteria for selecting a combination of research. Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and clinical practice guidelines are all forms of aggregated evidence analysis. These are usually comprehensive and will grade the quality of the individual evidence used as part of the aggregate.

Individual research that specifically studies one question or item can be strong or weak depending on the study design and variables used. Because these studies are not replicated if used alone, they are weaker than the aggregated evidence.

Even with the individual research, some studies are considered stronger (randomized clinical trials) than others (case-controlled studies).

**Transcript — Grading Evidence (strongest to weakest):**
1. Clinical practice guidelines
2. Meta-analyses
3. Systematic reviews
4. Randomized clinical trials
5. Cohort studies
6. Outcome research
7. Case-controlled studies
8. Expert opinion

## Section 10: Explore: Aggregated Evidence

Here are some forms of aggregated evidence.

### Tab: Clinical Practice Guidelines

This is a process of evidence collection and analysis that uses many different sources to summarize and create a guide for the practicing clinician. Usually, evidence is graded based on the quality, potential risks, study weaknesses, cost, and barriers to implementation. This is the most comprehensive quality and highest strength evidence.

### Tab: Meta-Analyses

This is a process of collection and analysis of evidence related to a specific question or problem. Statistical methods are used to summarize the results of the collected evidence. It is a very strong form of evidence.

### Tab: Systematic Reviews

This answers a specific research question by collecting and summarizing all evidence available that fits specific criteria. While this is like a meta-analysis, it does not use statistical methods to come to a conclusion.

## Section 11: Explore: Non-Aggregated Evidence

Here are some forms of non-aggregated evidence.

**Transcript:**

**Randomized Clinical Trials:** This is a study design that randomizes treatment and control groups to remove any type of bias in obtaining results. This type of research uses statistical methods to measure results. It is a strong form of research, but is limited to study design, sample size, and statistical methods used. To be stronger evidence, this study needs replication.

**Cohort Studies:** These studies follow participants across a point in time. They may measure a specific group or demographic and measure risk factors or other characteristics to match outcome. Because there is less control over variables, samples, and other criteria, this evidence is not as strong as other types of evidence.

**Outcome Research:** This measures the results of a specific population. There may be minimal control over treatment groups, and this may measure data after the fact. It cannot control for environmental factors that would skew the results.

**Case Controlled Studies:** This is an observational study that will look at two groups; one with, and one without a disease or condition. There is no control over environment or variables, and often this is done retrospectively. This is a lower grade of evidence because of all the variables and challenges in completing it.

**Expert Opinion:** Clinicians have experience, and they frequently will maintain their practice level. Expert opinion occurs within research when several people that specialize in the same topic or problem, as a community, decides an intervention to solve a problem. This is the weakest form of evidence.

## Section 13: Explore: Source Credibility

Several things can make a source credible. Here are a few examples:
- Source authors have strong clinical and/or educational credentials in the field of study.
- Source authors do not have a conflict of interest (personal or financial) with the study or the way the study is designed.
- The source is peer-reviewed.
- The publisher has no positive or negative financial connection to the outcome of the study.
- The source has a reasonable publication timeframe (reasonable time from date of study to date of publication).

## Section 15: Explore: Levels of Evidence

When research is collected for practice guidelines, a grading process is used to better categorize the strength of research. This is usually a four to five level rating scale that identifies the source type and the strength of evidence. This helps quantify for the reader how strong the research may be to help determine its translation to practice.

Good research should offer strong evidence. Most researchers will not accept popular media or social media as evidence except under specific conditions. For example:

Frieda is a verified user on Twitter who identifies herself as a registered nurse working at a Baywood hospital. She states in a tweet that the intensive care unit (ICU) at the hospital is at 70% capacity with influenza pneumonia patients. She could be considered a primary source of this statistic. Though this may be considered "level 5" on the chart below, the source needs significant verification before accepting her as a valid source.

Most of the time, nothing should be accepted as an appropriate source through social media unless it is linked to an appropriate website and is verifiable. **Social media have a high risk of disinformation; therefore, they are not a credible source.**

Here is an example of a rating system used to grade levels of evidence:

| Grade | Source Type |
| --- | --- |
| Level 1 | Multiple randomized controlled trials that are part of a systematic review, meta-analysis, or practice guidelines |
| Level 2 | Well designed controlled studies that may or may not be randomized |
| Level 3 | Case-controlled or cohort studies; Observational studies including retroactive chart reviews |
| Level 4 | Descriptive studies with qualitative or mixed-methodology components; Surveys with low sampling rate |
| Level 5 | Expert consensus or anecdotal evidence; Previous experience |
