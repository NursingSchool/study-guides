## Section 1: Introduction to Literature Review

Conducting a literature review is both an art and a science. Each step requires methodical attention to an established process with creativity when selecting and combining search terms. Whether beginning a research project or searching for evidence to improve client care, the nurse's ability to locate current evidence on the topic of interest is the key to success. This learning activity focuses on the literature search.

By completing the interactive learning activities, you will gain the knowledge and skills needed to:
- Describe the rationale for conducting a thorough review of the literature.
- Explain the components of a successful literature review.
- Select appropriate search terms when given a Patient/Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, Time (PICOT) question.
- Choose inclusion and exclusion criteria to narrow the scope of a literature search.
- Document the literature search.

For more information on this topic, review the How Do I…Learn to Search? topic in the Chamberlain University Library.

## Section 3: Explore: Literature Review: Purpose

A critical step of the research process is locating and evaluating all current evidence relevant to a specific clinical question or area of interest. Next, a researcher uses that evidence to discover information gaps that a researcher can fill. Finally, clinicians evaluate the evidence to determine if the evidence can improve client care.

Today's body of health-related research is vast and continues to grow daily. However, because quality research findings are available in various publications, locating the correct information is challenging. Therefore, nurses must master the knowledge and skills needed to search databases efficiently.

Before the researcher can write the literature review, they must do a literature search.

## Section 4: Explore: Steps of the Literature Review

A successful literature search requires an organized approach, pre-planning, and documentation of steps taken. The eight steps outlined below start after the nurse has identified a clinical problem and written the PICOT (short for patient/population, intervention, comparison, outcome, and time) question and ends when the findings have been summarized and synthesized (Houser, 2023).

**Transcript — Steps of the Literature Review:**
1. Identify the research problem and PICOT question
2. Select the resources to be searched
3. Set inclusion and exclusion criteria
4. Build the search strategy and conduct the search
5. Screen and organize the initial list
6. Retrieve full text and summarize applicable articles
7. Critically review the study quality and findings
8. Summarize and synthesize the findings

**Reference:** Houser, J. (2023). *Nursing research: Reading, using, and creating evidence* (5th ed.). Jones & Bartlett.

---
## Section 5: Explore: Step 1: The Problem and Question

Although most nurses have identified a problem and written the PICOT question before starting the literature search, how the question is stated determines how their search will progress. The search terms are derived from the research question based on the population of interest, intervention being evaluated, comparison intervention/control, the outcome being measured, and time differentiating the data being sought from related findings.

Review this example PICOT question, "In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?"

**Transcript (PICOT Example):**
- P (population) – In post-menopausal women
- I (intervention) – does hormone replacement therapy
- C (comparison) – compared to no hormone replacement therapy
- O (outcome) – impact the incidence of breast cancer?
- T (time) – [no time component]

## Section 6: Explore: Step 2: Select Resources

The resources used for the literature search vary from a single research article with a lengthy reference list to online databases with millions of published research findings. The bibliographic database indexes published works, making them readily searchable by keywords, subject, names of authors or journals, and a variety of other categories.

Nurse researchers and clinicians focus their searches on databases that contain nursing, medical, and allied health resources in their collections. One such database is CINAHL (Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature). CINAHL's content includes numerous active, indexed, peer-reviewed, and abstracted journals that can be searched in a matter of moments. There is a collection of open access (OA) journals as well.

Additional databases with research findings relevant to nursing include:
- MEDLINE, a free biomedical database available courtesy of the National Library of Medicine
- Cochrane Library, which houses systematic review and clinical trial information
- ProQuest, with a variety of resources focused on health and medicine, science and technology, and the social sciences
- Latin America & Iberia Database houses academic journals locally published in Latin American countries
- Google Scholar, an academic database curated by Google

**Note:** Limiting the number of databases searched can result in publication bias resulting in loss of findings, such as negative results, that influence how the literature review impacts the research study or nursing practice.

To search the Chamberlain University library databases, visit the library homepage and look for Database Shortcuts, which includes a link to the full database holdings.

## Section 7: Explore: Step 3: Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

To get the most useful studies, the researcher must consider what should be included or excluded from the search. While all criteria will have inclusion terms, not all will have exclusion terms.

Common criteria include:
- Study design
- Year of publication
- Population of interest
- Interventions
- Outcomes

Inclusion criteria should be as specific as possible. If the researcher wants to know the effects of hormone replacement therapy on the increased incidence of breast cancer, omitting the word "increased" will impact the search.

With the PICOT question, "In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer," the criteria might look like this:

| | Inclusion Criteria | Exclusion Criteria |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Study design | Quantitative | Qualitative |
| Year of publication | Within the last five years | None |
| Population of interest | Post-menopausal women | None |
| Interventions | Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) | No HRT, mastectomy, lumpectomy |
| Outcomes | Increased incidence of breast cancer | None |

## Section 9: Explore: Step 4a: Search Strategy

Before entering a search term, the nurse should create a search plan or strategy. The time spent creating a search strategy will improve the effectiveness of the literature search by determining the major concepts of the PICOT question, which terms will be searched for each concept, how the terms will be combined, and which limits will be set. See how a research question becomes a search strategy below.

Question: "In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?"

**Transcript (PICOT Example):**
- P (population) – In post-menopausal women
- I (intervention) – does hormone replacement therapy
- C (comparison) – compared to no hormone replacement therapy
- O (outcome) – impact the incidence of breast cancer?
- T (time) – [no time component]

### Tab: Search Concepts

Search concepts are the major themes or focuses of the research question. Typically, these broad terms will yield many results when searched and many may not align with your interest. The concepts are often the exact words and phrases used in the PICOT question.

**Literature Search Strategy Template**

Question: In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?

Concepts: post-menopausal women | hormone replacement therapy | breast cancer

### Tab: Search Terms

Search terms are the exact words or phrases used when searching. Terms may include variables in the research question, characteristics of the target population, or the theoretical framework of the research problem. Searching for similar terms helps avoid missing research studies that address the research question using slightly different terms.

**Literature Search Strategy Template**

Question: In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?

Concepts: post-menopausal women | hormone replacement therapy | breast cancer

Terms: post-menopausal women / older women | hormone replacement therapy / HRT | breast cancer / cancer

### Tab: Connectors

Boolean operators, such as "AND," "OR," and "NOT," are considered connector words and will make the database search for your keywords in a specific way. Entering them in all capital letters lets the database know they are connectors, not search terms.

Boolean operators impact the search in the following ways:
- AND will reduce your results and only show sources that contain both keywords it connects.
- OR will expand your results and show sources that have either keyword or both keywords.
- NOT will reduce your results by excluding sources that contain the keyword to its right.

Example: Adding the connector "AND" in between the keywords picked out from your assignment will make the database search for sources that contain both keywords. Common mistakes with Boolean operators include:

**Placing the Connecting Word in the Wrong Place:**
- Correct: post-menopausal women **AND** hormone replacement therapy **AND** breast cancer
- Incorrect: post-menopausal women **OR** hormone replacement therapy **AND** breast cancer

**Using Lower Case:**
- Correct: post-menopausal women **AND** hormone replacement therapy **AND** breast cancer
- Incorrect: post-menopausal women **and** hormone replacement therapy **and** breast cancer

**Literature Search Strategy Template**

Question: In post-menopausal women, does hormone replacement therapy compared to no hormone replacement therapy impact the incidence of breast cancer?

Concepts: post-menopausal women | hormone replacement therapy | breast cancer

Terms: post-menopausal women / older women | hormone replacement therapy / HRT | breast cancer / cancer

Connectors: post-menopausal women AND hormone replacement therapy AND breast cancer

## Section 11: Explore: Step 4b: Conduct the Search

Most databases provide filters and symbols that help narrow down or change the search altogether without changing the search terms and connectors. It is best to apply no more than one to two limiters initially. If the search yields too many articles, limiters can be adjusted.

### Tab: Search Filters

Search filters limit the search results based on parameters set by the researcher and by skipping articles that will not be useful.
- Date Range – limits the search to findings published within the set dates. Used to locate the most current research, typically within the last five years.
- Language – limits the search findings to those published in the language specified.
- Peer-Review – qualified professionals have evaluated these articles to determine the accuracy of the information and the methods used. Peer reviews increase the credibility of the research article.
- Full text only – setting the search to only articles that are available in full text only may limit the return and introduce bias to the review.

### Tab: Quotation Marks

Adding quotation marks to a phrase of two or more words lets the database search for those words together. For example, searching for "post-menopausal women" will prevent articles focused on all women from being included in the results.

### Tab: Parentheses

Grouping similar search terms within parentheses instructs the database to search for them as a single topic. For example, grouping (hormone replacement therapy OR HRT) tells the database to look for either term.

### Tab: Truncation

Truncation is used to shorten a search term with an asterisk (*) allowing the database to locate all word forms and is useful when search terms include different forms of the same word. For example, using the search term children may exclude relevant articles. By truncating it to child*, the database will search for child, children, childhood, and other words that start with child.

**Worked example — Limits:**

| | |
| --- | --- |
| Date range | 2016-2021 |
| Language | English |
| Peer reviewed | Yes, or checked |
| Quotation marks | "post-menopausal women"; "hormone replacement therapy" |
| Parentheses | ("hormone replacement therapy" OR HRT) |
| Truncation | None |

## Section 14: Explore: Step 5: Screen and Organize

The first search usually returns numerous results, many of which are not related to the research question. To avoid downloading and reading off-topic articles, use the inclusion and exclusion criteria set earlier in the planning process.

| | Inclusion Criteria | Exclusion Criteria |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Study design | Quantitative | Qualitative |
| Year of publication | Within the last five years | None |
| Population of interest | Post-menopausal women | None |
| Interventions | Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) | No HRT, mastectomy, lumpectomy |
| Outcomes | Increased incidence of breast cancer | None |

To refine the number of results after the first search, consider changing one or two limiters slightly by changing the range of dates or marking that only peer-reviewed publications should be used. Changing more than one or two limiters at a time may result in a drastic reduction of relevant articles.

Once the number of studies is manageable, save the search if that option is available. Then read the title of the article, looking for the inclusion and exclusion criteria. If the title is not helpful, read the abstract or skim the article. Finally, delete articles with exclusions from the search results list.

## Section 15: Explore: Step 6: Retrieve and Summarize

The searcher should use full-text versions of articles for the literature review and most articles in the Chamberlain University Library are available as full-text. However, for relevant articles not available as full-text, the searcher can request an interlibrary loan. This request is as simple as completing a form so the librarian can get access to the article for you to review.

Organizing and summarizing the texts in the literature review is like taking notes during a lecture, allowing the researcher to have essential information available in a format that can be sorted and referenced. For example, many researchers include the following details in summaries:
- Full APA citation (for creating the reference list)
- Purpose of the study
- Research design
- Sample size
- Methodology and measurements
- Treatment or treatments
- Results
- Findings
- Discussion
- Implications for practice

## Section 17: Explore: Step 7: Critically Review

Once the relevant articles are identified and obtained, the next step is to evaluate the author's quality of design and methodology. The researcher should note the answers to these questions and other information gathered during the evaluation of that article.

**Transcript:** All articles should be evaluated using the same criteria, often in the form of questions, like:
- Is the study relevant?
- How do the findings increase the body of knowledge?
- Is bias present?
- Is the conclusion aligned with the results?
- How does this study contribute to client care?

---
## Section 19: Explore: Step 8: Summarize and Synthesize

The final step of the literature review is to write a summary that presents the studies' key findings logically. Rather than reviewing each study, the researcher should organize the information to show the relationships or differences between the articles. The summary should show the significance of the reviewed research and link it to the research question, setting the stage for further study or implementation into practice.

At this time, the researcher is ready to design their study and the clinician is ready to disseminate their findings to stakeholders in the organization.
