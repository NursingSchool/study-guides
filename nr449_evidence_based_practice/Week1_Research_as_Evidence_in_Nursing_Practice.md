## Section 1: Introduction to Research in Nursing Practice

The definition of evidence-based nursing is a rigorous methodology in which all the research data for a particular problem or issue is analyzed together, then combined with patients' values and preferences, as well as professional expertise. This professional expertise includes a nurse's accumulated experience, education, and skills.

This concept will review how nurses can participate in the research process.

## Section 4: Explore: What is Research?

The first leg of evidence-based practice (EBP) focuses on finding the best scientific evidence: research. What exactly is research?

There are many definitions in the literature and a movement to increase nursing research volume is in progress. It involves collecting, organizing, and analyzing information to advance understanding of a topic or issue.

- Research has the broad intent of improving nursing practice. We cannot rely on doing things the same way year after year without ensuring there is not a better way or treatment.
- Research benefits large groups of people. Often findings support treatment options for certain genders or specific age groups.
- Participants are fully informed about study risks and sign consent. Research studies are regulated and must follow specific protocols.
- Research results are peer-reviewed and can be replicated. Additional researchers and colleagues ensure the study's validity before it is shared with the public as recommended changes.

In order to apply research into nursing practice, it must meet the patients' needs, be carefully assessed for credibility, and have external validity (the ability to generalize the findings from a research study to other groups of people, locations, and situations).

## Section 6: Explore: Nursing Research - Problem Identification

Applying nursing research to practice starts with problem identification.

The inspiration for identifying a research problem is all around us. As we reflect on our practice, we find ourselves asking questions that lead us to ideas for researchable problems:

- Why are we doing this?
- Should we be doing this?
- How could we do this better?

Nursing is a practice profession and nurses can identify problems from various sources in patient care, education, and leadership. Research helps us solve practice problems by determining the best answer. The textbook describes sources of researchable nursing problems in detail. As a baccalaureate-prepared nurse, research-based evidence is expected to be utilized for solving practice problems.

Through research, evidence, and synthesis of findings into practice, we now have evidence-based practice (EBP) protocols and guidelines. Below are a few examples of EBP:

- Catheterization Associated Urinary Tract Infection (CAUTI) protocols
- Intramuscular (IM) injections
- Medication errors

## Section 9: Explore: Nursing Research - Credibility

Once the nurse has identified the need for change in practice, the next step is to gather research evidence to be included in the review. When selecting articles to review, it is important to negate any possible bias, which can be avoided by a careful appraisal of the literature. The appraisal process begins by determining whether the journal, authors, and publication process are credible.

Credibility can be determined by the following:

### Tab: Is the source credible?

Researchers must disclose their professional degrees and what type of experience they have. They are often considered subject matter experts.

### Tab: Is there a conflict of interest or bias?

Reviewers must confirm that the researcher has nothing to gain, such as financial compensation or other benefits. Additionally, confirm that the editorial board that regulates and reviews the studies is unbiased and does not have any financial ties to the study.

### Tab: Has the research undergone a blind peer review?

A blind peer review is a neutral party who is not connected with the authors of the study who could complete the review.

### Tab: Has the study been published within a reasonable time frame?

Is the information still relevant? Evidence should be conducted and published within the last 3-5 years.

### Tab: Has the study been replicated?

If a researcher can replicate a study's results, it is more likely that those results can be generalized to the larger population. It increases its validity.

## Section 10: Explore: Nursing Research - Level of Evidence

Research to be included in the review can be graded based on the strength of the evidence. There are several rating scales that can be used to evaluate the strength of the research. One rating system includes the levels of evidence, with each level corresponding with the type of study conducted.

- Level I = multiple randomized control trials (RCT), large sample sizes and large effect sizes, that support a specific treatment
- Level II = randomized or non-randomized control studies that support a specific treatment
- Level III = evidence from case studies, studies of intact groups, non-randomized
- Level IV = chart reviews, reviews of other studies, expert opinions

| Strength of the Evidence | Strength of the Resulting Recommendation for Practice |
| --- | --- |
| RCTs or Reports of Multiple RCTs | Strong recommendation to use or not use the research findings |
| RCTs with limitations | Recommend to use or not use the research findings |
| Observations, Case Controls, Cohort Designs | Recommend to use or not use the research findings |
| Opinions, Case Studies | Action is optional |
