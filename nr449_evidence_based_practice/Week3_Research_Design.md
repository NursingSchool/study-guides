## Section 1: Introduction to Research Design

Understanding research design is an important step for the nurse researcher. The research design is a framework for the research study. The research design is selected to produce data that answers a credible research question.

---

## Section 3: Explore: Design Selection

Nurse researchers should have a well-thought-out research question before determining the study design. A research question is matched with a specific design. Review the factors that influence design selection.

### Interactive Transcript: Factors influencing design selection

**Variables:** Protecting participants is an important ethical role of the nurse researcher. Manipulating the research environment or putting research participants in danger are reasons not to implement a research design.

**Sampling and measurements:** Is the sampling instrument valid and reliable? Is the population available during the research time frame?

**Researcher:** What are the strengths and weaknesses of the researcher which may produce limitations for the study?

**Time frame:** The researcher must be aware of the length of time they require from start to finish of the research project.

**Amount of control:** Studies such as vaccine trials will need high levels of structure to control threats to validity.

**Audience:** The audience of the research results may expect specific types of research. It is important to understand the audience when choosing research designs.

**Resources:** What resources are available to the researcher? This can range from money, use of a copier for research surveys, and even participant pools for the researcher to utilize as participants.

## Section 4: Explore: Design Decisions

All nurse researchers should understand the decisions that go into choosing a research design. There are always tradeoffs as no research design is without flaws. The nurse researcher must understand the flaws of each type of design and the impact on research credibility. The nurse should ensure that the chosen design is rigorous and systematic.

### Infographic Transcript: The Three Phases of Decision Making

1. **Identify assumptions:** What assumptions are made about the knowledge to be gained from the study? This will help the researcher understand if a quantitative, qualitative, or a mixed-method design is required.
2. **Select a design:** Which design is appropriate for the purpose of the study?
3. **Implementation:** The researcher must develop detailed plans of how they will implement the study.

## Section 5: Explore: Design Approach

When a researcher examines the type of research design to use, they must understand how the design type will fit their research question. This is often based on the type of data and measurement they are looking to analyze.

### Tab: Quantitative Design

A quantitative design measures data and analyzes numbers related to the research question. For example, the researcher may seek to measure the blood pressure of clients who have had a myocardial infarction (MI) and completed cardiac rehabilitation versus clients who have had a MI and not done any rehabilitation.

### Tab: Qualitative Design

A qualitative design seeks to understand the lived experiences of research participants or the meaning of a phenomenon. For example, the researcher may be seeking to understand the lived experiences of nursing students who also work 20 hours or more a week.

### Tab: Mixed-Method Design

A mixed-method design is used when both qualitative and quantitative data are available. For example, the researcher may include the lived experiences of MI clients in the quantitative study. Adding qualitative data in addition to the quantitative data makes the design method a mixed-method design.

The aim of the study depends on whether the study is exploratory or confirmatory.

- Exploratory studies are usually qualitative or mixed-method and are descriptive, used to learn more and describe a phenomenon.
- Confirmatory studies are based on testing a relationship between variables and are used to determine the relationship between the variables or examine causality.

## Section 6: Explore: Variables

The concept that will be studied must be measurable. This measurable characteristic is called a variable, and can be a characteristic, event, or response to a research question. Variables are not used in qualitative research, but are the basis of quantitative research. Review each type of variable below.

### Tab: Descriptive Variables

Descriptive or measurable characteristics are not influenced by the study or researcher. Examples include:

- Participant information, such as age or gender
- Participant specific characteristics, such as height
- Measurable characteristics, such as blood pressure
- Attitudinal data, such as client satisfaction

For example, research that explores the influence of exposure to blue light on hours of sleep may not be influenced by descriptive variables, such as the gender of the participant.

### Tab: Research Variables

Research variables are introduced into a study to measure an expected effect. They may be independent or dependent.

- An independent variable is the cause in cause and effect. It is added to a study to measure an effect.
- A dependent variable is the effect in cause and effect. It is the outcome.

An example of a design with independent and dependent variables is research that explores the influence of exposure to blue light on hours of sleep. The independent variable is the exposure to blue light and the dependent variable is the hours of sleep.

### Tab: Extraneous Variables

Extraneous variables are not part of the planned research or experiment, but are factors that influence the outcome. These may confuse the interpretation of the research outcome.

For example, in research that explores the influence of exposure to blue light on hours of sleep, an extraneous variable could include blue light exposure outside of the research framework. This may not be part of the planned experiment and may influence outcomes of the research.

## Section 7: Explore: Descriptive Study Designs

Nurse researchers should understand study designs and how they influence research. Review the following types of descriptive study designs to increase your understanding of the variety of design choices in nursing research.

### Tab: Survey Design

Survey design uses a single point in time or a single sample characteristic. For example, when diagnosed with cancer, what do teen participants describe as their top information need? The single point in time is at diagnosis.

### Tab: Cross-Sectional Design

Cross-sectional design, single point in time, sample characteristics differ on a key characteristic. For example, when diagnosed with cancer, what do participants (aged 5-50 years old) describe as their top information need? The key characteristic difference is the age of the participants. The single point in time is at diagnosis.

### Tab: Longitudinal Study

Longitudinal study data is collected at select points over time to describe changes in characteristics or events. For example, a study of teen cancer clients at diagnosis, first treatment, and 6-month treatment to see what participants describe as their top information need.

### Tab: Case Study

Case study is a collection of detailed information using varied techniques to explore a single individual, program, event, or action. For example, a case study using participants from the Chamberlain University Tinley Park campus nursing program graduating class to explore what they perceive as their priority needs after graduation.

### Tab: Single Subject Design

Single subject design interventions are studied on individual's responses, either at baseline or ongoing. For example, a researcher does a repeated review of a small participant group's behavior over time regarding adherence to their blood pressure medication.

### Tab: Phenomenology

Phenomenology is the lived experience of a phenomenon among participants who have experienced the phenomenon. For example, the researcher may use a phenomenological study of nursing students who work 20 hours or more a week to investigate the meaning of their lived experiences as nursing students.

### Tab: Ethnography

Ethnography is a cultural study in which the researcher immerses themselves in the culture over a period of time. For example, a researcher may live within a tribe for years to understand their view of the environment.

## Section 8: Explore: Relationship Study Designs

Review the following types of relationship study designs to increase your understanding of the variety of design choices in nursing research.

### Tab: Correlation

A correlation is a relationship between two variables in a single population or one variable in two populations. For example, researching the relationship between maternal age and anxiety in mothers who participate in planned caesarean sections. The two variables are age and anxiety in a population of pregnant mothers with planned c-sections.

### Tab: Predictive Study

A predictive study relationship is between a predictor variable and an outcome variable. For example, research on medication adherence related to the age of the participant. The predictor variable is the participant's age and the outcome variable is medication adherence.

### Tab: Grounded Theory

Grounded theory is the development of a theory of process, action, or interaction based on analysis of participants' words. For example, nursing research that enables nurses to see patterns of health in groups, such as teen participants and medication adherence. This research helps guide practice in teaching teens about medication adherence.

### Tab: Test of Model Fit

Test of model fit uses a preconceived model to test theories of causal relationships between variables. For example, a researcher may study whether the introduction of a support group affects the coping skills of teens diagnosed with anxiety.

## Section 12: Explore: Causality

Causality study design aims to examine the effectiveness of nursing interventions. Causality must be established and quantified.

### Tab: Causality Study Criteria

There are three criteria that must be considered in a causality study.

- **Temporality:** The time sequence between the independent and dependent variables must support causation.
- **Influence:** The statistical soundness of the effect that the independent variable has on the dependent variable.
- **Specificity:** The elimination or control of other explanations for the relationship between the dependent and independent variables.

### Tab: Research Designs Exploring Causality

Review the research designs that explore causality:

- **Experimental design:** Introduces an intervention to the treatment group and compares the outcome to the group that has not had intervention (control group); subjects are randomly assigned to either group.
- **Quasi-experimental design:** Introduces a treatment to a group. There is no random assignment or control group used.
- **Causal-comparative design/case control:** Groups are matched by independent variables; nonexperimental study groups are selected by characteristics of interest and dependent variables are analyzed for differences.
- **Time series analysis:** Measures baseline, implements treatment, collects data about outcome over time at specified dates or periods of time.

## Section 14: Explore: Evaluating Evidence-Based Research Articles

The nurse researcher must be able to evaluate research articles to gain a better understanding of their research design and to see research that has been done already about their research question. The following should be considered when evaluating a research article.

### Interactive Transcript

**Identify the design and the standard language used to describe the design choice.**

- The research may describe the steps used within a phenomenological study of nursing students who work 20 hours or more a week to investigate the meaning of their lived experiences as nursing students. The reader would identify that a phenomenological descriptive design was used and clarify that standard research language was used in the study.

**The rationale is provided for the design choice.**

- The reader would identify the rationale the researcher had in the design, which is to gain a better understanding of the lived experiences of nursing students who also work while in school.

**The design selected can be clearly identified and linked to the research question.**

- The reader should be able to identify the design used and this design should be a phenomenological design to provide the best understanding of the nursing students' lived experiences.

**Design variables are identified and defined.**

- Are the students also parents? Do they pay their own rent and bills? How old are the students? These are all variables that may influence outcomes.

**The study can be replicated by others.**

- Is the study explained in a way that others can reproduce the study?

In qualitative design, the researcher has explained decisions made as the design has emerged. Qualitative design produces themes as the researcher examines feedback from participants. The reader should be able to understand decisions that have been made as the researcher moves through the research process.
