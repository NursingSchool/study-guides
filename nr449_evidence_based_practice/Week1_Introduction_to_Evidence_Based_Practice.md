## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Research and Evidence-Based Practice

Evidence-based practice (EBP) is the process nurses and other clinicians use to inform client care and delivery decisions. EBP is the foundation for safe and effective healthcare, and ensures that evidence is the basis for practice decisions rather than eloquence or tradition. In addition, there is a direct connection between research and evidence-based practice.

This concept reviews the integration of evidence-based practice and research into one's daily work while considering the newest trends and research.

## Section 3: Explore: Nursing Research & Its Evolution

The word *research* includes the stem *search*, which means "to seek", and the prefix "re-", which means "again." Therefore, research signifies a replication of a previous search.

Research is a systematic process of inquiry that uses rigorous guidelines to produce unbiased, trustworthy answers to questions about nursing practice.

**Timeline: Nursing Research Evolution**

**1854-1856:** When you think about nursing research, the story starts with Florence Nightingale. Florence was a nurse during the Crimean War, where she showed her calling and devotion as a nurse. In two years (1854-1856), Nightingale's critical thinking and problem analyses; implementation of interventions and positive outcomes; advancement of nursing practice based on evidence; detailed documentation and statistical analysis; and tenacious political advocacy provided the foundation for how to reform healthcare systems.

**1950s:** Fast forward about one hundred years, and nurses are more fully involved in research.

**1970s-1980s:** Publication opportunities increased, now spreading the information to a larger group of providers.

**1986:** The National Center for Research for Nursing (NCRN) opened as part of the National Institutes of Health (NIH).

**1993:** The NCRN became an institute to support clinical and basic research with a new name, the National Institute of Nursing Research (NINR).

**Client Management Tools:** Evidence can be the basis for order sets and algorithms, encourages consistent practice, and reduces variability.

## Section 7: Explore: Quality Improvement

Research must be broad and neutral so it can be published and generalized to impact a larger group of clients after peer review. Research subjects must be informed of their participation and provide informed consent. In a healthcare organization, research provides information that helps quality improvement (QI) projects generate more evidence to support care. Quality improvement is different from the research process because quality improvement projects are beneficial to specific organizations. Quality improvement is an internal process where data and evidence help direct practice changes for a specific organization.

For example, if a nursing unit has a high medication error rate, nurses can look at what research recommends to reduce errors and compare it to current practices. The facility may modify the medication room setup, documentation, or technology (computers/pyxis/medication storage) that is used. Staff should then receive education and training on practice changes.

**The Johns Hopkins Nursing Evidence-based Practice Model**

Inquiry > Practice Question, Evidence, Translation > Best Practices > Practice Improvements (Clinical, Learning, Operational)

## Section 9: Explore: Vocabulary and Current Goals of Research

Let's explore some common vocabulary used in research. Select each term below for more information.

### Tab: Peer Review

Practice experts review and evaluate the quality of the research. Peer review is how nurses become published or present at nursing conferences so that other nurses can learn about the new findings.

### Tab: Blinded Review

The experts evaluating the research are blinded to or do not see the researcher's name to prevent bias.

### Tab: Replication

When a repeated study has similar results as a prior study, nurses feel more confident in the findings and possibly consider making practice changes based on the evidence.

### Tab: Magnet Status

Organizations who contribute to new knowledge, use evidence-based practice, and strive for nursing excellence can obtain a Magnet designation. Achieving Magnet status is a priority for many organizations and includes involvement by bedside nurses.

**Some current goals of nursing research include:**

- Preparing the next generation of nurses and researchers
- Advancing practice
- Improving health promotion
- Highlighting gaps in the science of nursing

## Section 10: Explore: Nursing's Role in Research and Barriers

Most nurses, no matter their education level, hold positive views about the value of evidence-based practice (EBP).

**Nursing Education and Roles in Research:**

| Education Level | Typical Roles in Research |
| --- | --- |
| Baccalaureate | Understand basics of evidence-based practice; Participate on research teams; Application of evidence |
| Master's | Use evidence to create evidence-based practice (EBP) guidelines; Create and lead teams to continually discuss EBP; May initiate research |
| Practice-Focused Doctorates | Analyze data and tailor it to specific groups of people; Use advanced leadership knowledge to change practice; Partner with research scientists |
| Research-Focused Doctorates | Conduct research independently; Seek monetary support/grants to finance research; Invite others to participate in research studies |
| Post-Doctoral Programs | Pursue additional training in the research process; Teach the research process to others |

Occasionally, nurses face barriers when participating in research. Some examples, along with possible solutions to overcome those barriers, are listed in the table below.

| Barrier | Solution |
| --- | --- |
| Not enough time to keep up with evidence review given workload | Sign up for journal emails and join professional organizations. |
| Lack of adequate training and educational support for appraisal of evidence | Join a quality improvement committee and/or journal club. |
| Inability to understand statistical and research terminology | Join a journal club. |
| Inadequate organizational and leadership support | Attend committees to collaborate with clinical nurse specialists and staff educators, which may discuss new evidence. |
| Lack of access to databases and search strategies | Reach out to staff educators or local library for assistance. This can often be done remotely. |

## Section 12: Explore: Sources of Knowledge and Credibility

Nurses must ensure that they are accessing credible information as there are many sources that are not evidence-based. Current practice may be based upon the components listed in the CASTLE infographic below.

Just because nurses have always done something a certain way is not enough. Nurses need to verify that practice is supported by the most recent research and recommendations. Client safety should always be at the core of clinical practice decisions.

**CASTLE:**

- **C**ustoms and tradition: "We've always done it that way." This may not be best for the client.
- **A**ssembled information (quality improvement data): Data collected from client charts.
- **S**cientific research: The most objective source of nursing knowledge; research papers and articles.
- **T**rial and error: "If it works, we'll use it." This may not always be the safest or best practice.
- **L**ogical reasoning (inductive and deductive): This is basing a decision on current clinical signs.
- **E**xperts or authorities: Who conducted the research or wrote the article? A nurse or other provider should be the author versus lay people without nursing education and experience.
