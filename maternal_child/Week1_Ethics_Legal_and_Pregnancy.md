## Section 1: Introduction to Ethical and Legal Issues During Pregnancy

Some pregnancies are accompanied by additional ethical and legal issues for the mom, baby, or even both. Women may have other health issues, be involved in an abusive relationship, or be victims of human trafficking. Nurses play a key role in the assessment and treatment of these clients. To find solutions, nurses and other healthcare personnel should apply ethical theories and principles to determine the burdens and benefits of any course of action.

This concept will review some of the ethical and legal issues that occur in the maternal-child population and the role of the nurse.

**Core Values:**
- Ethics
- Integrity
- Respect
- Honesty

## Section 3: Explore: Ethical Models

**Vignette — Kate:**
Kate is a 28-year-old client, unmarried, and 6 weeks pregnant. She was the victim of a rape that occurred when she was leaving work late one evening. She is a marketing executive with a college degree who lives alone not far from her job. Kate comes from a close-knit group of family and friends, with whom she spends time each week.

Kate has an appointment at her obstetrician's office later today and plans on requesting an abortion as she cannot imagine carrying the baby for nine months, even if it means placing the baby for adoption. She does not want to keep the baby, knowing that the baby was conceived through sexual assault.

For many, this is an ethical situation that may cause some nurses to question their beliefs and the rights of the client.

### Tab: Deontological Model

The deontologic model determines what is right by applying ethical principles and moral rules. The solution does not vary according to individual situations.

For some religions, killing is considered a sin, so abortion would fall under those rules. The fact that the child was conceived by rape does not matter in this model.

### Tab: Utilitarian Model

The utilitarian model approaches ethical dilemmas by analyzing the benefits and burdens of any course of action to find a solution that will result in the greatest amount of good. Appropriate actions may vary with the situation when using the utilitarian model.

In this model, the fact that Kate was raped is taken into consideration. Some questions may include:
- How will Kate handle this pregnancy emotionally and physically?
- Could Kate care for a baby that was a result of rape?
- Who is the father? Will there be any genetic concerns?

### Tab: Human Rights Model

The human rights model is the belief that each person has human rights and this is the basis for making ethical decisions.

In this model, the fetus has a right to live even if the mother does not feel that she could or should continue with the pregnancy.

### Tab: American Nurses Association (ANA) Code of Ethics (2015)

The Code of Ethics for Nurses With Interpretive Statements (American Nurses Association, 2015) was developed as a guide for carrying out nursing responsibilities in a manner consistent with quality of nursing care and the ethical obligations of the profession.

- With the support of their work organizations, colleagues, and policies, nurses strive to achieve the best patient outcomes.
- Some provisions include compassion, respect, scholarly inquiry, collaboration, and social justice, which nurses strive to uphold.

**Remember!** When a nurse speaks with a rape victim, the most helpful thing the nurse can do is listen and let the survivor talk. A victim who feels understood may no longer feel alone and more in control of the situation. It is critical to avoid placing blame on the victim. Once the nurse has established a rapport with the victim and established trust by listening non-judgmentally, the nurse will be better poised to explain procedures and follow-up care.

**Reference:** American Nurses Association. (2015). *Code of ethics for nurses with interpretive statements*. https://www.nursingworld.org/coe-view-only

## Section 5: Explore: Malpractice

**Vignette — Lauren:**
Lauren, 37 weeks pregnant, presents to the labor and delivery unit with uterine contractions every 2 minutes. This is the second baby for Lauren and her partner, Pat. Lauren had a quick labor with her first child three years ago. Lauren does not have any risk factors and is 4 cm dilated on admission. The nurse contacts the healthcare provider to make them aware of Lauren's arrival and her history of short labor.

After less than an hour of labor, Lauren cries out, stating that the uterine contractions won't stop and that there is blood between her legs. The nurse feels a rigid belly and urgently contacts the provider again. The healthcare provider shares that he cannot get there quickly as he is about 90 minutes away and asks the nurse to keep the client comfortable. He did not set up a colleague to be his back-up, as he did not anticipate any women going into labor. Lauren needs to be delivered as quickly as possible as both she and her baby are in danger of serious harm.

Negligence is the failure to perform as a reasonable or prudent person of similar background would act in a similar situation. Negligence may consist of doing something that should not be done or failing to do something that should be done. Malpractice is negligence by professionals, such as nurses and healthcare providers, in the performance of their duties. (Murray et al., 2019, p. 39)

Many reasons exist for perinatal malpractice claims. Complications are usually unexpected because parents view pregnancy and birth as normal. However, emergencies can happen in a matter of seconds. In this case, the healthcare provider should have been within 30 minutes of the hospital or have a colleague who can cover in their absence.

Healthcare agencies and individual nurses should work together to prevent malpractice claims. Nurses are responsible and accountable for their own actions. Therefore, they should be aware of the limits of their knowledge and scope of practice, and they should practice within those limits. Documentation of nursing care can defend and support a nurse and demonstrate that prudent care was given to the client. Nurses should also complete ongoing education and skills practice to maintain their level of expertise. Staff education records may be reviewed as part of a lawsuit.

**Reference:** Murray, S., McKinney, E., Holub, K, & Jones, R. (2019). *Maternal-newborn and women's health nursing.* Elsevier

**Elements of Negligence:**

### Tab: Duty

The nurse has a duty to act or give care to the client. It must be part of the nurse's responsibility. Nurses who provide care to pregnant mothers have a duty to keep their clients safe. It is their role.

### Tab: Breach of Duty

A violation of that duty must occur. The nurse fails to conform to established standards in performing that duty. The healthcare provider asks the nurse to keep the client comfortable and states that he will be there in 90 minutes. The nurse cannot wait for the doctor and must take action (initiate chain of command) or it will be a breach of duty.

### Tab: Damage

Actual injury or harm to the client as a result of the nurse's breach of duty must occur. If the baby is not delivered urgently, the baby and mother could be harmed by the blood loss and potential lack of oxygen.

### Tab: Proximate Cause

The nurse's breach of duty must be proved to be the cause of harm to the client. If this case results in a lawsuit, the nurse's actions will be reviewed and the legal system will decide if there is proximate cause.

## Section 6: Ethical Principles

View the scenario below and learn how each ethical principle is applied to the context.

**Vignette — Kelly:**
Kelly is a 34-year-old pregnant client who is seen at the office for a 20-week ultrasound after an abnormal alpha-fetoprotein test. Kelly is married to Jay and they have four boys who range from 2 to 12 years old. They were trying again to see if they could have a girl, and a few weeks earlier they found out that they were indeed pregnant with one. They have already picked out a name and the other kids talk to "Baby Gabby" each day.

The doctor confirms that Gabby has significant anomalies. The baby could be compatible with life but would probably not walk or talk. There is also an issue with the kidneys, which could require dialysis or a transplant at some point. Delivery would require a cesarean section. They also discover that Kelly has a placenta accreta that causes a high risk for hemorrhage after delivery and potential risks to the uterus in general.

Ethical principles are important for solving ethical dilemmas. These principles guide decision making but, in some situations, the application of one principle conflicts with another. In such cases, one principle may outweigh another in importance.

**Autonomy** — Clients have the right to self-determination. This includes the right to respect, privacy, and information necessary to make decisions based on personal values and beliefs.

Example: Kelly knows that she will need to share her wishes with her healthcare providers.

**Beneficence** — Make a decision that produces the greatest good or the least harm.

Example: Kelly has to think about the long-term quality of life for Gabby. Does living a life with pain and suffering outweigh the decision to end the pregnancy?

**Nonmaleficence** — Avoid risking or causing harm to others.

Example: The health of Kelly has to be taken into consideration as well. If the pregnancy is maintained, Kelly's life could be in danger. With four boys at home, can she risk her own life?

**Justice** — All people should be treated equally and fairly regardless of disease or social or economic status.

Example: The healthcare team must provide the same care for Kelly as they would for any other client. Kelly's married status or financial situation should not impact decision making.

**Fidelity** — Keep promises, and do not make promises that cannot be kept.

Example: When explaining another high risk to Kelly and Jay, the healthcare provider shows the statistics and tells the couple that they won't be able to promise any type of outcome. It's difficult to predict exactly how the delivery will go or the health of Gabby and Kelly until that time. They can anticipate and prepare, but there are many unknowns.

**Veracity** — Tell the truth.

Example: Although the information was devastating to Kelly and Jay, they appreciated hearing all of the facts and that their healthcare providers told them the reality of what could potentially happen with the pregnancy and delivery.

**Confidentiality** — Keep information private.

Example: The next day, a team of healthcare providers are meeting about the case and sharing it with the ethics committee. One provider is in a crowded elevator when a call comes in regarding the case. He asks to call them back and not discuss the client's case in front of strangers.

**Accountability** — Accept responsibility for actions as a healthcare professional.

Example: The nurse and the healthcare provider consistently reflect on their actions and if they should do anything different in the future, as they are responsible for each interaction and the care of their clients.

## Section 8: Explore: Human Trafficking

**Vignette — Lisa:**
Lisa, a 17-year-old pregnant woman, presents to the labor and delivery unit. She avoids eye contact and does not answer any questions. She keeps looking over at the male who accompanied her, and he controls the entire conversation. Because she did not receive prenatal care, all labs were drawn on admission. Lisa is positive for chlamydia and has a hemoglobin of 8.2 g/dL.

More than one in four women become pregnant while trafficked, indicating that maternity services offer an important contact point for identification and care. Given the prevalence of sexual exploitation and abuse among trafficking survivors, clinicians should ensure antenatal care and screening for sexually transmitted infections can be readily accessed by women.

Clinicians require specialized training alongside designated pathways and protocols with clear referral options to ensure confidential maternity care is tailored to each woman's needs. Always look for signs of human trafficking when providing care for women.

The National Human Trafficking Hotline is 1-888-373-7888.

**Signs of human trafficking that nurses should look for:**
- false identification
- someone else speaking for the client
- client avoids eye contact
- late prenatal care
- bruises in various stages
- partner who won't leave the client's bedside
- sexually transmitted infections

## Section 10: Explore: Intimate Partner Violence

**Vignette — Melanie:**
Melanie, 28 weeks pregnant, presents to the obstetrical unit complaining of vague pain, increased urinary frequency, and pain with urination. The nurse asks Melanie to collect a clean-catch urine sample so they can check for infection. Melanie's partner insists on being in the bathroom and steps inside the door with her.

The nurse waits for Melanie to come out and observes both partners' body language as Melanie returns to the triage bed. Melanie is on edge and trembling. While placing the fetal monitors, the nurse notices various stages of bruising on Melanie's lower abdomen and groin area. The partner starts answering all of the questions and will not leave the bedside. Melanie states that it is probably better if they go home as she is already feeling better.

Intimate partner violence remains a serious and frequent aspect of women's intimate relationships, and women and children suffer health consequences as a result. Intimate partner violence rates increase during pregnancy. It happens with all socioeconomic situations. Nurses have a significant role to play in working toward the prevention and early intervention of intimate partner violence.

### Tab: Physical Signs

When assessing women, nurses should be aware that some of the following physical signs of injuries might be related to intimate partner violence:
- bruising in the chest, abdomen, or private areas
- multiple injuries
- minor lacerations
- ruptured eardrums
- delay in seeking medical attention
- patterns of repeated injury

### Tab: Other Symptoms

However, it is unlikely women will present with a physical injury. They will more likely present with issues such as:
- stress-related illness
- anxiety, panic attacks, stress, and/or depression
- substance use including tranquilizers and alcohol
- chronic headaches, asthma, vague aches and pains
- abdominal pain, chronic diarrhea
- sexual dysfunction, vaginal discharge
- joint pain, muscle pain
- sleeping and eating disorders
- suicide attempts, psychiatric illness
- gynecological problems, miscarriages, and chronic pelvic pain

### Tab: Psychological Signs

The woman may also:
- appear nervous, ashamed, or evasive
- describe her partner as controlling or prone to anger
- seem uncomfortable or anxious in the presence of her partner
- be accompanied by her partner, who does most of the talking
- give an unconvincing explanation of the injuries
- be recently separated or divorced
- be reluctant to follow advice

## Section 12: Explore: Nurse Assessment and Support

Nurses are encouraged to ask questions that may help identify intimate partner violence or human trafficking, along with providing support. The response of nurses to women in these circumstances can have a profound effect on their willingness to open up or seek help.

### Tab: Questions Asked Indirectly

- How are things at home?
- How are you and your partner relating?
- Is there anything else happening that may be affecting your health?

### Tab: Questions Based on Clinical Observations

- You seem very anxious and nervous. Is everything okay at home?
- When I see injuries like this, I wonder if someone may have hurt you?
- Is there anything else that we haven't talked about that might be contributing to this condition?

### Tab: Questions Asked Directly (When a Partner is Absent)

- Are there ever times when you are frightened of your partner?
- Are you concerned about your safety or the safety of your children?
- Does the way your partner treats you ever make you feel unhappy or depressed?
- I think there may be a link between your illness and the way your partner treats you. What do you think?

Healthcare providers have an obligation to report suspected or confirmed abuse to the proper authorities. Each state has different laws and nurses should become familiar with laws in the states where they practice. Reports typically include client history, full physical assessment, and any lab work completed. Failure to report could result in criminal convictions and be considered neglect.
