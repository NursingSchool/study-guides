## Section 1: Introduction to High Risk Newborn Development

Gestational age (or the duration of pregnancy) is measured in weeks from the first day of the mother’s last menstrual period to the day of delivery.

The average gestational age is 40 weeks (280 days). However, not all women with normal pregnancies deliver at exactly 40 weeks.

New gestational age designations were developed by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG, 2022) to further define the 5-week range considered "term".

### Tab: Preterm

Infants born before 37 weeks gestation:
- **extremely preterm** is infants born before 28 weeks gestation
- **late preterm** is infants born between 34 and 36 6/7 weeks gestation

### Tab: Term

Infants born between 37 and 41 6/7 weeks gestation:
- **early term** is infants born between 37 and 38 6/7 weeks gestation
- **full term** is infants born between 39 and 40 6/7 weeks gestation
- **late term** is infants born between 41 and 41 6/7 weeks gestation

### Tab: Postterm

Infants born after 42 weeks gestation

Most infants are born at term and have the lowest risk of complications in the newborn period. Those that are not delivered at term may face complications and need assistance from nurses and providers.

This concept will explore these newborns and the care they need.

**References**

American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists. (2022). Definition of term pregnancy. https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/committee-opinion/articles/2013/11/definition-of-term-pregnancy

## Section 3: Explore: Preterm Infant

Baby Lauren was born at 33 weeks and 3 days. She would be considered a premature infant since she was born before 37 completed weeks of gestation. Her mom smoked and worked a high-stress job.

Preterm labor may be the result of one or more risk factors that include previous preterm labor, incompetent cervix, hypertension (chronic and gestational), being pregnant with multiples (twins or more), abdominal trauma or stress, poor prenatal care, and use of alcohol or drugs during pregnancy. Other causes can include being pregnant with a fetus that has known birth defects or conception by in vitro fertilization (IVF).

Born less than term (37 weeks), Lauren could face some acute and long-term health issues for which nurses must monitor, including temperature regulation, lower resistance to infection, an immature liver, elimination problems, and sensory-perceptual function changes.

**Transcript — Preterm Infant Issues**
- **Temperature regulation** — preterm infants can’t maintain their temperature and often need an extra layer of clothing
- **Resistance to infections** — preterm infants are more susceptible to germs and increased risk of infections
- **Immature liver** — predisposes the infant to jaundice
- **Elimination problems** — the intestinal bacteria can infiltrate the mucosa causing infection
- **Sensory-perceptual functions** — preterm infants may face long-term vision issues

**Lauren's nurse should prepare to:**
- Provide respiratory support.
  - Tachypnea and grunting are common.
- Perform the following assessments.
  - Assess heart sounds for the presence of murmurs.
  - Assess pulse and perfusion—Cyanosis is possible.
  - Monitor blood pressure, heart rate, and pulse pressure.
- Provide adequate fluids, electrolytes, and nutrition.
- Maintain a neutral thermal environment.
- Prevent infection.
  - Wear personal protective equipment (PPE).
  - Wash hands frequently.
  - Wipe down equipment after each use.

## Section 5: Explore: Late Preterm Issues

Baby Michael was born at 35 2/7 weeks. He would be considered a late preterm infant since he falls between 34 weeks and 36 6/7 weeks (ACOG, 2021). He may be cared for in the regular nursery or remain with his mom in her postpartum room. He will be looked at as developmentally mature with a low risk of morbidity by those who care for him.

The issues encountered by late preterm infants are caused by physiologic and metabolic immaturity. Late preterm infants are at higher risk of developing medical complications that result in increased rates of mortality and morbidity during birth hospitalization when compared to term infants. Michael will also have a higher rate of hospital readmission during the neonatal period than newborns born after 39 weeks.

### Tab: Late Preterm Infant Symptoms

Late preterm infants may have the following symptoms:
- feeding difficulties
- hypoglycemia
- extra vernix
- jaundice
- temperature instability
- apnea
- respiratory distress

Late preterm infants may need:
- intravenous infusion
- sepsis evaluation
- mechanical ventilation

### Tab: Late Preterm Infant Nursing Care

Nurses will assess and perform the following:
- Assess heart sounds for the presence of murmurs.
- Provide respiratory support.
- Assess pulse and perfusion.
- Monitor blood pressure, heart rate, and pulse pressure.
- Provide adequate fluids, electrolytes, and nutrition
  - Late preterm infants may need higher calorie formula/frequent feedings.
  - If breastfeeding, a human fortifier may be added.
- Maintain a neutral thermal environment.
- Prevent infection.
  - Nurses should wear PPE and wash their hands frequently.
  - Equipment should be wiped down after each use.

**Reference**

American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists. (2021). *Medically indicated late-preterm and early-term deliveries*. https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/committee-opinion/articles/2021/07/medically-indicated-late-preterm-and-early-term-deliveries

## Section 6: Explore: Small for Gestational Age

Baby Kyle was born on his due date at 40 weeks gestation, weighing 6 pounds. At a gestational age of 40 weeks, boys who weigh less than about 6 pounds 9 ounces (3 kilograms) are considered small for gestational age (SGA). Girls who weigh less than about 6 pounds 3 ounces (2.8 kilograms) are considered small for gestational age.

An SGA infant is one whose length, weight, and head circumference are below the 10th percentile of the normal variation for gestational age as determined by neonatal examination. The SGA infant may be preterm, term, or postterm. In Kyle's case, he was term.

Causes of SGA include both genetic abnormalities and maternal factors.

### Tab: Genetic Abnormalities

- genetic problems, cardiac anomalies, kidney deformities, or renal insufficiency
- infection, such as toxoplasmosis, cytomegalovirus, or HIV
- congenital anomalies, such as heart defects or neural tube defects

### Tab: Maternal Factors

- height, weight, age, parity, and ethnicity
- maternal disease, including hypertension or preeclampsia
- chronic maternal disease, such as inflammatory bowel disease, epilepsy, or anemia
- lifestyle factors, including smoking, alcohol or drug use, and obesity
- social stressors
- multiple pregnancies
- incorrect pregnancy dating

**Some notes from Kyle's assessment include:**
- soft tissue wasting and dysmaturity
- loose, dry, and scaling skin

**Kyle is also at risk of:**
- perinatal asphyxia, impaired thermoregulation, hypoglycemia, polycythemia, and poor feeding and weight gain

## Section 9: Explore: Large for Gestational Age

Large for gestational age (LGA) is an indication of a high prenatal growth rate, often defined as a weight (or length, or head circumference) that lies at or above the 90th percentile for that gestational age due to overproduction of growth hormone in utero.

Audrey was born today and weighs 4213 grams. She is 39 and 3/7 weeks old. Based on her weight, she is considered large for gestational age (4000-4500 g).

Audrey may be a big baby because her mom had diabetes (poorly controlled), obesity, or was a member of a certain ethnic group. Another possibility is that Audrey's parents could just be larger individuals.

In Audrey's situation, the mom is 6'2" and the partner is 6'4". The mom weighed about 180 pounds.

### Tab: Audrey's Assessment

Audrey's assessment was completed and a fractured right clavicle was discovered due to the disproportionate size of the newborn to the birth passageway. This is a common complication with LGA infants and the nurse should:
- Monitor and manage birth injuries and resulting complications.
- Assess the infant for crepitus, hematoma, or deformity over the clavicle.
- Note if there is decreased movement of the arm on the affected side.
- Check Moro reflex.
- Limit arm motion by pinning the infant's sleeve to the shirt.
- Manage the pain.

### Tab: Common Conditions of LGA

Other common conditions of LGA:
- Facial nerve injury
  - Nursing assessment includes assessing for symmetry of the mouth while crying.
  - Clinical manifestations include wrinkles that are deeper on the unaffected side and the paralyzed side is smooth with a swollen appearance.
- Phrenic nerve palsy
  - Assess for respiratory distress.
  - Clinical manifestations include asymmetric movement of the abdomen during respirations.
- Additional fractures
  - Assess for pain, guarding, or lack of movement at the affected area.
  - Clinical manifestations often include local swelling.

## Section 11: Explore: Postterm Infant

Postterm pregnancy extends beyond 42 weeks gestation. The postterm infant may be large for gestational age (LGA), average for gestational age (AGA), or small for gestational age (SGA), depending on the status of the placenta. As the placenta ages, it becomes unable to provide adequate oxygen to the fetus, resulting in late decelerations of the fetal heart rate.

Justin was born yesterday at 42 weeks and 2 days. There were concerns on his fetal monitoring tracing with late decelerations. His mom initially declined to be induced as she wanted minimal interventions but agreed to avoid complications with the baby.

### Tab: Postterm Infant Assessment

Justin's newborn assessment showed:
- dry peeling skin, also known as desquamation
- creases that cover the sole
- meconium stained, green-tinged skin
- minimal vernix (cheesy substance) and lanugo (hair)
- long hair and nails
- 6 mm breast bud

### Tab: Postterm Infant Nursing Care

Nursing actions include:
- chest physiotherapy with suctioning completed to remove excess meconium and secretions
- supplemental oxygen and respiratory support to stabilize
- serial blood glucose measurements
- feedings, breast or formula, to prevent hypoglycemia
- skin cleaned and dried to maintain integrity
  - no creams or lotions applied
  - use of tape avoided
