## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Care: Newborn Assessment

This module will review the key areas of nursing focus in the initial and postpartum assessment of the normal newborn as it adapts to extrauterine life, from the delivery room through the postpartum period. Nursing interventions and screenings are discussed. With proper nursing observation, complications can be detected and treated early for optimal newborn outcomes.

## Section 3: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Head

The head-to-toe physical assessment of the newborn takes place after delivery and throughout the postpartum stay.

Following a vaginal delivery and depending on the degree to which the newborn's skull had to accommodate the birth canal, the newborn's head is typically misshapen to some degree. The newborn's skull is not fused. During labor and delivery, as the head fits through the vaginal canal, the unfused cranial bones will override (overlap). This often results in:

- overriding sutures – ridges that can be palpated
- molding - the head is somewhat elongated and misshapen
- caput - a soft pad of edema on the skull often seen with molding
- head circumference measurement – may be affected and need to be remeasured later

Head circumference is measured around the fullest part of the head (the occiput) and just above the eyebrows. The normal range is between 13-15 inches (32-38 cm). The head is disproportionately large for the body. The measurement will also be affected by conditions that impact the brain and skull, such as hydrocephalous or microcephaly.

### Tab: Fontanelles

Fontanelles are soft spaces between the unfused skull bones. A sunken fontanelle may indicate poor hydration. A bulging fontanelle may indicate increased intracranial pressure (ICP).

### Tab: Milia

Milia are small white spots found on the nose and chin of the newborn, similar in appearance to acne.

### Tab: Eyes

The eyelids of the newborn are edematous and no tears are present. From birth, newborns can best see objects held 8-10 inches away.

### Tab: Ears

The ears should be even and set appropriately.

- Low-set ears may indicate chromosomal abnormalities.
- The presence of skin tags or dimples may indicate chromosomal abnormalities, hearing problems, or kidney defects.

## Section 4: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Chest and Abdomen

Review chest and abdomen newborn assessment below.

### Tab: Chest

- At birth, the newborn must quickly begin breathing and the heart must quickly reverse blood flow within its chambers. Up until this point, the lungs were not responsible for filtering oxygen from the air through inhaling and exhaling. The mother's blood supplied the needed oxygen.
- The lungs are filled with protective fluids. When the nurse is auscultating the newborn's lungs after birth, it is not uncommon to hear adventitious lung sounds. The first breaths begin to clear out this fluid. The mouth and nares are suctioned with a bulb syringe. The newborn's lungs will sound wet but will quickly begin improving the more it cries, forcing the fluids out. After the initial assessment, it is best to auscultate the lungs when the newborn is asleep.
- Respirations should not be labored and there should be no retractions, barrel-shaped chest, or use of accessory muscles. The normal range for the respiratory rate is 30-60 breaths per minute with brief periods of apnea being normal.
- The normal range for the apical heart rate is 120-160 beats per minute. Murmurs are common in the newborn due to the heart's ongoing transition to close off previously open pathways within the structure of the heart. Though common, all newborn murmurs must be reported to the healthcare provider for further evaluation. Always note the newborn's activity level when assessing the heart rate, as crying can increase the heart rate and sleeping can reduce the heart rate.
- Chest circumference is measured at the level of the nipples, keeping the tape measure even and taut. The normal range is between 12-14 inches (30-36 cm).

### Tab: Abdomen

- The abdomen should be soft, round, and not distended. It should protrude slightly. Bowel sounds should be able to be heard 15 minutes after birth.
- The nurse will visually inspect the stump of the umbilical cord at delivery, after it has been clamped, to ensure it contains Wharton's jelly (gelatinous protective tissue), one vein, and two arteries. A two-vessel cord is associated with congenital anomalies. The cord will dry and begin turning black after 2-3 days. The area surrounding the umbilical cord should be free of redness, drainage, odor, or other signs of infection. This area must be kept dry and should not be covered by a diaper. The nurse removes the clamp before discharge after the cord has dried sufficiently.

## Section 6: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Skin

Review some components of a newborn assessment of the skin.

### Slide 1: Skin

The newborn has been surrounded by sterile, warm, amniotic fluid. Vernix, a cheesy substance, covers the skin as a protectant but dissolves away as the fetus nears term. The body is also covered in fine hair (lanugo) until the fetus reaches term. Once exposed to the external environment, non-contagious newborn rashes are common.

### Slide 2: Temperature

The temperature of the neonate should be verified with an axillary thermometer and measured frequently. Newborns are very vulnerable to hypothermia. Axillary temperature is the standard route in newborns. The normal axillary temperature is 97.7° to 99.5°F (36.5° to 37.5°C). When not skin-to-skin, newborns are kept wrapped in blankets with their heads covered.

### Slide 3: Dry Skin

Newborns may have dry skin, particularly those whose gestational age is over 40 weeks. It may crack and peel in some areas.

### Slide 4: Jaundice

Many newborns experience jaundice (yellowing skin) resulting from hyperbilirubinemia. Jaundice can be either physiologic (normal) or non-physiologic (pathologic). Timing of jaundice is the most important differentiator with non-physiologic occurring in the first 24 hours. The nurse will assess for jaundice at least every 8-12 hours. It begins at the head and moves down the body as the bilirubin levels increase. Jaundice is visible when the bilirubin level reaches 5 mg/dL. The nurse can obtain a transcutaneous bilirubin measurement using a noninvasive device, called a bilirubinometer. A serum bilirubin level may also be ordered by the healthcare provider.

### Slide 5: Mongolian Spots

Mongolian spots occur most commonly in newborns of African American, Asian, and Hispanic ethnic backgrounds. They look similar to a bruise and appear in various locations, such as buttocks, sacrum, and shoulders.

### Slide 6: Birthmarks

Strawberry birthmarks and stork bites (reddened areas that become more prominent with crying) are not uncommon.

## Section 7: Explore: Newborn Physical Assessment - Arms, Legs, Spine, and Genitalia

Review the components of a newborn assessment of arms, legs, spine, and genitalia.

### Slide 1: Arms and Legs

The arms and legs usually remain flexed against the body. Traumatic delivery may result in a fractured clavicle or brachial palsy. There should be 10 fingers and 10 toes, free of malformations, extra digits, or webbing. The hands and feet commonly have a bluish-purple color, called acrocyanosis, as the distal circulation improves over the first 7-10 days. The areas of central circulation, however, should not be cyanotic.

### Slide 2: Spine

The spine should be straight without indentations, curves, or tufts of hair that may indicate a hidden spina bifida (neural tube defect).

### Slide 3: Female Genitalia

Labia may be swollen, there may be white, mucousy vaginal discharge, and even a small amount of vaginal bleeding, which are normal findings. The remaining maternal hormones circulating in the newborn's body can cause pseudo-menstruation.

### Slide 4: Male Genitalia

The testicles are normally descended at birth. The nurse will palpate the scrotum to determine if the testes have descended. A hydrocele, a collection of fluid, may be present and palpated. The placement of the urethral opening should also be assessed. An opening below the midline is called hypospadias.

## Section 8: Explore: Newborn Reflexes

Various reflexes are assessed at birth to assess the nervous system.

### Slide 1: Palmar Grasp Reflex

Elicited by placing a finger on the palm of the newborn's hand. The newborn grasps the finger.

### Slide 2: Plantar Grasp Reflex

Elicited by placing a finger or stroking on the bottom of the foot. The toes curl downward.

### Slide 3: Babinski Reflex

Elicited by stroking the heel upward to the outer toes. The big toe dorsiflexes and the other toes fan out.

### Slide 4: Tonic Neck Reflex

Elicited by turning the head to one side. The arm and leg on the same side extend while the opposite arm and leg flex.

### Slide 5: Moro or Startle Reflex

Elicited by a sudden noise or movement. The arms extend outward and then return to the body.

### Slide 6: Crawling Reflex

Elicited by placing the newborn prone and applying pressure to the foot. The newborn makes a crawling motion.

### Slide 7: Stepping Reflex

Elicited by holding the newborn upright with feet touching a flat surface. The legs move in a stepping motion.

### Slide 8: Sucking Reflex

Elicited by an object touching the roof of the mouth. Begins to suck.

### Slide 9: Rooting Reflex

Elicited by stroking the cheek or side of the mouth. Turns head towards the stroking and opens mouth.

## Section 9: Explore: Assessing for Hypothermia and Hypoglycemia

Hypoglycemia and hypothermia are interrelated complications for the newborn that the nurse must assess.

Newborns are vulnerable to hypothermia because of their inability to thermoregulate. Even slight heat loss will cause hypoglycemia along with increased oxygen consumption in a newborn as their bodies work to burn stored fat (called brown fat), glucose, and oxygen for the first time.

### Tab: Hypoglycemia

Using a standard bedside monitor, the nurse may check the newborn's blood glucose by collecting a sample from the thickest area of the heel. The normal glucose level in the term newborn ranges between 40-60 mg/dL on the day of birth and 50-90 mg/dL from then on.

The nurse will help prevent and detect hypoglycemia to prevent additional complications. Newborns whose temperatures are low may also show signs of hypoglycemia such as jitteriness, lethargy, and poor feeding. The presence of maternal diabetes, those who are premature or post-term, and those who are large or small for gestational age are most at risk. These newborns may be put on a hypoglycemia protocol which includes regular blood glucose monitoring and strict feeding schedules and amounts.

### Tab: Hypothermia

The newborn will produce heat by metabolizing stored brown fat, a type of fat unique in the neonate, in a process called non-shivering thermogenesis. Newborns rarely shiver when they are cold. Nurses can help minimize heat loss using the following.

- Radiant warmers may be used to bring up body temperatures to prevent complications.
- Skin-to-skin contact is encouraged throughout the postpartum period unless contraindicated. Nursing assessment and actions are completed while in the mother's arms as much as possible. The newborn's first bath is delayed to allow for body temperature stabilization (as well as breastfeeding and bonding).
- Warmed blankets are used to dry and swaddle the newborn at birth and the nurse ensures the newborn is adequately dressed and wrapped throughout the postpartum period. The nurse also ensures there are no extra blankets in the crib with the newborn as this can cause suffocation. The nurse reinforces safe sleep practices with the mother and family to help prevent sudden infant death syndrome, including the use of clothing and blanket wraps to keep the newborn warm, along with a cap and a warm room.

## Section 11: Explore: Gestational Age Assessment

Calculation of the newborn's gestational age is another critical area of assessment typically completed by the nurse as part of the initial newborn care. Neonates born before or after term are at higher risk for complications, so it is important to verify the amount of time the newborn gestated (from conception to birth), a more accurate assessment than assuming the estimated date of delivery (EDD or due date). Even with an ultrasound during pregnancy, the EDD is still an estimate of gestation time.

This assessment helps answer the question, "Does this newborn who was born after gestating for xx weeks (based on the estimated date of delivery) look and act like most other newborns born at xx weeks gestation?" If not, why? What is the newborn at risk for that we can assess and treat early?

### Tab: New Ballard Score

The New Ballard Score is a rating and classification system commonly used to compare certain neuromuscular and physical characteristics of maturity displayed by the newborn to the expected characteristics exhibited by most newborns of the same gestational age. While under the warmer, the newborn's responses to 6 tests of neuromuscular maturity and 7 tests of physical maturity are scored. The total score of all assessed characteristics determines the newborn's gestational age, accurate within 2 weeks.

*[Figure: New Ballard Score — from Ballard et al., 1991]*

#### New Ballard Scale Example

Let's consider Alex, who was born on January 8th. Alex's EDD was January 1st. We estimate that Alex was born after 41 weeks of gestation, and we anticipate that Alex will look and act like most other 41-week newborns.

The 13 areas of physical characteristics are assessed using the New Ballard Score. Alex's score for those 13 areas totals 35. According to the maturity rating table, Alex's score of 35 equates to a gestational age of 38 weeks. We expected him to have the physical characteristics of a newborn who gestated for 41 weeks. There is a discrepancy there.

The nurse will plot the newborn's weight, length, and head circumference on a graph of intrauterine development relative to this estimated gestational age. Where the measurements fall off the graph illustrates how they compare to other newborns of the same gestational age. How well did the newborn grow for the amount of time it spent gestating compared to other newborns who spent the same amount of time?

- Small for Gestational Age (SGA): the newborn's size is below the 10th percentile on the graph.
- Appropriate for Gestational Age (AGA): the newborn's size falls between the 10th and 90th percentiles on the graph.
- Large for Gestational Age (LGA): the newborn's size is above the 90th percentile on the graph.

Alex's measurements are plotted on a graph that shows where most other newborns' measurements are at 41 weeks gestation. Alex's measurements are in the 10th percentile, which means they are lower than 90% of other newborns who gestated for 41 weeks.

Alex is SGA, small for gestational age, and is likely at risk of some complications and will require additional observation and intervention.

## Section 12: Explore: Routine Newborn Screenings

Routine newborn screenings completed during inpatient care include:

### Tab: Hearing Screening

- The nurse assesses the newborn's hearing by observing reactions to sudden loud, high-pitched, or rhythmic noises as well as response to the mother's voice.
- Prior to discharge, a hearing screening involving otoacoustic emissions or acoustic brainstem response tests is completed by an audiologist technician while the newborn is asleep. If the newborn does not pass the initial or follow-up test, the mother will be referred to a specialist for further testing after discharge.

### Tab: Phenylketonuria (PKU) Screening

- Phenylalanine is an amino acid (and a common protein in foods) that needs to be converted to tyrosine by the liver enzyme phenylalanine hydrolase to be used. Phenylketonuria (PKU) is a genetic disorder in which phenylalanine hydrolase is deficient. Phenylalanine can reach toxic levels in the blood, causing central nervous system damage and severe cognitive impairment.
- Most state laws require all newborns to be screened for PKU after 24 hours of age and prior to discharge in order to identify the presence of this genetic condition. This delay allows enough time for proteins to be consumed.
- The nurse typically collects the blood sample for this test, which requires a heel stick and enough blood to fill several circles on the testing form.
- If found, a low-phenylalanine diet will begin immediately to prevent the toxic build-up of phenylalanine and central nervous system damage.

## Section 14: Explore: Assessment of Newborn Nutrition - Intake

During the postpartum hospital period, the nurse ensures that the newborn is receiving adequate nutrition in a safe manner by assessing both the amount and quality of intake, as well as physical cues that reflect adequate nutrition. Newborns' bodies do not adequately balance energy storage and consumption, thermoregulation, and oxygen demand without proper nutrition. Without adequate nutrition, they are prone to hypoglycemia and hypothermia.

The newborn will be breastfed, bottle-fed, or a combination of both.

### Tab: Breastfeeding

The first milk (colostrum) contains protein, calories, and immunity boosters, and is thin by design to pass through the newborn's digestive tract easily. The breast milk components are perfectly balanced for the newborn and change as the newborn's body adapts to extrauterine life.

The intake of milk after birth is the first introduction of bacteria through the digestive system, which remained sterile in utero. Intestinal flora populate the intestinal tract, making the newborn able to digest the thicker, more dense milk that will come.

### Tab: Bottle Feeding

The mother may choose or need to use formula rather than breastfeed the newborn. These newborns may eat less often than breastfed newborns because the formula is dense and satisfying right away, and it takes longer to digest. Some may prefer bottle feeding for this reason and the reassurance of adequate nutrition that comes with the ability to measure the exact volume of intake.

- Manufacturers produce formulas that come as close as possible to creating a breast milk substitute. Commercially available formulas made from powdered, modified cow's milk are most common. Other variations are available for special needs, such as milk allergy or sensitivity (soy or lactose-free formula) or the need for a different balance of macronutrients (preterm infants requiring more calorically dense milk).
- The nurse ensures that the newborn is bottle-fed every 3-4 hours, depending on the newborn's needs and hunger signs. For the first 24 hours, about 15-30 mL at a time is best and then gradually increases by 15 mL intervals without forcing the newborn to take more.

### Tab: Burping

At first, newborns may regurgitate mucous left in their stomachs from swallowing amniotic fluid in utero and may not keep milk down. Whether breastfeeding or bottle feeding, burping the newborn at intervals during feeding helps prevent some regurgitation due to swallowed air.

## Section 15: Explore: Assessment of Newborn Nutrition - Output

Nursing assessment of urinary output and bowel movements is accomplished by visualization of the diaper. Typically, the newborn has more than 3 wet diapers + 3 stools per day by the 3rd day postpartum (3*3*3).

### Slide 1: Urinary Elimination

The first urination should take place within 24 hours of delivery, but most newborns void within 12 to 24 hours of birth. The nurse carefully monitors for the first urine to take place and documents this. The absence of urine output may indicate anomalies requiring further testing. It can be difficult to tell when the first void occurs because it is usually a small amount and can easily be missed during the chaos of delivery and the transition interventions taking place.

- Some facilities weigh diapers to determine the exact urinary output, but normally a general visual assessment of the amount is adequate. The newborn will only void small amounts and as little as once or twice during the first 2 days of life. By day 4, the newborn will have > 6 wet diapers, which indicates adequate fluid intake.
- Occasionally, reddish or pink stains will appear with urine in the diaper and may concern parents. The nurse can assure them that this is caused by the presence of uric acid crystals and not a sign of blood in the urine.

### Slide 2: Bowel Elimination

Newborn bowel movements are called meconium. The first stool may be passed while still in utero, sometimes the result of post-term or traumatic deliveries. This can be a dangerous situation if the fetus inhales some of the meconium-stained amniotic fluid into the lungs (MSAF).

- Meconium is a sterile, odorless black tarry stool that is not the result of intake postpartum but digestion of amniotic fluid in utero. As the newborn digests breast milk or formula, the intestinal flora is established and the stools gradually change from meconium to a more frequent, thinner, mustard color with an odor. The characteristic of the stool is also an indication that breast milk or formula is being ingested and digested.
- Typically, the first stool is passed within the first 12-48 hours of birth. By day 4, the newborn will pass 4+ stools per day.

## Section 17: Explore: Assessment of Newborn Nutrition - Weight Loss and Gain

The birth weight of a term infant ranges between 5 pounds, 8 ounces and 8 pounds, 13 ounces (2500-4000 grams). The birth weight, along with the other birth measurements and assessment criteria, is used to evaluate the newborn's overall health and is compared to the normal ranges for the newborn's gestational age.

- Newborns typically lose up to 7% of their birth weight in the first 24-48 hours. At birth, the bladder and colon contain urine and stool from swallowed amniotic fluid in utero. The urine and stool not yet excreted are included in the birth weight measurement.
- This weight loss is anticipated by the nurse, who assures the mother that this is normal and a sign that the newborn is receiving adequate nutrition.
- The birth weight provides a baseline by which the normal amount of initial weight loss is determined. The newborn's weight will be measured daily until discharge.
- The newborn will typically return to or exceed their birth weight by day 14 and gain around 0.7-1 oz/day for the first 3 months.
