## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Care: Postpartum Mother

The amount of time the mother and newborn stay in the hospital during the postpartum period has decreased over time, leaving less time for the nurse to assess and intervene. It is crucial for the postpartum nurse to facilitate bonding and recognize physical and psychosocial warning signs to provide both the mother and newborn with the best possible chance of positive outcomes.

Stage four of labor and delivery, known as the recovery period, begins immediately after delivery of the placenta. Many changes are happening in both the mother and the newborn during this time, and the nurse acts fast to complete a lot of important tasks.

In this module, we will discuss the basics of maternal postpartum nursing care, including nursing assessment of the mother and maternal newborn bonding.

## Section 2: Prepare: Nursing Care of a Postpartum Mother

*(Q&A format — key facts from each question and feedback)*

**Q: In what situation might a pregnant woman remain in the same hospital room from labor/induction admission through postpartum discharge?**
**A: In a maternity unit set up with labor, delivery, recovery, and postpartum (LDRP) rooms.**
In a maternity unit set up with LDRP rooms, the mother and newborn continue their care with the same nurse in the same room following labor and delivery. In some maternity units, there are separate units for the various phases of labor and delivery and there may be a separate newborn nursery.

**Q: How long does it typically take for a woman who has delivered a baby to return to a pre-pregnant physical state?**
**A: 6 weeks.**
Immediately after delivery, a woman's body begins returning to nonpregnant status. The woman's body is back to a pre-pregnancy state in 6 weeks, undoing the last 9 months of anatomical and physiological changes.

**Q: Which findings may indicate a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in a lower extremity in a postpartum woman? (Select all that apply)**
**A: Weak pedal pulse in the affected extremity; Tenderness; Localized warmth.**
During the postpartum period, a woman's blood is hypercoagulable and, in combination with inactivity following delivery, she is at risk for DVTs. The lower extremities are assessed for presence of localized redness, warmth, edema, and tenderness. Weak pedal pulses in the affected extremity may indicate obstruction due to thrombus formation. *Note: Pedal edema is a normal finding in a postpartum woman; localized (not bilateral) redness would indicate a possible DVT.*

## Section 3: Explore: Postpartum Assessment of the Mother — BUBBLE-HEER

Stage four of labor and delivery, known as the recovery period, begins immediately after delivery of the placenta. Many changes happen in both the mother and the newborn during this time, and the nurse acts fast to complete many important tasks.

In a maternity unit set up with labor, delivery, recovery, and postpartum (LDRP) rooms, the mother and newborn continue their care with the same nurse in the same room following labor and delivery. In some maternity units, there are separate units for the various phases of labor and delivery and there may be a separate newborn nursery.

A typical delivery recovery lasts 1-2 hours. For the first 6 hours after delivery, the mother is monitored closely. She needs emotional support and close physical assessment as her mind and body adjust to significant changes. Her body immediately begins returning to nonpregnant status, undoing in just 6 weeks the last 9 months' worth of anatomical and physiological changes.

The main areas of nursing assessment are represented by the acronym **BUBBLE-HEER**.

---

### B — Breasts

At the time of delivery, they have enlarged, the nipples have areolas that have darkened and become more pronounced in response to hormonal changes in preparation for breastfeeding. In supine position, palpate both breasts for engorgement, inspect nipples for soreness or skin breakdown. Assessment of the breasts also occurs when assisting with breastfeeding, which includes determining if the woman is producing early milk which is thin, watery and slightly yellow (colostrum) or if she has begun producing true milk.

### U — Uterus

After delivery, the uterus gets smaller and smaller, receding to the level of the umbilicus and eventually back under the symphysis pubis. This is called involution. Natural oxytocin hormone and oxytocin administered intravenously cause contractions that compress blood vessels at the open venous site of placental attachment on the uterine wall. The uterine lining is also shed out through the vagina, and this material is called lochia. In supine position, with the bladder empty, use deep palpation to determine the location of the firm uterine fundus. It should be midline and somewhere between 2 fingerbreadths above, at, or below the umbilicus. Fundal massage should firm up a boggy uterus and may elicit a flow of lochia. Breastfeeding should also stimulate contractions. A firm uterus feels like a grapefruit. Upon palpating the abdomen to assess the uterus, it may be observed that the rectus muscles are not present anteriorly; the large uterus separates these muscles laterally as pregnancy progresses.

### B — Bladder

Postpartum assessment of bladder status is considered the "new vital sign" to prevent complications associated with retained urine and a full bladder. A full bladder will cause the uterus to deviate, contributing to subinvolution, increasing the risk of hemorrhage, or contributing to the development of a urinary tract infection. Monitor for pain with urination that may indicate structural damage, infection, or an unnoticed laceration. Monitor for difficulty urinating, particularly after an epidural, so that the bladder does not fill up. Whenever possible, have her empty her own bladder, but empty the bladder by catheterization if necessary. Women who have had a C/S will have an indwelling catheter in place until they can ambulate, and after removal they may initially have difficulty emptying their bladder.

### B — Bowel

The bowel becomes an important assessment of the postpartum woman in both vaginal and C/S deliveries. Hormonal influence (progesterone), pain medications, dehydration (blood loss), decreased maternal activity, and surgical interventions can lead to constipation during the postpartum period. It is not uncommon for the woman to be discharged with a stool softener medication and without having had a bowel movement if bowel sounds are active and flatulence is present. Provide education regarding constipation prevention with ambulation, adequate fluid intake and a fiber-rich diet.

### L — Lochia

The fluid shed from the uterus through the vagina consisting of uterine blood and tissue is called lochia. It has a distinct odor, similar to menstrual flow, but should not be foul-smelling. A steady flow is normal, and a stream of lochia with small clots with ambulation or uterine palpation is normal.

Assess how many peri pads she is filling in an hour. **One pad saturated every 30 minutes is cause for concern** and further assessment of the uterus and perineum. Document using the acronym **COAT** (color, odor, amount, and time):

- **Days 1–3** — Lochia rubra: bright red, dark red, or reddish brown
- **Days 3–10** — Lochia serosa: pink or brown-tinged, thins over time
- **Days 10–14** — Lochia alba: white, cream, or light yellow

Excessive bright red (lochia rubra) may indicate a retained placental fragment or other issue. With excessive blood loss, sanitary pads should be weighed (dry weight subtracted from used weight) to determine accurate blood loss.

### E — Episiotomy (and Lacerations: Perineum Assessment)

Episiotomy and lacerations are common following labor and delivery. Lacerations are characterized as first-degree (superficial) through fourth-degree (laceration goes into the rectal wall). Third- and fourth-degree lacerations may impact the woman's ability to have a bowel movement. Hematomas can occur in this area as well and are silent causes of postpartum hemorrhage.

In supine position with adequate lighting, ask the mother to relax her knees then inspect the perineum. Episiotomy and laceration repairs should be well approximated and remain intact, free of hematomas and drainage. Assess for hemorrhoids, common after a long 3rd stage (pushing).

Document perineal assessment using the acronym **REEDA**:
- **R** = Redness
- **E** = Edema
- **E** = Ecchymosis
- **D** = Drainage
- **A** = Approximation

Initially, ice packs are recommended for swelling, vasoconstriction, and comfort. Other assistive measures: peri-donut, witch hazel pads, anesthetic sprays, sitz bath, peri-bottle. Teach the mother to **pat dry after voiding** (not wipe) and to always change peri pads **front to back**.

### H — Hematoma & Hemorrhoids

**Hematoma vulvar** is caused by rapid bleeding into soft tissue causing severe pain often described as "pressure," associated with precipitous delivery or vacuum/forceps delivery. May require surgical intervention to ligate the bleeding vessel.

**Hemorrhoids** are varicosities of the rectum from straining or pressure from the enlarged pregnant uterus. Usually resolve after delivery but can be treated for pain/pressure relief.

### E — Extremities

The woman's blood during the postpartum period is hypercoagulable and, in combination with inactivity following delivery, she is at risk for deep vein thrombosis (DVT). Lower extremities are assessed for localized redness, warmth, edema, and tenderness. Weak pedal pulses in the affected extremity may indicate obstruction due to thrombus formation. Pedal pulses should be assessed bilaterally. **Encouraging early ambulation can prevent DVT.**

A **Homan's sign** (discomfort in the calf upon dorsiflexion of the foot) may be used to gauge DVT presence, although it is nonspecific and not sensitive. A DVT may not always cause pain — all assessment findings should be reported to the healthcare provider.

### E — Emotional Status

Women go through a rush of emotions during the postpartum period. Assess for signs of bonding with the newborn, engagement in parenting skills, and the relationship with her partner. Postpartum depression is not uncommon.

Assess ability to rest and amount of sleep. A breastfeeding baby will want to nurse every 2-3 hours at least; encourage her to sleep when the newborn sleeps. If further assessment is needed, collaborate interdisciplinarily with the provider and social worker.

**Puerperal phases of maternal adaptation:**
- **Taking-In Phase:** Mother is focused on her own needs, may be content to allow others to make decisions, may have difficulty assimilating information. Allow her to discuss her labor and delivery experience as often as needed.
- **Taking-Hold Phase:** Mother may have anxiety about being a mother. Encourage her to provide as much care for the baby as possible.
- **Letting-Go Phase:** Parents give up previous roles as a carefree couple and relinquish part of infant care to a caretaker. Provide opportunity to discuss unexpected feelings.

### R — RhoGAM, Rubella, and Reflexes

- **RhoGAM:** Required for all mothers who are Rh(−) who carried an Rh(+) fetus. Administered via IM injection **within 72 hours of delivery**. At birth, the baby receives a direct Coombs test from the umbilical cord to perform the antibody titer.
- **Rubella vaccine:** Subcutaneous (SQ) injection for nonimmune or equivocal rubella status. Rubella is a live virus; administered after childbirth to prevent acquiring rubella during subsequent pregnancies. Avoid pregnancy for 1 month after vaccination.
- **Reflexes:** Check brachial, radial, and patellar deep tendon reflexes (DTRs). 1+ and 2+ are normal; 3+ and 4+ are hyperactive and indicate cerebral irritability — may suggest preeclampsia. Check **clonus**: dorsiflexion of foot sharply while knee is flexed. Positive clonus = rapid muscle contraction/relaxation ("beats of clonus") against examiner's hand.

## Section 6: Explore: Parent-Newborn Bonding After Delivery

Let's look closer at the emotional status aspect of the BUBBLE-HEER postpartum assessment in relation to bonding.

Currently, under normal circumstances, the mother and newborn stay in the hospital for **48 hours** after vaginal delivery, and **72 hours** after cesarean delivery. The mother may choose to be discharged earlier at the healthcare provider's discretion.

The assessment of the parent-newborn relationship begins early in the pregnancy. The nurse considers how the woman and her family are reacting to the news of an impending addition to the family. At delivery, the nurse observes the mother's initial reaction to seeing her child — this varies and there isn't necessarily one "right" reaction.

### Facilitating the Initial Parent-Newborn Bond

The nurse plays an important role in helping to facilitate the initial parent-newborn bond after delivery.

- Postpartum bonding is encouraged from the moment of birth through early physical contact between the newborn and the mother.
- A common practice is to lay the newborn on the mother's chest immediately after delivery before the cord is even cut. Even in cesarean deliveries, the newborn is brought to the mother's chest while still in the operating room.
- The mother makes eye contact with her newborn and begins talking to it. The newborn can best see objects held **8-10 inches away**. The newborn sees the mother's face already at birth and responds to her differently than other sights and sounds. For this reason, the oily prophylactic antibiotic eye drops used routinely at delivery are **withheld for about an hour after delivery** to allow the infant to clearly see the mother and father.
- When the newborn is soothed by being in the mother's arms, this attachment is strengthened further.
- The postpartum nurse will conduct most, if not all, necessary assessments and interventions while the newborn remains on the mother's chest, as long as the infant remains stable. This includes auscultating heart and lung sounds, warming and drying the newborn, and assessing Apgar scores.
- The newborn will likely express interest in breastfeeding at delivery — sucking on its hand, rooting around searching for the nipple. The **first bath is often withheld for up to 24 hours** after delivery to allow for constant skin-to-skin contact and intermittent breastfeeding on demand to further strengthen this initial bond.
- The partner will often exhibit **engrossment** right away: eye-to-eye contact with the infant and intense fascination.

## Section 7: Explore: Promoting and Understanding the Parent-Newborn Bond

**What can postpartum nurses do to promote bonding and parenting skills?**
- Compliment parents on their newborn.
- Reassure parents when their newborn's assessment is normal and when their appearance will change. Often, the sight of a facial rash, swelling, etc. causes the parents concern and may impact bonding.
- Encourage sibling participation and involvement.
- Be welcoming to the woman's family and significant others.
- Reassure parents as they learn newborn care skills and praise them when they accomplish a new task.
- Act as a role model and demonstrate attentive newborn care.

**Consider that women in various circumstances may react differently to seeing their newborn for the first time:**
- a woman who is alone and is giving her child up for adoption
- a woman who has a supportive spouse and family
- a young teenager with self-image problems
- a woman whose values/beliefs do not include the outward showing of emotion

Each of these women has a unique story that plays into how they bond with their newborn. **The nurse's role is to provide:**
- physical support
- emotional comforting
- parenting guidance
- acceptance without judgment

## Section 9 — Explore: Postpartum Assessment of the Mother - Vital Signs

### Tab: Normal Postpartum Vital Signs

Normal postpartum vital signs for the mother are:

- **Temperature:** may be elevated for 24 hours after delivery due to dehydration
- **Pulse:** may be low for 6–8 days after delivery due to increased cardiac output and stroke volume
- **Respirations:** should be normal following delivery
- **Blood Pressure:** may be low when standing (orthostatic hypotension) due to anatomical changes as the body returns to normal

### Tab: Monitoring

After a normal vaginal delivery, vital signs are monitored every 15 minutes during recovery, and then every 4 hours for 24 hours, and then every 8 hours until discharge, if the mother remains stable.

**Causes for alterations in vital signs include:**

- Analgesic or anesthetic medications
- Hypovolemia due to excessive delivery blood loss
- Presence of pain
- Presence of infection after 24 hours
- Complications from epidural or spinal placement may alter respirations

**Monitor for signs and symptoms of hypovolemic shock:**

- Filling a peri pad every 15–30 minutes or more
- Dizziness with standing
- Hypotension and tachycardia
- Cold, clammy skin
- Anxiety
- Uterine atony (boggy, loss of tone)
- Notify the healthcare provider immediately. Provide supplemental oxygen and prepare to provide medications, intravenous (IV) fluid, and perhaps prep for surgery.

## Section 11 — Explore: Maternal Postpartum Nursing Actions - Pain Control

The primary areas of maternal postpartum nursing actions in addition to assessment are pain control, self-care, promoting the return to pre-pregnancy physical status, education, and emotional support.

**Vaginal Delivery Comfort Measures**

- After a vaginal delivery, most women experience uterine cramping and perineal pain.
- Ice packs for the perineal area are a common remedy used by postpartum nurses.
- Intense vaginal or perineal pain may be an indication that a hematoma is present and must be investigated immediately.

**Cesarean Delivery Comfort Measures**

- After a Cesarean delivery, if the woman did not go through labor first, the presence of normal post-operative pain is in addition to uterine cramping pain.

**General Comfort Measures**

- There are women who go through hours of difficult labor, and possibly even an episiotomy, only to require a Cesarean delivery anyway. These women in particular experience many discomforts.
- Keeping pain under control helps encourage the woman to resume normal activity, which aids her body in returning to pre-pregnancy status.
- Pharmaceutical pain management may be needed.

## Section 12 — Explore: Maternal Postpartum Nursing Actions - Ambulation and Self-care

The first time the woman ambulates after delivery involves ensuring her physical readiness.

**Slide 1 — The nurse should consider the following:**

Are her vital signs stable? Is her blood pressure low? Is she losing an excessive amount of blood? Is her uterus firm? Is her bladder distended? If she had an epidural, has the feeling returned to her lower extremities?

**Slide 2 — Following a cesarean delivery:**

The woman will have an indwelling catheter in place for a period of time. The catheter is keeping her bladder empty. Following a vaginal delivery, the nurse must make sure her bladder does not fill and compete with the uterus for space. This will cause excessive blood loss because the uterus is unable to adequately contract and occlude the blood vessels where the placenta was attached. Therefore, the initial void should take place within 4–6 hours of delivery if not sooner.

**Slide 3 — Slow and in steps:**

The nurse will take things slow and in steps. First, the patient will sit up on the side of the bed and allow her blood pressure to normalize. Does she feel dizzy? Next, she can touch her toes to the floor and see if they feel tingly. Then, she can stand up in place at the bedside. If she is not dizzy and she can bear weight easily, two nurses will typically assist her to the bathroom monitoring continuously.

**Slide 4 — Once in the bathroom:**

The bed sheets from delivery are changed. It may take several minutes for her to be able to urinate due to edema in the area from delivery as well as anesthesia. Sometimes, turning on the faucet and letting her listen to the sound of the water is enough to initiate a urine stream. The nurse then uses a peri bottle filled with lukewarm water to spray the perineal area. The woman pats dry with toilet paper rather than wiping which will irritate the perineum and disrupt the sutures. A new peri pad is put in place and on top she will typically also use witch hazel pads and possibly an ice pack. She may at this time also spray her perineum with lidocaine spray.

**Slide 5 — Keep the perineum clean:**

She washes her hands and returns to the bed or chair. This process is repeated every time she uses the bathroom. She may need assistance with this several times before she is safe to do so on her own. After she is able to ambulate several times without difficulty and her vital signs remain stable, the woman can shower and ambulate as tolerated. Ambulation is important for many reasons, including return of normal bowel function. It is very important to keep the perineum clean to prevent infection. Baths, other than sitz baths, are not permitted for at least 6 weeks because of the risk of infection.

**Slide 6 — Fundal and lochia assessment:**

The nurse may lay her back down and assess her fundus and bladder after urination to ensure she was able to empty it and that her uterus is firm and has normal placement. Uterine fundal and lochia assessments continue every 15 minutes for up to 2 hours after delivery or until stable. As discussed, the uterus should be firm, midline and be going lower in the abdomen in relation to the umbilicus. A boggy uterus should firm up with fundal massage. Abnormalities in this area may necessitate postpartum hemorrhage medications.
