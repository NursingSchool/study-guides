# Week 3: Nursing Care: High-Risk Mother

## Section 1: Introduction to Nursing Care of the High-Risk Mother

Numerous maternal and fetal adaptations must occur in an orderly sequence for the childbearing process to be considered normal. If physiologic or emotional problems develop, complications can arise, threatening the well-being of the mother and/or the developing fetus.

Infant mortality rate in the United States is surprisingly high, mostly associated with prematurity, birth defects, maternal complications, sudden infant death syndrome, and birth-related injuries.

Women experiencing high-risk pregnancy require additional medical interventions and close observation to limit complications. Nurses play a key role through:
- identifying risk factors
- providing education
- implementing quality care

In this lesson, we will explore some of the risk factors and causes of high-risk pregnancy including maternal pregestational and gestational conditions.

## Section 3: Danger Signs During Pregnancy

The woman's body will naturally respond and adapt to a normal, healthy pregnancy. However, complications interfere with normal adaptation.

Let's discuss some signs in pregnancy that may indicate the presence of a problem requiring medical attention. The nurse will assess for these signs throughout the antepartum period and, when documented in the electronic medical record, many of these will trigger a maternal early warning (MEWS) alert requiring specific follow-up interventions. The healthcare provider may order various laboratory and diagnostic testing to analyze the symptoms. It is important for the nurse to educate the pregnant woman regarding these warning signs and to emphasize the need to report them. For more information regarding maternal early warning (MEWS) criteria and interventions, visit the Council on Patient Safety in Women's Health Care (n.d.).

- May Indicate: Dehydration/fluid-electrolyte imbalance; Circulation or nerve compression; Eclampsia

**Card 9 of 13**

- Danger Sign: Severe abdominal or epigastric pain that starts suddenly, worsens, and/or is sharp, stabbing, or cramp-like

**Card 10 of 13**

- Danger Sign: Pain and/or redness and swelling that is worse in one calf or leg when compared with the other
- May Indicate: DVT is brought on by pregnancy-related poor circulation and pooling of fluid in the lower extremities and/or the hypercoagulable state of pregnancy. If the DVT becomes mobile, the woman is at risk for myocardial infarction, stroke, or pulmonary embolism.

**Card 11 of 13**

- Danger Sign: Vaginal bleeding (no matter how slight)
- May Indicate: Spontaneous abortion (pregnancy loss prior to 20 weeks gestation); Abruptio placentae (the placenta separates from the uterus); Placenta previa (the placenta is covering the opening to the cervix)

**Card 12 of 13**

- Danger Sign: Vaginal drainage (aside from normal mucus)
- May Indicate: Infection; Ruptured or leaking amniotic fluid (the amniotic sac/fluid protects the fetus from pathogens; therefore, once the amniotic sac is ruptured, the fetus may need to be delivered soon to avoid uterine infection)

**Card 13 of 13**

- Danger Sign: Fatigue (severe)
- May Indicate: Cardiac issue or anemia brought on or worsened by pregnancy; Gestational diabetes; Depression

## Section 5: High-Risk Pregnancy: Common Causes

Most pregnancies are considered low-risk; however, sometimes preexisting factors or gestational complications develop that increase pregnancy risk.

Many psychosocial, medical, and obstetric factors directly impact the health of a pregnancy. Listed below are the most common risk factors impacting pregnancy. For more information regarding providing support for women after a severe maternal event, visit the Council on Patient Safety in Women's Health Care (2020).

### Tab: Genetics and Congenital Anomalies

Genetic abnormalities may interfere with normal fetal development and impact the health of the pregnancy, such as Down syndrome, heart defects, and neural tube defects.

**Explore:** For more information regarding common birth defects, visit the March of Dimes to learn more.

### Tab: Financial and Socioeconomic Burdens

Financial and socioeconomic burdens may prevent some clients from receiving adequate prenatal care, contributing to the risk of complications.

### Tab: Impaired Nutritional Status

Impaired nutritional status of the mother will impact fetal growth and development. Adolescents and clients with socioeconomic burdens are most at risk for nutritional deficiencies. Obesity in pregnancy may cause complications as well. Adequate weight gain, determined by prepregnancy BMI, and consumption of sufficient nutrients are essential to support the health of both the mother and the fetus.

### Tab: Maternal Behaviors

Maternal behaviors such as smoking, drinking alcohol, and drug use increase pregnancy risks. When a pregnant woman uses a substance, the fetus experiences the same systemic effects but often more severely and for a longer time.

- Tobacco is the most common harmful substance used by pregnant women. Nicotine causes vasoconstriction and reduces blood flow and oxygen through the placenta.
- Alcohol consumption can cause both mental delays and birth defects in the developing fetus. Intake during the first trimester has the largest negative impact. However, no amount of alcohol consumption at any time is considered safe during pregnancy.
- Pregnant women abusing opioids and amphetamines has increased dramatically over the last decade. Increased education and close monitoring is necessary.

### Tab: Age

Adolescents and women of advanced maternal age have increased pregnancy risks.

- Teenage pregnancy increases the risk for pregnancy-related hypertension, anemia, poor nutrition, preterm labor and birth, depression, substance abuse, and intimate partner violence.
- Delayed pregnancy increases the risk for miscarriage, multiple gestations, and chromosomal abnormalities, such as Down syndrome.

### Tab: Multifetal Pregnancies

Multifetal pregnancy exerts an increased burden on the mother's body systems and increases the risk for various complications, including pregnancy-related hypertension, anemia, preterm labor and birth, and postpartum hemorrhage.

### Tab: Environmental Pathogens

Environmental factors contribute to pregnancy risks through chemical exposures via air, water, soil, food, and other consumer products. The physiologic changes that occur in pregnancy increase sensitivity to chemicals that may impact the pregnancy and the woman's long-term health (Boyles et al., 2021).

## Section 6: Explore: Hyperemesis Gravidarum

Hyperemesis gravidarum refers to severe nausea and vomiting lasting beyond the first trimester, causing dehydration, nutritional deficits, weight loss, and fluid and electrolyte imbalances. Some women require hospitalization because the severe, chronic vomiting can lead to acid-base imbalance, and kidney and cardiac dysfunction. Left untreated, hyperemesis gravidarum may lead to fetal death.

The human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) hormone causes mild nausea and vomiting in the first trimester of pregnancy. In hyperemesis gravidarum, the HCG levels are very high or rise rapidly.

Medical treatment of hyperemesis gravidarum focuses on preventing severe vomiting and restoring physiologic balance using intravenous (IV) hydration and possibly enteral nutrition.

Pharmacologic treatment may include:

- pyridoxine (vitamin B6)
- doxylamine
- metoclopramide
- ondansetron
- proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole)
- antacids (calcium carbonate)
- H2 histamine blockers (famotidine)

Nurses will:

- Administer the medications as ordered.
- Encourage small, frequent meals consisting of bland, non-greasy foods.
- Teach other ways to counteract nausea.
- Provide empathy and compassion.

## Section 7: Ectopic Pregnancy

Ectopic pregnancy (sometimes called a tubal pregnancy) develops in the early stages of conception when the zygote implants in the fallopian tube (or other areas) rather than the uterus. The embryo continues to develop and grow until the narrow fallopian tube can no longer accommodate it.

### Tab: Pathophysiology

**Pathophysiology of Ectopic Pregnancy**

- Fertilization of the ova occurs in the fallopian tube. The zygote (first cell of a new person) travels from the fallopian tube to the uterus, which takes 3 - 4 days.
- During this time, the zygote is rapidly undergoing mitosis (cell division) and becomes a blastocyst consisting of a circular mass of cells with a fluid-filled cavity in the middle that is surrounded by 2 cell layers.
- If the zygote is prevented from leaving the fallopian tube and cell division continues, the blastocyst/embryo may implant itself in the fallopian tube, resulting in an ectopic pregnancy.

The woman's body will continue as if the pregnancy is normal and implantation has occurred in the uterus, resulting in missed menses and a positive pregnancy test. However, as the displaced embryo grows, she will begin to feel persistent or severe abdominal pain and experience vaginal spotting or bleeding that signal an ectopic pregnancy. If medical intervention does not occur, the fallopian tube will eventually rupture, causing severe bleeding. Ectopic pregnancies are a major cause of maternal death from hemorrhage when the condition is not quickly recognized.

(Video: "What is Ectopic Pregnancy?" — Cleveland Clinic, 2020 [1:56])

### Tab: Treatment

**Treatment of Ectopic Pregnancy**

Once the embryo has implanted itself in the wrong location, there is no longer any chance of viability. An ectopic pregnancy must be removed to avoid rupture of the fallopian tube and subsequent hemorrhaging. Surgical removal of an ectopic pregnancy before tubal rupture may damage the tube, resulting in an increased risk for future ectopic pregnancies. If the tube ruptures, future fertility is impacted. Depending on the size of the embryo, the ectopic pregnancy may be treated with medications such as methotrexate. The goal is to preserve the fallopian tube and the woman's future fertility.

Ectopic pregnancy is a very emotional experience for the woman, who is not only losing the pregnancy but also future fertility may be impacted. Nursing care focuses on early detection, pain control, and emotional support. Nurses who are administering methotrexate, a chemotherapeutic agent, to treat ectopic pregnancy must handle the medication carefully and follow all safety protocols associated with chemotherapeutic agents.

## Section 8: High-Risk Pregnancy: Maternal Substance Use

> About 7% of women reported using prescription opioid pain relievers during pregnancy. . . [and] of those, 1 in 5 reported misuse of prescription opioids, defined as getting them from a non-healthcare source or using them for a reason other than to relieve pain.
>
> — Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2024, Opioid Use section)

The use of alcohol, tobacco, or illicit drugs (cocaine, marijuana) by the mother increases her risk for medical complications and subsequent outcomes for the newborn. Substance abuse has become a more common occurrence in modern society.

### Tab: Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

**Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome**

Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) is a disorder in which infants exposed to opiates before birth demonstrate signs of drug withdrawal after birth. SSRIs and other antidepressants taken during pregnancy can result in NAS behaviors as well. Many newborn nurseries and intensive care units have dedicated neonatal abstinence syndrome areas just to manage the number of newborns with this condition. When it is determined that a newborn has neonatal abstinence syndrome, treatment begins to safely and comfortably detox the newborn.

According to 2020 data, about 6 newborns were diagnosed with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) for every 1,000 newborn hospital stays. That is about 1 baby diagnosed with NAS every 24 minutes in the United States, or more than 59 newborns diagnosed every day (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2024, Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome section). The number of babies born with NAS in the United States increased by 82% from 2010 to 2017.

### Tab: NAS Symptoms

**Symptoms of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome**

Signs of drug exposure in the neonate usually occur within 24 to 72 hours after birth, but symptoms may be delayed up to 2 weeks. Appearance of manifestations is determined by the specific drug, dose, and timing of the mother's last use. Drug use in the late third trimester results in a later onset of symptoms in the neonate, but the symptoms tend to be more severe.

Symptoms of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS) include:

- **behavioral:** jitteriness, tremors, increased muscle tone, exaggerated Moro reflex, high-pitched cry, difficulty to console, poor sleeping patterns, and yawning
- **feeding:** exaggerated rooting reflex, excessive sucking, uncoordinated sucking and swallowing, regurgitation, and weight loss
- **cardiovascular and respiratory:** tachypnea, apnea, retractions, and tachycardia
- **others:** fever, mottling, diaphoresis, and hypertension

### Tab: Common Substances' Impacts

**Common Substances' Impacts**

The long-term effects of neonatal abstinence syndrome are still being researched, but this condition is believed to possibly cause developmental delays (walking, talking, social, and thinking skills), motor problems (bones, muscles, and movement), behavior and learning problems, speech and language problems, sleep problems, ear infections, and vision problems (March of Dimes, 2021).

For more information regarding obstetric care for women with Opioid Use Disorder, visit the Council on Patient Safety in Women's Health Care (https://safehealthcareforeverywoman.org/).

## Section 9: High-Risk Pregnancy: Fetal-Maternal Blood Type Incompatibility

Certain hemolytic diseases can put the pregnancy and the newborn at risk for complications.

### Tab: Rh Incompatability

**Rh Incompatibility**

Rh incompatibility occurs when the mother is Rh- and the fetus is Rh+

- Fetal red blood cells contain the Rh antigen (Rh+). When the blood cells of the fetus enter maternal circulation, the mother's body reacts as it would to a foreign substance by developing antibodies to destroy the invading antigen. This antigen then enters fetal circulation and begins destroying red blood cells.
- This process often occurs at delivery but may occur from small leaks of fetal blood across the placenta during the pregnancy.
- Issues resulting from this incompatibility can typically be prevented with the use of antepartum RhoGAM.

**Prenatal Management of Rh Incompatibility**

- Rh immune globulin (RhoGAM) is administered to unsensitized Rh-negative women at 28 weeks' gestation to prevent sensitization.
  - RhoGAM is a commercial preparation of passive antibodies against Rh factor. It prevents the formation of active antibodies in maternal circulation.
- If the neonate is Rh+, RhoGAM administration is repeated within 72 hours after birth.
- RhoGAM should also be administered to unsensitized Rh negative women following an abortion, ectopic pregnancy, chorionic villus sampling, and amniocentesis, regardless if previously administered.

### Tab: ABO Incompatability

**ABO Incompatibility**

ABO incompatibility occurs when the mother is blood type O and the fetus is blood type A, B, or AB.

- A mother who has type O blood will naturally have anti-A and anti-B antibodies. These antibodies may cross the placenta and cause hemolysis of fetal red blood cells.
- If the mother is A and the infant is B, or if the mother is B and the infant is A, incompatibility can also occur.
- No specific prenatal care is needed. However, the nurse should be aware of the possibility of ABO incompatibility.

### Tab: Clinical Manifestations

**Clinical Manifestations**

As fetal red blood cells are destroyed, fetal bilirubin levels increase. Clinical manifestations of both Rh and ABO incompatibility include:

- Fetal
  - **anemia** caused by RBC destruction
  - **erythroblastosis fetalis** (rapid production of erythroblasts that cannot carry oxygen)
  - **fetal hydrops** (generalized edema) results from severe anemia and leads to congestive heart failure
- Neonatal
  - **pathologic jaundice** (hyperbilirubinemia) occurs within the first 24 hours after birth
  - **kernicterus** (a toxic accumulation of bilirubin in central nervous system tissues) resulting in brain damage or death

### Tab: Laboratory Testing

**Laboratory Testing**

- Indirect Coomb's test (maternal blood) is done in early pregnancy to determine if antibodies are present in maternal circulation.
  - If positive, the mother is termed "sensitized."
  - If negative, repeat testing is done at 28 weeks gestation.
- Direct Coomb's test (neonatal blood) is done after delivery to determine the presence of antibody-coated red blood cells in neonatal circulation.
- Direct bilirubin levels will be drawn after delivery to detect hyperbilirubinemia in the neonate.

U.S. National Library of Medicine. (2021, February 2). *Rh incompatibility*. MedlinePlus. https://medlineplus.gov/ency/presentations/100217_1.htm

## Section 10: High-Risk Pregnancy: Multifetal Pregnancy

Multifetal pregnancy (twins, triplets, etc.) contributes to complications for a variety of reasons. Pregnancy challenges each body system to adapt to the increasing demands of the fetus. When multiple fetuses are present, physiologic adaptation poses more potential problems.

The type of multifetal pregnancy may impact the degree of risk.

- Identical (monozygotic [MZ]) twins result from a fertilized ovum that splits in two. Fetuses usually have two amnions, a single chorion, and share a placenta; however, other configurations are possible.
- Fraternal twins (dizygotic [DZ]) start as two separate fertilized ovums that both implant in the uterus and are two separate pregnancies occurring at the same time.
- Triplets, quadruplets, and beyond result in even greater risks to the pregnancy.

### Tab: Medical Management and Nursing Care

Medical management and nursing care of multifetal pregnancies are focused on keeping the pregnancy healthy for as long as possible to prevent preterm delivery complications. This often involves reduced activity, frequent prenatal visits, close monitoring, constant nutritional assessment, and management of physical discomforts.

The woman and her support system may be concerned about increased financial, emotional, and health risks that accompany multifetal pregnancy. The nurse can provide emotional support, anticipatory guidance, education, and resources.

### Tab: Developing High-Risk Pregnancy

In many situations, the woman's body accommodates a multifetal pregnancy as it would a single pregnancy. However, women with preexisting conditions that are already complicated by pregnancy will likely have more problems.

Multifetal pregnancies are more likely to result in spontaneous abortion (of one or more fetuses), gestational hypertension, polyhydramnios (more than normal amniotic fluid volume), placenta previa, preterm deliveries, and tangled or nuchal (wrapped around the fetus' neck) cords.

### Tab: Amniotic Fluid Embolism

Multifetal pregnancies are also at higher risk for amniotic fluid embolism (also known as anaphylactic syndrome of pregnancy).

- Although rare, this medical emergency occurs during labor or within 30 minutes of delivery. It happens quickly and can be fatal. Amniotic fluid enters the mother's circulatory system and the body's allergic-like reaction causes rapid respiratory failure, hypotension, and cardiac arrest followed by blood clotting or hemorrhaging with no apparent explanation (Cleveland Clinic, 2019).
- Risk factors for amniotic fluid embolism include multifetal pregnancy, advanced maternal age, fetal distress, and operative deliveries (Cleveland Clinic, 2019).
- The nurse must act fast to save the lives of the mother and the fetus. While amniotic fluid embolism cannot be prevented, the nurse can help identify it as quickly as possible. Rapid emergency treatment by the healthcare team may include blood transfusions, supplemental oxygen, urgent delivery, and CPR.

## Section 12: High-Risk Pregnancy: Placenta Previa

Sometimes the placenta is not implanted in the correct location in the uterus, contributing to pregnancy risk. This complication is called placenta previa.

Ideally, the placenta should implant near the fundus, but instead it implants near (marginal) or completely over the cervical opening.

- The classic sign of placenta previa is the sudden onset of painless, bright red uterine bleeding occurring in the last half of pregnancy. Bleeding (scant or perfuse) results from tearing of the placental villi from the uterine wall as the lower uterine segment thins and the internal os dilates near term.
- Placenta previa is diagnosed through an ultrasound during the antepartum period. This condition is more common with delayed pregnancy, multiparity, fetal malposition, previous cesarean birth, and prior uterine surgery. Other risk factors include use of tobacco and the male fetus.
- Once the pregnancy reaches term, a cesarean section will be scheduled to safely deliver the fetus. If the amount of bleeding is heavy, close fetal and maternal monitoring is required. Delivery may need to occur before the pregnancy reaches term.

> **Reminder:** You will not perform a vaginal exam on a patient with any vaginal bleeding or a known placenta previa!

## Section 13: High-Risk Pregnancy: Abruptio Placentae

Another type of placental dysfunction is abruptio placentae (typically referred to as an abruption, or a placental abruption).

### Tab: Abruptio Placentae

**Abruptio Placentae**

Abruptio placentae occurs when the placenta prematurely detaches from the uterus prior to delivery, either partially or completely.

- Placental abruption is often caused by physical trauma (such as a car accident or a fall down the stairs) or pregnancy-related hypertension, but sometimes the cause is unknown.
- Classic signs of placental abruption include bleeding (which may be concealed in the uterus or apparent) and a sudden onset of severe abdominal pain with uterine rigidity. When evaluating with an external fetal monitor, the uterine resting tone is elevated and fetal heart tones are non-reassuring. If the amniotic sac ruptures, amniotic fluid will appear bloody.
  - Other signs include increased fundal height (as the uterus fills with blood).
- When the placenta detaches before the baby is delivered, the blood vessels from the mother feeding into the placenta are left exposed and hemorrhage occurs. The fetus no longer receives adequate blood flow and oxygen from the placenta. The degree of detachment determines the severity of maternal hemorrhaging and fetal hypoxia.
  - **Marginal abruption** occurs with partial detachment and external bleeding.
  - **Partial abruption** occurs with detachment of the center of the placenta only and concealed bleeding.
  - **Complete abruption** occurs with complete detachment and either concealed or apparent bleeding.

### Tab: Nursing Actions

**Nursing Actions**

- A placental abruption is a life-threatening emergency for both the mother and the fetus. Regardless of gestational age, abruptio placentae most likely results in fetal delivery. The healthcare team must recognize and respond quickly to early warning signs and act quickly.
- The nurse's role is to monitor fetal well-being and prepare for medical or surgical management.
- Medical management involves managing maternal hemorrhage and maintaining fetal perfusion until emergency delivery can occur. If the degree of placental separation is minor and fetal response is stable, delivery may be delayed.

## Section 16: Nutritional and Age-Related High-Risk Pregnancies

Adolescents and mothers of advanced maternal age both have an increased incidence of high-risk pregnancy. Delayed pregnancy, beyond the age of 35 years, is becoming more common in American society. Select each item below to learn more about these risks.

### Tab: Adolescents

- Adolescents who become pregnant often endure additional psychological and emotional stress during pregnancy.
  - For those with a support system, the risks are minimized. For those with psychosocial risk factors, pregnancy can be a difficult experience.
  - The nurse can greatly impact the health of an adolescent pregnancy with education and recognition of danger signs.
- Adolescent pregnancy presents physiologic complications. The nurse provides anticipatory guidance, emotional support, and physiologic nursing care.
  - Antepartum: increased risk for gestational hypertension, cephalopelvic disproportion (the pelvis too small or the wrong shape to accommodate vaginal delivery), abruptio placentae, low birth weight, intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), anemia, infections, and preterm delivery
  - Intrapartum: increased anxiety and fear, and decreased preparation for labor
  - Postpartum: impaired bonding and deficits of knowledge about infant care

### Tab: Delayed Pregnancy

- Women who become pregnant after the age of 35 have an increased risk for certain maternal and fetal complications, particularly when a chronic preexisting condition exists, such as diabetes mellitus or hypertension.
  - Maternal complications include spontaneous abortion and multiple gestations.
  - Fetal complications include an increased risk for chromosome disorders, such as Down syndrome.
  - Prenatal testing is done to diagnose complications.
- Advantages of delayed childbirth include higher maternal education level and socioeconomic status. This results in increased emotional and financial stability.

### Tab: Nutritional Factors

Nutritional factors can increase pregnancy risk.

- Impaired Nutrition
  - Socioeconomic status can contribute to a diet deficient in necessary nutrients.
  - Low prepregnancy weight and lack of adequate weight gain can contribute to pregnancy complications.
  - Eating disorders are associated with miscarriage, preterm birth, and congenital anomalies.
  - Skipping meals and convenience foods may result in diet deficiency.
- Obesity
  - Obesity in pregnancy can worsen related comorbidities like hypertension, diabetes mellitus and asthma.
  - Obese pregnant women are at increased risk for stillbirth, spontaneous abortion, gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, blood clots, preterm birth, and operative deliveries.
  - Nurses focus on helping the woman with education, support, and treatment.
- Multiparity
  - The number and spacing of pregnancies influence nutritional requirements.
  - Previous pregnancies can result in ongoing nutritional deficits.
- Substance Abuse
  - Cigarette smoking, excessive caffeine use, and alcohol and drug abuse can further complicate a pregnancy.
