## Section 3: Explore: Infection and Pregnancy

When the mother has an infection in pregnancy, it often impacts the fetus as well. However, not all infections in the mother reach the fetus but may still impact fetal well-being even if the mother is only mildly ill.

Pregnant women are more susceptible to vaginal infections due to hormone related anatomical changes, and some infections in the female can cause lifelong infertility.

The acronym TORCH is used to group certain organisms that can cross the placenta and reach the fetus.

### Slide 1: T = Toxoplasmosis

Toxoplasmosis is contracted from eating raw, contaminated meats or coming into contact with the feces of infected cats. Pregnant women do not need to give up their cats but should not change the cat litter prior to or during pregnancy.

Toxoplasmosis symptoms may not appear but can be passed on to the fetus and cause serious symptoms later in life, including blindness and mental disability. To prevent toxoplasmosis, pregnant women should avoid uncooked meats and contact with cat feces.

### Slide 2: O = Other Diseases

- Hepatitis A is caused by poor hand washing after defecation. Exposure in early pregnancy is most serious for the fetus. A preventive hepatitis A vaccine may be ordered by the pregnant woman's healthcare provider.
- Hepatitis B is caused by bodily fluid exchange. The hepatitis B vaccine is routinely given to all mothers and newborns. If the mother has hepatitis B, the newborn is at highest risk of becoming infected with hepatitis B during vaginal delivery. Hepatitis B can cause lifelong complications such as liver problems and cancer.
- Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) is transmitted from the mother to the fetus through the placenta. Prevention of transmission can be accomplished by administering antiretroviral medication during pregnancy.
- Vaginal infections caused by sexually transmitted diseases, such as syphilis, gonorrhea, and chlamydia, seriously impact the fetus when contracted during vaginal birth. Active vaginal infections may require a cesarean delivery to avoid passing the infection on to the newborn.

### Slide 3: R = Rubella

Rubella virus can be contracted by coming into contact with an unvaccinated person. Transmission to the fetus in the first trimester, when development is underway, causes serious congenital defects and fetal death. Rubella immunity is checked routinely during antepartal care. If the client is found to be nonimmune, she must be cautious to avoid exposure, and the vaccine will be given to her postpartum and prior to discharge.

### Slide 4: C = Cytomegalovirus

Cytomegalovirus (CMV) is a very common virus, and the infected person may not even exhibit symptoms. It is spread by means such as close contact, kissing, and breastfeeding. CMV infection during pregnancy can cause serious birth defects. The best means for preventing contraction is to reduce contact with saliva and urine from babies and young children.

### Slide 5: H = Herpes Genitalis

*Herpes genitalis* causes painful genital lesions that can infect the fetus in the uterus if the amniotic membranes rupture when there are active lesions. Cesarean delivery is done when there are active lesions present.

## Section 6: Explore: HIV and Pregnancy

For a pregnant woman living with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), it is no longer an inevitability that the infection will be passed on to the fetus. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2021), "advances in HIV research, prevention, and treatment have made it possible for many women with HIV to give birth to babies who are free of HIV" (para. 3). Since the early 1990s, the annual number of perinatal HIV transmissions in the U.S. has declined more than 95% (CDC, 2021). Perinatal HIV infections vary by race in the United States.

**Diagnoses of Perinatal HIV Infections in the US and Dependent Areas by Race/Ethnicity, 2018***

Most perinatal HIV diagnoses were among Black/African American children.+

- Black/African American:++ 65%
- White: 14%
- Hispanic/Latino**: 9%
- Multiple Races: 8%
- Asian: 5%

*In 2018, there were no cases of perinatal HIV among Native Hawaiians/Other Pacific Islanders and American Indians/Alaska Natives.

+Children refers to children under the age of 13.

++Black refers to people having origins in any of the Black racial groups of Africa. African American is a term often used for Americans of African descent with ancestry in North America.

**Hispanics/Latinos can be of any race.

Source: CDC. Diagnoses of HIV infection in the United States and dependent areas, 2018 (updated). *HIV Surveillance Report* 2020;31.

HIV testing is vital to reduce the rate of children born with HIV. Being tested prior to pregnancy can prevent transmission, and HIV testing is part of routine perinatal care in the United States (CDC, 2021). The CDC (2021) also states that a pregnant woman living with HIV can take medication to reduce the viral load of HIV to an undetectable level, minimizing the risk of perinatal transmission, as long as the medication is taken strictly as directed throughout the pregnancy and childbirth. Also, a cesarean delivery can help prevent perinatal HIV transmission (CDC, 2021). The CDC recommends that mothers do not breastfeed newborns to prevent HIV transmission after delivery (2021).

This is all good news, of course, but think about those who are struggling economically. Can they afford infant formula? Pregnant women with HIV who do not have access to healthcare will not have access to HIV medications. As we have learned, access to healthcare is a social determinant of health, particularly for certain racial groups in the United States.

## Section 7: Explore: TORCH Infections - Nursing Implications

The nursing implications of caring for women and newborns impacted by TORCH infections (organisms that can cross the placenta and reach the fetus) include:

**T: Toxoplasmosis**

Assess the woman for fatigue, muscle pains, and swollen glands. If the fetus survives pregnancy, assess the newborn for jaundice and increased bleeding.

**O: Other Infections**

- Hepatitis A: Practice proper hand hygiene, particularly to prevent transfer of fecal matter.
- Hepatitis B: The vaccination of newborns of non-positive mothers is routine. For newborns of mothers who are HBsAg-positive, the nurse will administer the hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG) vaccine prior to the routine Hepatitis B vaccine.
- Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV): The nurse will assess fetal well-being in HIV positive women and may administer antiviral medications. Newborns of HIV positive mothers will be asymptomatic at birth and may not test positive for the virus. These newborns should not breastfeed because the virus can enter through the breastmilk.
- Vaginal infections such as bacterial vaginosis or those caused by sexually transmitted diseases such as syphilis, gonorrhea, and chlamydia:
  - Bacterial vaginosis - Assess for vaginal discharge, itching and burning, and treat with medication as prescribed.
  - Syphilis - Administer antibiotic during pregnancy (typically penicillin); may cross the placenta in pregnancy so monitor for signs of congenital syphilis in the newborn (skin lesions, enlarged liver, pneumonia, hepatitis).
  - Gonorrhea (administer antibiotic during pregnancy, typically a cephalosporin) and chlamydia, which often appear together - Monitor for signs of ophthalmic infection in the newborn which will likely be treated with antibiotic eye ointment.

**R: Rubella**

If the fetus of a mother with active rubella survives to term or the newborn's mother is not immune to rubella, assess for infection in the newborn (fever, malaise, and a maculopapular rash on the face and body).

**C: Cytomegalovirus (CMV)**

Symptoms in pregnancy are vague and may be missed. Assess for infection in newborn present at birth (growth restrictions, jaundice, and microcephaly) and assist with diagnosis by obtaining urine specimen for culturing.

**H: Herpes Genitalis (herpes simplex virus [HSV])**

Assess for signs of preterm labor, intrauterine growth retardation (IUGR), and active vaginal lesions which appear as papules, vesicles, or shallow ulcers, and eventually crust over. Assess the neonate born vaginally to a woman with active lesions for signs of infection, lethargy, jaundice, and seizures.

## Section 9: Explore: Chorioamnionitis

Chorioamnionitis is an infection of the chorion and amnion (the amniotic membrane or sac) and the amniotic fluid usually caused by bacterial and viral organisms.

Most often, the infection results from the amniotic fluid being ruptured for a prolonged period of time. The longer the barrier surrounding and protecting the fetus from the outside world (and the organisms in the mother's vagina, anus or urinary tract) is ruptured, the longer these organisms can proliferate and cause infection and inflammation. Once the amniotic membranes have been ruptured for more than 24 hours, the risks to both mother and fetus increase more and more.

The nurse's clock begins ticking once the amniotic fluid has ruptured. It is important to note when it occurs in order to monitor the increasing risk for chorioamnionitis. The woman's temperature and heart rate will be monitored regularly for increases that may be signs of infection. Her abdomen may become tender to touch and foul-smelling vaginal discharge or leaking of amniotic fluid may be present.

It is best to deliver the fetus before infection sets in and increases the risk for additional issues for both mother and fetus. Amniocentesis can diagnose chorioamnionitis if the membranes are not ruptured; otherwise, the leaking amniotic fluid can be tested to diagnose. Antibiotics will likely be administered in addition to a speedy delivery.

The nurse must use excellent standard precautions and use personal protective equipment (PPE) to avoid further spreading of the infection. After delivery, the newborn may require additional testing and perhaps antibiotics to prevent or treat the infection.

## Section 10: Explore: Infection and Pregnancy - Nursing Implications

There are a lot of opportunities for exposure to blood and bodily fluids when providing antepartal and intrapartal care. Many organisms can be transmitted to the fetus in pregnancy and to the newborn during delivery. Many of these are spread by means of bodily fluids. Universal precautions are key to nursing care in all areas. The nurse plays a key role in preventing the spread of infection.

To prevent infection spread and exposure to these fluids as part of antepartal, labor, and delivery care, the nurse uses personal protective equipment (PPE) in situations such as:

- During amniotomy and delivery, the nurse wears clean gloves along with a mask, gown, and glasses.
- When assisting with breastfeeding (or touching the breasts for assessment, for example), the nurse wears clean gloves.
- When touching the perineal area to assist with perineal care or assessing a vaginal laceration or episiotomy, the nurse wears clean gloves.
- Before the newborn's first bath, the nurse wears clean gloves when handling the newborn and with all diaper changes.
- When suctioning or resuscitating the newborn, the nurse wears a facial barrier.

The use of internal monitoring electrodes, particularly the spiral fetal scalp electrode that actually penetrates the scalp, also introduces bacteria from the outside into the warm, moist, dark, uterine environment. To help prevent intrauterine infection, healthcare providers will put in effort in allowing the amniotic membranes to remain intact for as long as possible. This membrane is an infection barrier for the fetus against many organisms. Once ruptured, delivery should occur within 24 hours regardless of the presence of a known infection. A woman who has an episiotomy and/or laceration, or has had a cesarean birth, is obviously more at risk for infection. However, the infection is relatively uncommon.

Newborns are also at risk for infection, particularly from having come into contact with maternal uterine or vaginal bacteria or viruses. One method to prevent eye infections in newborns is the use of prophylactic erythromycin (antibiotic) ophthalmic ointment or drops. They are administered in each of the newborn's eyes to prevent infection from exposure to bodily fluids during vaginal delivery.

## Section 12: Explore: Pregnancy Infection Prevention

Nurses can help prevent pregnancy infection by providing client education.

### Tab: Vaccinations

- Rubella immunization should be obtained before a woman's childbearing years as part of the standard MMR vaccine in childhood. If this vaccination was not obtained and the woman is found to be rubella non-immune as part of routine antepartum testing, she should receive the vaccination postpartum.
- Hepatitis B immunization and vaccination will also be part of antepartum and postpartum care.
- The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2021) also recommends that pregnant women receive the flu vaccine.

### Tab: Safe Sex

Women should be encouraged to use safe sex practices to avoid contracting sexually transmitted diseases and hepatitis B.

### Tab: Toxoplasmosis

Pregnant women should avoid contact with raw meat and cat litter to prevent toxoplasmosis.

### Tab: Cytomegalovirus

Pregnant women should avoid or use caution when in close contact with others to avoid infections such as cytomegalovirus (e.g., kissing, breasts are being touched while breastfeeding).

### Tab: Vaginal Infections

Women with vaginal infections and *Herpes genitalis* lesions should use measures such as sitz baths, cotton underwear, avoidance of sexual contact during outbreaks, and keeping the genital area dry.

**Additional CDC recommendations:**

- To prevent bacteria and viral transmission, particularly hepatitis A, use proper hand hygiene with soap and water after using the bathroom, handling pets, being around people who are sick, touching soil or dirt, and changing diapers.
- Listeria is another infectious disease of concern during pregnancy which can be prevented by avoiding unpasteurized (raw) milk and foods (feta, brie, etc.).
- Group B strep status will be evaluated towards the end of pregnancy. This bacteria is commonly carried in the vagina without a woman's knowledge. A woman who is Group B strep positive can pass this infection to the newborn during vaginal delivery. Prophylactic antibiotics will be administered to these women and the newborns will be monitored closely.
