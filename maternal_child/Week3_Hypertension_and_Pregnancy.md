# Week 3: Hypertension and Pregnancy

## Section 1: Introduction to Hypertension and Pregnancy

Women who have chronic hypertension prior to pregnancy will experience a worsening of the condition and an increased likelihood of developing preeclampsia in pregnancy. Gestational hypertension (GH) is brought on by pregnancy and develops after 20 weeks gestation without the presence of protein in the urine.

This module will discuss hypertension brought on by pregnancy and the nursing implications.

---

## Section 3: Explore: Gestational Hypertension

Gestational hypertension develops during pregnancy after 20 weeks of gestation in a client who was previously normotensive. The mother will present with systolic blood pressure ≥ 140 mm Hg or a diastolic blood pressure ≥ 90 mm Hg. Some clients have chronic hypertension and the hormones and physical demands brought on by pregnancy will likely worsen the condition, possibly leading to preeclampsia, or even eclampsia. Gestational hypertension was previously called pregnancy-induced hypertension (PIH).

Different from chronic hypertension, gestational hypertension has these characteristics.

- It is a hypertensive condition unique to pregnancy, whether the woman has a history of hypertension or not.
- It is brought on by pregnancy.
- It develops after 20 weeks gestation without the presence of protein in the urine.
- It will resolve within 6-12 weeks postpartum.

**Interactive: Hypertension in Pregnancy**

**Gestational Hypertension Risk Factors:**
- Primigravida
- African American race
- Age (< 18 and > 35 increased occurrence)
- Personal and/or family history of hypertension
- Obesity
- Diabetes
- Gestational trophoblastic disease

**If gestational hypertension progresses and worsens, preeclampsia and eclampsia can develop.**

**Preeclampsia and Eclampsia — Progression Scenario:**
- A woman who is not pregnant has chronic hypertension.
- She is now 10 weeks pregnant and has a blood pressure of 150/80. She does not have gestational hypertension but is at risk for developing it later in pregnancy.
- She is now 25 weeks pregnant. Her blood pressure is 150/80 but she has no other symptoms. She has gestational hypertension.
- She is now 28 weeks pregnant. Her blood pressure is 150/80 and she has 2+ protein in her urine. The gestational hypertension has progressed to preeclampsia.

## Section 4: Explore: Preeclampsia and Eclampsia

### Tab: Preeclampsia & Eclampsia

When gestational hypertension progresses into preeclampsia, which can be mild or severe, high blood pressure temporarily impairs the kidneys' ability to filter albumin (a protein found in the blood), causing protein to appear in the urine (proteinuria). Kidney impairment also leads to generalized edema. Other body systems are affected, including:

- Intravascular volume is reduced as fluid moves out of the vessels (hemoconcentration), resulting in increased blood viscosity and tissue edema.
- Decreased liver perfusion leads to impaired liver function and elevated liver enzymes.
  - Hepatic edema causes epigastric or right upper quadrant abdominal pain.
- Cerebral edema leads to increased central nervous system irritability, causing headaches, hyperreflexia, ankle clonus, and seizures.
- Decreased blood flow to the retina causes visual disturbances and blurred vision.

When preeclampsia progresses into the very serious condition of eclampsia (formerly known as toxemia), seizures occur.

### Tab: Case Study

Meet Mary, a 17-year-old gravida 1, para 0 (G1P0) at 29 weeks' gestation who has arrived for her antepartum visit. Mary is African American and has no personal or family history of hypertension or diabetes. She is 5'4" and currently weighs 210 pounds. Her baseline blood pressure (BP) has been around 120/75.

Today, Mary's BP is 140/82. Her urine does not contain protein. We know that Mary is at risk for gestational hypertension (GH), preeclampsia or eclampsia because of her age, her primigravida pregnancy and gestational age being over 20 weeks, her African American race, and her obesity. At this point, Mary has likely developed GH. Her BP is elevated, she has several risk factors, but she does not have protein in her urine.

Mary comes back for another visit at 33 weeks' gestation. At this point, her BP is 160/92. Her face and ankles are swollen, she has gained 4 pounds since her last visit, and she has 1+ proteinuria. Mary has likely now progressed to mild preeclampsia.

**Characteristics of mild preeclampsia:**
- Blood pressure: increases in pregnancy; ≥ 140/90 mm Hg
- Edema: face, hands, ankles, periorbital edema (more ominous finding)
- Weight: 2nd trimester increase > 3 pounds or more per month; 3th trimester increase > 1 pound per week
- Urine: 1+ to 2+ protein; output is at least 500 mL/24 h

Mary could also develop severe preeclampsia; symptoms may appear suddenly.

**Characteristics of severe preeclampsia:**
- Blood pressure: ≥ 160/110 mm Hg x 2 (6 hours apart), even when on bedrest
- Edema: more obvious
- Weight: increases dramatically; 1-2 pounds gained in 3-7 days
- Urine: 3+ to 4+ protein; output < 500 mL/24 h
- Headache, blurred vision, and epigastric discomfort
- Hyperreflexia and clonus (indicates a seizure is likely to occur)

The most severe form of preeclampsia is eclampsia. If Mary's condition progresses to that point, she will actually begin having tonic-clonic seizures.

This type of seizure activity is characterized by tonic (muscular tension) and clonic (spasms, muscular contractions) phases. They are often followed by a coma and may lead to death.

## Section 6: Explore: Hypertension in Pregnancy — Nursing Care

The nurse assesses for the common clinical manifestations that can develop as a result of chronic hypertension and those that are unique to hypertension as a result of pregnancy. These symptoms can cue the nurse to recognize the condition may be worsening, as high blood pressure impacts the body systemically over time.

A woman with gestational hypertension (GH) will have elevated blood pressure but proteinuria will not be present. The nurse will monitor the blood pressure and assess for symptoms of progression to preeclampsia that impact many body systems.

### Tab: Nursing Actions

The nurse should monitor and assess:

**Blood pressure**
- Measure at least every 4 hours.
- Blood pressure will typically be ≥ 140 mm Hg systolic or ≥ 90 mm Hg diastolic.

**I&O, edema, and daily weights**
- Renal perfusion may be decreased due to high blood pressure.
- Decreased output
- Excessive or rapid weight gain accompanied by edema indicates fluid retention.
- Edema amount and pitting/non-pitting

**Protein in the urine**
- ≥ 1+ protein in the urine and/or ≥ 0.3 g protein in a 24-hour urine specimen

**Deep tendon reflexes**
- Measured using a scale that indicates the response when the reflex is tested
- Reflexes become increasingly hyperactive as preeclampsia progresses and are an indicator of impending seizures.
  - 4+ hyperactive, brisk with intermittent clonus (take note of how many beats of clonus occur)
  - 3+ brisk, slightly hyperactive
  - 2+ active, normal
  - 1+ sluggish, diminished
  - 0 no response

**Other symptoms**
- Symptoms that may indicate arterial spasms and retinal edema: blurred vision, double vision, spots before the eyes
- Symptoms that may indicate impending seizure activity: continuous headache, drowsiness, mental confusion, epigastric pain, or upset stomach (a particularly ominous sign)

### Tab: Nursing Care

In addition to close monitoring of the client, the nurse will:

- Administer medications as prescribed.
- Maintain seizure prevention and precautions.
- Provide emotional support, comfort measures, and a calm, quiet environment. Clients may be hospitalized during pregnancy at any time, and it may last through and/or after delivery. This can mean many weeks of hospitalized bed rest and is very stressful for the client, particularly if other children are at home and she is missing work.
- Assess fetal well-being through monitoring of fetal heart rate, pattern, and daily kick counts (fetal activity).

## Section 8: Explore: Diagnostic Tests and Treatment

What kind of diagnostic testing and medical management would be done for Mary? In addition to analyzing her assessment findings, her healthcare provider will also look for other diagnostic cues. Recall from previous knowledge how high blood pressure impacts the human body, restricting blood flow to key areas such as the heart, kidneys and liver. In addition, blood flow is restricted to the fetus. Diagnostic testing looks for these impacts on the body systems.

**Diagnostic tests:**
- Serum testing: hematocrit, blood urea nitrogen, complete blood cell count, clotting studies, liver enzymes, platelet count
- Urine: specific gravity and protein, 24-hour urine collection (measure creatinine and protein clearance)
- Fetal well-being and lung maturity (if labor induction becomes necessary): amniocentesis, ultrasound scan, nonstress tests, contraction stress tests, biophysical profiles

**Medical management:**

Treatment of Mary's preeclampsia will be like treating hypertension in any client, except that in pregnancy, the health of the fetus is a factor too. Mary's healthcare provider will likely treat her with:

- Activity restrictions and frequent rest; a left lateral recumbent position promotes best perfusion to the fetus
- Hospitalization, depending on severity, with IV access available
- Well-balanced diet with adequate protein; avoid high sodium foods
- Severe preeclampsia or eclampsia IV medications: magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄) to prevent seizure activity, antihypertensives for BP > 160/100 mm Hg

**Prognosis:**

The only cure for preeclampsia and gestational hypertension is delivery of the baby, and even 6 weeks later the mother may have residual effects (bleeding problems, liver rupture, stroke, and death).

## Section 10: Explore: Seizure Precautions

Preeclampsia can progress to the most severe form, eclampsia, in which seizures are very likely, often followed by a coma or even death. For women with severe preeclampsia, seizure precautions must be taken.

### Tab: Seizure Precautions

Seizure precautions include:

- Noise and stress reduction in the environment
- Padded side rails
- Preparation for emergency delivery; cesarean may be needed quickly
- Keep suction and an oxygen mask nearby
- Prepare to administer emergency seizure medication:
  - hydralazine — antihypertensive vasodilator
  - magnesium sulfate — Central Nervous System (CNS) depressants
  - magnesium sulfate antidote — calcium gluconate, in case of toxicity of magnesium sulfate

### Tab: In the Event of a Seizure

In the event of a seizure:

- Do not leave the client unattended and press the call light for additional help.
- Turn the client to the left lateral position for perfusion and aspiration prevention.
- Mentally note when the seizure started and the phases.
- Monitor the client's airway, providing supplemental oxygen by mask at 8-10 L/minute.
- Monitor oxygen saturation (SpO2).
- Suction the mouth and nose after the seizure to prevent aspiration.
- Observe fetal heart rate and pattern for non-reassuring signs (increased/decreased rate and/or variability, late decelerations), which may recover with the use of oxygen.
- Notify the healthcare provider.

## Section 12: Explore: HELLP Syndrome

Severe preeclampsia can develop into what is known as HELLP syndrome. It has unique diagnostic characteristics showing severe liver impairment and occurs in up to 12% of preeclampsia cases (Cleveland Clinic, 2021).

Nursing assessment for early detection and treatment is crucial to prevent severe complications and health risks. Indications of HELLP syndrome include:

- Peripheral blood smear will reveal hemolysis of red blood cells
- Development of thrombocytopenia (platelets < 100,000/mm³)
- Development of abnormal elevations of the liver enzymes alanine aminotransferase (ALT) or aspartate aminotransferase (AST)
- Severe continuous pain in the right upper quadrant or the epigastric area described as a "stabbing" pain over the region of the liver
- Malaise and flu-like symptoms that worsen at night

Treatment is the same as with preeclampsia, along with the possible need for blood transfusion if bleeding occurs and persists. Preterm birth may be needed.

For more information regarding preeclampsia for healthcare providers: Preeclampsia Foundation — https://www.preeclampsia.org/healthcare-providers

**Remember HELLP mnemonic:**
- H: hemolysis
- EL: elevated liver enzymes
- LP: low platelet count (less than 100,000/mm³)
