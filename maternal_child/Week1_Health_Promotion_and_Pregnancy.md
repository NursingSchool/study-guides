# Week 1: Health Promotion and Pregnancy

## Section 1: Introduction to Health Promotion

The nurse plays a key role in the prevention of pregnancy and childbirth-related complications through the delivery of quality health care preconception (before pregnancy), prenatally (during pregnancy), and interconception (between pregnancies). For many women, family planning and sexual health services are the only medical care they receive. This presents many preconception-related health promotion opportunities, risk identification opportunities, and risk reduction opportunities in order to improve pregnancy outcomes.

This concept will review the importance of health promotion during pregnancy.

## Section 3: Explore: Healthy People 2030

Once pregnancy is determined, antepartum (prenatal) care should begin. Women are often very motivated to improve health practices during pregnancy for the benefit of their unborn child, providing many general health benefits and pregnancy-related health promotion opportunities. The cognitive and physical development of infants and children may be influenced by the health, nutrition, and behaviors of their mothers during pregnancy and early childhood. Increasing the number of pregnant women receiving early and adequate prenatal care is a major focus of many organizations, such as Healthy People 2030.

Healthy People 2030 focuses on preventing pregnancy complications and maternal deaths and helping women stay healthy before, during, and after pregnancy, as women's health can have a major impact on the infant's health and well-being.
- Women who get recommended health care services before they become pregnant are more likely to be healthy during pregnancy and to have healthy babies.
- Strategies to help women adopt healthy habits and get health care before and during pregnancy can help prevent pregnancy complications.
- Interventions to prevent unintended pregnancies can help reduce negative outcomes for women and infants.
- Strategies to help pregnant women get medical care and avoid risky behaviors—like smoking or drinking alcohol—can also improve health outcomes for infants.

The Healthy People 2030 website offers many examples of the initiatives in place, such as preventing drug and alcohol abuse and family planning.

## Section 4: Explore: Preconception Care and Health Promotion

Preconception care and health promotion are important to women's health. The following issues are of significance:

### Tab: Prevention of Unintended and Adolescent Pregnancies

Often, women do not or cannot seek prenatal healthcare services when the pregnancy is unintended. Preventable birth defects, low birth weight, and postpartum depression occur.

### Tab: Early Detection and Prevention

Early detection and prevention of gestational diabetes, pre-eclampsia, sexually transmitted infections (STIs), and intimate partner violence is important before they negatively impact the pregnancy and/or cause unwanted infertility.

### Tab: Pre-Pregnancy Nutritional Status

Pre-pregnancy nutritional status also plays an important role in pregnancy outcomes. Early nutrition support includes the reversal and prevention of obesity. Obesity places women at higher risk of pregnancy and childbirth complications, including high blood pressure and diabetes.

### Tab: Early Education

The use of some prescription and over-the-counter medications may cause birth defects. Approximately 3% of women take these medications in early pregnancy.

### Tab: Early, Daily Folic Acid Supplementation

Folic acid is believed to greatly decrease the risk for newborn neural tube defects, such as spina bifida, which form in the early weeks of fetal development. Many women are not even aware they are pregnant at this point. For this reason, a folic acid supplement is recommended for all women of childbearing age. Increasing the proportion of women of childbearing age who have optimal red blood cell folate concentrations is a Healthy People 2030 goal. The most recent data provided indicates that only 82.6% of non-pregnant women aged 15-44 years had optimal red blood cell folate concentrations.

## Section 5: Explore: Nutrition During Pregnancy

Nutrition is important since maternal nutritional status is a significant health factor that can prevent a variety of health problems for both the mother and baby. When a pregnant woman has inadequate nutrition, the baby can be born with a low birth weight or preterm. There is continuing evidence to indicate that maternal nutrition and lifestyle have long-term effects on children's health. Nutritional assessment starts in the preconception stage and continues throughout pregnancy.

A widely used government program called Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC) provides federal grants to states for supplemental foods, healthcare referrals, and nutrition education for low-income pregnant women and breastfeeding postpartum women and to infants and children up to age 5 who are found to be at nutritional risk.

### Slide 1: Key Points

The goal of preconception nutrition is for all women to achieve a desirable weight before conception because fetal risks increase when the pregnant woman is significantly underweight or overweight when pregnancy begins.

### Slide 2: Key Points

Adequate nutrition is important during pregnancy. However, there are many social determinants that may affect a woman's ability to consume adequate nutrition. As a nurse, it is important to assess the pregnant woman's access to nutritional foods and, if needed, find ways to obtain an adequate supply of food.

### Slide 3: Key Points

The body needs vitamins and other nutrients to stay healthy and strong. During pregnancy, the growing baby depends on these nutrients for growth and development. Recommended prenatal vitamins, along with eating healthy foods, can help clients get the nutrients needed to support a healthy pregnancy.

### Slide 4: Key Points

Vegan diets do not contain sufficient quantities of vitamins B12 and B6, calcium, and zinc; therefore, pregnant women following this dietary pattern must consume fortified foods and take prenatal vitamins.

### Slide 5: Reference

USDA Food and Nutrition Services — Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children (WIC)

## Section 8: Explore: Weight Gain Recommendations During Pregnancy

During pregnancy, appropriate weight gain is important. Less than or greater than expected weight gain can be due to preexisting conditions and can increase the likelihood of complications. However, more important than weight gain is the nature of the nutrients contained in the food consumed.

Recommended weight gain during pregnancy is based on the client's pre-pregnancy body mass index (BMI).

Women are not "eating for two." The amount of calories that should be consumed during pregnancy is based on the trimester:
- **First trimester:** same as pre-pregnancy
- **Second trimester:** an additional 340 kcal/day
- **Third trimester:** an additional 452 kcal/day

**Practice Questions — Recommended weight gain during pregnancy:**

Q: 18-year-old pregnant woman, pre-pregnancy BMI 17 (underweight)
A: **12.5-18 kg / 28-40 lb**

Q: 41-year-old pregnant woman, pre-pregnancy BMI 32 (obese)
A: **5-9 kg / 11-20 lb**

Q: 35-year-old pregnant woman, pre-pregnancy BMI 29 (overweight)
A: **6.8-9.1 kg / 15-25 lb**

## Section 10: Explore: Physical Activity

Click through each slide below to explore more.

### Slide 1: Physical Activity

Changes in activity are not necessary during a healthy pregnancy; however, high risk activities should be avoided. Exercise in pregnancy is typically recommended for many benefits including childbirth preparation.

### Slide 2: Varicose Veins

Varicose veins and dependent edema are common. They are believed to be caused by increased intra-abdominal pressure. Support hose and elevating the legs are common remedies.

### Slide 3: Growing Uterus

The growing uterus puts pressure on and stretches the ligaments in the lower abdomen, commonly causing round ligament pain and tenderness in the area. Certain exercises and supportive garments can help reduce the pain and tenderness.

### Slide 4: Kegel Exercises

Kegel exercises strengthen the pubococcygeal muscles that stop the flow of urine. These muscles support the pelvic floor, bladder, and urethra. These muscles can become weakened during pregnancy and childbirth due to increased pressure and trauma. Kegel exercises (tightening and relaxing these muscles) should be done daily and for the rest of the woman's life.

### Slide 5: Rest and Sleep

Fatigue begins early in pregnancy as the body adjusts to pregnancy-related changes and energy needs. Napping and lying on the left side to promote oxygenation through the placenta are recommended. As pregnancy progresses, it becomes increasingly difficult for women to find a comfortable position, which makes resting and sleeping more challenging. Later in pregnancy, the uterus may compress the vena cava, then the supine position needs to be avoided as it can cause hypotension. The gravid uterus pressing on the vena cava can result in syncope, vertigo, and decreased perfusion to the fetus. A side-lying position is recommended.

### Slide 6: Pillows

Use of pillows under the legs and abdomen, elevation of the feet, and a side-lying position will improve rest and sleep during pregnancy.

## Section 11: Explore: Dental Care

Clients should tell their dentists if they think they could be pregnant. Keeping up regularly scheduled checkups is important. Increased progesterone and estrogen in a woman's body during pregnancy can increase the risk for certain oral health problems. Gums are more likely to become inflamed or infected because of hormonal changes and increased blood flow during pregnancy.

Some women may fear having dental work during pregnancy. However, there is no proven increase in preterm births or other adverse outcomes for pregnant women who received dental care.

Food choices could change during pregnancy and these choices could affect dental health—a desire to eat more sweets or salty foods, for example.

Some women brush and floss their teeth less than before they got pregnant due to fatigue, nausea, or gum tenderness.

## Section 13: Explore: Drug and Alcohol Use During Pregnancy

During pregnancy, women should not use tobacco, alcohol, marijuana, illegal drugs, or prescription medications for nonmedical reasons. Avoiding these substances and getting regular prenatal care are important to having a healthy pregnancy and a healthy baby. Counseling and treatment are available if a client is having a hard time quitting unhealthy substances.

Early education and interventions regarding smoking, alcohol, and substance abuse are critical to preventing fetal, infant, and childhood disorders and poor outcomes. The following highlights some of the harm that could result from women using drugs and alcohol during pregnancy.

**Drug and Alcohol Use During Pregnancy — Potential Harms:**

**Congenital Disorders/Birth Defects**
- drug exposure

**Developmental and Behavioral Disorders**
- legal and illegal substances

**Drug Withdrawal Disorders**
- prescription drugs
- illegal substances

**Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD)**
- no minimum amount of alcohol is deemed safe during pregnancy

**Low Birth Weight**
- smoking, drug use

**Miscarriage**
- smoking, drug use

**Pre-term labor/Pre-term birth**
- drug use—often due to stimulants

**Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDs)**
- tobacco use

## Section 15: Explore: Antepartal Care Health Promotion

According to The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC, 2024), more than 700 American women die each year from pregnancy or delivery related complications, two-thirds of which could be prevented. The CDC has a campaign to empower pregnant women and her support systems to recognize and verbalize the warning signs of serious pregnancy-related complications.

This health promotion campaign includes interactive symptom activities. Resources include:

- **Listening and Acting Quickly could help save her life** — review and save this PDF detailing urgent maternal warning signs and how you can help. (PDF, CDC, 2021)
- **Urgent Maternal Warning Signs** — What to watch for during pregnancy and in the year after delivery. Review the urgent maternal warning signs. (CDC, 2024)
- **Hear Their Stories** — Help prevent pregnancy-related deaths. Women share potentially life-saving messages about urgent warning signs. (YouTube, CDC, 2021)

**Reference:** Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2024). *Hear her campaign: An overview.*
