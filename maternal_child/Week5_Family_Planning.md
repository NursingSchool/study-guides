## Section 1: Introduction to Family Planning

The major functions of a nurse in family planning are to provide care, reassure the client, and provide information. During the consultation, the client may feel a greater rapport with the nurse than with the healthcare provider and may show greater willingness to speak about medical history or significant symptoms. The nurse carefully explains prescriptions after the provider consultation and instructs the client if any additional tests or procedures are required.

Nurses who participate in client education for family planning must understand birth control methods. Client education is the focus of this module. The method of family planning selected by a client is personal and may involve cultural, religious, and lifestyle influences. The nurse must reinforce information received by the healthcare provider and help support the client in their decision making.

This module will review the role of the nurse in caring for clients during the family planning process.

## Section 2: Prepare: Family Planning

### Q1: Birth Control Implant

**Question:** A woman asks the nurse, "What should I know about the birth control implant in the arm and how it prevents pregnancy?" What should the nurse explain about the impact of birth control implants? Select all that apply.

**Correct answers:**
- There may be some temporary tenderness or swelling around the implant after insertion.
- It provides continuous contraception for 3 years.
- A possible side effect is irregular bleeding (spotting), especially in the first 6-12 months.

**Feedback:** The implant is placed in the client's upper inner arm with the use of a local anesthetic. There may be some temporary tenderness or swelling around the implant. A possible side effect is irregular bleeding (spotting), especially in the first 6-12 months. The failure rate of this device during the first year of use is 0.05% (99.5% effective). The device releases progestin continuously to provide 3 years of contraception.

### Q2: Pregnancy Prevention

**Question:** Fill in the blanks.

- Abstinence means **avoiding any activity that would allow sperm to enter the vagina**.
- The only completely effective method of preventing pregnancy is: **abstinence**.

**Feedback:** Abstinence as a means to avoid pregnancy is considered avoiding any activity that would allow sperm to enter the vagina. It is the only completely effective method of preventing pregnancy.

It is important to note that sexually transmitted infections (STI) can still be communicated through other means of intimacy. Celibacy is the only completely effective method to prevent STI and pregnancy.

## Section 3: Explore: Purpose of Family Planning

Family planning is a way of thinking and living that is adopted to delay, prevent, or actualize a pregnancy. It is also called a reproductive life plan. Each plan is unique to the client's personal goals and dreams.

The nurse will counsel and educate clients on contraceptive methods to prevent unintended pregnancies. Education must also be directed to the individual needs of the client. Culture, personal concerns, and preferences should all be taken into consideration.

*Scenario: Mary is a 19-year-old client going to her gynecologist appointment to discuss family planning. She is dating a man named Jake and desires to be sexually active. She states that she does not want to get pregnant because she is still in college and eager to have a career as a nurse after graduation.*

*Mary wants to know her options and has never really thought about family planning. She and her previous boyfriend used condoms when they had sex. Right now, Mary does not want to get pregnant and wants to delay having a baby for at least 5 years. Once she has a child, she will probably wait at least another 2 years to get pregnant again.*

**Note:** Although using condoms is part of family planning, learning more and being proactive are critical for those who are sexually active.

## Section 4: Explore: Client Education and Contraception Methods

Family planning methods include natural family planning, barrier methods, oral contraceptives, transdermal contraceptive patch, contraceptive vaginal ring, long-acting reversible contraceptives, and sterilization. The nurse must assess the client's goals and support their decision making. Safety, protection from sexually transmitted infections, effectiveness, acceptability, convenience, benefits, side-effects, effects on spontaneity, availability, cost, personal preference, and religious and cultural beliefs all must be taken into consideration.

**Contraception methods include:** condom, female condom, sponge, cervical cap, oral contraceptives, vaginal ring, intrauterine device, hormone injections, contraceptive implant, diaphragm, calendar method, contraceptive patch, spermicide, and surgical sterilization.

## Section 5: Explore: Natural Family Planning Methods

Some women prefer not to have any chemicals or foreign materials introduced into their bodies. This could be related to religious beliefs or just personal preferences. Natural family planning methods are also called fertility awareness methods and use physiologic cues to predict ovulation. These methods can also assist a client who is trying to become pregnant. None of these methods provide any protection against sexually transmitted infections.

Natural family planning methods require the client to be highly motivated because they must abstain from intercourse during certain times of the month. Errors in predicting ovulation may lead to pregnancy. These methods (all types) have a **24% failure rate** during the first year of use.

### Calendar Method

**Method:** based on the assumption that ovulation occurs approximately 14 days before the onset of menses

**Considerations:**
- The client must abstain or use a barrier method during the days calculated to be fertile.
- This method is unreliable because not all women have regular menstrual cycles.

### Cervical Mucus Method

**Method:** Cervical mucus is checked daily to estimate when ovulation occurs.
- scant, thick, and sticky = before and after ovulation
- thin, slippery, and clear (spinnbarkeit) = during ovulation

**Considerations:**
- Vaginal infections, sexual arousal, some medications, and semen can affect mucus consistency.
- Avoid intercourse from the first thin, slippery mucous until 4 days after the last thin, slippery mucous.

### Lactational Amenorrhea Method

**Method:** Lactation inhibits ovulation because prolactin interferes with the secretion of gonadotropin-releasing hormone and luteinizing hormone.

**Considerations:**
- The frequency, intensity, and duration of nursing are important for inhibiting ovulation.
- The use of formula or solid foods decreases the frequency and duration of breastfeeding.
- The menstrual cycle generally resumes by 6 months after birth, even in women who breastfeed exclusively.

### Basal Body Temperature (BBT)

**Method:** Slight elevation in temperature occurs just before ovulation.

**Considerations:**
- Basal body temperature (BBT) rises near ovulation and remains higher during the second half of the menstrual cycle.
- BBT remains higher if conception occurs and falls about 2–4 days before menstruation if there is no conception.
- Oral temperature should be checked daily upon awakening in the morning but before rising from bed.
- BBT may be altered by illness, inadequate sleep, disrupted sleep, alcohol intake, and sleeping under an electric blanket.

### Symptothermal Method

**Method:** Combines assessment of basal body temperature and cervical mucous daily.

**Considerations:**
- Consider other symptoms that signal ovulation, such as weight gain, abdominal bloating, and pain.
- Using all three indicators of ovulation increases effectiveness.

### Coitus Interruptus

**Method:** the removal of the penis from the vagina before ejaculation

**Considerations:**
- Requires great control by the male partner.
- Preejaculate fluid may contain sperm.
- The least reliable of all methods, with a **54% failure rate** in the first year of use.

## Section 6: Explore: Barrier Methods of Birth Control

These barrier methods of contraception provide a barrier that prevents the male sperm from reaching the female egg. Some of these methods can be used to decrease exposure to sexually transmitted infections.

**Failure rates during the first year of use vary by method:**
- Male condom = 18%
- Female condom = 21%
- Diaphragm/Cervical Cap = 12%
- Sponge = 12%
- Spermicide (used alone) = 28%

### Spermicide

**Method:** chemical that kills sperm

**Considerations:**
- Available in many forms (creams, gels, tablets, suppositories, film).
- Effective for less than 1 hour.
- Most effective when used in combination with another barrier method.
- Available without a prescription, inexpensive, and easy to use.
- It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.

### Male Condom

**Method:** sheath that covers the penis to prevent sperm from entering the vagina

**Considerations:**
- Latex condoms provide the best protection against sexually transmitted infections.
- Avoid if allergic to latex.
- Other types of condoms are available but are more likely to break and do not provide adequate protection against infections.
- Available without a prescription and inexpensive, but does interfere with spontaneity.

### Female Condom

**Method:** sheath inserted into the vagina with a flexible ring inside the closed end that fits over the cervix; another ring extends outside the vagina to partially cover the perineum

**Considerations:**
- Female and male condoms should not be used together.
- Provides protection against sexually transmitted infections.
- Available without a prescription but more expensive than male condoms.
- Avoid if allergic to latex.
- It may be inserted up to 8 hours before intercourse.

### Sponge

**Method:** soft polyurethane that traps and absorbs semen and contains spermicide when activated with water

**Considerations:**
- Provides contraception for 24 hours without the need to add spermicide for repeated intercourse.
- Available without a prescription and easy to use.
- It can be inserted just before or hours ahead of intercourse.
- It should remain in place for 6 hours after intercourse.
- It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.

### Diaphragm

**Method:** latex dome surrounded by a spring or coil that covers the cervix and prevents passage of sperm

**Considerations:**
- Place spermicide into the dome and around the rim before insertion.
- Do not use for the first 6 weeks after birth.
- Avoid if allergic to latex or has history of toxic shock syndrome.
- It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.
- It may be inserted up to 6 hours before intercourse and must be left in place for at least 6 hours after intercourse.

### Cervical Cap

**Method:** cuplike device placed over the cervix to prevent sperm from entering

**Considerations:**
- Similar to a diaphragm but must be fitted by a healthcare provider.
- Place spermicide on both sides and in the vagina after placing the cap.
- It does not protect against sexually transmitted infections.
- It may be inserted up to 6 hours before intercourse and must be left in place for at least 6 hours after intercourse.
- Avoid if allergic to latex or have history of toxic shock syndrome.

## Section 7: Explore: Nursing Education for Barrier Methods

Now that you have learned about how barrier methods work, let's look at the nursing education needed for your clients who are interested in using a barrier method of birth control.

**Diaphragm Instruction:**
- How to insert and remove the device — allow for practice
- Insert prior to intercourse, but must remain in for 6–8 hours after intercourse
- Empty bowel and bladder prior to insertion
- Cleaning, storing, and inspect for damage
- Must be refitted after pregnancy or weight change

**Cervical Cap Instruction:**
- How to insert and remove the device — allow for practice
- Cleaning, storing, and inspect for damage
- Cannot be used with cervical inconsistencies or pelvic inflammatory disease

**Male Condom Instruction:**
- Application: roll sheath along erect penis, leave room at the end for semen
- Removal: hold condom after ejaculation and when withdrawing from the vagina to prevent emptying sperm in the vagina
- Fingernails may tear the condom
- Petroleum jelly deteriorates latex condoms, only use water-based lubricants
- Should be applied only one time

**Female Condom Instruction:**
- How to lubricate, insert and remove device — allow for practice
- More expensive than male condoms

## Section 10: Explore: Hormonal Birth Control

Hormonal birth control is considered safe for most women today as it contains a relatively small amount of hormones. All hormonal methods of birth control require a prescription for use.

### Combination Oral Contraceptives

**Description:**
- contains progestin and estrogen or progestin only
- prevents ovulation, but does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
- should be avoided in the first month postpartum and if breastfeeding (may use progestin only)
- must be taken daily

**Side effects:** weight gain, nausea and vomiting, breakthrough bleeding, breast tenderness, headache, and irritability; risks for thromboembolism, stroke (especially over age 35 and a smoker), gallstones, benign liver tumors, myocardial infarction

**Benefits:**
- does not interfere with spontaneity
- highly effective (91%)
- regulates menstrual cycle
- ovulation returns within 3 months of discontinuation
- reduces risk for benign breast disease and ovarian, endometrial, and colorectal cancers
- improves acne, endometriosis, bone mass, and dysmenorrhea

**Teaching:**
- If client misses one pill, take as soon as remember and take the next dose at the usual time.
- If client misses two pills in the first two weeks, take both as soon as remember, then take the next dose at the usual time.
- If client misses two pills in the third week, take one active pill each day until finished, and discard the inactive pills. Start a new pack the same day. Use backup contraception for 7 days if unable to start a new pack of pills.
- Use backup contraceptives if taking certain medications, including anticonvulsants, antiretrovirals, and rifampin.

### Emergency Contraception

**Description:**
- contains levonorgestrel
- taken due to contraceptive failure or unprotected intercourse
- not used as primary birth control
- delays or inhibits ovulation
- involves taking one or two pills as soon as possible (up to 120 hours) after unprotected intercourse

### Hormone Injections

**Description:**
- medroxyprogesterone acetate injected intramuscularly or subcutaneously to prevent ovulation for 15 weeks
- injections scheduled every 13 weeks
- does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
- may be used with breastfeeding
- failure rate during the first year of use is 6%

**Side effects:** amenorrhea, breakthrough bleeding, headaches, decreased libido, weight gain, depression, decreased bone density; return to fertility may take 6–12 months or more after discontinuing

**Benefits:**
- does not interfere with spontaneity
- avoids the need for daily use of medication

### Contraceptive Implant

**Description:**
- single rod implant inserted subcutaneously into the upper inner arm
- releases progestin continuously and provides 3 years of contraception
- does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
- may be used with breastfeeding
- highly effective (99.5%)

**Side effects:** irregular bleeding

**Benefits:**
- does not interfere with spontaneity
- safe during lactation
- fertility returns immediately after removal

### Intrauterine Device (IUD)

**Description:**
- inserted into the uterus to provide continuous pregnancy prevention
- two types: copper and levonorgestrel containing
- copper effective for 10 years; levonorgestrel effective for 3–5 years
- does not protect against sexually transmitted infections
- do not use in women with history of pelvic infections, ectopic pregnancy, or bleeding disorders
- may be used with breastfeeding
- highly effective (99.2–99.8%)

**Side effects:** increased menstrual bleeding, ectopic pregnancy, pelvic infection, perforation of the uterus, infertility

**Benefits:**
- does not interfere with spontaneity
- low long-term cost
- fertility returns once device removed
- safe for use in women who cannot use estrogen-containing contraception

### Transdermal Contraceptive Patch

**Description:**
- patch that releases small amounts of estrogen and progestin which are absorbed through the skin to suppress ovulation
- new patch applied weekly to a different site for 3 weeks, then removed for 1 week to allow for menstruation
- as effective as combination oral contraceptive pills
- no protection against sexually transmitted infections
- may not be used with breastfeeding
- highly effective (91%)

**Side effects:** breakthrough bleeding, breast tenderness, headaches, skin reactions; less effective in women who weigh more than 198 lbs; risk for blood clots is higher than with pills

**Benefits:**
- used weekly instead of daily
- fertility returns within one month of discontinuation
- adherence is not disrupted by water or sweat
- does not interfere with spontaneity
- regulates menstrual cycle

### Contraceptive Vaginal Ring

**Description:**
- soft, flexible vinyl ring that releases small amounts of progestin and estrogen continuously to prevent ovulation
- inserted into the vagina and left in place for 3 weeks
- after 3 weeks the ring is removed for 1 week to allow for menstruation then reinserted
- no protection against sexually transmitted infections
- may not be used with breastfeeding
- highly effective (91%)

**Side effects:** similar to combination oral contraceptives; vaginitis; vaginal discharge

**Benefits:**
- does not interfere with spontaneity
- no fitting required

### ACHES — Warning Signs for Hormonal Birth Control

Hormonal birth control that includes both progestin and estrogen (combination oral contraceptives, vaginal ring, or transdermal patch) increases the risk for thrombosis and high blood pressure, which can cause a heart attack or stroke. These are rare but serious side effects of hormonal birth control.

The risks are increased in women over 35 years old and in women who smoke. A black box warning label exists for smoking and oral contraceptives.

**Contraindications for use:** pregnancy, thromboembolic disorders, uncontrolled hypertension, migraines, breast or endometrial cancer

**Remember ACHES** — Seek medical care while on hormonal birth control if you experience:
- **A**bdominal pain
- **C**hest pain
- **H**eadaches
- **E**ye problems
- **S**peech disturbances, severe leg pain, or swelling

## Section 11: Explore: Surgical Methods: Permanent Methods

Permanent methods of sterilization are performed through surgical procedures. These procedures eliminate the chance of pregnancy but do not protect against sexually transmitted infections. Though possibly reversible, these methods should be considered permanent.

### Tubal Sterilization

**Method:** Fallopian tubes are sterilized by crushing, litigating, clipping, or plugging.
- Methods include: tube with plastic band; tube cut and cauterized; tube cut and tied

**Side effects:** Include bowel injury, hemorrhage, or infection.

**Teaching:**
- May be performed at time of cesarean birth. The optimal time is within 48 hours of vaginal birth.
- Report fever, severe pain, bleeding, or incisional discharge.

### Vasectomy

**Method:** Bilateral litigation and resection of vas deferens, which carries sperm from the testes to the penis.

**Side effects:** hematoma, edema, infection, pain

**Teaching:**
- It may take up to 3 months for complete sterilization.
- Semen specimen should be analyzed at 8–16 weeks after the procedure.
