## Section 1: Introduction to Diabetes and Pregnancy

This module will discuss diabetes during pregnancy, the complications, and subsequent nursing implications.

Diabetes mellitus type 1 (DM1) or diabetes mellitus type 2 (DM2) that are diagnosed before pregnancy are known as pregestational diabetes mellitus or pre-existing diabetes mellitus. Gestational diabetes mellitus refers to diabetes that is diagnosed during the second or third trimester of pregnancy. Diabetes diagnosed in the first trimester should be considered pregestational diabetes mellitus as women may have undiagnosed DM2 prior to pregnancy.

## Section 3: Explore: Gestational Diabetes: Risk Factors

Gestational diabetes mellitus is, just as the name suggests, diabetes mellitus that occurs during pregnancy. However, to distinguish it from possible pre-existing undiagnosed diabetes, it refers to diabetes that is diagnosed during the second or third trimester of pregnancy. The development of gestational diabetes results from the developing fetus increasing the mother's need for both glucose and insulin, and placental hormones causing insulin resistance.

Many women develop gestational diabetes without any known risk factors. Some women's bodies, particularly those with the following risk factors, are unable to meet this increased demand and develop diabetes mellitus that extends beyond their pregnancy.

Risk factors:
- Older than age 25 at the time of pregnancy
- First degree relative with diabetes
- History of gestational diabetes
- High risk ethnic groups (African American, Hispanic, American Indian, Asian American, or Pacific Islander)
- Overweight, obese, or morbidly obese
- History of polycystic ovary syndrome

**Reference:** Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2022, April 5). *Diabetes risk factors*. https://www.cdc.gov/diabetes/basics/risk-factors.html

## Section 4: Explore: Gestational Diabetes: Diagnostic Tests

Women are typically screened for gestational diabetes between 24-28 weeks gestation, regardless of particular risk or history, as part of the provider's preferred practice. Some practitioners may only screen based on risk factors or symptomology.

Screening tools include the glucose challenge test (GCT) and the oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT).

| | One Hour Glucose Challenge Test (GCT) | Three-Hour Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) |
|---|---|---|
| **Characteristics** | simple; typically for initial screening; no need to fast | complex; need to fast |
| **Steps** | Drink 50 g of a glucose solution. Test the blood glucose level 1 hour later. | Fast. Test the fasting blood glucose level. Drink 100 g of a glucose solution. Test the blood glucose levels at 1, 2, and 3 hours later. |
| **Test Results** | If the test result is higher than 140 mg/dL, the client needs to proceed to OGTT. | Gestational diabetes is diagnosed if two or more of the values meet or exceed the following thresholds: Fasting Glucose > 95 mg/dL; 1 hour > 180 mg/dL; 2 hours > 155 mg/dL; 3 hours > 140 mg/dL |

## Section 7: Explore: Complications of Gestational Diabetes Mellitus

With diabetes, both mother and fetus are at risk for complications, particularly if blood glucose levels are not controlled during pregnancy.

**Fetal Complications:**

- **Macrosomia:** A mother with diabetes is at risk for having a fetus with macrosomia, meaning that the fetus is large in relation to gestational age. Macrosomia results from hyperglycemia that stimulates fetal insulin production, which in turn acts as a growth hormone.
- **Neonatal hypoglycemia following birth:** The most common newborn complication after birth is hypoglycemia. Maternal insulin does not cross the placental barrier and the fetus produces insulin in utero for the present maternal glucose in circulation. Neonatal hyperinsulinemia after birth can then cause hypoglycemia when the maternal glucose is no longer in the fetal circulation. Closely related to hypoglycemia is newborn thermoregulation as hypothermia can be a sign of hypoglycemia in the newborn or hypothermia can contribute to hypoglycemia.
- **Neonatal hyperbilirubinemia following birth:** The excess blood glucose causes damage to red blood cells, which leads to excess bilirubin in the circulation as well. This is manifested as jaundice in the newborn.
- **Respiratory distress syndrome:** Neonatal hyperinsulinemia reduces cortisol production, which can have an effect on the surfactant level, which is responsible for keeping the alveoli open after birth. This is further complicated if the newborn is also born prematurely, which affects lung maturity because surfactant has not reached adequate levels.

**Maternal Complications:**

- **Delivery and postpartum:** A larger fetus is more difficult to deliver vaginally and can lead to complications with vaginal delivery, such as shoulder dystocia, injury to maternal tissues, or caesarian section. In addition to a large fetus, the woman with diabetes may also have excessive amounts of amniotic fluid (hydramnios). A large fetus or hydramnios can also lead to postpartum hemorrhage related to an overextended uterus that can cause uterine atony.
- **Infections:** Increased risk for infection, such as urinary tract and vaginal infections, are often associated with diabetes due to the excess circulating glucose. Infections must be quickly treated as some raise a risk of ketoacidosis or preterm birth.
- **Other:** Women with diabetes are also at increased risk for hypertension and preeclampsia, particularly if vascular impairment is present. Pre-existing diabetes increases these risks and ketoacidosis complications.

## Section 10: Explore: Nursing Implications

Nursing assessment and care of a woman with gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is similar, although not identical, to the care of any patient with diabetes mellitus (DM). The primary focus in both GDM and DM includes monitoring blood glucose levels and maintaining them through diet, exercise, and pharmacological support. An additional focus with GDM is monitoring the fetal well-being throughout the pregnancy and after transition to extrauterine life.

### Tab: Nursing Support and Education

A pregnant woman newly diagnosed with gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) will need nursing support on:

- Understanding how GDM will impact the plan of care both during and after pregnancy, as well as the plan of care for the newborn.
- Taking an active role in monitoring and maintaining normal glucose levels to minimize complications.
- Need for additional fetal surveillance, such as kick counts, ultrasound, nonstress test, biophysical profile, or fetal lung maturity.
- Understanding pharmacological management, if part of a treatment plan. Insulin needs change throughout the pregnancy and postpartum, so frequent adjustments to the regimen may be necessary.
- Typically, insulin needs are lowest during the first trimester and after delivery, while the insulin needs increase during the second and third trimester. During labor, treatment is often with a continuous IV infusion of regular insulin in order to keep glucose levels between 80-110 mg/dL (or per agency policy), to prevent neonatal hypoglycemia following birth.
- Time and space for freely sharing concerns and feelings.

> **Remember!** Insulin does not cross the placental barrier and reach the fetus. Insulin needs will change during pregnancy, so dosage adjustments may be needed as the pregnancy progresses.

### Tab: Monitoring

The nurse will closely monitor for the impact of gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) on the fetus and newborn.

- During pregnancy:
  - Fetal macrosomia is the most common fetal/newborn complication of GDM. Fetal size will be monitored throughout pregnancy through fundal height measurements and ultrasound. Often, more frequent prenatal visits are included in the plan of care.
- After birth:
  - The most common newborn complication after birth is hypoglycemia. Maternal insulin does not cross the placental barrier and the fetus produces insulin in utero for the present maternal glucose in circulation. Neonatal hyperinsulinemia after birth can then cause hypoglycemia when the maternal glucose is no longer in the fetal circulation.
  - The nurse will closely monitor the newborn's intake and output and blood glucose levels.
  - Closely monitoring body temperature and ensuring that the newborn is skin-to-skin or remains wrapped in blankets. This is especially important with newborns born to mothers with GDM. Hypothermia can be a sign of hypoglycemia and hypothermia can cause hypoglycemia.
  - Other complications to monitor are jaundice and respiratory distress syndrome. Neonatal hyperinsulinemia reduces cortisol production, which can have an effect on the surfactant level, which is responsible for keeping the alveoli open after birth. This is further complicated if the newborn is also born prematurely, which also affects lung maturity because surfactant has not reached adequate levels.
