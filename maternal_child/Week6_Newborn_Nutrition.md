## Section 1: Introduction to Newborn Nutrition

Newborn nutrition is a critical aspect of caring for newborns and infants. Appropriate feeding practices stimulate bonding with the caregiver and psychosocial development. This leads to improved nutrition and physical growth and reduced susceptibility to common childhood illnesses. Improved health outcomes in young children have health effects throughout the lifespan.

This concept will review newborn nutrition, with the exception of breastfeeding, and how nurses can support clients with feeding their newborns.

## Section 2: Prepare: Newborn Nutrition

**Q1.** The nurse is teaching a client about proper techniques for bottle feeding. Which action by the client indicates an understanding of the instructions?

- [ ] Holds the infant in a supine position
- [x] Keeps the nipple full of formula while feeding
- [ ] Avoids burping the infant until the end of the feeding
- [ ] Refrigerates the remaining formula in the bottle

**Feedback:** When bottle feeding an infant, the nipple should remain full of formula throughout the feeding to avoid the infant swallowing air while sucking. The client should burp the infant after 1/2 to 1 ounce of formula has been consumed throughout the feeding. The infant should be held with the head elevated 45 degrees to avoid aspiration. Remaining formula left in the bottle after feeding must be discarded.

---

**Q2.** When preparing formula from powder, the client should mix with _____ water and warm the formula _____. Prepared but unused formula may be stored in the refrigerator for _____ hours.

- [x] tap | by running under warm tap water | 48
- [ ] sterile | in the microwave | 72

**Feedback:** Formula comes in several preparations, including powder, ready-to-feed liquid, and liquid concentrate. When preparing formula from powder, the client should mix the powder with tap water and warm the formula by running under warm tap water. Heating formula in the microwave results in uneven heating and "hot spots" that may scald the baby's mouth and throat. However, formula can be served chilled, warmed, or at room temperature. Prepared but unused formula may be stored in the refrigerator for up to 48 hours. Any remaining formula left in the bottle after feeding must be discarded.

**Q3.** Which statement best represents recommendations from the American Academy of Pediatrics about infant nutrition?

- [ ] Begin complementary foods no sooner than 1 year of age
- [ ] Avoid baby formula unless medical contraindications to breastfeeding
- [x] Exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life
- [ ] Do not promote breastfeeding in all hospital settings

**Feedback:** The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life, followed by continued breastfeeding as complementary foods are introduced. The mother should continue to breastfeed for 1 year or longer as desired by the mother and infant (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2012). The client's decision to breastfeed or formula feed should be supported in all healthcare settings.

## Section 3: Explore: Nutritional Needs of the Newborn

The full-term newborn needs sufficient calories per kilogram of body weight daily to meet energy needs, prevent use of body stores, and provide growth.

- 85–100 calories per kilogram if breastfeeding
- 100–110 calories per kilogram if formula feeding

Both breast milk and formula provide 20 calories per ounce.

Newborns need larger amounts of fluid in relationship to size than an adult.

- 60–100 mL per kilogram on days 1 to 5 of life
- 150–175 mL/kg after day 5 of life

Remember, it is not unusual for newborns to lose up to 10% of birth weight in the first week of life. This is a result of normal excretion of extracellular water and meconium, as well as consuming fewer calories than needed.

- The newborn has a small stomach capacity.
- They may fall asleep before feeding adequately.
- They may sleep through feeding times.

Nurses should closely monitor the urine output and weigh the newborn daily. If weight loss exceeds 7% of birth weight in the first 3 days of life or if birth weight is not regained by 2 weeks of age, intervention may be needed.

**Calculating newborn weight loss:**

- Subtract the current weight from the birth weight. This number represents the amount of weight lost.
- Take the weight lost and divide it by the birth weight. Then multiply by 100 to get the percentage of weight loss.

*Practice question: The newborn's birth weight is 3350 grams. Today's weight is 2950 grams. What is the newborn's percentage of weight loss? **Answer: 12%** (400 ÷ 3350 × 100 = 11.94%)*

## Section 4: Explore: Formula Feeding

Madison, a new mom, was undecided about breastfeeding. She was formula-fed as a baby and was unsure if she could breastfeed and work full time. During her breastfeeding class, the nurse shared information about formula feeding. The nurse felt it was important for moms and their support persons to also hear about alternative feeding options.

A woman may choose to formula feed and should be empowered to make the choice that is best for them. Even women who are educated about the advantages of breastfeeding may still decide to solely formula feed. Cultural beliefs, as well as myths and misconceptions about breastfeeding, influence women's decision making.

Many women see formula feeding as more convenient or less embarrassing than breastfeeding. Some view formula feeding as a way to ensure that the partner, other family members, and daycare providers can feed the baby.

Some women lack confidence in their ability to produce breast milk of adequate quantity or quality. Women who have had previous unsuccessful breastfeeding experiences may choose to formula feed subsequent infants. Some women see breastfeeding as incompatible with an active social life or think that it will prevent them from going back to work. Modesty issues and societal barriers exist against breastfeeding in public. A major barrier for many women is the influence of family and friends.

A nurse's personal feelings about breastfeeding and formula feeding should not influence the information provided to the client.

In this case, Madison needed to do what was best for her and her baby.

### Tab: Formula Composition

Manufacturers must adapt formula to match the components of breast milk as closely as possible. An exact match is not possible and formula will lack the immunologic components of breast milk.

An iron-fortified, commercially prepared formula is recommended for all clients who choose not to breastfeed. Unmodified cow's milk is not recommended for infants under 1 year of age.

### Tab: Formulas For Infants With Special Needs

Many formulas exist for infants with special needs, including:

- soy formula for infants with lactase deficiency or vegetarian families
- protein hydrolysate formula for infants with allergies and malabsorption disorders
- concentrated formula with more calories in less liquid for preterm infants

### Tab: Choosing to Formula-Feed

It is important for the mother to understand newborn implications when choosing to formula-feed:

- more likely to develop allergies
- nutritional properties do not change according to the infant's needs
- not as easily digested and nutrients are not as well absorbed
- can be improperly diluted
- can be contaminated by water supply
- more easily overfed and constipation is more common

## Section 7: Explore: Formula Bottle Feeding

The hospital typically provides a commercial newborn formula. There are a variety of types available. The choice of which formula to use is a joint effort between the healthcare provider and the client. There are milk-based, soy-based, and other specialty formulas for special needs. Unmodified cow's milk should not be given to newborns as it provides inadequate calories. There are also several sizes of disposable nipples available.

Commercially prepared formula comes in several preparations, including powder, ready-to-feed liquid, and liquid concentrate.

The nurse teaches proper bottle-feeding techniques, including:

- Infant held in a semi-upright position when feeding and never lying down, which can cause aspiration.
- Avoid overfeeding.
- Burp the newborn every ½ to 1 oz of formula.
- Feed every 3–4 hours.
- Do not prop the bottle, which increases the chance of aspiration.
- Begin with 15–30 mL during the first 24 hours, then increase by 15 mL.
- Do not force the infant to finish the bottle.

The nurse documents the amount of formula taken in by the newborn and the newborn's tolerance of the feeding. Watch for any reactions or signs of allergies.

Madison decided to bottle feed her baby, and needed to prepare her first formula bottle.

### Tab: Preparing Formula Bottles (Ready-to-Feed)

- She washed her hands.
- She washed the lid of the can, checked the expiration date, and shook the container before opening it.
- She poured the proper amount of formula into a clean bottle and placed a clean nipple on top.
- She checked the nipple flow to ensure that it was not too slow or too fast.
- She understands she must use the bottle within 48 hours (if untouched by the baby).
  - Once the infant has used the bottle, any remaining formula must be discarded.

### Tab: Preparing Formula Bottles (Powder or Concentrate)

- She washed her hands and gathered clean supplies.
- She washed the lid of the can and checked the expiration date.
  - Powdered formula is not recommended for use with infants who are premature or immunocompromised as the powder is not sterile.
- She read the formula label and diluted the formula exactly as recommended.
- She used cold tap water to prepare the formula.
- She understands never to heat the formula in a microwave, but instead to run warm water over the bottle.
- She tested the bottle temperature and nipple flow before feeding.
- She understands she must use the bottle within 48 hours (if untouched by the baby).
  - Once the infant has used the bottle, any remaining formula must be discarded.

### Tab: Combination Feeding

Some clients wish to use a combination of breast and bottle feeding. It is best to delay giving the newborn a bottle or formula until lactation has been well established around 4 weeks after birth. Giving formula to a breastfeeding infant often decreases feeding frequency and leads to decreased milk production.

If the client wishes to combination feed, the nurse should be supportive of this decision so that both the mother and infant receive the benefits of breastfeeding at least part of the time.

Madison now has bottles to clean, so she bought a bottle and nipple brush to ensure she washed them well. Hot, soapy water is needed or bottles and nipples can be placed in the dishwasher or sterilizer. Madison still needs to check for wet and stool diapers to ensure that her baby, Taylor, is getting enough formula. The stool will be darker and more firm compared to the breastfed infant's stool.

## Section 10: Explore: Role of the Nurse

Though mothers are capable of feeding their newborns in the hospital, the nurse plays a key role in assessing and supporting the mother during their stay.

### Tab: Nursing Care Tips

- Assess the mother's knowledge of newborn feeding to provide a starting point for teaching.
- Observe a feeding session to provide a baseline assessment for positive reinforcement and problem identification.
- Describe and demonstrate ways to stimulate the sucking reflex, proper positions for feeding, and the use of pillows during a session to promote maternal and neonatal comfort.
- Teach the mother ways to stimulate the neonate to maintain an awake state by diapering, unwrapping, massaging, or burping to complete a feeding session thoroughly and satisfactorily.
- The nurse can remind the client to support the baby's head during feeding and to burp frequently.
- Make sure the mother has written information on all aspects of feeding to reinforce oral instructions and demonstrations.
- Refer to support groups, lactation consultants, or both, if needed, to provide further information and group support.

### Tab: Diagnosis, Outcomes & Interventions

**Nursing Diagnosis:** Ineffective infant feeding pattern related to inability to coordinate sucking and swallowing.

**Expected Outcomes:** The neonate will coordinate sucking and swallowing to accomplish an effective feeding pattern.

**Nursing Interventions/Rationales:**

- Assess for factors that may contribute to ineffective sucking and swallowing to provide a basis for a plan of care.
- Teach the mother to observe feeding readiness cues to enhance effective feeding.
- Modify feeding methods as needed to maintain hydration status and nutritional requirements.
- Promote a calm, relaxed atmosphere to provide a pleasant feeding experience for the mother and neonate.
