# SECTION 2 — ANSWER KEY, RATIONALES & STRATEGIES

**1. B** | Health Promotion & Maintenance | Application
Progressive cervical dilation and effacement is the only definitive sign of true labor. A and C describe false labor (relieved by comfort measures, irregular). D is a sign of fetal well-being, not labor. *Strategy: true labor = cervical change. Anything relieved by rest/water is false labor.*

**2. C** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Application
8-10 cm with 60-90 second contractions every 2-3 min and an irritable, nauseated, withdrawn client is the transitional phase of stage 1. Active phase is 4-7 cm; latent is 0-3 cm. Stage 2 begins at full (10 cm) dilation with pushing. *Strategy: match the cervical number to the phase first, then confirm with behavior.*

**3. C** | Safe & Effective Care Environment: Management of Care | Application
Airway is always the priority for the newborn (ABCs). Eye ointment and cord care can wait; the 1-minute Apgar is assessed at 1 minute, not within the first minute of active stabilization. *Strategy: ABCs outrank everything; airway before assessment scores.*

**4. B** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Application
A placenta not delivered within 30 minutes is retained and needs manual or surgical removal. Third stage normally takes 5-10 min regardless of parity. Inversion is the uterus turning inside out, not a delayed placenta. *Strategy: memorize the 30-minute retained-placenta threshold.*

**5. A, B, D, E** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Application
Signs of placental separation: gush of blood, cord lengthening, change in uterine shape, fundus rising. A soft, boggy uterus (C) is uterine atony, a hemorrhage sign, not normal separation. *Strategy: judge each option independently; "boggy" is never a good word postpartum.*

**6. B** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Analysis
A gradual deceleration starting after the contraction peak and recovering after it ends is a late deceleration (uteroplacental insufficiency), which is non-reassuring: reposition left lateral, IV fluids, oxygen, notify provider. It is not early (head compression). *Strategy: timing names the deceleration; "late" = placenta = act.*

**7. C** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Application
VEAL CHOP: Variable = Cord compression. Early = Head compression, Acceleration = OK, Late = Placenta. *Strategy: lock in VEAL CHOP; variable and cord both start with the cord being squeezed.*

**8. B** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Application
Moderate variability is the single best indicator of adequate fetal oxygenation and an intact CNS. Baseline rate, absent accelerations, and early decels do not confirm oxygenation as reliably. *Strategy: variability is king; absent variability is the red flag.*

**9. A, B, C, D** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Analysis
Baseline 100 (bradycardia), absent variability, and recurrent late decels = Category III (abnormal). Intrauterine resuscitation: reposition, stop oxytocin, oxygen, increase maintenance IV fluids. E is wrong; this is not Category I. *Strategy: Category III triggers the full resuscitation bundle, not routine monitoring.*

**10. [1] b, [2] b, [3] b** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Application
Normal FHR baseline is 110-160 bpm. Minimal variability is caused by a fetal sleep cycle or CNS depressants like opioids. Marked variability is associated with maternal stimulant/cocaine use. *Strategy: tie each variability level to its cause; sleep/opioids dampen, stimulants exaggerate.*

**11. B** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Epidural causes vasodilation and maternal hypotension, reducing placental perfusion (prolonged decel). Treat with left lateral position, IV fluid bolus, and oxygen; supine worsens vena cava compression. The catheter is not removed, and cesarean is premature. *Strategy: epidural + hypotension = fluids, side-lying, oxygen first.*

**12. A, B, D** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Opioids cross the placenta and cause maternal sedation/respiratory depression, decreased FHR variability, and newborn respiratory depression at delivery. DTRs relate to magnesium, not opioids, and dilation rate is not a drug effect. *Strategy: opioid worries = breathing (mom and baby) plus FHR variability.*

**13. B** | Safe & Effective Care Environment: Safety & Infection Prevention and Control | Application
Membrane rupture with an unengaged presenting part plus a sudden FHR drop signals cord prolapse, an emergency. Lift the presenting part off the cord with a sterile gloved hand and place the client knee-chest (or Trendelenburg) to relieve compression. Oxytocin and delay would worsen hypoxia. *Strategy: ruptured membranes + sudden bradycardia = prolapse; relieve pressure now.*

**14. B** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Application
Turtle sign indicates shoulder dystocia. McRobert's maneuver (knees sharply to shoulders) plus suprapubic pressure is the first-line response. Fundal pressure and pulling on the head worsen impaction and risk injury. *Strategy: shoulder dystocia = McRobert's + suprapubic, never fundal pressure.*

**15. B** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Contractions more frequent than every 2 minutes, prolonged duration, rising resting tone, and late decels = tachysystole with fetal compromise. Stop the oxytocin first. Increasing it or pushing worsens hypoxia. *Strategy: oxytocin + tachysystole or late decels = turn it off first.*

**16. B** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Betamethasone (or dexamethasone) accelerates fetal lung maturity to reduce respiratory distress syndrome in preterm birth. Tocolytics stop contractions; antibiotics treat infection; it is not an antihypertensive. *Strategy: antenatal steroids = fetal lungs.*

**17. B** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Analysis
Lethargy, diminished DTRs, and respiratory rate of 10 are classic magnesium toxicity (CNS and respiratory depression). This is not therapeutic or normal; eclampsia presents with seizures. *Strategy: mag toxicity = lose reflexes, then respirations; watch DTRs and RR closely.*

**18. Expected: A, D | Reportable: B, C, E** | Safe & Effective Care Environment: Safety & Infection Prevention and Control | Analysis
With membranes ruptured 26 hours (prolonged), infection is the concern. Clear odorless fluid and HR 88 are expected. Maternal fever 38.4°C, foul drainage, and fetal tachycardia 175 suggest intrauterine infection (chorioamnionitis/triple I). *Strategy: after ROM, fever + foul fluid + fetal tachycardia = infection; report.*

**19. C** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Analysis
An unfavorable cervix (Bishop 2, closed, -2 station) needs ripening before oxytocin; dinoprostone (PGE2) is appropriate, especially with a misoprostol allergy. Oxytocin needs a favorable cervix; amniotomy commits to delivery and is contraindicated here. *Strategy: low Bishop = ripen first; oxytocin only when the cervix is favorable.*

**20. A, B, C, E** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Oxytocin risks: tachysystole, hypertension, water intoxication (decreased urine output), and fetal distress. Maternal bradycardia is not associated. *Strategy: oxytocin pushes the uterus and BP up and urine output down; bradycardia does not fit.*

**21. B** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Application
A classical (vertical) incision carries the highest rupture risk and generally eliminates VBAC as an option. The incision type strongly affects delivery planning. *Strategy: classical incision = no VBAC; low transverse keeps VBAC possible.*

**22. B** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Application
After amniotomy the priority is the FHR pattern, because the fluid gush can carry the cord ahead of the presenting part (prolapse) and compress it. Temperature and fluid color are assessed but are not the immediate priority. *Strategy: any rupture event (spontaneous or AROM) = check the FHR for prolapse first.*

**23. B** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Application
A boggy, elevated, laterally displaced fundus points to a full bladder impeding contraction. Have her void (or catheterize), then reassess; massage if still boggy. Provider notification and fluid bolus come only if bleeding is uncontrolled after these steps. *Strategy: boggy + displaced + high = bladder; empty it first.*

Endometritis: fever after 24 hours (often day 3), foul purulent lochia, uterine tenderness, chills, and tachycardia. Lochia that lightens and decreases is normal healing. *Strategy: infection = fever + foul lochia + tender uterus; normal lochia trends lighter.*

**30. B** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Application
Unilateral calf swelling, warmth, and tenderness suggest DVT. Notify the provider and keep the client on bed rest to avoid dislodging an embolus. Never massage a suspected DVT. *Strategy: suspected DVT = do not massage, do not ambulate; report and rest.*

**31. C** | Psychosocial Integrity | Analysis
Thoughts of harming self or the baby are an emergency (postpartum depression/psychosis risk) requiring immediate action. Weepiness with retained enjoyment is baby blues; cramping with breastfeeding and fatigue are normal. *Strategy: any self-harm or harm-to-baby statement jumps to the top of the priority list.*

**32. [1] b, [2] a** | Health Promotion & Maintenance | Application
POST-BIRTH: chest pain or trouble breathing warrants 911 (possible PE/cardiac). A temperature of 100.4°F or higher is a provider call (infection). Mild afterpains and a firm fundus are normal. *Strategy: breathing/chest/seizure = 911; fever/incision/leg = call provider.*

**33. B** | Health Promotion & Maintenance | Application
Six or more wet diapers per day plus audible swallowing confirm adequate intake. Long sleep stretches, pre-feed fullness, and 5-minute feeds do not confirm intake. *Strategy: output (wet diapers) and swallowing are the objective proof of intake.*

**34. B** | Health Promotion & Maintenance | Application
Bilateral hard, taut, painful breasts at 72-96 hours is engorgement: warm compresses before feeding to soften and trigger let-down, nurse frequently to empty, cold packs after to slow refilling. Stopping or restricting fluids worsens stasis. *Strategy: engorgement = warm before, cold after, keep emptying.*

**35. B** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Application
Mastitis (unilateral, wedge-shaped, flu-like symptoms, usually after week 4): continue emptying the breast by feeding or pumping and take prescribed antibiotics. Stopping promotes stasis and abscess. The infant may still feed unless an abscess develops. *Strategy: mastitis = keep the milk moving plus antibiotics; do not wean.*

**36. A, B, C** | Health Promotion & Maintenance | Application
Room temp ~4 hours, refrigerate ~72 hours (optimal), freeze up to 6 months. Never microwave (uneven heating) and never refreeze thawed milk. *Strategy: judge each independently; microwave and refreeze are always wrong for breast milk.*

**37. A, B, C, E** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Estrogen-containing contraceptives are contraindicated with blood clots, uncontrolled hypertension, migraines, breast cancer (also stroke, diabetes with vascular involvement, smokers over 35). A prior cesarean is not a contraindication. *Strategy: estrogen + clot/stroke history = no; the cesarean distractor is the trap.*

**38. B** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Copper or levonorgestrel IUDs provide continuous, highly effective contraception without estrogen, appropriate when estrogen is contraindicated and there is no pelvic infection/ectopic history. The patch, ring, and combined pills all contain estrogen. *Strategy: "no estrogen" plus "long-acting" points to IUD or implant.*

**39. C (6)** | Physiological Integrity: Reduction of Risk Potential | Analysis
HR >100 = 2; slow/irregular weak cry = 1; some flexion = 1; grimace = 1; acrocyanosis (body pink, extremities blue) = 1. Total = 6 (moderate difficulty, needs intervention/repeat). *Strategy: score each of the 5 categories 0-2; acrocyanosis is always 1, never 2.*

**40. B** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Analysis (Trend)
Falling temperatures (36.6 → 35.9°C) with jitteriness and poor feeding show progressive hypothermia driving hypoglycemia. Warm the newborn and check blood glucose. This is neither stable nor a fever. *Strategy: in newborns, cold leads to low sugar; treat both together.*

**41. B** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | Analysis
Jaundice within the first 24 hours is non-physiologic (pathologic) and must be reported. Physiologic jaundice appears after 24 hours. Jaundice progresses head-to-toe, not feet-up. *Strategy: timing is the key differentiator; under 24 hours = pathologic = report.*

**42. A-1, B-2, C-3, D-4** | Health Promotion & Maintenance | Application
Rooting = stroke cheek/mouth; Moro/startle = sudden noise or movement; Babinski = stroke heel upward to outer toes (toes fan); Tonic neck = turn head to one side (fencing posture). *Strategy: link each reflex to the exact stimulus; Babinski involves the sole, tonic neck involves the head turn.*

**43. B** | Pharmacological & Parenteral Therapies | Application
Vitamin K prevents vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB) because the newborn gut is sterile and cannot yet synthesize it; given IM in the vastus lateralis. It is not a vaccine, not subcutaneous/deltoid, and does not aid digestion. *Strategy: vitamin K = bleeding prevention, IM, vastus lateralis.*

**44. 9% (8.8%), yes — requires follow-up** | Physiological Integrity: Basic Care & Comfort | Application (Calculation)

## Case Study (45-50) — Postpartum Hemorrhage

**45. Concerning findings: boggy fundus 2 cm above umbilicus and deviated right; one pad saturated in 15 minutes with clots; lightheadedness; has not voided.** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | NCJMM 1: Recognize cues | Analysis
A boggy, elevated, displaced fundus, a pad saturated in 15 minutes, lightheadedness, and no voiding are all abnormal. BP 108/64 and HR 96, intact approximated perineum are within normal limits. *Strategy: gather the deviations from normal before deciding what they mean.*

**46. B** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | NCJMM 2: Analyze cues | Analysis
Macrosomic (4,200 g) overdistention + prolonged oxytocin induction + a full bladder (no void, displaced uterus) point to uterine atony as the cause. Perineum is intact (not a laceration), placenta delivered intact (not retained). *Strategy: connect the history (big baby, long oxytocin) to the mechanism (atony).*

**47. C** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | NCJMM 3: Prioritize hypotheses | Analysis
Ongoing atony bleeding leads most immediately to hypovolemic shock. Infection, DVT, and mastitis are later or unrelated risks. *Strategy: with active hemorrhage, the imminent threat is hypovolemia/shock.*

**48. Central problem: (a) Uterine atony with hemorrhage. Actions: (a) Massage the fundus until firm + (b) Assist the client to empty her bladder. Monitor: (a) Fundal tone/position + (b) Lochia amount and clots.** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | NCJMM 4: Generate solutions | Analysis
Ice to perineum and fluid restriction do not address atony; DTRs and bowel sounds are not the relevant monitors here. *Strategy: bowtie = pair the action to the problem, then choose the parameter that proves the action worked.*

**49. A, B, D, E** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | NCJMM 5: Take action | Application
Massage and empty the bladder, ensure IV access and anticipate an oxytocic, monitor vital sign trends, and draw H/H and platelets. High-Fowler's and fluid restriction (C) are wrong; the client needs volume and bed rest to support venous return. *Strategy: hemorrhage actions = firm the uterus, replace volume, watch trends, get labs.*

**50. A** | Physiological Integrity: Physiological Adaptation | NCJMM 6: Evaluate outcomes | Analysis
A firm, midline fundus at the umbilicus with lochia reduced to moderate shows the interventions worked. Continued saturation, a persistently boggy fundus, or a rising heart rate (96 → 122) all indicate failure and worsening hypovolemia. *Strategy: success = firm fundus + lessening lochia + stable vitals; rising HR is early shock.*
