## Section 1 — Introduction to Postpartum Teaching

Throughout pregnancy and the postpartum period, the nurse provides self-care education the woman will need to remember and perform after she is discharged. Typically, the mother will return for a postpartum visit with her healthcare provider between 4–6 weeks following a vaginal delivery and 2 weeks after a cesarean delivery. In the meantime, she will need to know what is normal and what should be reported to her healthcare provider. The education the nurse provides prior to discharge is key to health maintenance and promotion for the mother and infant, particularly in between well-care visits.

In this module, we will discuss maternal postpartum teaching. We will discuss components of newborn postpartum teaching in another module.

Postpartum Support International is a great resource for healthcare providers to share with the postpartum woman.

## Section 2 — Prepare: Postpartum Teaching

**Q1 — Postpartum Discharge** *(Multiple Choice)*

**Correct Answer:** The mother may be discharged quickly, possibly as soon as 24 hours postpartum.

**Feedback:** Standard discharge is 48 hours following a vaginal birth and 72–96 hours following a cesarean birth. However, early discharge at 24 hours is possible. Regardless of birth experience level, all mothers are fatigued from managing pain, visitors, and infant care.

**Q2 — Postpartum Follow-Up Care** *(Fill in the Blank)*

**Correct Answer:** 4–6 weeks after vaginal birth; 2 weeks after cesarean birth.

**Feedback:** The mother is typically seen by her healthcare provider 4 to 6 weeks after vaginal birth and 2 weeks after cesarean birth.

**Q3 — Postpartum Infection** *(Select All That Apply)*

**Correct Answers:**
- Temperature greater than 100.4°F (38°C)
- Redness, drainage, separation of cesarean incision
- Chills
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge

**Feedback:** The warning signs of infection for the postpartum mother include a temperature greater than 100.4°F (38°C), chills, redness, drainage, separation of cesarean incision, and/or foul-smelling vaginal discharge. Lochia that becomes lighter and decreases in amount is a normal finding. Hemorrhoids and constipation are not warning signs of infection.

## Section 3 — Explore: Postpartum Teaching — Maternal Self-Care

Let's look at some of the key statements the nurse will make to a client throughout her postpartum stay and reinforce prior to discharge.

### Handwashing

Handwashing is one of the most important tools you have to keep yourself and your newborn healthy. Always wash your hands thoroughly before you touch your breasts, after diaper changes, after urinating or having a bowel movement, after changing your peripads, and always before you touch the baby.

### Uterus

Your uterus is working hard to return to where it was pre-pregnancy, under your pubic bone. For that to happen, it will continue to cramp and contract, sometimes called "afterpains," particularly when you are breastfeeding. It should feel firm like a softball and stay in line with your belly button. It should go down the width of a finger every day. Remember to check your uterus and massage it through your abdomen. If it goes off to one side, doesn't feel firm, or isn't as low as it should be, empty your bladder and massage it again. That should make it firm up.

Around postpartum day 14, you won't be able to feel your uterus through your abdomen anymore. You should begin menstruating again between 10 weeks to 6 months postpartum but use a contraceptive when you are cleared to resume sexual activity because you may still become pregnant before your menses return. Breastfeeding does not protect you from pregnancy.

### Lochia

The vaginal bleeding you have is called lochia. It is normal and will dwindle and change over time, but you might continue to feel a gush of it when you're breastfeeding for a little while longer. It is important to keep an eye on the amount of lochia and what it looks like, because that tells us that your uterus is healthy and healing properly. You noticed that today you had about a 4-inch stain of lochia on your peripad every hour, but with breastfeeding, it is about 6 inches. You also noticed that it has changed to more of a pink-brown color. This amount and color are normal at this stage.

Around postpartum day 10, it should lighten even further until it stops sometime between day 14 and up to 3–6 weeks. Keep using the peripads until it stops and use a fresh one every time you go to the bathroom to keep that area clean. If you begin passing large clots, the lochia becomes foul-smelling, or you go back to saturating the peripad in 1 hour, massage the top of your uterus, rest, and call your healthcare provider. Also, if you have a temperature greater than 100.4°F (38°C) and/or chills, let your healthcare provider know because this may be a sign of infection.

### Breasts

Your nipples may be sore from breastfeeding, so use only warm water and keep them dry between feedings. You can use lanolin cream between feedings to soothe sore nipples and it does not need to be wiped off before breastfeeding. If your nipples become cracked or blistered, let your healthcare provider know. It is important to prevent an infection in your breasts, called mastitis, due to germs getting in through open areas of injured nipples. Use excellent hand hygiene.

Also, your breasts have enlarged quite a bit, but in particular, because you are breastfeeding. Make sure you continue to wear a supportive bra at all times to help regulate how much your breasts fill. If an area of the breast has redness, is warm to the touch, you feel a hard lump, or there is a sore area, be sure to call your healthcare provider. This may be normal engorgement as your breasts fill for a feeding, but over time this should not be uncomfortable. Make sure your breasts empty with each feeding and do not skip feedings. If the milk sits in the breasts, you may get an infection. You can use the breast pump to empty if you need to. If there is a sore area, you can apply moist heat and massage the area. If you decide not to breastfeed, and you want your milk to dry up, keep wearing a supportive bra but avoid things that stimulate milk production (warm showers and expressing milk). Instead, use cold compresses or cold cabbage leaves that have been washed and dried inside the bra.

## Section 6 — Explore: Postpartum Teaching — Maternal Self-Care

Use the buttons below the slides to view maternal self-care information.

### Slide 1 — Perineum

You had an episiotomy (or laceration) in the area between your vagina and your anus. Your healthcare provider stitched it up and it has remained intact. These sutures will dissolve on their own and should begin to heal in about 2–3 weeks, but it could take 4–6 months to completely heal. You must continue the routine taught in the hospital each time you go to the bathroom. Continue to use the peri bottle after each time you urinate or have a bowel movement. Spray the water from front to back, pat dry, and use a fresh peripad. You can continue to use the spray and witch hazel pads. You can also use a sitz bath 2–4 times a day but do not submerge in a bathtub until your healthcare provider clears you to do so after your first postpartum visit in 6 weeks. The body needs time to rest and heal, so new moms are advised not to use any tampons, douche, or have sexual intercourse until their healthcare provider clears them to do so after their first postpartum visit in 6 weeks.

### Slide 2 — Bowel Function

You may get constipated because of everything your body is going through, and prenatal vitamins can have that effect too. Do not strain to have a bowel movement because it will put stress on your perineum and your healing episiotomy. Your healthcare provider will prescribe a stool softener for you to help prevent constipation. Eating a healthy diet, drinking a lot of water, and staying active in a safe way will help prevent constipation.

### Slide 3 — Bladder Function

You may notice that you leak some urine when you sneeze, laugh, or cough. This is because your bladder has been bumped around and has lost some tone because of pregnancy and delivering vaginally. This may improve in a few months, but it may continue to some degree as well. It is important to keep your bladder empty because it will get in the way of your uterus if it is full, and that will make the vaginal bleeding worse. If you feel pain or burning when you urinate or don't feel like you can empty, let your healthcare provider know.

### Slide 4 — Nutrition

Be sure to continue eating a healthy diet and drink about 2–3 liters of fluid a day. Keep taking your prenatal vitamins and following your pregnancy nutrition guidelines, particularly while you are breastfeeding. Five hundred additional calories are needed for breastfeeding mothers compared to pre-pregnancy requirements. You should continue to lose most of the pregnancy-related weight within the first 3 months postpartum, and breastfeeding will help with that as well.

### Slide 5 — Rest and Exercise

It is very important to get enough sleep, which is difficult with a newborn. Try to sleep between feedings and allow family and friends to help as much as possible. Staying active is important for your overall health, helping your body recover, and preventing constipation. Kegel exercises will help strengthen your pelvic floor muscles. Your healthcare provider will let you know if there are any restrictions.

## Section 8 — Explore: Maternal Postpartum Teaching

Click each tab below to review maternal postpartum teachings.

### Emotions

There are a lot of hormonal changes happening right now which, along with lack of sleep and stress, cause many women to experience the baby blues. You may experience fluctuations in mood, sometimes feeling happy and other times tearful.

- It is normal for emotions to fluctuate, but if you experience prolonged, increased, or unmanageable tearfulness, sleeping difficulties, or loss of appetite, let your healthcare provider know as these can be signs of postpartum depression. Sometimes, you don't even know it is happening but if you have any doubts, be sure to call your healthcare provider right away.
- Many hormonal changes are happening in your body and you will feel very tired at times. This is normal and can be treated. Ask for help from others so that you can rest and then when you are with your baby you are better able to bond and enjoy the experience.
- Postpartum blues pass after 2 weeks postpartum, whereas postpartum depression is more serious and makes it difficult to deal with daily life, lasts beyond the first 2 weeks, and requires therapeutic management to recover.

For more information regarding postpartum depression, visit the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists website for answers to frequently asked questions.

### Legs

Your blood was more prone to clots while you were pregnant and that is why you were advised to keep active and tell us if you felt pain, tenderness, or swelling in your calves, or warmth and tenderness behind your knees. Keep following these instructions until around week 6 postpartum, when the risk of clots from pregnancy is over.

### Medications

Be sure to take the medications prescribed by your healthcare provider as directed for cramping and pain in your perineum. The medication will not harm the baby. The pain should lessen over time as your body heals.

### Incision Care

Because you've had a cesarean birth, it is very important to keep an eye on your incision and keep it from becoming infected. If you notice redness, drainage, separation of the incision, or have a temperature over 100.4°F (38°C), let your healthcare provider know right away. The staples will be removed before you go home and the sutures that remain will dissolve away over time. To help ensure that the incision doesn't separate, do not lift anything heavier than your baby and increase your activity gradually. You will check your uterus and bleeding as we talked about, but don't press down on the incision. Keep the area clean by letting shower water wash over it and then pat it dry. Your healthcare provider is sending you home with a prescription for pain medications that we will discuss.

## Section 10 — Explore: Postpartum Maternal Danger Signs

As part of maternal postpartum teaching, the nurse should provide education regarding postpartum maternal warning signs to monitor and report to the healthcare provider. These include fever, breast issues (pain, swelling, redness), abdominal tenderness (persistent), pelvic fullness/pressure, perineal pain (persistent), urination issues, abnormal lochia, leg issues (edema, tenderness, warmth), and cesarean incision issues.

The Association of Women's Health, Obstetric and Neonatal Nurses (AWHONN, 2021) suggests the acronym "POST BIRTH" to help women remember the postpartum warning signs requiring them to call 911 and those to report to their healthcare provider.

"These post-birth warning signs can become life-threatening if you don't receive medical care right away because:"

- **P**ain in the chest.
- **O**bstructed breathing or shortness of breath (trouble catching your breath) may mean you have a blood clot in your lung or a heart problem.
- **S**eizures may mean you have a condition called eclampsia.
- **T**houghts or feelings of wanting to hurt yourself or someone else may mean you have postpartum depression.
- **B**leeding (heavy), soaking more than one pad in an hour, or passing an egg-sized clot or bigger may mean you have an obstetric hemorrhage.
- **I**ncision that is not healing, increased redness, or any pus from episiotomy or cesarean section site may mean you have an infection.
- **R**edness, swelling, warmth, or pain in the calf area of your leg may mean you have a blood clot.
- **T**emperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher, bad smelling vaginal blood, or discharge may mean you have an infection.
- **H**eadache (very painful), vision changes, or pain in the upper right area of your belly may mean you have high blood pressure or post-birth preeclampsia.
