## Section 1: Introduction to Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

Neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) is a result of the sudden discontinuation of fetal exposure to substances that were used by the mother during pregnancy. Withdrawal from licit or illicit substances is becoming more common among neonates in both developed and developing countries. Without proper treatment and care, the infant born with NAS is at significant risk of neonatal morbidity and mortality.

This concept will review the effects of these drugs on the neonate and how nursing care can support these children in their recovery.

## Section 3: Explore: Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome Statistics

It is important to understand the incidence of neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS). The number of cases has been steadily increasing in the U.S.

**Transcript — NAS Statistics**

In the United States, the incidence of neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) has increased 383% during 2000-2012 (CDC, 2016).

For every 1000 infants born, seven will be diagnosed with NAS (CDC, 2021).

In 2019, 7% of pregnant women reported using prescription opioids, and one in five of those women, admitted to misuse (CDC, 2021).

Drugs that women abuse include:
- Opioids, such as heroin and prescribed medicines such as codeine and oxycodone
- Stimulants, such as amphetamines or cocaine
- Antidepressant medicines, such as selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)
- Depressants, such as barbiturates, or alcohol, or marijuana

Most medications have been shown to cross the placenta. Often, women who abuse one drug are at risk of abusing additional medications or illicit substances.

**References**

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2021, July 16). *Data and statistics about opioid use during pregnancy*. https://www.cdc.gov/pregnancy/opioids/data.html

Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. (2016). *Incidence of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome — 28 States, 1999–2013*. Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report, 65(31), 799-208. http://dx.doi.org/10.15585/mmwr.mm6531a2

## Section 4: Explore: Prenatal Support

The increasing use of opioid pain relievers and other addictive substances among pregnant women warrants the need for:
- screening and education of expectant mothers
- increased access to follow-up facilities
- early intervention services for high-risk mothers and infants
- drug abuse treatment and prevention programs aimed at improving the health outcomes of mother–infant couples

Women should speak to their provider about quitting before just stopping any medication. It is not recommended for women to just stop abruptly without support and monitoring.

Throughout this lesson, we'll discuss Tiffany, a client who has been using opioids since a car accident 3 years ago. She hurt her back and physical therapy alone was not enough to control the pain. She started misusing pain medication and every time she tries to stop, she relapses.

Tiffany was connected early on with a treatment program. It was determined that she would not discontinue her medication completely, but instead, her provider transitioned her to methadone.

Methadone has been the gold standard for treating pregnant women who are addicted to opiates since 1995. Pregnant women in a methadone treatment program should be acknowledged though for choosing a healthier path for themselves and their babies. Note that methadone withdrawal is more severe and prolonged than heroin withdrawal. However, the benefits of quitting outweigh the risks.

**Methadone Use During Pregnancy**
- Mothers are more likely to have prenatal care.
- They typically have more stable living conditions.
- Women tend to participate in fewer high-risk social behaviors.
- Mothers have improved nutrition and deliver newborns with higher birth weights.

## Section 6: Explore: Finnegan Neonatal Abstinence Score

Tiffany attends a class in the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) in anticipation of her upcoming delivery. She learns about the newborn behaviors that she should be prepared for.
- poor feeding or sucking or slow weight gain
- breathing problems, including tachypnea
- body shakes (tremors), seizures (convulsions), overactive reflexes (twitching), and tight muscle tone
- fussiness, excessive crying, or having a high-pitched cry (spend more time crying than feeding)
- fever, sweating, or blotchy skin
- trouble sleeping and lots of yawning
- diarrhea or throwing up
- stuffy nose or sneezing

Newborns are not considered to have a substance-use disorder but, instead, are considered drug-exposed. A commonly used tool for assessing infants exposed in utero to psychoactive drugs, particularly opioids/opiates is the Finnegan Neonatal Abstinence Score (FNAS). It assesses the neurologic, behavioral, and stress/abstinence function of the neonate, and scores various NAS symptoms—the lower the score, the fewer withdrawal symptoms.

Signs of NAS depend on a variety of factors:
- What drug was used during pregnancy (can urine, blood, meconium be tested)?
- How much did they use and how long did they take it?
- How are the drugs broken down in the body?
- The baby's gestational age at birth.

**Additional Resources**

Additional resources for assessment can be found through the National Center of Substance Abuse and Child Welfare: https://ncsacw.samhsa.gov/topics/neonatal-abstinence-syndrome.aspx#expand-all

More information on the U.S. opioid crisis as it affects maternal and infant health can be found in this infographic from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention: https://www.cdc.gov/reproductivehealth/opioid-use-disorder-pregnancy/pdf/MMWR-Opioids-Use-Disorder-Pregnancy-Infographic-h.pdf

## Section 8: Explore: Additional Complications From Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome

Tiffany delivers her baby, Amanda, and the infant is taken to the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) due to grunting and tachypnea. She meets with the neonatologist who shares some additional information about newborns with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS).

NAS infants are at increased risk of:
- low birthweight (less than 5 pounds, 8 ounces)
- jaundice and seizures
- sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)

There are still many unknowns about long-term effects. More research needs to be conducted as each case is different and each baby responds a little differently to drug exposure.

Evidence shows that infants may experience:
- developmental delays and motor problems—delayed or difficulty with sitting, walking
- issues with their bones and muscles, affecting their ability to do simple movements
- sleep problems, ear infections, and vision problems
- speech, language, behavior, and learning problems

## Section 9: Explore: Non-Pharmacologic Interventions

Non-pharmacologic interventions for an infant experiencing neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) include decreasing external stimulation, holding the infant, nonnutritive sucking, swaddling, pressure/rubbing, and rocking. These interventions meet the goals of nonpharmacologic interventions, which are to facilitate parental attachment and decrease external stimuli.

The goals of nonpharmacologic interventions are to facilitate supportive parenting behaviors and decrease the external stimuli that tend to exacerbate withdrawal signs. A multilayered and comprehensive approach is crucial to nurturing strong maternal/family-infant interactions in this population. Nonpharmacologic care is often described as modification of the environment and social interactions that support neurodevelopmental and physiologic stability.

To promote infant comfort and sleep, baby Amanda receives skin-to-skin care (often known as kangaroo care) for 1 hour after feeding. The nurses provide her mom, Tiffany, with an area where Tiffany has privacy. Tiffany has tried to be present for each feeding and when she must leave, the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) has volunteers who provide the kangaroo care for Amanda. Kangaroo care has been found to be safe and effective with decreasing length of hospital stay and improving measures of growth, breastfeeding, and maternal-infant bonding. Another supportive measure includes music therapy. Sometimes people sing or play instruments, and both have improved the neonate's outcomes.

## Section 11: Explore: Nursing Interventions

Review interventions the nurse can take with neonatal abstinence syndrome.

**Transcript — Nursing Interventions for Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS)**

The care that the nurse provides can dramatically improve the recovery and long-term health of the newborn with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS).
- **Decreased environmental stimuli:** Room should be quiet, dimly lit, and use slow infant handling.
- Frequent small/demand feedings may help in infants with motor or tone dysregulation.
- Breastfeeding is not contraindicated, unless the mother is taking street drugs, is involved in polydrug abuse, or is infected with HIV.
- Holding – Infants who have poor motor control (thrashing or exaggerated rooting) respond to gentle head/limb restraint, helping them regulate.
- Nonnutritive sucking helps organize a dysregulated infant and prevents disorganization.
- Swaddling helps maintain regulation, promotes self-soothing, and increases tolerance to stimulation (such as diaper change).
- Containment – Gentle containment or pressure supports motor and tone control.
- Rubbing is often better than patting when burping during feedings to avoid triggering Moro reflex. Vertical rocking facilitates relaxation and eye contact; more soothing than "regular" rocking or side to side motion.
- **Avoid eye contact:** it can cause overstimulation. Once the infant is recovering, eye contact is typically more tolerable.

## Section 13: Explore: Nursing Attitude Towards Clients

Nurses may hold negative attitudes or judge pregnant women who have a substance use disorder and use substances during pregnancy. Some nurses may lack knowledge about substance use and its treatment during pregnancy.

What can be done to combat negative attitudes and judgment towards pregnant women with a substance use disorder?
- Training programs and workshops to improve nurses' knowledge help change attitudes toward patients with substance-related problems. This can result in positive outcomes and improved client relationships.
- Nurses need to be aware of their biases and, with training, are much more successful in helping clients to change their drug behaviors.
- When there is a positive connection between nurses and moms, moms are often better able to recognize and interpret infant cues, thus enhancing mother-infant interactions.
- All infants with neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) should be managed with nonpharmacologic nursing interventions as first-line treatment in an atmosphere of kindness and sensitivity.
