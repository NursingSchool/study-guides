## Section 1 — Introduction to Breastfeeding

The choice to breastfeed or not is one that every parent makes based on a personal set of beliefs, values, and unique circumstances. The nurse plays a major role in ensuring the success of their choice.

In this module, you will review the benefits of breastfeeding, the basic techniques of breastfeeding, and the resources available to breastfeeding mothers.

## Section 2: Prepare — Breastfeeding

### Q1: Breastfeeding Duration (Fill in the Blank)

**Question:** How long should infants be exclusively breastfed, and for how long ideally?

**Correct Answer:** 6 months exclusively; ideally at least 12 months total.

**Feedback:** It is recommended that for the first 6 months of life infants are exclusively breastfed, but ideally should be breastfed for at least the first 12 months of life (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2012).

### Q2: Infant Feeding Cues (Multiple Choice)

**Question:** When caring for a newborn, which action indicates the newborn is ready to breastfeed?

**Correct Answer:** Places hands in mouth

**Feedback:** Infant hunger cues include rooting, sucking on hands/fingers, and rapid eye movement. Crying is a late sign of hunger. An infant that is sleeping quietly is not showing signs of hunger. Spitting up clear mucous is common in the first days of life and is not a sign of hunger. Turning the head to the side while opening the mouth (rooting reflex) is a sign of hunger. However, turning the head toward sound is not.

### Q3: First Newborn Stool (Select All That Apply)

**Question:** The nurse is assessing the newborn's initial meconium stool. What characteristics should the nurse expect the stool to have? Select all that apply.

**Correct Answers:**
- Thick
- Dark green
- Odorless

**Feedback:** At birth, the newborn bowel is sterile, and so are the thick, dark green, tarry, odorless plugs of stool (meconium). Meconium forms as a result of swallowing and digesting sterile amniotic fluid in utero. As the newborn digests the initial colostrum first and then breastmilk, the intestinal flora are established and the stools gradually change from meconium to more frequent, thinner, and yellow-colored stools with an odor.

## Section 3: Explore — Breastfeeding Overview

Melissa and her partner attended a breastfeeding class at their local hospital when she was about 6 months pregnant. The class was taught by one of the nurses, who is also a lactation consultant.

Breastfeeding is a natural extension of pregnancy and childbirth; it is much more than simply a means of supplying nutrition for infants. Women most often breastfeed their babies because they are aware of the benefits to the infant.

Melissa is excited about the bonding experience she will have with her daughter, Taylor. She dreams of having three children and plans to breastfeed all of them. Melissa's partner, John, is very supportive of the decision to breastfeed. From his partner's prenatal class, he learned that his opinion about breastfeeding could influence Melissa. Women who perceive that their partners prefer breastfeeding are more likely to choose this method of infant feeding. Women are more likely to breastfeed successfully when partners and family members are positive about breastfeeding and have the skills to support breastfeeding.

### Tab 1: Breastfeeding Benefits

Human breast milk meets the balanced macro- and micro-nutrient needs of the newborn. It is the most easily digested newborn nutrition source and supplies the newborn with some of the mother's immunity to various pathogens. Other benefits to the newborn include decreased risk for:

- Gastrointestinal infections
- Respiratory complications, such as otitis media and asthma
- Celiac disease
- Sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)
- Developing diabetes mellitus (type 1 and type 2)
- Leukemia

Breastfeeding provides hormone regulation benefits for the mother, which promotes uterine involution, thereby reducing blood loss. Breastfeeding also reduces the incidence of some cancers (breast and ovarian), is less expensive than formula, and the associated infant bonding provides psychological benefits.

### Tab 2: Recommendations for Breastfeeding

The full-term newborn's caloric requirements when breastfed are 39 to 45 kcal per pound of body weight, but higher if formula fed (45 to 50 kcal/lb) because of the differences in nutritional makeup.

**NOTE:** It is recommended that for the first 6 months of life, infants are exclusively breastfed. Breastfeeding should continue for at least 12 months while introducing complementary foods. Breastfeeding may continue beyond the first year of life as long as the parent and infant desire (American Academy of Pediatrics, 2012).

There are national and international efforts underway to increase the percentage of breastfed infants until at least 6 months of age. Studies indicate that the incidence of infant deaths as well as maternal deaths is less when mothers breastfeed exclusively for 6 months. Some birth facilities are seeking designation as "baby friendly" for their efforts in providing education to staff and parents regarding breastfeeding and ensuring their facility is ideally suited to breastfeeding on-demand, without pacifiers or the use of formula unless medically necessary.

### Tab 3: Breast Milk Composition

Breast milk is specifically made for human infants and proportioned appropriately for the neonate. It will vary to meet the newborn's changing needs. Breastmilk provides protection against infection and is easily digested. It contains maternal immunoglobulins, leukocytes, antioxidants, enzymes, and hormones that are important for growth. These components are not available in formula.

Protein, carbohydrates, and fats are present in the amounts and proportions needed by the infant. Vitamins A, E, and C are high in breast milk.

### Tab 4: Decisions Not to Breastfeed

It is recognized that formula does not match breastmilk in its benefits for optimal maternal and newborn health. However, not every newborn is breastfed for a variety of reasons. Occasionally, there are maternal and/or newborn medical reasons that require the use of formula instead. For example:

- The mother may be receiving medications that would cross into the breastmilk or has a condition restricting her from breastfeeding, such as HIV.
- Sometimes the newborn is physically unable to breastfeed, such as with a cleft lip or palate. The mother may be able to manually pump breastmilk and feed the baby using a bottle.
- Some mothers choose not to breastfeed for personal reasons.

**Remember!** Whatever the situation or decision, the nurse supports the mother's choice and provides assistance and guidance.

## Section 4: Explore — Prepare for Breastfeeding During Pregnancy

For the postpartum client who wants to breastfeed, the preparation for success begins in pregnancy.

- The client must be physically and psychologically prepared for the challenges that may come with breastfeeding.
- Maternal nutrition and a strong support system play a big role in breastfeeding success.
- Education about breastfeeding is essential to success.
- Nurses can provide education and guidance on preparing to breastfeed before delivery.

Hormone changes occur after delivery in preparation for breastfeeding.

- Decrease in estrogen and progesterone once placenta is delivered allows prolactin levels to rise.
- Prolactin levels rise as physical stimulation (infant sucking) triggers milk production.
- Oxytocin is released by nipple and areola stimulation during nursing to encourage milk let down from alveoli and milk ducts.

Before delivery, milk production has already begun and a supply of the first milk, called colostrum, is already present. Colostrum contains immunoglobulins (IgA) and protein.

## Section 5: Explore — Breastfeeding at Delivery

Breastfeeding should be initiated within the first hour of life, if mother and baby are stable. This action stimulates milk production and promotes bonding.

### Tab 1: Newborn Assessment

At delivery, the newborn, if stable, should be placed on the mother's bare chest for assessment and intervention rather than taking the newborn to the radiant warmer. This may include activities such as vital signs, suctioning, warming and drying the newborn, and assessing the Apgar scores.

### Tab 2: Oxytocin & Let-Down Reflex

Breastfeeding within the first hour is an important step in which oxytocin is released from the posterior pituitary gland. From the first moment the placenta is removed, the newborn attempts to latch to the breast (called suckling) and the mother's body is signaled to release hormones that continue milk production.

Oxytocin is responsible for the milk let-down from the breast ducts. This let-down reflex is also stimulated by the thought, sight, or sound of the infant and is felt by the mother when it is time to feed. The oxytocin also stimulates uterine contractions, the same way it does in labor, and causes the uterus to constrict. Therefore, breastfeeding can help prevent postpartum hemorrhage due to the release of oxytocin.

### Tab 3: Skin-to-Skin Contact

The newborn will likely be alert and express interest in breastfeeding soon after delivery. Signs that the infant is ready to feed include sucking on its hands/fingers, rooting around searching for the nipple, and rapid eye movements. The newborn is soothed when brought to the mother's bare skin and breast. For this reason, the first bath is often delayed for 8-12 hours after delivery to allow for constant skin-to-skin contact and intermittent breastfeeding on demand.

## Section 7: Explore — Breastfeeding Anatomy and Techniques

Assisting mothers with breastfeeding newborns in the hospital setting involves teaching correct techniques and providing encouragement. Nurses must understand breast anatomy and the phases of lactogenesis.

### Tab 1: Breast & Nipple Anatomy

The characteristics of the nipples impact how the woman will breastfeed. The newborn struggles to latch if the nipples are flat or inverted rather than protruding. Breast size may also impact the shape of the nipples, which impacts latch.

There are 15-20 lobes in each breast. Each lobe contains lobules that provide a system of ducts that end at the opening of the nipple. Milk is produced in the alveoli, contained in each lobule.

### Tab 2: Breastfeeding

**Breastfeeding Techniques:** It is best to attempt breastfeeding on-demand and when the newborn is awake and alert but not crying. Clients should be taught to recognize infant feeding cues. Breastfeeding should occur 8-12 times in 24 hours with varying amounts of time in between each session. Each feeding needs to be long enough for the newborn to receive enough milk, particularly in the early postpartum days when the let-down reflex can take up to 5 minutes. The newborn at this stage should typically be allowed to feed for as long as a consistent sucking pattern exists, typically around 15 minutes or more.

**Establish a Sucking Pattern:** By the second or third day, the newborn will become more alert and fussier, showing more signs of readiness to eat. The infant often starts cluster feeding (several times close together) and then waits a longer period in between as the newborn's appetite fluctuates with growth spurts. When the sucking pattern becomes non-nutritive (choppy without swallowing), the newborn can be unlatched, burped, and moved to the other breast if desired. Switching sides also helps minimize sore nipples and provides equal stimulation of milk production in each breast.

**Position of the Mother's Hands:** Hand position on the breast is important. The client can use either a "C" or a "U" position so that her fingers and thumb are parallel to the baby's mouth. Fingers should remain behind the areola so the infant has room to latch. As the infant becomes more adept at feeding, the mother will no longer need to support the breast.

**Remember!** The nurse works with the mother to recognize the right time to attempt breastfeeding and to ensure enough spacing to allow the mother to rest without impeding newborn nutrition, breastfeeding progress, or bonding.

### Tab 3: Lactogenesis

Lactogenesis is the process of breastmilk production. It comes in three stages.

- **Lactogenesis I:** Begins during the second half of pregnancy. By late pregnancy, some clients can express some colostrum. Colostrum production continues for the first several days after birth.
- **Lactogenesis II:** Begins on day 2-3 after birth, transitional milk production begins. Transitional milk contains lactose, fat, and calories.
- **Lactogenesis III:** Begins around day 10 after birth, mature milk is produced. Mature milk contains 20 calories/ounce.

The length of feeding is important because it impacts the type of breastmilk the newborn receives. Clients should nurse for up to 15 minutes on each breast once breastmilk is established.

- Foremilk comes first and is thinner.
- Hindmilk follows at the end of feeding, is fattier, and leads to infant weight gain.

## Section 8 — Explore: Breastfeeding Positions

Assisting mothers with breastfeeding newborns in the hospital setting involves assisting with correct techniques and encouragement. It can be difficult at first, particularly for first-time mothers, but it is a wonderful bonding experience.

Melissa noted that the baby had the rooting reflex. She was amazed when her nipple was brushed across Taylor's lips; Taylor searched for the nipple and her instinct was there to latch. A proper latch is crucial to preventing nipple soreness and injury, as well as milk letdown. Review the different breastfeeding positions that can help ensure proper latch.

### Breastfeeding Positions (Transcript)

- Cradle
- Cross-cradle
- Football hold
- Laid-back
- Side-lying

### Breastfeeding Tips

- To break the suction of the latch, a finger is placed between the baby's lips and the breast rather than pulling the breast out of the newborn's mouth. This can prevent nipple soreness.
- Many mothers worry that their newborn cannot catch their breath properly when at the breast because their nostrils are pressed into the breast tightly. Educate mothers that newborn nostrils are wide set to allow for breathing while at the breast but positioning also assists with this. Melissa did well with the cradle hold.
- Breastfeeding time will vary for each baby. The newborn will establish its own pattern.
- Burping in between sides is encouraged. This will help prevent trapped air from causing regurgitation of milk.
- Reinforce with mothers that the presence of the initial milk (colostrum) is thin but very beneficial to the newborn. It can take several days after delivery for the mother's breast milk to come in. Some mothers worry that this means the newborn is not getting enough to eat and may wish to supplement with formula or stop breastfeeding altogether.
- Avoid nipple confusion by not offering supplemental formula, pacifiers, or soothers until breastfeeding has been established.
- Newborns should breastfeed at least 8 to 12 times within a 24-hour period, but each baby will have its own schedule and mothers should always look for feeding cues and feed as often as the baby needs.
- Melissa made sure to unwrap Taylor and talk to her when it was time to eat so the baby would latch easier.

## Section 9 — Explore: Breastfeeding Latching

Many hospitals and birthing facilities employ registered nurses (RNs) who are certified lactation consultants. They have specialized training to provide inpatient and outpatient services for postpartum breastfeeding assistance and assessment of infant nutrition status. Lactation consultants can troubleshoot breastfeeding complications and evaluate the newborn for appropriate weight gain.

### Tab 1: Eliciting Latch

Instruct the mother to brush the nipple against the infant's lips. When the mouth is wide open, aim the nipple toward the roof of the infant's mouth. Then instruct the mother to quickly bring the infant close so that the infant can latch on to the areola with the chin touching the breast first.

### Tab 2: Latching Position

There are various holds that can be used based on the mother's comfort level and needs. The newborn's head and body should face the breast directly so that the ear, shoulder and hip are in a straight line. The newborn's neck should be slightly extended to aid in swallowing.

The infant's lips should be positioned on the areola to allow the nipple to be drawn toward the back of the mouth. Most of the areola on the underside is covered by the infant's mouth; some of the areola on the top side may be visible. The tongue is between the lower gum and the breast, and the infant's lips are flared outward.

Many mothers worry that their newborn cannot breathe properly when at the breast because their nostrils are pressed into the breast tightly. Educate mothers that newborn nostrils are wide set to allow for breathing while at breast but positioning also assists with this.

### Tab 3: Assessing Latching

The nurse assists the mother and newborn with proper latch as needed. This is crucial to ensure the nipples are not damaged and the newborn gets a good feeding without becoming frustrated or losing interest.

**The LATCH Scoring Tool** — The LATCH scoring system is used by nurses to assess and document the degree to which breastfeeding assistance is needed.

- **L** — Latching of infant onto the breast
- **A** — Amount of audible swallowing
- **T** — Type of nipple (inverted, flat or protruding)
- **C** — Comfort of mother
- **H** — Help needed to hold baby to breast

A score of 0, 1, or 2 is given to the five letters of the LATCH acronym. The higher the score, the better the latch. A score of less than 7 indicates the need for assistance with breastfeeding.

### Tab 4: Unlatching

In addition to a proper latch, it is also important to remove the infant from the breast carefully to avoid nipple injury. To break the suction of the latch, a finger is placed between the baby's lips and the mother's breast rather than pulling the breast out of the newborn's mouth.

## Section 11 — Explore: Common Breastfeeding Challenges

### Tab 1: Is my baby getting enough milk?

Both the initial disinterest and sleepiness of newborns along with their subsequent fussiness and eagerness to eat often lead many women to believe they are not producing enough milk and their newborn is not getting enough to eat. Some mothers decide to supplement breastfeeding with formula or completely switch to bottle-feeding because of this concern.

As milk is demanded by the infant, milk supply is increased. Therefore, it is important to teach mothers that breastfeeding at least 8-12 times daily is essential to ensuring adequate breastmilk supply.

Additionally, it is important to teach mothers how to know that the infant is getting enough breastmilk. These signs include:

- Listen for frequent swallowing
- Change at least 6-8 wet diapers every day
- Notice that breasts feel softer after nursing the infant
- Observe that the infant seems content after feeding
- Change at least one diaper with stool daily

### Tab 2: Can I give my baby a pacifier?

It is recommended that formula supplementation or the use of pacifiers be avoided when the newborn's demand increases during the first four weeks of life. This can be a very difficult concept for some families to understand.

The newborn may become confused by the nipple of the bottle or pacifier and have more difficulty latching to the breast, which is softer and shaped differently. Also, the interest in feeding should be used to latch the baby to the breast to stimulate milk production so that a breastfeeding pattern can be established.

### Tab 3: Do I have enough breastmilk?

Colostrum production begins in late pregnancy and continues into the first few days after birth. Though not much in quantity, colostrum is a highly nutritious first milk for the newborn to receive. Within 2-3 days after birth, transitional milk production begins with increased quantity. Breastmilk is the best food for a newborn during the adjustment to extrauterine life.

The first breastmilk available at birth is colostrum. It is gentle, thick, and yellow, containing just the right mix of protein and other macro- and micro-nutrients that are perfectly suited to this point in the newborn's life. It also provides the necessary infection-preventing immunoglobulins, has a laxative effect that expels meconium, and establishes the infant's normal intestinal flora in preparation for the mature milk to come. Adequate nutrition and hydration are essential for milk production.

As the newborn becomes more alert and interested in the comfort of breastfeeding, the increased demand stimulates milk production and transition from colostrum to the more nutrient-dense transitional and mature milk. This gradual progression is gentler on the newborn's maturing digestive system. So, the mother can be reassured that the increased demand is expected rather than a sign that the newborn is uncomfortable or hungry.

The more the infant nurses, the more milk the breasts will produce.

### Tab 4: How do I treat sore nipples?

Nipple soreness can occur with or without skin breakdown and is common in the first few days of breastfeeding. However, it can be the result of poor latch on the breast, faulty sucking habits, or continuous sucking after the breast is empty. If nipple soreness lasts longer than one week, evaluation is necessary.

After evaluating for breastfeeding problems, the nurse should instruct the mother on comfort measures, including:

- Ice to nipples and areola for a few minutes before feeding
- Allow breasts to air dry after feeding
- Express a few drops of milk onto dry nipples to promote healing
- Change breast pads frequently
- Begin nursing on the least sore breast first
- Avoid the use of petroleum based products or soap on the nipples

**NOTE:** It is important to teach mothers not to delay feedings or switch to bottle feeding due to sore nipples.

### Tab 5: How do I wake my baby?

To maintain breastmilk production and ensure adequate infant weight gain, newborns and young infants need to feed every 2-3 hours. Sometimes waking a drowsy baby to feed can be challenging.

Nurses should teach mothers strategies to wake a sleepy baby for feeding, including:

- Touch and talk to the baby
- Touch the infant's face with a warm, wet washcloth
- Change the infant's diaper
- Unwrap and undress the infant

Additionally, mothers must be taught infant feeding cues.

### Tab 6: Is my baby gaining weight?

It is common for newborns to lose up to 10% of birth weight in the first week of life. However, this weight should be regained by two weeks of age. Birth weight should double by 6 months of age and triple by one year. Infants should gain approximately 5-7 ounces/week during the first six months of life.

The best way for a mother to ensure appropriate weight gain is with frequent feedings and observing for signs of dehydration, such as sunken fontanelles, lack of wet diapers, and fewer tears when crying.

## Section 12 — Explore: Feeding Difficulties and Weaning

Melissa worried whether Taylor was getting enough milk. Her milk came in about day three postpartum, right on time. Melissa knew that she should count the diapers and there were eight wet ones that day (6 is the minimum). The baby had at least 3-6 bowel movements that day and the stool was yellow and soft. Taylor was getting enough breastmilk.

After about two weeks, Melissa started noticing that Taylor was spitting up a lot after each feeding. Newborns and infants have immature digestive systems. Occasionally, some antigens in foods the mother has eaten may pass into her milk. If the infant reacts to the mother's diet, the offending food should be identified and eliminated.

### Tab 1: 6 Months Later

Melissa continued to breastfeed but also started the baby on some solid foods.

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months after birth and breastfeeding in combination with solid foods until at least age 1. Breastfeeding is recommended if the client and the baby wish to continue.

It is often easiest to begin weaning when the baby starts the process. Sometimes it happens when solid foods are introduced around 6 months of age, but each baby is different.

### Tab 2: Taylor's 1st Birthday

Taylor started to decline her breastfeeding sessions. She was more interested in solid foods and pushed away from the breast. Melissa knew it was time to start the weaning process. She was careful to do it slowly.

### Tab 3: Weaning

The best way to begin weaning is slowly tapering off how long and how often a woman breastfeeds each day over the course of weeks or months. This will cause the milk supply to gradually diminish and prevent engorgement. Some suggest dropping a midday breastfeeding session first. Once one feeding is dropped, moms can work on dropping another.

## Section 15: Explore: Breastfeeding Complications: Engorgement

Engorgement occurs when the breasts fill, the milk ducts become swollen, and then cannot sufficiently empty.

### Causes of Engorgement

Transitional fullness due to venous congestion is most common at 72-96 hours postpartum, when milk production begins and the body is still balancing supply and demand. This uncomfortable fullness should only last 24 hours.

After the let-down reflex occurs, it is normal for the breasts to become firm, congested, and uncomfortably tight. Milk often spontaneously leaks. This happens less often as feedings settle into a pattern and the milk production is able to balance itself.

Engorgement can become an issue when feedings are spaced too far apart, skipped, or too brief to empty the breast sufficiently. Wearing a bra that is too tight can also prevent sufficient emptying. Engorged breasts are hard, painful, warm, and appear taut and shiny.

**NOTE:** The most important aspect of ongoing milk production and successful breastfeeding is regular emptying of the breast.

*(Image: Left image: Normal breast. Right image: Engorged breast with full milk glands and ducts.)*

### Prevention of Engorgement

To prevent engorgement of the breast, the mother should nurse the infant consistently. Infants should suckle for at least 15 minutes per feeding per breast and feed 8-12 times in 24 hours. If the baby is unable to feed this frequently, the mother should pump her breasts to remove breastmilk and avoid engorgement.

### Interventions

The nurse should teach the mother comfort measures if engorgement occurs, including:

- Apply warm compresses to the breasts before feeding to stimulate let-down and soften the breasts for latching
- Apply ice packs after nursing to slow refilling and provide comfort
- Choose a well-fitting bra to support the breasts and wear it daily
- Feed the infant consistently

## Section 16: Explore: Breastfeeding Complications-Mastitis

Mastitis is an inflammation of breast tissue in lactating women usually caused by an infection. When the breasts fill and cannot empty, milk stasis can lead to infection of the breast. A breast infection is typically caused by Staphylococcus aureus, methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), Escherichia coli, and streptococci entering through an injury such as a cracked or blistered nipple. Mastitis is usually unilateral and seldom occurs before the fourth week postpartum.

The postpartum nurse may not encounter mastitis but will teach the client how to prevent it using good hygiene practices before and after breastfeeding and keeping the breasts clean and dry.

The client should follow the guidelines for preventing engorgement and nipple injury and report flulike symptoms such as fever, chills, malaise, and headache. Often the area is painful, red, warm, and a lump (wedge-shaped) may be present which can progress to an abscess if left untreated.

Mastitis is treated medically with antibiotics. Comfort measures may include the use of moist heat, ice, breast support, and mild analgesics. If mastitis develops, it is important to evaluate the client's nipples and the infant's latch.

The client must continue to empty the breast through pumping or breastfeeding. The baby may still breastfeed during mastitis. However, if an abscess develops, the milk must not be consumed by the infant.

## Section 17: Breastfeeding: Nursing Care

At the time of delivery, the breasts have enlarged and the nipples have areolas that have darkened and become more pronounced in response to hormonal changes in preparation for breastfeeding.

The breasts should be smooth with even pigmentation. For the first few postpartum days, they should feel soft. As transitional milk arrives, the breasts become warm and fuller. Nipples should be supple, intact, and erect when stimulated.

Assessment of the breasts occurs when assisting with breastfeeding. It is also an opportunity to reinforce teaching regarding breastfeeding techniques and breast self-care.

The nurse provides the following guidance to the woman who is breastfeeding:

### Hygiene

- Use only warm water and allow your breasts to air dry.
- Wash your hands before and after breastfeeding.

### Breastmilk Storage

- If you pump extra milk, the expressed milk can be:
  - Used at room temperature within 4 hours
  - Refrigerated at under 4°C (39°F) for 72 hours (optimal); 5 days is acceptable
  - Frozen under −18°C (0°F) for up to 6 months
- Do not defrost or warm the milk in the microwave because it will heat unevenly. Instead, defrost it in the refrigerator and heat it up under warm water. It will need to be inverted a few times in the bottle to gently mix the separated milk components. Any that is not used within two hours should be discarded.
- Once frozen breastmilk has been thawed, do not refreeze it.

*(Medela, n.d.)*

### Formula Supplementation

Avoid the use of formula supplementation in the hospital unless medically indicated. Supplements may impede successful breastfeeding because they decrease feeding frequency and milk production.

### Drug Transfer to Breastmilk

Most medication taken by the client is compatible with breastfeeding; however, some drugs interfere with milk production. All prescription and over-the-counter medications should be approved by a healthcare provider.

If the client must take a medication that is harmful to the infant, she should pump her breasts and discard the milk while taking the medication. Some drugs may be taken right after feeding and may not reach the infant in harmful amounts.

The National Library of Medicine (2006) provides the LactMed database with information on the effects of drugs and other chemicals on breastfeeding. It also includes information on therapeutic alternatives, where appropriate.

### Other Guidelines

- Continue taking prenatal vitamins while breastfeeding.
- Continue to increase caloric intake by 400 calories/day.
- Consume adequate protein (71 grams/day).
- Continue to hydrate well, consuming 2-3 L of water/day.
- Avoid drinking alcohol, smoking tobacco, or using illicit substances while breastfeeding.
- Avoid excessive caffeine consumption (<200 mg/day).
- Be sure to use a contraceptive to avoid pregnancy, because breastfeeding does not prevent pregnancy.
- Remember to use proper breastfeeding and latching techniques.
