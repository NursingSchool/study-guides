# Week 1: Social Determinants of Health and Maternal-Child Nursing

## Section 1: Introduction: Social Determinants of Health

Pre-pregnancy health behaviors and health status are influenced by a variety of environmental and social factors, such as access to medical care and chronic stress. Some of these factors can affect and compound others, creating a vicious cycle. For instance, factors ranging from age to medical insurance coverage affect a woman's general health status. A woman's health status directly influences her risk of pregnancy complications and her child's cognitive and physical development. For some, the cycle will continue with future pregnancies.

Understanding the many factors that affect women and infants—both negatively and positively—is key to improving the health of all Americans, particularly the next generation.

This concept will explore some of the determinants of health and how a nurse can positively influence maternal outcomes.

## Section 3: Explore: What Are Social Determinants?

Social determinants of health are the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age. These circumstances are shaped by the distribution of money, power, and resources at global, national, and local levels. They are mostly responsible for health inequities—the unfair and avoidable differences in health status within and between countries.

Some examples of social determinants include:
- availability of resources to meet daily needs (safe housing, local food markets)
- access to educational, economic, and job opportunities
- access to health care services (insurance, providers)
- public transportation options (bus, train, car)
- social support (friends, family)
- substance abuse (alcohol, drugs)
- access to technology (lacking phone, internet access)
- social norms and attitudes (discrimination, racism, distrust of government)
- exposure to crime, violence, and social disorder (presence of trash, lack of cooperation in a community)
- socioeconomic conditions (concentrated poverty and the stressful conditions that accompany it)
- culture (e.g., food, religion, and healthcare practices)

**Social Determinants of Health (Healthy People, 2030):**
- Health Care Access and Quality
- Neighborhood and Built Environment
- Social and Community Context
- Economic Stability
- Education Access and Quality

## Section 5: Explore: Teenage Sex and Pregnancy

Molly is a 16-year-old who attends a local public high school. She spends a lot of time alone, as her mom works two jobs trying to make ends meet. Molly's mother got pregnant when she was 16 and her father left home when she was still an infant, so she has never met him. Molly has always looked for attention from boys and started having sex six months ago due to pressure from her boyfriend. Molly's period is now a week late. The only sex education her mother provided was abstinence, other birth control options were not discussed.

Teenage sexual activity and pregnancy are influenced by many factors.

### Slide 1: Peer Pressure

Peer pressure may occur from boys or girls within the peer group.

### Slide 2: Lack of Education

No or minimal sex education.

### Slide 3: History

Kids having kids is generational. Daughters of teen mothers are three times more likely to be teen parents.

### Slide 4: Income

Girls and women in lower socioeconomic situations may not have adequate education and don't always have money for birth control.

### Slide 5: Lack of Role Models

Teenagers need encouragement from positive role models and to hear reasons for avoiding pregnancy.

### Slide 6: Health Care Professionals

Nurses can play a key role in providing education in schools, clinics, and hospital settings. Teenagers need a non-judgmental provider that can encourage them to practice safe sex, answer questions, and avoid risky behaviors.

### Slide 7: Culture

In some cultures, high value is placed on early pregnancy and becoming pregnant validates one's feminine role. The family helps raise the child. Teenagers without this support need to consider the risks of raising a baby alone.

## Section 7: Explore: Race and Access to Care Impact on Health Outcomes

Race impacts a woman's preconception, prenatal, and interconception health care.
- African American, Native American/American Indian, and Alaska Native women are more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes than Caucasian women (CDC, 2024).
- Causes of pregnancy-related deaths in the United States include infection or sepsis, hemorrhage, cardiovascular conditions, cardiomyopathy, and thrombotic pulmonary embolism (CDC, 2024).

**Think About It:**

Lack of access to healthcare is also linked to negative maternal, infant, and child health outcomes.

For example, women who do not have access to healthcare services prior to pregnancy are more likely to engage in risky behaviors, such as smoking and drinking alcohol, which affect the woman's pregnancy and infant outcomes.

## Section 9: Explore: Abuse, Housing, Environmental Impact

**Social Determinants — Case Study: Maureen**

Maureen is 28 weeks pregnant with a history of diabetes. She has been living in and out of her car ever since she escaped an abusive relationship with the father of the baby, John. She occasionally goes to safe havens, but she wants to stay on the move as she is worried John will find her.

Maureen isn't consistent with her insulin as she doesn't always have the money for her medication. She lost her cell phone the last time she was at a shelter. The phone was most likely stolen. Thankfully, she just secured a job at a fitness facility and will be eligible for health insurance next month. Maureen's boss, Mary, allows her to take a shower in the locker-room each morning and has her clothes washed with the towel cleaning service.

Mary realizes that anything that she can help with during this pregnancy, and after the delivery, could possibly decrease risk factors for Maureen and the baby. Maureen needs to attend every prenatal appointment.

**Health Disparities:**

Pregnant with Diabetes or Hypertension:
- Primary C-section
- Preterm birth
- Shoulder dystocia due to risk of large baby
- Risk of large or small babies, depending on control of symptoms

Intimate Partner Violence & Exposure to Abuse:
- Limited prenatal care
- Increased risk of hospitalization
- Low birth weight infants

Unsafe Neighborhoods & Adverse Environmental Exposure:
- Preterm birth
- Lower birth weight infants

**Disparities specific to Maureen:**

Pregnant with diabetes or hypertension:
- Primary C-section
- Shoulder dystocia due to risk of large baby

Intimate partner violence & exposure to abuse:
- Limited prenatal care

Unsafe neighborhoods:
- Preterm birth

Notice that Maureen has risk factors in each section above. She is at risk of needing a C-section, having a large baby (diabetes), and jeopardizing her own health if she doesn't stabilize her diabetes and receive care throughout her pregnancy.

## Section 11: Explore: Nursing Role

Maria is a nurse who works for the health department tracking data regarding disparities for pregnant women and pregnancy mortality. She is preparing training for local hospitals and clinics.

Some data Maria could use in her presentation includes:
- According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2020), reducing racial disparities in pregnancy-related mortality will require the participation of multiple systems to address the factors affecting these disparities.
- These disparities are not unique to the United States. Resources include:
  - World Health Organization Maternal and Reproductive Health
  - World Health Organization Statistics

**What can nurses do?**
- Refer to the CDC for more information about reducing disparities in pregnancy-related mortality.
- Implement standardized protocols in quality improvement initiatives, especially among facilities that serve disproportionately affected communities.
- Identify and address implicit bias in healthcare that would likely improve client-provider interactions, health communication (empathy), and health outcomes.
- Provide education on:
  - peripartum racial and ethnic disparities and their root causes
  - best practices for shared decision-making
  - hiring diverse staff
  - service learning
- Engage diverse client, family, and community advocates who can represent important community partnerships on quality and safety leadership teams.
- Promote curricular integration with a focus on how to better educate nurses to understand the connections between social determinants of health and the challenges clients face.
- State and local Enhancing Reviews & Surveillance to Eliminate Maternal Mortality (ERASE MM) offer the best opportunity for further identifying priority strategies that will reduce disparities in pregnancy-related mortality.
