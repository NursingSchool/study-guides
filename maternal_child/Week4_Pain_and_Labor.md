## 1. Introduction to Pain and Labor

Labor and childbirth can be painful processes. Many medical, pharmaceutical, and non-pharmaceutical interventions can increase comfort, reduce pain, and decrease possible fear or anxiety during labor and childbirth.

This module will review some of these interventions and methods.

## 3. Explore: Pain and the 5Ps

All pain is subjective, difficult to define and quantify, and very personal. The pain associated with childbirth is unique for many reasons.

There are five components of labor that each impact the other throughout the labor and childbirth process: powers, passage, passenger, position, and psyche (5 Ps). (Interactive hotspot image; full transcript below.)

**The Five Ps of Birth**

**Passenger:** The size of the fetus's head and shoulders (in relationship to the mother's body) may increase the difficulty of delivery—causing an increase in pain. Compound presentations (the fetus's hand on the head) and posterior presentation are a few examples where the fetus may cause increased pain.

**Powers:** The force of uterine contractions and the fetus's body being pressed into the pelvis and through the birth canal creates a unique kind of pain. First, it is intermittent rather than constant. Even during late labor, a woman may be relatively comfortable between contractions. Also, childbirth pain is of two types: visceral and somatic.

- Visceral pain is a slow, deep, poorly localized pain that is often described as dull or aching. Visceral pain dominates during the first stage of labor as the uterus contracts and the cervix dilates. A woman may describe a little discomfort with contractions during early labor, while others describe it as the worst pain imaginable.
- Somatic pain is a quick, sharp pain that can be precisely localized. Somatic pain is most prominent during the late phase of the first stage of labor and during the second stage of labor as the descending fetus puts direct pressure on maternal tissues.

**Position:** Standing or walking during labor helps work with gravity and allows the pelvis to open and the fetus to move down into the birth canal, reducing pain overall. Leaning over encourages the fetus to move forward, taking the pressure off a woman's back.

**Psyche:** Several psychosocial variables influence a woman's experience of childbirth pain, including culture, anxiety, fear, previous experiences with pain, support system, preparation for childbirth, and expectations about pain control. For many, knowing that childbirth pain is part of a normal process, not related to injury or illness, brings comfort and strength.

- Many take comfort in knowing that labor pain will result in the birth of their child. This expectation and the care of her baby often increase labor pain tolerance. However, those for whom the arrival of a child is not a happy occasion may be very anxious and have more difficulty managing the psychological aspect of their labor pain.
- The time component of childbirth pain impacts the psyche in unique ways as well. In contrast to non-labor-related pain, the pregnant woman knows childbirth pain will happen and uses several months to mentally prepare for labor and to learn anxiety reduction techniques that will help her manage labor pain.
- Childbirth pain is self-limiting and has a foreseeable end. Although it may be intense, a woman can expect her labor to end in hours, rather than days, weeks, or months. Other kinds of pain may also be brief, but the baby's birth brings a rapid decrease in pain.

**Passageway:** Pain can instinctively lead a woman to assume different positions in labor to allow room for fetal descent through her pelvis.

## 4. Explore: Labor Pain Management - Opioid Analgesic Medications

Many women choose to use analgesic medications for pain management in childbirth. Opioid analgesics are the most common systemic, parenteral, pharmaceutical options used to manage childbirth pain.

The opioid analgesics to be administered are prescribed by the provider. These medications cause central nervous depression, which decreases the woman's perception of pain but causes sedation and respiratory depression as well. Because these medications cross the placenta and affect the fetus, causing depressed respiratory efforts of the neonate at delivery, it is important to administer them cautiously and at the right time in relation to impending delivery. If the fetus delivers close to the administration of the medication, the newborn's respiratory efforts may be decreased and may need medical intervention such as opioid antagonist administration.

The most common opioid analgesic medications used during labor and their nursing implications are:

**Nalbuphine Hydrochloride (Nubain) or Butorphanol Tartrate (Stadol)**
- These medications are not given to opiate-dependent women.
- These medications are often given just before an epidural for mild pain relief until the epidural begins working.
- Respiratory depression in the newborn may occur if these medications are given too close to delivery (monitor maternal and fetal effects closely).

**Meperidine Hydrochloride (Demerol)**
- This is a synthetic, morphine-like compound.
- If given too close to delivery, respiratory depression in the newborn may occur (monitor maternal and fetal effects closely).

**Nursing Implications**
- The nurse will monitor maternal vital signs and for signs of hypotension and respiratory depression.
- The fetal heart rate (FHR) pattern is also monitored closely by the nurse following the administration of parenteral, opioid analgesics. These drugs may impact the fetus indirectly if they cause maternal hypotension, or directly impact the fetal central nervous system. These impacts will often be displayed in the form of decreased FHR variability on the electronic fetal monitor.
- After delivery, nursing assessment of the newborn will focus on respiratory depression, circulation, and neurologic impairment (which will also impact the initial respiratory efforts).
- The antidote to these medications is naloxone (Narcan). It is an opioid antagonist that may be needed for the newborn to reverse the effects of a narcotic-opioid analgesic given too close to delivery to prevent narcotic-induced respiratory depression in neonates.

## 5. Explore: Labor Pain Management - Anesthetic Medications

Regional anesthetic pain management is commonly used at various points in the childbirth process. Unlike opioid analgesics, anesthetics provide pain relief along with a measure of numbness without impacting the level of consciousness or respiratory effort.

**Epidural Nerve Block**

An epidural nerve block is a common pain control method that provides both analgesia and anesthesia for vaginal labor and delivery as well as cesarean delivery. The epidural space is the area outside the dura mater, between the dura and the spinal cord.

(Slideshow transcript — 4 slides)

An anesthesiologist or certified nurse-anesthetist (CRNA) injects a particular concentration of local anesthetic and opioid combination into a tiny epidural space in the lumbar area of the spine for substantial vaginal labor and delivery pain relief. Breakthrough pressure may still be felt, but it is usually minimal and helps the woman when it comes time to push.

If a cesarean delivery is planned or becomes necessary after a failed labor, the level of the epidural on the spine can be extended upward to provide full anesthesia. The provider administers a higher concentration of anesthetic, resulting in loss of motor and sensory function when complete numbing is needed for a surgical abdominal incision.

After the medication is injected into the spine, the remaining thin catheter protrudes out from the insertion site in the back. The medication will likely continue as an infusion on a patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) pump with parameters ordered by the provider. A dressing is placed on top of the insertion site and tubing. The tubing is removed after delivery.

Maternal vasodilation and hypotension often result from the nerve blocking effects of the epidural medication, most likely within the first 15 minutes of initial or bolus administration, but may occur within the first hour. This may then be reflected in prolonged decelerations in the fetal heart rate pattern as the perfusion to the placenta is reduced. To prevent hypotension, a bolus of intravenous fluid will be infused prior to epidural placement and blood pressure will be closely monitored during and following epidural placement.

**Other Anesthetic Medications**

General anesthesia use is uncommon and typically only when an emergency cesarean is needed and there is no time for an epidural or spinal block to be inserted or if the woman is not a candidate for them.

Additional regional and local anesthetics may be used by the delivery provider in vaginal deliveries to cause numbness, for example, when an episiotomy will be done, or a laceration needs to be repaired. Also, a pudendal block may be used to numb the perineum for delivery.

## 7. Explore: Nursing Implications - Anesthetic Medications

Nursing care related to an epidural focuses on monitoring maternal and fetal status before, during, and after epidural placement and medication administration.

**Prior to and During Epidural Placement**

The nurse will ask the woman to empty her bladder one last time, ensure a pre-epidural bolus of the intravenous solution has been infused, continuously monitor maternal blood pressure and fetal heart rate pattern, assist the woman to maintain correct positioning during the procedure, and provide reassurance as the pain continues despite the need to hold still.

**After Epidural Placement**

*Maternal and Fetal Stability*
- Assist the woman to a comfortable position that maintains ideal maternal and fetal circulation; continue close monitoring of maternal blood pressure and fetal heart rate pattern. Maternal hypotension may occur following epidural administration, which can affect fetal perfusion.
- Monitor the medication's effects on maternal pain, motor, and sensory sensations.
- Placement of an indwelling urinary catheter may be necessary to keep the bladder empty.
- Occasionally, cerebral spinal fluid (CSF) leaks out if the dura is punctured during injection of the epidural, causing a spinal headache.
- Although maternal fever following epidural anesthesia is common, the reasoning is not clear. It does not necessarily indicate an infection, which can be ruled out by the absence of additional infection assessment cues.
- Other common side effects of epidural anesthesia include pruritis, shivering, nausea, and vomiting.
- The nurse monitors and maintains the epidural dressing and site to ensure it remains clean, dry, and intact.

*Impacts on Labor*

The relaxation that occurs following epidural pain relief can sometimes accelerate the labor progress and fetal descent without the sensations that may alert the patient and the nurse to impending delivery. For example:
- sudden fetal descent into the pelvis
- increasingly strong contractions
- sudden nausea and vomiting
- complete cervical dilation and effacement
- the feeling of pressure and/or an urge to push

*Impacts on Delivery*

Epidural may affect the ability to push during delivery because the patient has reduced feeling. The nurse will need to assist the mother with getting into a delivery position and provide additional interventions and guidance to aid in focusing on her pushing.

After delivery and possible perineal repairs, the epidural infusion is turned off and the catheter removed. The nurse should ensure that the catheter tip is intact and that a dressing is applied to the insertion site. The nurse monitors the woman's risk for falls, blood pressure, and for the presence of headaches.

## 9. Explore: Labor Pain - Nonpharmacologic Techniques and Support

**Common Interventions**

Although they may be appropriate for the well-being of a woman and fetus, common interventions often add discomfort to the natural pain of labor.

- Intravenous (IV) lines cause pain when inserted and remain noticeable to many women during labor. Fetal monitoring equipment is uncomfortable for some women, but others want to hear the sounds. Both may hamper a woman's mobility, which she might use to assume a more comfortable position. A woman may expect routine electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) and tolerate the inconvenience for reassurance of her baby's well-being with technology.
- A woman whose labor is induced or augmented often reports more pain and increased difficulty coping with it because contractions reach peak intensity quickly. Labor is more interventional with induction or augmentation. Vaginal examinations, amniotomy, and insertion of internal fetal monitoring devices also increase a woman's discomfort briefly because of vaginal and cervical stretching. Vaginal manipulation often stimulates a contraction because of a reflex known as Ferguson's reflex.

**Medications and Nonpharmacologics**

Women may choose to manage their labor pain with pain medications, nonpharmacologic techniques, or a combination of both. Nonpharmacologic techniques used in labor that can be calming and reduce pain include:

- Relaxation techniques, such as tactile stimulation, music, distraction, hydrotherapy, mental stimulation, and breathing techniques (such as Lamaze).
- The woman's partner, who is familiar with her likes and dislikes, can be instrumental in supporting these techniques and adjusting them as needed. This also helps the partner feel involved and helpful in the process.
- Some women employ birth coaches or doulas to support them during labor and childbirth, instead of or in addition to a partner. Doulas are trained, experienced, childbirth support providers that some women, and even couples, hire to provide reassurance, touch, companionship, and comfort during labor. They do not perform any medical skills, but their care is shown to reduce pain, complications, and duration of labor in some cases.

**Activity and Exercise**

- Ambulation and/or physical movement in labor, if permitted by the healthcare provider, may be a more comfortable pain control option for the woman in labor.
- Ambulation may be contraindicated after the administration of analgesics in order to maintain continuous monitoring of the fetus and to assess for the impact of the medication on the fetus. Also, if the membranes are ruptured and the presenting part is not fully engaged in the pelvis, cord prolapse is a higher risk when ambulating.
- Often, in the labor and delivery unit, women walk the halls with their support person, stop during contractions, go in their rooms, possibly rock back and forth while leaning forward on the bed, or perhaps take a bath or shower. When labor advances to a certain point, it may be too uncomfortable for the woman to ambulate.

## 12. Explore: Labor Pain - Nursing Care

Pain is personal and unique to each person, influenced by many variables. Labor and delivery nurses must be sensitive to the woman's cultural and religious values and beliefs, which may impact the woman's feelings about childbirth and how pain is expressed.

**Expressions**

The variations in expression are not quantifiable indications of pain level and may be affected by cultural norms. A woman who tends to express labor pain in an expressive manner is not necessarily in more pain than a woman who expresses labor pain in a stoic manner.

Also, the family's role in labor may vary based on cultural beliefs and practices. For example, a very quiet partner may seem uncaring to some, but it may be common practice in their culture. The nurse should not assume that a particular cultural background predicts how the woman will express pain, or how the family members will interact during labor.

**Language Barrier**

A language barrier in labor and childbirth can increase anxiety for women who cannot verbally express their needs and feelings to the healthcare team providing care or understand the explanations of procedures and reassurances that are provided.

**Remember!** Whenever possible, it is best that a bilingual nurse cares for the family with a language barrier. If that is not possible, a translator must be made available.

**Cultural Sensitivity**

Nursing considerations related to culturally sensitive pain control during labor involve encouraging women to express themselves in the way they feel most comfortable. Avoid praising expressions of pain that reflect one's own beliefs in how pain should be expressed (for example, praising a woman for being strong and brave when she is stoic and calm during contractions). Conversely, avoid correcting expressions of pain that reflect one's own belief of what constitutes inappropriate behavior (for example, assuming weakness is the reason a woman is being more expressive during contractions).

**Physical Nursing Care**

If welcomed by the laboring woman, physical nursing care in labor can include: (slideshow transcript — 5 slides)

- Assisting with coaching of breathing during contractions to ensure adequate oxygenation and maximum calm and focus.
- Deep, cleansing breaths before and after contractions, avoid rapid breathing during contractions.
- Guidance during the second stage of labor to ensure the woman is pushing correctly to avoid complications.
- Provide non-pharmaceutical comfort and pain relief measures such as guided imagery, calming environment, ice chips, cool cloths, and back rubs.
- Continual monitoring of maternal well-being including emotional status.
